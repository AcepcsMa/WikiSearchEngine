<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="September_1_-_Fri">September 1 - Fri</span></h2>
<div style="box-sizing: border-box; margin:0.5em; width: 600px; border: 3px #ccccff solid; padding: 11px; text-align: center; background-color: white;">
<div style="margin-bottom: 0.3em; font-size: 150%;"><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Picture_of_the_day" title="Wikipedia:Picture of the day">Picture of the day</a></div>
<div style="display: inline-block; width: 320px; vertical-align: middle;"><a href="/wiki/File:Las_Meninas,_by_Diego_Vel%C3%A1zquez,_from_Prado_in_Google_Earth.jpg" class="image" title="Las Meninas"><img alt="Las Meninas" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/31/Las_Meninas%2C_by_Diego_Vel%C3%A1zquez%2C_from_Prado_in_Google_Earth.jpg/320px-Las_Meninas%2C_by_Diego_Vel%C3%A1zquez%2C_from_Prado_in_Google_Earth.jpg" width="320" height="368" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/31/Las_Meninas%2C_by_Diego_Vel%C3%A1zquez%2C_from_Prado_in_Google_Earth.jpg/480px-Las_Meninas%2C_by_Diego_Vel%C3%A1zquez%2C_from_Prado_in_Google_Earth.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/31/Las_Meninas%2C_by_Diego_Vel%C3%A1zquez%2C_from_Prado_in_Google_Earth.jpg/640px-Las_Meninas%2C_by_Diego_Vel%C3%A1zquez%2C_from_Prado_in_Google_Earth.jpg 2x" data-file-width="26065" data-file-height="30000" /></a></div>
<div style="display: inline-block; margin-left: 4px; width: 244px; vertical-align: middle;"><i><b><a href="/wiki/Las_Meninas" title="Las Meninas">Las Meninas</a></b></i> is a 1656 painting in the <a href="/wiki/Museo_del_Prado" title="Museo del Prado">Museo del Prado</a> in <a href="/wiki/Madrid" title="Madrid">Madrid</a>, by <a href="/wiki/Diego_Vel%C3%A1zquez" title="Diego Velázquez">Diego Velázquez</a>, the leading artist of the <a href="/wiki/Spanish_Golden_Age" title="Spanish Golden Age">Spanish Golden Age</a>. It shows a large room in the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Alc%C3%A1zar_of_Madrid" class="mw-redirect" title="Royal Alcázar of Madrid">Royal Alcázar of Madrid</a> during the reign of King <a href="/wiki/Philip_IV_of_Spain" title="Philip IV of Spain">Philip IV of Spain</a>, and presents several figures, including the young <a href="/wiki/Margaret_Theresa_of_Spain" title="Margaret Theresa of Spain">Infanta Margaret Theresa</a> surrounded by her entourage. <i>Las Meninas</i> has been recognised as one of the most important paintings in <a href="/wiki/Western_art_history" class="mw-redirect" title="Western art history">Western art history</a>, and its complex and enigmatic composition has frequently been the subject of analysis. It has been described as "Velázquez's supreme achievement, a highly self-conscious, calculated demonstration of what painting could achieve, and perhaps the most searching comment ever made on the possibilities of the easel painting".<small style="display: block; margin-top: 0.8em;">Painting: <a href="/wiki/Diego_Vel%C3%A1zquez" title="Diego Velázquez">Diego Velázquez</a></small>

The central <a href="/wiki/Religious_text" title="Religious text">religious text</a> of <a href="/wiki/Islam" title="Islam">Islam</a>, the Quran is believed by <a href="/wiki/Muslim" title="Muslim">Muslims</a> to have been <a href="/wiki/Revelation" title="Revelation">revealed</a> by <a href="/wiki/God_in_Islam" title="God in Islam">God</a> to <a href="/wiki/Muhammad_in_Islam" title="Muhammad in Islam">Muhammad</a> through the angel <a href="/wiki/Gabriel" title="Gabriel">Gabriel</a>. The Quran consists of 114 <a href="/wiki/Surah" title="Surah">chapters</a> (<i>surah</i>), each comprising a different number of <a href="/wiki/Ayah" title="Ayah">verses</a> (<i>ayat</i>). Shown in this folio is parts of <a href="/wiki/Al-Fath" title="Al-Fath">Al-Fath</a> (48), verses 27-28.<small style="display: block; margin-top: 0.8em;">Folio: Unknown</small>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="September_3_-_Sun">September 3 - Sun</span></h2>
<div style="box-sizing: border-box; margin:0.5em; width: 600px; border: 3px #ccccff solid; padding: 11px; text-align: center; background-color: white;">
<div style="margin-bottom: 0.3em; font-size: 150%;"><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Picture_of_the_day" title="Wikipedia:Picture of the day">Picture of the day</a></div>
<div style="display: inline-block; width: 380px; vertical-align: middle;"><a href="/wiki/File:Hafnium_ebeam_remelted.jpg" class="image" title="Hafnium"><img alt="Hafnium" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/af/Hafnium_ebeam_remelted.jpg/380px-Hafnium_ebeam_remelted.jpg" width="380" height="321" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/af/Hafnium_ebeam_remelted.jpg/570px-Hafnium_ebeam_remelted.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/af/Hafnium_ebeam_remelted.jpg/760px-Hafnium_ebeam_remelted.jpg 2x" data-file-width="4256" data-file-height="3592" /></a></div>
<div style="display: inline-block; margin-left: 4px; width: 184px; vertical-align: middle;"><b><a href="/wiki/Hafnium" title="Hafnium">Hafnium</a></b> is a <a href="/wiki/Chemical_element" title="Chemical element">chemical element</a> with the symbol Hf and <a href="/wiki/Atomic_number" title="Atomic number">atomic number</a> 72. A <a href="/wiki/Lustre_(mineralogy)" title="Lustre (mineralogy)">lustrous</a>, silvery gray, <a href="/wiki/Tetravalence" title="Tetravalence">tetravalent</a> <a href="/wiki/Transition_metal" title="Transition metal">transition metal</a>, hafnium chemically resembles <a href="/wiki/Zirconium" title="Zirconium">zirconium</a> and is found in many zirconium <a href="/wiki/Mineral" title="Mineral">minerals</a>. Its existence was <a href="/wiki/Mendeleev%27s_predicted_elements" title="Mendeleev's predicted elements">predicted by Dmitri Mendeleev</a> in 1869, though it was not identified until 1923, making it the last <a href="/wiki/Stable_isotope" class="mw-redirect" title="Stable isotope">stable</a> element to be discovered. Hafnium is named for <a href="/wiki/Copenhagen#Etymology" title="Copenhagen">Copenhagen</a>, where it was discovered.<small style="display: block; margin-top: 0.8em;">Photograph: <a href="/wiki/User:Alchemist-hp" title="User:Alchemist-hp">Heinrich Pniok</a></small>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="September_4_-_Mon">September 4 - Mon</span></h2>
<div style="box-sizing: border-box; margin:0.5em; width: 600px; border: 3px #ccccff solid; padding: 11px; text-align: center; background-color: white;">
<div style="margin-bottom: 0.3em; font-size: 150%;"><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Picture_of_the_day" title="Wikipedia:Picture of the day">Picture of the day</a></div>
<div style="display: inline-block; width: 300px; vertical-align: middle;"><a href="/wiki/File:Vittore_Carpaccio_-_Young_Knight_in_a_Landscape_-_Google_Art_Project.jpg" class="image" title="Young Knight in a Landscape"><img alt="Young Knight in a Landscape" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0e/Vittore_Carpaccio_-_Young_Knight_in_a_Landscape_-_Google_Art_Project.jpg/300px-Vittore_Carpaccio_-_Young_Knight_in_a_Landscape_-_Google_Art_Project.jpg" width="300" height="431" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0e/Vittore_Carpaccio_-_Young_Knight_in_a_Landscape_-_Google_Art_Project.jpg/450px-Vittore_Carpaccio_-_Young_Knight_in_a_Landscape_-_Google_Art_Project.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0e/Vittore_Carpaccio_-_Young_Knight_in_a_Landscape_-_Google_Art_Project.jpg/600px-Vittore_Carpaccio_-_Young_Knight_in_a_Landscape_-_Google_Art_Project.jpg 2x" data-file-width="20879" data-file-height="30000" /></a></div>
<div style="display: inline-block; margin-left: 4px; width: 264px; vertical-align: middle;"><i><b><a href="/wiki/Young_Knight_in_a_Landscape" title="Young Knight in a Landscape">Young Knight in a Landscape</a></b></i> is a painting by the <a href="/wiki/Italian_Renaissance" title="Italian Renaissance">Italian Renaissance</a> artist <a href="/wiki/Vittore_Carpaccio" title="Vittore Carpaccio">Vittore Carpaccio</a>. Dated 1510, it has been called the earliest full-length portrait in <a href="/wiki/Western_painting" title="Western painting">Western painting</a>. The identity of its subject has been debated, with proposed sitters including the <a href="/wiki/Duchy_of_Urbino" title="Duchy of Urbino">Duke of Urbino</a>, <a href="/wiki/Francesco_Maria_I_della_Rovere,_Duke_of_Urbino" title="Francesco Maria I della Rovere, Duke of Urbino">Francesco Maria I della Rovere</a>; as well as the Venetian patrician <a href="/wiki/Gabrielli_(Gabrielli_di_Gubbio)" title="Gabrielli (Gabrielli di Gubbio)">Marco Gabriel</a>. It is now held by the <a href="/wiki/Thyssen-Bornemisza_Museum" title="Thyssen-Bornemisza Museum">Thyssen-Bornemisza Museum</a> in <a href="/wiki/Madrid" title="Madrid">Madrid</a>.<small style="display: block; margin-top: 0.8em;">Painting: <a href="/wiki/Vittore_Carpaccio" title="Vittore Carpaccio">Vittore Carpaccio</a></small>

<div style="box-sizing: border-box; margin:0.5em; width: 600px; border: 3px #ccccff solid; padding: 11px; text-align: center; background-color: white;">
<div style="margin-bottom: 0.3em; font-size: 150%;"><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Picture_of_the_day" title="Wikipedia:Picture of the day">Picture of the day</a></div>
<div style="display: inline-block; width: 280px; vertical-align: middle;"><a href="/wiki/File:Sidney_Hall_-_Urania%27s_Mirror_-_Cancer.jpg" class="image" title="Cancer (constellation)"><img alt="Cancer (constellation)" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ce/Sidney_Hall_-_Urania%27s_Mirror_-_Cancer.jpg/280px-Sidney_Hall_-_Urania%27s_Mirror_-_Cancer.jpg" width="280" height="400" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ce/Sidney_Hall_-_Urania%27s_Mirror_-_Cancer.jpg/420px-Sidney_Hall_-_Urania%27s_Mirror_-_Cancer.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ce/Sidney_Hall_-_Urania%27s_Mirror_-_Cancer.jpg/560px-Sidney_Hall_-_Urania%27s_Mirror_-_Cancer.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3280" data-file-height="4690" /></a></div>
<div style="display: inline-block; margin-left: 4px; width: 284px; vertical-align: middle;"><b><a href="/wiki/Cancer_(constellation)" title="Cancer (constellation)">Cancer</a></b> is a medium-size constellation and one of the twelve <a href="/wiki/Constellation" title="Constellation">constellations</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Zodiac" title="Zodiac">zodiac</a>. Its name is <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> for <a href="/wiki/Crab" title="Crab">crab</a>, and it is commonly represented as one. Cancer's stars are rather faint, with its brightest star <a href="/wiki/Beta_Cancri" title="Beta Cancri">Beta Cancri</a> having an <a href="/wiki/Apparent_magnitude" title="Apparent magnitude">apparent magnitude</a> of 3.5. It contains two stars with known <a href="/wiki/Exoplanet" title="Exoplanet">planets</a>. Located at the center of the constellation is <a href="/wiki/Beehive_Cluster" title="Beehive Cluster">Praesepe</a> (Messier 44), one of the closest <a href="/wiki/Open_cluster" title="Open cluster">open clusters</a> to Earth and a popular target for amateur astronomers.
<p>Cancer is shown here as depicted in <i><a href="/wiki/Urania%27s_Mirror" title="Urania's Mirror">Urania's Mirror</a></i>, a set of constellation cards published in London in about 1825.<small style="display: block; margin-top: 0.8em;">Illustration: <a href="/wiki/Sidney_Hall" title="Sidney Hall">Sidney Hall</a>; restoration: <a href="/wiki/User:Adam_Cuerden" title="User:Adam Cuerden">Adam Cuerden</a></small></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="September_8_-_Fri">September 8 - Fri</span></h2>
<div style="box-sizing: border-box; margin:0.5em; width: 600px; border: 3px #ccccff solid; padding: 11px; text-align: center; background-color: white;">
<div style="margin-bottom: 0.3em; font-size: 150%;"><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Picture_of_the_day" title="Wikipedia:Picture of the day">Picture of the day</a></div>
<div style="display: inline-block; width: 300px; vertical-align: middle;"><a href="/wiki/File:Sassoferrato_-_Jungfrun_i_b%C3%B6n.jpg" class="image" title="Mary, mother of Jesus"><img alt="Mary, mother of Jesus" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/48/Sassoferrato_-_Jungfrun_i_b%C3%B6n.jpg/300px-Sassoferrato_-_Jungfrun_i_b%C3%B6n.jpg" width="300" height="377" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/48/Sassoferrato_-_Jungfrun_i_b%C3%B6n.jpg/450px-Sassoferrato_-_Jungfrun_i_b%C3%B6n.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/48/Sassoferrato_-_Jungfrun_i_b%C3%B6n.jpg/600px-Sassoferrato_-_Jungfrun_i_b%C3%B6n.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3360" data-file-height="4226" /></a></div>
<div style="display: inline-block; margin-left: 4px; width: 264px; vertical-align: middle;"><b><a href="/wiki/Mary,_mother_of_Jesus" title="Mary, mother of Jesus">Mary</a></b> was a 1st-century <a href="/wiki/Galilean" title="Galilean">Galilean</a> Jewish woman of <a href="/wiki/Nazareth" title="Nazareth">Nazareth</a>, and the mother of <a href="/wiki/Jesus" title="Jesus">Jesus</a> according to the <a href="/wiki/New_Testament" title="New Testament">New Testament</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Quran" title="Quran">Quran</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Canonical_gospels" class="mw-redirect" title="Canonical gospels">gospels</a> of <a href="/wiki/Gospel_of_Matthew" title="Gospel of Matthew">Matthew</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gospel_of_Luke" title="Gospel of Luke">Luke</a> describe Mary as a <a href="/wiki/Virginity" title="Virginity">virgin</a>, and Christians believe that she <a href="/wiki/Virgin_birth_of_Jesus" title="Virgin birth of Jesus">conceived her son while a virgin</a> by the <a href="/wiki/Holy_Spirit_(Christianity)" class="mw-redirect" title="Holy Spirit (Christianity)">Holy Spirit</a>. The birth of Jesus took place when Mary was already <a href="/wiki/Betrothal" class="mw-redirect" title="Betrothal">betrothed</a> to <a href="/wiki/Saint_Joseph" title="Saint Joseph">Joseph</a> and was awaiting the concluding rite of marriage. Mary has been <a href="/wiki/Veneration" title="Veneration">venerated</a> since <a href="/wiki/Early_Christianity" title="Early Christianity">Early Christianity</a>, and is considered by millions to be the most meritorious <a href="/wiki/Saint" title="Saint">saint</a> of the religion.
<p>Mary is depicted here in <i>The Virgin in Prayer</i>, a 17th century <a href="/wiki/Oil_painting" title="Oil painting">oil painting</a> on canvas by the Italian artist <a href="/wiki/Giovanni_Battista_Salvi_da_Sassoferrato" title="Giovanni Battista Salvi da Sassoferrato">Giovanni Battista Salvi da Sassoferrato</a>.<small style="display: block; margin-top: 0.8em;">Painting: <a href="/wiki/Giovanni_Battista_Salvi_da_Sassoferrato" title="Giovanni Battista Salvi da Sassoferrato">Giovanni Battista Salvi da Sassoferrato</a></small></p>

<div style="display: inline-block; margin-left: 4px; width: 214px; vertical-align: middle;">The <b><a href="/wiki/Queen_Elizabeth_II_Great_Court" title="Queen Elizabeth II Great Court">Queen Elizabeth II Great Court</a></b> is the central <a href="/wiki/Quadrangle_(architecture)" title="Quadrangle (architecture)">quadrangle</a> of the <a href="/wiki/British_Museum" title="British Museum">British Museum</a> in London. Based on a 1970s design by <a href="/wiki/Colin_St_John_Wilson" title="Colin St John Wilson">Colin St John Wilson</a>, it was developed during the late 1990s by <a href="/wiki/Foster_and_Partners" title="Foster and Partners">Foster and Partners</a> after the <a href="/wiki/British_Library" title="British Library">British Library</a> relocated to its own building. In its centre is the <a href="/wiki/British_Museum_Reading_Room" title="British Museum Reading Room">British Museum Reading Room</a>, formerly the main reading room of the Library. The Court, covered by a <a href="/wiki/Tessellated_roof" title="Tessellated roof">tessellated roof</a>, also includes twelve sculptures from the British Museum's collection.<small style="display: block; margin-top: 0.8em;">Photograph: <a href="/wiki/User:Diliff" title="User:Diliff">David Iliff</a></small>

<div style="display: inline-block; margin-left: 4px; width: 174px; vertical-align: middle;">The <b><a href="/wiki/1804_dollar" title="1804 dollar">1804 dollar</a></b> is a <a href="/wiki/Dollar_coin_(United_States)" title="Dollar coin (United States)">dollar coin</a> struck by the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Mint" title="United States Mint">Mint of the United States</a>; despite the date on the coin, none were struck before the 1830s. First created for use in special <a href="/wiki/Proof_coinage" title="Proof coinage">proof coin</a> sets used as diplomatic gifts during <a href="/wiki/Edmund_Roberts_(diplomat)" title="Edmund Roberts (diplomat)">Edmund Roberts</a>' trips to <a href="/wiki/Thailand" title="Thailand">Siam</a> and <a href="/wiki/Muscat_and_Oman" title="Muscat and Oman">Muscat</a>, several restrikes were ordered in the 1840s. Today, only fifteen specimens are known to exist, divided into three slightly different classes. The Class III example pictured is one of only six known, and sold in a 2009 auction for <a href="/wiki/United_States_dollar" title="United States dollar">USD</a> $2,300,000.<small style="display: block; margin-top: 0.8em;">Coin: <a href="/wiki/United_States_Mint" title="United States Mint">Mint of the United States</a>; photograph: <a href="/wiki/Heritage_Auctions" title="Heritage Auctions">Heritage Auctions</a></small>

<div style="padding-top: 0.3em;">Act I, Scene I of <i><b><a href="/wiki/King_Lear" title="King Lear">King Lear</a></b></i>, a play by <a href="/wiki/William_Shakespeare" title="William Shakespeare">William Shakespeare</a> first performed in 1606, as depicted by <a href="/wiki/Edwin_Austin_Abbey" title="Edwin Austin Abbey">Edwin Austin Abbey</a>. Based on the legend of <a href="/wiki/Leir_of_Britain" title="Leir of Britain">Leir of Britain</a>, it depicts <a href="/wiki/King_Lear" title="King Lear">King Lear</a>'s gradual descent into madness after he disposes of his kingdom to two of his three daughters based on their flattery. This tragedy has frequently been adapted for the stage and motion pictures, with the title role coveted by many of the world's most accomplished actors.
<p>In this scene, <a href="/wiki/Cordelia_(King_Lear)" title="Cordelia (King Lear)">Cordelia</a>, the youngest of Lear's three daughters, is banished for refusing to profess her love in return for one third of the kingdom. <div style="display: inline-block; margin-left: 4px; width: 264px; vertical-align: middle;"><b><a href="/wiki/Elisha" title="Elisha">Elisha</a></b> was a <a href="/wiki/Hebrew" class="mw-redirect" title="Hebrew">Hebrew</a> <a href="/wiki/Prophet" title="Prophet">prophet</a> and a <a href="/wiki/Thaumaturgy" title="Thaumaturgy">wonder-worker</a> who is venerated in <a href="/wiki/Judaism" title="Judaism">Judaism</a>, <a href="/wiki/Christianity" title="Christianity">Christianity</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Islam" title="Islam">Islam</a>. According to the <a href="/wiki/Books_of_Kings" title="Books of Kings">Books of Kings</a>, he was anointed the successor of <a href="/wiki/Elijah" title="Elijah">Elijah</a> and served as the "prophet in Israel" for more than six decades. During this time, many miracles were attributed to him.
<p>Shown here is <i>The Miracle at the Grave of Elisha</i>, an oil painting on panel which was completed by the Dutch painter <a href="/wiki/Jan_Nagel_(painter)" title="Jan Nagel (painter)">Jan Nagel</a> in 1596. It depicts part of the <a href="/wiki/Bible" title="Bible">Biblical</a> account of Elisha, a <a href="/wiki/Miracle" title="Miracle">miracle</a> that occurred after his death. Following 2&#160;Kings&#160;13:21, the painting shows a dead man who was "revived, and stood up on his feet" after touching Elisha's interred bones.<small style="display: block; margin-top: 0.8em;">Painting: <a href="/wiki/Jan_Nagel_(painter)" title="Jan Nagel (painter)">Jan Nagel</a></small></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="September_29_-_Fri">September 29 - Fri</span></h2>
<div style="box-sizing: border-box; margin:0.5em; width: 600px; border: 3px #ccccff solid; padding: 11px; text-align: center; background-color: white;">
<div style="margin-bottom: 0.3em; font-size: 150%;"><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Picture_of_the_day" title="Wikipedia:Picture of the day">Picture of the day</a></div>
<div style="display: inline-block; width: 380px; vertical-align: middle;"><a href="/wiki/File:Vanguard_at_Faslane_02.jpg" class="image" title="HMS Vanguard"><img alt="HMS Vanguard" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b1/Vanguard_at_Faslane_02.jpg/380px-Vanguard_at_Faslane_02.jpg" width="380" height="287" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b1/Vanguard_at_Faslane_02.jpg/570px-Vanguard_at_Faslane_02.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b1/Vanguard_at_Faslane_02.jpg/760px-Vanguard_at_Faslane_02.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2547" data-file-height="1921" /></a></div>
<div style="display: inline-block; margin-left: 4px; width: 184px; vertical-align: middle;">The <a href="/wiki/Ballistic_missile_submarine" title="Ballistic missile submarine">ballistic missile submarine</a> <b><a href="/wiki/HMS_Vanguard_(S28)" title="HMS Vanguard (S28)">HMS <i>Vanguard</i></a></b>, lead ship of <a href="/wiki/Vanguard-class_submarine" title="Vanguard-class submarine">her class</a>, arrives at <a href="/wiki/HMNB_Clyde" title="HMNB Clyde">HMNB Clyde</a> in <a href="/wiki/Faslane,_Scotland" class="mw-redirect" title="Faslane, Scotland">Faslane, Scotland</a>, following a patrol. Ballistic missile submarines like <i>Vanguard</i> were introduced during the <a href="/wiki/Cold_War" title="Cold War">Cold War</a>, and serve as the third leg of the <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_triad" title="Nuclear triad">nuclear triad</a> in countries which also operate nuclear-armed land-based missiles and aircraft. Their intended role in the nuclear triad is to provide a <a href="/wiki/Second-strike" class="mw-redirect" title="Second-strike">second-strike</a> capability in a defensive capacity, or to offer nuclear-armed nations a <a href="/wiki/First-strike" class="mw-redirect" title="First-strike">first-strike</a> weapons platform. In addition to the first- and second-strike capabilities, <i>Vanguard</i> also carries a <a href="/wiki/Letters_of_last_resort" title="Letters of last resort">handwritten letter</a> from the <a href="/wiki/Prime_Minister_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Prime Minister of the United Kingdom">Prime Minister of the United Kingdom</a> which contains orders on what action to take in the event that an enemy nuclear strike has destroyed the <a href="/wiki/Government_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Government of the United Kingdom">British government</a>.<small style="display: block; margin-top: 0.8em;">Photograph: Tam McDonald</small>

<div style="display: inline-block; width: 270px; vertical-align: middle;"><a href="/wiki/File:Frederick_Sleigh_Roberts,_1st_Earl_Roberts_by_John_Singer_Sargent.jpg" class="image" title="Frederick Roberts, 1st Earl Roberts"><img alt="Frederick Roberts, 1st Earl Roberts" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/60/Frederick_Sleigh_Roberts%2C_1st_Earl_Roberts_by_John_Singer_Sargent.jpg/270px-Frederick_Sleigh_Roberts%2C_1st_Earl_Roberts_by_John_Singer_Sargent.jpg" width="270" height="422" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/60/Frederick_Sleigh_Roberts%2C_1st_Earl_Roberts_by_John_Singer_Sargent.jpg/405px-Frederick_Sleigh_Roberts%2C_1st_Earl_Roberts_by_John_Singer_Sargent.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/60/Frederick_Sleigh_Roberts%2C_1st_Earl_Roberts_by_John_Singer_Sargent.jpg/540px-Frederick_Sleigh_Roberts%2C_1st_Earl_Roberts_by_John_Singer_Sargent.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2400" data-file-height="3751" /></a></div>
<div style="display: inline-block; margin-left: 4px; width: 294px; vertical-align: middle;"><b><a href="/wiki/Frederick_Roberts,_1st_Earl_Roberts" title="Frederick Roberts, 1st Earl Roberts">Frederick Roberts, 1st Earl Roberts</a></b> (1832–1914), was a British soldier who was one of the most successful commanders of the 19th century. He served in the <a href="/wiki/Indian_rebellion_of_1857" class="mw-redirect" title="Indian rebellion of 1857">Indian Rebellion</a>, the <a href="/wiki/1868_Expedition_to_Abyssinia" class="mw-redirect" title="1868 Expedition to Abyssinia">Expedition to Abyssinia</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Second_Anglo-Afghan_War" title="Second Anglo-Afghan War">Second Anglo-Afghan War</a> before leading British Forces to success in the <a href="/wiki/Second_Boer_War" title="Second Boer War">Second Boer War</a>. He also became the last <a href="/wiki/Commander-in-Chief_of_the_Forces" title="Commander-in-Chief of the Forces">Commander-in-Chief of the Forces</a> before the post was abolished in 1904.<small style="display: block; margin-top: 0.8em;">Painting: <a href="/wiki/John_Singer_Sargent" title="John Singer Sargent">John Singer Sargent</a></small>
