<p><b>Dr. Ernst Freiherr von Bibra</b> (9 June 1806 in <a href="/wiki/Schwebheim" title="Schwebheim">Schwebheim</a> – 5 June 1878 in <a href="/wiki/Nuremberg" title="Nuremberg">Nuremberg</a>) was a German Naturalist (<a href="/wiki/Natural_history" title="Natural history">Natural history</a> scientist) and author. Ernst was a botanist, zoologist, metallurgist, chemist, geographer, travel writer, novelist, duellist, art collector and trailblazer in <a href="/wiki/Ethnopsychopharmacology" title="Ethnopsychopharmacology">ethnopsychopharmacology</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biography">Biography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ernst_von_Bibra&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Biography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Ernst's father, Ferdinand Johann <a href="/wiki/Von_Bibra" class="mw-redirect" title="Von Bibra">von Bibra</a>, (*1756; † 1807), fought under General <a href="/wiki/Jean_Baptiste_Donatien_de_Vimeur_Rochambeau" class="mw-redirect" title="Jean Baptiste Donatien de Vimeur Rochambeau">Rochambeau</a> in the <a href="/wiki/American_Revolutionary_War" title="American Revolutionary War">American Revolutionary War</a> on behalf of the colonies. Later he married his brother's daughter, Lucretia Wilhelmine Caroline <a href="/wiki/Von_Bibra" class="mw-redirect" title="Von Bibra">von Bibra</a>,(1778. + 1857). Ernst's father died when he was 1½ years old and Baron Christoph Franz von Hutten (d. 1830) raised Ernst in Würzburg. He graduated at nineteen from a boarding school in Neuberg on the Danube. Baron Ernst <a href="/wiki/Von_Bibra" class="mw-redirect" title="Von Bibra">von Bibra</a> started studying law at <a href="/wiki/W%C3%BCrzburg" title="Würzburg">Würzburg</a> but soon changed over to the natural sciences, especially chemistry. ("Dr. med. &amp; phil." Doctor of medicine &amp; PhD) Martin Haseneier, in his Foreword to the 1995 translation <i>Plant Intoxicants</i> relates that Ernst fought in no less than 49 <a href="/wiki/Duels" class="mw-redirect" title="Duels">duels</a> as a young man! Many of his works have been reprinted in recent years. Besides the castle and estate at Schwebheim, Ernst was the owner of a half interest in the castle and estate at <a href="/wiki/Willershausen_(Herleshausen)" title="Willershausen (Herleshausen)">Willershausen (Herleshausen)</a>. Ernst sold his half of the castle and estate of <a href="/wiki/Willershausen_(Herleshausen)" title="Willershausen (Herleshausen)">Willershausen (Herleshausen)</a> in 1850 to the Landgrave Carl August of Hesse.</p>
<p>He produced: <i>Chemical Research on Various Varieties of <a href="/wiki/Pus" title="Pus">Pus</a></i> (Berlin 1842); <i>Chemical Research on the Bones and Teeth of Humans and Other Vertebrates</i> (Schweinfurt 1844) and <i>Helpful Tables for the Recognition of the Substances of Zoological Chemistry</i> (Erlangen 1846). Then in cooperation with Geist he published: <i>Investigations of the Diseases of the Workers in the Phosphorus Match Factories</i> (Erlangen 1847) as well as with Harleß <i>The Events of the Investigations of the Effects of Sulphur Fumes</i> (Erlangen 1847). After he had published <i>Chemical Fragments Concerning the Liver and Gall-Bladder</i> (Braunschweig 1849), he went to Brazil and around Cape Horn. He reported on this trip in his <i>Trips in South America</i> (Mannheim 1854, 2 vols.). After his return he lived mostly in Nuremberg where he also set up his rich collections of natural history <a href="/wiki/Ethnographic" class="mw-redirect" title="Ethnographic">ethnographics</a> and died on 5 June 1878. Here he published <i>Comparative Investigations of the Human Brain and Those of Other Vertebrates</i> (Mannheim 1854); <i>The Narcotic Substances of Enjoyment and the Human Being</i> (Nuremberg 1855); <i>Bread and the Various Grains</i> (Nuremberg 1860); <i>Coffee and its Substitute</i> (Reports at the Meetings of the Academy of Sciences in Munich, 1858); <i>The Bronze and Copper Alloys of the Old and Most Ancient Peoples</i> (Erlangen 1869) and <i>Concerning Old Discoveries of Iron and Silver</i> (Nuremberg 1873).</p>
<p>Ernst work on <a href="/wiki/Narcotics" class="mw-redirect" title="Narcotics">narcotics</a> is his most famous and was recently translated into English and publish under the name of <i>Plant Intoxicants</i>. (<a href="/wiki/International_Standard_Book_Number_(identifier)" class="mw-redirect" title="International Standard Book Number (identifier)">ISBN</a>&#160;<a href="/wiki/Special:BookSources/0-89281-498-5" title="Special:BookSources/0-89281-498-5">0-89281-498-5</a>). This was one of the first books to examine the cultivation, preparation, and consumption of the world’s major <a href="/wiki/Stimulants" class="mw-redirect" title="Stimulants">stimulants</a> and inebriants. The book includes seventeen chapters&#160;: 1) coffee, 2)coffee leaves as a beverage 3) tea, 4) Paraguayan Tea (<a href="/wiki/Yerba_mat%C3%A9" class="mw-redirect" title="Yerba maté">yerba maté</a>), 5) <a href="/wiki/Guarana" title="Guarana">Guarana</a>, 6) chocolate, 7) Fahan Tea (the orchid Angraecum fragrans Thouars as a source of <a href="/wiki/Coumarin" title="Coumarin">coumarin</a>), 8) <a href="/wiki/Khat" title="Khat">Khat</a>, 9) <a href="/wiki/Fly_Agaric" class="mw-redirect" title="Fly Agaric">Fly Agaric</a> (Amanita muscaria) opiate derived from the "poison lettuce,"10) <a href="/wiki/Datura" title="Datura">thorn apple</a>, 11) <a href="/wiki/Coca" title="Coca">coca</a>, 12) <a href="/wiki/Opium" title="Opium">opium</a>, 13) <a href="/wiki/Lactucarium" title="Lactucarium">Lactucarium</a> 14) <a href="/wiki/Hashish" title="Hashish">hashish</a>, 15) tobacco 16) <a href="/wiki/Betel" title="Betel">Betel</a> and Related Substances (<a href="/wiki/Areca_catechu" title="Areca catechu">Areca catechu</a>, <a href="/wiki/Areca_nut" title="Areca nut">areca nut</a>; Piper siriboa, the betel leaf) and 17) <a href="/wiki/Arsenous_acid" title="Arsenous acid">arsenous acid</a> or arsenic trioxide (As<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub>). Because of Ernst's early investigation and writing on coffee, he is occasionally referenced in modern coffee literature.</p>
<p>Starting with travel sketches and culturally historic descriptions rendered in novelistic style (<i>Memories of South America</i>, Leipzig 1861, 3 vols.; <i>About Chile, Peru and Brazil</i>, Leipzig 1862, 3 vols. and others) von Bibra preferred to busy himself in his later years with fictional works and developed an astonishing fruitfulness in this field. Of these writings which stand out especially because of successful characterizations and descriptions of beautiful landscapes we mention: <i>A Jewel</i> (Leipzig 1863); <i>A Woman with a Noble Heart</i> (<i>Ein edles Frauenherz</i> was featured in a radio broadcast on 26 August 2006 reading and concert)(2nd ed., Jena 1869); <i>The Adventures of a Young Peruvian in Germany</i> (Jena 1870); <i>The Nine Stations of Mr. von Scherenberg</i> (2nd ed. Jena 1880); <i>The Children of the Rogue</i>:(Nuremberg 1872); <i>Brave Women</i> (Jena 1876.)</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="von_Bibra_Family">von Bibra Family</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ernst_von_Bibra&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: von Bibra Family">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Von_Bibra" class="mw-redirect" title="Von Bibra">von Bibra</a></div>
<p>Ernst was a member of the aristocratic <a href="/wiki/Franconia" title="Franconia">Franconian</a> von Bibra family which among its members were <a href="/wiki/Lorenz_von_Bibra" title="Lorenz von Bibra">Lorenz von Bibra</a>, Prince-Bishop of <a href="/wiki/W%C3%BCrzburg" title="Würzburg">Würzburg</a>, Duke in <a href="/wiki/Franconia" title="Franconia">Franconia</a> (1459–1519), Lorenz’ half brother, <a href="/wiki/Wilhelm_von_Bibra" title="Wilhelm von Bibra">Wilhelm von Bibra</a> Papal emissary, <a href="/wiki/Conrad_von_Bibra" title="Conrad von Bibra">Conrad von Bibra</a>, Prince-Bishop of Würzburg, Duke in Franconia (1490–1544) and <a href="/wiki/Heinrich_von_Bibra" title="Heinrich von Bibra">Heinrich von Bibra</a>, Prince-Bishop, Prince-Abbot of <a href="/wiki/Fulda" title="Fulda">Fulda</a> (1711–1788).</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Germanisches_Nationalmuseum">Germanisches Nationalmuseum</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ernst_von_Bibra&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Germanisches Nationalmuseum">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Ernst was one of the co-founders of the <a href="/wiki/Germanisches_Nationalmuseum" title="Germanisches Nationalmuseum">Germanisches Nationalmuseum</a> (formerly "Germanischen Museums“) located in <a href="/wiki/Nuremberg" title="Nuremberg">Nuremberg</a>. Founded in 1852, led by close friend and fellow Franconian baron, <a href="/wiki/Hans_von_und_zu_Aufsess" class="mw-redirect" title="Hans von und zu Aufsess">Hans von und zu Aufsess</a>, whose goal was to assemble a "well-ordered compendium of all available source material for German history, literature and art". Ernst donated much of his personal collection, which included art, a rich natural history and ethnographic collection, to the museum. The "Bibra-Stube" was installed in the museum in 1887–1888.</p>
<h2><span id="&quot;Theory_on_the_action_of_ether&quot;_Anesthesia"></span><span class="mw-headline" id=".22Theory_on_the_action_of_ether.22_Anesthesia">"Theory on the action of ether" Anesthesia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ernst_von_Bibra&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: &quot;Theory on the action of ether&quot; Anesthesia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>An <a href="/wiki/Outdated_theories_of_anaesthetic_action" class="mw-redirect" title="Outdated theories of anaesthetic action">outdated theory of anaesthetic action</a>, Ernst von Bibra and Emil Harless, in 1847, were the first to suggest that <a href="/wiki/General_anaesthetic" title="General anaesthetic">general anaesthetics</a> may act by dissolving in the fatty fraction of brain cells. They proposed that anaesthetics dissolve and remove fatty constituents from brain cells, changing their activity and inducing anaesthesia. Below is the abstract of a recent German scientific paper on their work.</p>
<dl>
<dd><i>Frühe Erlanger Beiträge zur Theorie und Praxis der äther- und Chloroformnarkose&#160;: Die tierexperimentellen Untersuchungen von Ernst von Bibra und Emil Harless</i> U. v. Hintzenstern, H. Petermann, W. Schwarz Der Anaesthesist Issue Volume 50, Number 11 / November, 2001</dd>
</dl>
<blockquote>
<p><i>Abstract of Article:</i></p>
<p>Just three months after the first application of sulphuric ether to a patient in German-speaking countries the monography <i>Die Wirkung des Schwefeläthers in chemischer und physiologischer Beziehung</i> was published. In this book Ernst von Bibra and Emil Harless presented their experimental research on the effects of ether on humans and compared it to those on animals. The contents of the book are described. The authors "Theory on the action of ether" will be discussed in the context of contemporary criticism. Their hypothesis affected the discussion on the mechanisms of anaesthetic action up to the twentieth century.</p>
</blockquote>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="What_others_thought_about_Ernst_von_Bibra">What others thought about Ernst von Bibra</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ernst_von_Bibra&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: What others thought about Ernst von Bibra">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span id="Richard_Evans_Schultes,_&quot;Father_of_Modern_Ethnobotany&quot;_on_Ernst_von_Bibra"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Richard_Evans_Schultes.2C_.22Father_of_Modern_Ethnobotany.22_on_Ernst_von_Bibra">Richard Evans Schultes, "Father of Modern Ethnobotany" on Ernst von Bibra</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ernst_von_Bibra&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Richard Evans Schultes, &quot;Father of Modern Ethnobotany&quot; on Ernst von Bibra">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Richard_Evans_Schultes" title="Richard Evans Schultes">Richard Evans Schultes</a>, Ph.D., F.L.S. who was the Curator of Economic Botany and Executive Director, <a href="/wiki/Harvard_Botanical_Museum" class="mw-redirect" title="Harvard Botanical Museum">Harvard Botanical Museum</a> wrote in <i>The plant kingdom and hallucinogens</i> (1970):</p>
<blockquote>
<dl>
<dd>In 1855, Ernst Freiherr von Bibra published the first book of its kind, Die narkotischen Genussmittel und der Mensch, in which he considered some 17 plant narcotics and stimulants and urged chemists to study assiduously a field so promising for research and so fraught with enigmas.</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dd>A review of the scientific literature of the last half of the past century indicates that von Bibra's suggestions were followed, and an interdisciplinary interest in narcotics began to take hold and grow. It proved to be the spark that eventually engendered today's extraordinarily extensive and complex literature in many fields on narcotic substances.</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dd>Half a century later, in 1911, another outstanding book-in reality, a much expanded and modernized successor of von Bibra's work-appeared in C. Hartwich's <i>Die menschlichen Genussmittel</i>. This volume considered at great length and with interdisciplinary emphasis about 30 vegetal narcotics and stimulants and mentioned many others in passing. Hartwich pointed out that von Bibra's pioneer work was out of date, that research on the botanical aspects and chemical constituents of these curiously active plants had, in 1855, scarcely begun but that, by 1911, such studies were either progressing well or had already been completed.</dd>
</dl>
</blockquote>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Albert_Hofmann_on_Ernst_von_Bibra">Albert Hofmann on Ernst von Bibra</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ernst_von_Bibra&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Albert Hofmann on Ernst von Bibra">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Albert_Hofmann" title="Albert Hofmann">Albert Hofmann</a> writes and quotes Ernst in his book <i>LSD — My Problem Child</i>, Chapter 7. "Radiance from Ernst Jünger"</p>
<dl>
<dd>I visited <a href="/wiki/Ernst_J%C3%BCnger" title="Ernst Jünger">Ernst Jünger</a> occasionally in the following years, in Wilfingen, Germany, where he had moved from Ravensburg; or we met in Switzerland, at my place in Bottmingen, or in Bundnerland in southeastern Switzerland. Through the shared LSD experience our relations had deepened. Drugs and problems connected with them constituted a major subject of our conversation and correspondence, without our having made further practical experiments in the meantime.</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dd>We exchanged literature about drugs. Ernst Jünger thus let me have for my drug library the rare, valuable monograph of Dr. Ernst Freiherrn von Bibra, <i>Die Narkotischen Genussmittel und der Mensch</i> [Narcotic pleasure drugs and man] printed in Nuremberg in 1855. This book is a pioneering, standard work of drug literature, a source of the first order, above all as relates to the history of drugs. What von Bibra embraces under the designation "Narkotischen Genussmittel" are not only substances like opium and thorn apple, but also coffee, tobacco, khat, which do not fall under the present conception of narcotics, any more than do drugs such as coca, fly agaric, and hashish, which he also described.</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dd>Noteworthy, and today still as topical as at the time, are the general opinions about drugs that von Bibra contrived more than a century ago: The individual who has taken too much hashish, and then runs frantically about in the streets and attacks everyone who confronts him, sinks into insignificance beside the numbers of those who after mealtime pass calm and happy hours with a moderate dose; and the number of those who are able to overcome the heaviest exertions through coca, yes, who were possibly rescued from death by starvation through coca, by far exceed the few coqueros who have undermined their health by immoderate use. In the same manner, only a misplaced hypocrisy can condemn the vinous cup of old father Noah, because individual drunkards do not know how to observe limit and moderation.</dd>
</dl>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Arthur_Schopenhauer_on_Ernst_von_Bibra">Arthur Schopenhauer on Ernst von Bibra</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ernst_von_Bibra&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Arthur Schopenhauer on Ernst von Bibra">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The famous philosopher, <a href="/wiki/Arthur_Schopenhauer" title="Arthur Schopenhauer">Arthur Schopenhauer</a> (1788–1860), was extremely critical of Ernst for his <a href="/wiki/Vivisection" title="Vivisection">vivisection</a> of animals in <i><a href="/wiki/Parerga_and_Paralipomena" title="Parerga and Paralipomena">Parerga and Paralipomena</a></i> (quoting Payne's translation).</p>
<dl>
<dd>Deserving of special mention is the atrocity, perpetrated in Nuremberg by Baron von Bibra and reported by him <i>tanquam re bene gesta</i> to the public with inconceivable <i>naïveté</i> in his <i>Vergleichende Untersuchungen über das Gehirn des Menschen und der Wirbelthiere</i> (Mannheim, 1854, pp. 131 ff.). He deliberately arranged for the death by starvation of two rabbits in order to carry out a useless and superfluous research as to whether the chemical constituents of the brain underwent a change in their proportions through <i>death by starvation</i>! For the benefit of science, <i>n’est-ce-pas</i>? Does it never occur to these gentlemen of scalpel and crucible that they are human beings first and chemists afterwards?</dd>
</dl>
<p>Schopenhauer further on states</p>
<dl>
<dd>If Bibra’s cruel act could not be prevented, was it left unpunished? At any rate, anyone who has still as much to learn from books as has that von Bibra, should remember that to extort the final answers on the path of cruelty is to put nature on the rack in order to enrich his knowledge, to extort her secrets which have probably long been known.</dd>
</dl>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Westminster_Review">The Westminster Review</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ernst_von_Bibra&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: The Westminster Review">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Westminster Review. July and October, 1854. New Series Vol. VI. London: John Chapman History, Biography, Voyages and Travels. page 282</p>
<p>In contrast to other Germans, Ernst was found to be a consummate outdoors man.</p>
<dl>
<dd>He can rough it like a backwoodsman, ride like an Anglo-Indian, and shoot condors on the wing with a single ball. Besides this, he is a cultivated, humorous gentleman, with a considerable knowledge of human things, and an able naturalist as well, knowing how to look about him wherever he is, and capable of making scientifically useful observations.</dd>
</dl>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Historian_Ferdinand_Gregorovius">Historian Ferdinand Gregorovius</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ernst_von_Bibra&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Historian Ferdinand Gregorovius">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Ferdinand_Gregorovius" title="Ferdinand Gregorovius">Ferdinand Gregorovius</a> described vividly his visit to the eccentric Ernst published in 1893.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<dl>
<dd>To-day von Kreling took me to call on a wonderful old gentleman, Baron von Bibra, whose house, furnished in old Frankish style, he wished me to see. The owner bought it several years ago, and has arranged within it a collection of specimens of old Nuremberg wares&#160;: dark, airless rooms are crowded with a thousand Frankish things—glass, majolica, arms, books piled in heaps. A Faust-like atmosphere pervades the whole; out of doors rain and hanging plants, that swaying, darkened the windows. Could not join in Kreling's admiration of these superfluous and fantastic objects. The Freiherr has traveled a great deal in America, and has carefully arranged a collection of vessels, skulls, and implements belonging to the wild Indians. In the musty workroom, filled to overflowing with archaic household furniture, stand innumerable glasses, retorts, instruments, flasks, and a furnace, and here he makes analytical experiments in the morning. At four in the afternoon he sits down and writes—novels! An entire compartment in his dusty library stands filled with elegantly bound books, upwards of sixty little volumes, mainly novels, which he wrote between 1861 and 1871. This, the most eccentric of all Freiherrn that I have yet seen, goes about in his chaos of a house, a happy mortal, clad in a grey dressing-gown, his neck bare, calm self-possession, the product of self-satisfaction, written on his face. He opened a book in which I was obliged to write my name. Rain prevented me from going to Ratisbon to-day.</dd>
</dl>
