			<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Johann Philipp von Wurzelbauer</h1>
									<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
									<div id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
								<div id="contentSub"></div>
												<div id="jump-to-nav" class="mw-jump">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>
				</div>
				<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p><br />
<b>Johann Philipp von Wurzelbauer</b> (also spelled <b>Wurzelbaur</b>, <b>Wurzelbau</b>, <b>Wurtzelbaur</b>, <b>Wurtzelbau</b>) (28 September 1651 – 21 July 1725) was a <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">German</a> <a href="/wiki/Astronomy" title="Astronomy">astronomer</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biography">Biography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Johann_Philipp_von_Wurzelbauer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Biography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A native of <a href="/wiki/Nuremberg" title="Nuremberg">Nuremberg</a>, Wurzelbauer was a merchant who became an astronomer. As a youth, he was keenly interested in <a href="/wiki/Mathematics" title="Mathematics">mathematics</a> and <a href="/wiki/Astronomy" title="Astronomy">astronomy</a> but had been forced to earn his living as a merchant. He married twice: his first marriage was to Maria Magdalena Petz (1656–1713), his second to Sabina Dorothea Kress (1658–1733). Petz bore him six children.</p>
<p>He first published a work concerning his observations on the <a href="/wiki/Great_comet" title="Great comet">great comet</a> of 1680, and initially began his work at a private castle-<a href="/wiki/Observatory" title="Observatory">observatory</a> on Spitzenberg 4 owned by <a href="/wiki/Georg_Christoph_Eimmart" title="Georg Christoph Eimmart">Georg Christoph Eimmart</a> (completely destroyed during <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>), the director of Nuremberg's painters' academy. Wurzelbauer was 64 when he began this second career, but proved himself to be an able assistant to Eimmart. A large <a href="/wiki/Quadrant_(instrument)" title="Quadrant (instrument)">quadrant</a> from his days at Eimmart's observatory still survives.</p>
<p>After 1682, Wurzelbauer owned his own astronomical observatory and instruments, and observed the transit of <a href="/wiki/Mercury_(planet)" title="Mercury (planet)">Mercury</a>, <a href="/wiki/Solar_eclipse" title="Solar eclipse">solar eclipses</a>, and worked out the geographical <a href="/wiki/Latitude" title="Latitude">latitude</a> of his native city. After 1683, he had withdrawn himself completely from business life to dedicate himself to astronomy.</p>
<p>By 1700, Wurzelbauer had become the most well-known astronomer in <a href="/wiki/Nuremberg" title="Nuremberg">Nuremberg</a>. For his services to the field of astronomy, he was ennobled in 1692 by <a href="/wiki/Leopold_I,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Leopold I, Holy Roman Emperor">Leopold I, Holy Roman Emperor</a> and added the <i>von</i> to his name. He was a member of the <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">French</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Prussia" title="Prussia">Prussian</a> academies of the sciences.</p>
<p>The crater <a href="/wiki/Wurzelbauer_(crater)" title="Wurzelbauer (crater)">Wurzelbauer</a> on the <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">Moon</a> is named after him.</p>
