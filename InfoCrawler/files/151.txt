<p>In architecture, <b>tracery</b> is the stonework elements that support the glass in a <a href="/wiki/Gothic_architecture" title="Gothic architecture">Gothic</a> window.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> The term probably derives from the 'tracing floors' on which the complex patterns of late Gothic windows were laid out.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> There are two main types, <i>plate tracery</i> and the later <i>bar tracery</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-HF_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HF-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Plate_tracery">Plate tracery</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tracery&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Plate tracery">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Fenetre.cathedraleSoissons.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1c/Fenetre.cathedraleSoissons.png/220px-Fenetre.cathedraleSoissons.png" width="220" height="364" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1c/Fenetre.cathedraleSoissons.png/330px-Fenetre.cathedraleSoissons.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/1/1c/Fenetre.cathedraleSoissons.png 2x" data-file-width="435" data-file-height="720" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Fenetre.cathedraleSoissons.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<i>Plate tracery</i> in the nave aisle windows of <a href="/wiki/Soissons_Cathedral" title="Soissons Cathedral">Soissons Cathedral</a> (c.1200).</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The earliest form of window tracery, typical of Gothic architecture prior to the early 13th century, is known as plate tracery because the individual lights (the glazed openings in the window) have the appearance of being cut out of a flat plate of masonry. Romanesque church windows were normally quite small, somewhat taller than wide and with a simple round-headed ('segmental') arch at the top. From around the 1140s, the pointed-arch Gothic window (employed by <a href="/wiki/Abbot_Suger" class="mw-redirect" title="Abbot Suger">Abbot Suger</a> for the redesign of the choir at <a href="/wiki/Basilica_of_St_Denis" title="Basilica of St Denis">St Denis</a>) started to take over.</p>
<p>As the buttressing systems of early Gothic architecture reduced the structural need for broad expanses of thick walls, window openings grew progressively larger and instead of having just one very large window per bay division (which would create problems with supporting the glass), the typical early-Gothic 'twin <a href="/wiki/Lancet_window" title="Lancet window">lancet</a> plus oculus' form of plate tracery developed. This consists of two (sometimes three) tall thin lights topped with pointed arches, with a round or <a href="/wiki/Trefoil" title="Trefoil">trefoil</a> opening placed above them, often contained within a blind arch which gives the whole assemblage a pointed lancet shape (see the example from <a href="/wiki/Soissons_Cathedral" title="Soissons Cathedral">Soissons Cathedral</a>, right). With this type of design, the <a href="/wiki/Spandrel" title="Spandrel">spandrels</a> (i.e. the spaces between the tops of the lancet windows and the oculus) are just blank wall. The practicalities of building window tracery in this way severely limited the complexity of designs that could be produced and although plate tracery designs evolved over the course of the 12th and early 13th centuries, in practice, the only real variation was in the number and size of lancets and in the <a href="/wiki/Trefoil" title="Trefoil">trefoils</a>, <a href="/wiki/Quatrefoil" title="Quatrefoil">quatrefoils</a> and oculi used to fill the spaces above them.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Rose_window" title="Rose window">rose windows</a> of early- and high-Gothic cathedrals, such as the example in the north <a href="/wiki/Transept" title="Transept">transept</a> of <a href="/wiki/Laon_Cathedral" title="Laon Cathedral">Laon Cathedral</a> (1170's) or the west facade at <a href="/wiki/Chartres_Cathedral" title="Chartres Cathedral">Chartres</a> (c.1210), also employed plate tracery. This greatly limited the overall amount of light admitted to the interior by these windows, as well as restricting the complexity of patterns that could be created.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Bar_tracery">Bar tracery</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tracery&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Bar tracery">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Meneaux.fenetre.cathedrale.Reims.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f8/Meneaux.fenetre.cathedrale.Reims.png/220px-Meneaux.fenetre.cathedrale.Reims.png" width="220" height="362" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f8/Meneaux.fenetre.cathedrale.Reims.png/330px-Meneaux.fenetre.cathedrale.Reims.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f8/Meneaux.fenetre.cathedrale.Reims.png/440px-Meneaux.fenetre.cathedrale.Reims.png 2x" data-file-width="624" data-file-height="1026" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Meneaux.fenetre.cathedrale.Reims.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Bar tracery in the clerestory windows at <a href="/wiki/Reims_Cathedral" title="Reims Cathedral">Reims Cathedral</a> (1230s). Note the cross section through a mullion shown within the left lancet.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The earliest bar tracery designs were made for the aisle windows at Reims around 1215. The Reims windows still used the same 'two lancets plus oculus' pattern (as in the Soissons example above) but now the glass panels were held between narrow stone <a href="/wiki/Mullion" title="Mullion">mullions</a> made up of carefully shaped lengths of masonry (fitted together with mortar and metal pins) quite distinct from the wall surrounding them. These mullions were much more slender than the corresponding elements in plate tracery windows and crucially, the previously solid wall areas such as the spandrels could also now be glazed, greatly increasing the amount of light admitted.</p>
<p>The cross-section of each mullion or tracery bar was important both for the structural integrity of the window and for the visual effect. As can be seen in <a href="/wiki/Viollet-le-Duc" class="mw-redirect" title="Viollet-le-Duc">Viollet-le-Duc</a>'s diagram (<i>right</i>) there was normally a <a href="/w/index.php?title=Roll-moulding&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Roll-moulding (page does not exist)">roll-moulding</a> on both the inside and outside of the windows, which made the mullions appear even more slender than they actually were. The shoulder marked 'B' on the diagram is the glazing slot, into which the metal frame (<a href="/wiki/Armature_(sculpture)" title="Armature (sculpture)">armature</a>) of the window glass is mounted. Unlike with plate tracery, where each stone had to be individually shaped, the elements of bar tracery could be mass-produced to standard templates in the mason's yard - work that could continue even when it was too cold for <a href="/wiki/Lime_mortar" title="Lime mortar">lime mortar</a> to set. The technical aspects of the windows at Reims clearly fascinated <a href="/wiki/Villard_de_Honnecourt" title="Villard de Honnecourt">Villard de Honnecourt</a>, who visited the construction site, probably in the 1220s, and made a detailed sketch of the various templates, using a key to show how they fitted into the different parts of the window (the templates are in the lower half of folio 32 recto; the symbols besides the templates match similar ones on the detailed drawing of the Reims elevations on the facing page, folio 31 verso).</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Blind_and_open_tracery">Blind and open tracery</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tracery&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Blind and open tracery">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>As bar tracery opened the way for more complex patterns, masons started applying those same patterns to other surfaces as well as the actual window openings. When used on an otherwise solid walls, such motifs are known as blind tracery, a decorative effect first applied on the west facade of the church of St Nicaise at Reims (1230's). Conversely, tracery was also constructed as <a href="/wiki/Openwork" title="Openwork">openwork</a> screens, which could either match the window tracery behind them (e.g. the <a href="/wiki/Basilica_of_Saint_Urbain,_Troyes" class="mw-redirect" title="Basilica of Saint Urbain, Troyes">Basilica of Saint Urbain, Troyes</a>) or create a visual counterpoint to it, as on the exterior of the west facade of <a href="/wiki/Strasbourg_Cathedral" title="Strasbourg Cathedral">Strasbourg Cathedral</a>. Open tracery in particular was a key feature of the later phases of <a href="/wiki/Rayonnant" title="Rayonnant">Rayonnant</a> Gothic.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Tracery_patterns_and_the_phases_of_Gothic">Tracery patterns and the phases of Gothic</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tracery&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Tracery patterns and the phases of Gothic">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Most 19th-century histories of Gothic architectural style used a series of rather arbitrary categories based on the supposed evolution of the dominant patterns of window tracery. Such <a href="/wiki/Teleology" title="Teleology">teleological</a> models are now regarded as oversimplistic and are generally shunned by art historians,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> although they live on in the popular literature. In terms of the overall development of Gothic architecture, the crucial development was not so much the use of any particular tracery patterns but the transition from plate- to bar-tracery, which was what made the <a href="/wiki/Rayonnant" title="Rayonnant">Rayonnant</a> and subsequent styles possible.</p>
<h2><span id="Tracing_floors_and_épures"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Tracing_floors_and_.C3.A9pures">Tracing floors and <i>épures</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tracery&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Tracing floors and épures">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>As the complexity of tracery increased, so did the need for masons to draw out their designs in advance, either as a way of experimenting with patterns or as a way of communicating their designs to other craftsmen or to their patrons. Because of the cost and size limitations of parchment sheets, such designs would normally be drawn by incising onto a whitewashed board or a conveniently placed section of flat wall. In the latter case, the wall would be prepared with a thin layer of plaster, which would show the design more clearly.</p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:20130406_Ely_Cathedral_01.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8d/20130406_Ely_Cathedral_01.jpg/220px-20130406_Ely_Cathedral_01.jpg" width="220" height="353" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8d/20130406_Ely_Cathedral_01.jpg/330px-20130406_Ely_Cathedral_01.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8d/20130406_Ely_Cathedral_01.jpg/440px-20130406_Ely_Cathedral_01.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2246" data-file-height="3600" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:20130406_Ely_Cathedral_01.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Tracery in a window of <a href="/wiki/Ely_Cathedral" title="Ely Cathedral">Ely Cathedral</a>.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>A number of churches and cathedrals still show the faint remains of these tracings (or <i>épures</i> as they are known in France), from where the mason's compass points scratched through the plaster and into the masonry below. (Examples include some experimental 14th century window tracery patterns at the eastern end of the south wall inside the Galillee porch of <a href="/wiki/Ely_Cathedral" title="Ely Cathedral">Ely Cathedral</a>, or the extensive series of tracings on the flat aisle roofs of <a href="/wiki/Clermont-Ferrand_Cathedral" title="Clermont-Ferrand Cathedral">Clermont-Ferrand Cathedral</a>.) A number of major building sites (including <a href="/wiki/Westminster_Abbey" title="Westminster Abbey">Westminster Abbey</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wells_Cathedral" title="Wells Cathedral">Wells Cathedral</a> and <a href="/wiki/York_Minster" title="York Minster">York Minster</a>) originally had dedicated tracery chambers, where the architects could prepare their designs in relative comfort. The availability of a large flat floor surface meant that designs could be drawn life-size and the individual elements of bar tracery laid out on the plan to test their goodness of fit, before hoisting them up the scaffolding for installation in the actual window openings. This also meant that masons could carry on working through the winter season, when building work would normally grind to a halt.</p>
<p>The tracing floors themselves were covered with plaster of paris, which could be relaid and smoothed down after each set of designs were finished with. The 14th-century tracing house at York (also known as the Mason's Loft) survives to this day on the upper storey of the corridor leading to the <a href="/wiki/Chapter_House" class="mw-redirect" title="Chapter House">Chapter House</a>, the complex web of lines and curves scratched into the floor serving as witnesses to the countless different designs that were worked out in there. The high-quality carpentry and the inclusion of a <a href="/wiki/Garderobe" title="Garderobe">garderobe</a> and fireplace in the York tracing house also give an indication of the rising status of the architect around the 14th century.</p>
