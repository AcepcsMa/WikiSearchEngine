<p><b>Saarbrücken</b> (<small>German pronunciation:</small> <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Standard_German" title="Help:IPA/Standard German">[zaːɐ̯ˈbʁʏkn̩]</a></span><small class="nowrap metadata">&#160;(<span class="unicode haudio"><span class="fn"><span style="white-space:nowrap"><a href="/wiki/File:De-Saarbr%C3%BCcken.ogg" title="About this sound"><img alt="About this sound" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/11px-Loudspeaker.svg.png" width="11" height="11" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/17px-Loudspeaker.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/22px-Loudspeaker.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="20" data-file-height="20" /></a>&#160;</span><a href="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/5/54/De-Saarbr%C3%BCcken.ogg" class="internal" title="De-Saarbrücken.ogg">listen</a></span></span>)</small>, <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a>: <span lang="fr" xml:lang="fr"><i>Sarrebruck</i></span><sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/French" title="Help:IPA/French">[saʁbʁyk]</a></span>, <a href="/wiki/Rhine_Franconian_dialects" title="Rhine Franconian dialects">Rhine Franconian</a>: <i>Saarbrigge</i> <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[zaːˈbʁɪgə]</span>) is the capital and largest city of the state of <a href="/wiki/Saarland" title="Saarland">Saarland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a>. Saarbrücken is Saarland's administrative, commercial and cultural centre. The city is situated next to the <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">French</a> border at the heart of the metropolitan area of Saarland.</p>
<p>Saarbrücken was created in 1909 by the merger of three towns, Saarbrücken, St.&#160;Johann, and Malstatt-Burbach. It used to be the industrial and transport centre of the Saar coal basin. Products included iron and steel, sugar, beer, pottery, optical instruments, machinery, and construction materials.</p>
<p>Historic landmarks in the city include the stone bridge across the <a href="/wiki/Saar_River" class="mw-redirect" title="Saar River">Saar</a> (1546), the Gothic church of St.&#160;Arnual, the 18th-century <a href="/wiki/Saarbr%C3%BCcken_Castle" title="Saarbrücken Castle">Saarbrücken Castle</a>, and the old part of the town, the <i>Sankt Johanner Markt</i> (Market of St.&#160;Johann).</p>
<p>Twice in the 20th century Saarbrücken was separated from Germany: in 1920–35 as capital of the <a href="/wiki/Saar_(League_of_Nations)" class="mw-redirect" title="Saar (League of Nations)">Territory of the Saar Basin</a> and in 1947–56 as capital of the <a href="/wiki/Saar_(protectorate)" class="mw-redirect" title="Saar (protectorate)">Saar Protectorate</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Toponymy">Toponymy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Saarbr%C3%BCcken&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Toponymy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In modern German, <i>Saarbrücken</i> literally translates to <i>Saar bridges</i> (<i>Brücken</i> is the plural of <i><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Br%C3%BCcke" class="extiw" title="wiktionary:Brücke">Brücke</a></i>), and indeed there are about a dozen bridges across the Saar river. However, the name actually predates the oldest bridge in the historic center of Saarbrücken, the <i>Alte Brücke</i>, by at least 500 years.<sup id="cite_ref-de_Saarbr.C3.BCcken_name_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-de_Saarbr.C3.BCcken_name-3">[wp 1]</a></sup></p>
<p>The name <i>Saar</i> stems from the <a href="/wiki/Celtic_languages" title="Celtic languages">Celtic</a> word <i>sara</i> (<i>streaming water</i>), and the <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Roman</a> name of the river, <i>saravus</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-DrNeumann_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DrNeumann-4">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>However, there are three theories about the origin of the second part of the name Saar<i>brücken</i>.</p>
<h3><span id="Briga_(rock)"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Briga_.28rock.29">Briga (rock)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Saarbr%C3%BCcken&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Briga (rock)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The most popular theory states that the historical name of the town, <i>Sarabrucca</i>, derived from the <a href="/wiki/Celtic_languages" title="Celtic languages">Celtic</a> word <i><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/briga" class="extiw" title="wiktionary:briga">briga</a></i> (<i>hill</i>, or <i>rock</i>, <i>big stone</i><sup id="cite_ref-DrNeumann_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DrNeumann-4">[3]</a></sup>), which became <i><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Brocken" class="extiw" title="wiktionary:Brocken">Brocken</a></i> (can mean <i>rock</i> or <i>boulder</i>) in <a href="/wiki/High_German" class="mw-redirect" title="High German">High German</a>. The castle of <i>Sarabrucca</i> was located on a large rock by the name of <i>Saarbrocken</i> overlooking the river Saar.<sup id="cite_ref-memotransfront_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-memotransfront-5">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span id="Brucca_(ford)"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Brucca_.28ford.29">Brucca (ford)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Saarbr%C3%BCcken&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Brucca (ford)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A minority opinion holds that the historical name of the town, <i>Sarabrucca</i>, derived from the <a href="/wiki/Old_High_German" title="Old High German">Old High German</a> word <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.duden.de/suchen/dudenonline/brucca"><i>Brucca</i></a><span class="languageicon">(in German)</span>, meaning <i>bridge</i>, or more precisely a <a href="/wiki/Corduroy_road" title="Corduroy road">Corduroy road</a>, which was also used in fords. Next to the castle, there was a ford allowing land-traffic to cross the Saar.<sup id="cite_ref-Stadtluft8_9_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Stadtluft8_9-6">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span id="Bruco_(swamp)"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Bruco_.28swamp.29">Bruco (swamp)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Saarbr%C3%BCcken&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Bruco (swamp)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A mostly rejected theory claims that the historical name of the town, <i>Sarabrucca</i>, derived from the <a href="/wiki/Germanic_languages" title="Germanic languages">Germanic</a> word <i>bruco</i> (<i>swamp</i>, <i>marsh</i>). There is an area in St&#160;Johann called <i>Bruchwiese</i> (<i>wiese</i> meaning <i>meadow</i>), which used to be swampy before it was developed, and there were <a href="/wiki/Flood-meadow" title="Flood-meadow">flood-meadows</a> along the river, and those are often <a href="/wiki/Wet_meadow" title="Wet meadow">marshy</a>. However, the Saarbrücken area was first settled by <a href="/wiki/Celts" title="Celts">Celts</a> and not by <a href="/wiki/Germanic_peoples" title="Germanic peoples">Germanic peoples</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-de_Saarbr.C3.BCcken_name_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-de_Saarbr.C3.BCcken_name-3">[wp 1]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Saarbr%C3%BCcken&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Roman_Empire">Roman Empire</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Saarbr%C3%BCcken&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Roman Empire">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:20110418Roemerkastell_Saarbruecken10.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/48/20110418Roemerkastell_Saarbruecken10.jpg/220px-20110418Roemerkastell_Saarbruecken10.jpg" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/48/20110418Roemerkastell_Saarbruecken10.jpg/330px-20110418Roemerkastell_Saarbruecken10.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/48/20110418Roemerkastell_Saarbruecken10.jpg/440px-20110418Roemerkastell_Saarbruecken10.jpg 2x" data-file-width="900" data-file-height="674" /></a>

<p>In the last centuries BC, the <a href="/wiki/Mediomatrici" title="Mediomatrici">Mediomatrici</a> settled in the Saarbrücken area.<sup id="cite_ref-LHSSB_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-LHSSB-7">[6]</a></sup> When Julius Caesar conquered Gaul in the 1st century BC, the area was incorporated into the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Empire" title="Roman Empire">Roman Empire</a>.</p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Saarbr%C3%BCcken_Halberg_Mithrash%C3%B6hle.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ae/Saarbr%C3%BCcken_Halberg_Mithrash%C3%B6hle.JPG/220px-Saarbr%C3%BCcken_Halberg_Mithrash%C3%B6hle.JPG" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ae/Saarbr%C3%BCcken_Halberg_Mithrash%C3%B6hle.JPG/330px-Saarbr%C3%BCcken_Halberg_Mithrash%C3%B6hle.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ae/Saarbr%C3%BCcken_Halberg_Mithrash%C3%B6hle.JPG/440px-Saarbr%C3%BCcken_Halberg_Mithrash%C3%B6hle.JPG 2x" data-file-width="1600" data-file-height="1200" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Saarbr%C3%BCcken_Halberg_Mithrash%C3%B6hle.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The <a href="/wiki/Mithraeum" title="Mithraeum">Mithras shrine</a> at <i>Halberg</i> hill</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>From the 1st century AD to the 5th century,<sup id="cite_ref-kastell_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kastell-8">[7]</a></sup> there was the <a href="/wiki/Gallo-Roman_culture" title="Gallo-Roman culture">Gallo-Roman</a> settlement called <i>vicus Saravus</i> west of Saarbrücken's <i>Halberg</i> hill,<sup id="cite_ref-Saarlandbilder_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Saarlandbilder-9">[8]</a></sup> on the <a href="/wiki/Roman_road" class="mw-redirect" title="Roman road">roads</a> from <a href="/wiki/Metz#Ancient_history" title="Metz">Metz</a> to <a href="/wiki/Worms,_Germany#Celts_and_Romans" title="Worms, Germany">Worms</a> and from <a href="/wiki/History_of_Trier#Roman_Empire" title="History of Trier">Trier</a> to <a href="/wiki/Argentoratum" title="Argentoratum">Strasbourg</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Stadtluft8_9_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Stadtluft8_9-6">[5]</a></sup> Since the 1st or 2nd century AD,<sup id="cite_ref-Stadtluft8_9_6-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Stadtluft8_9-6">[5]</a></sup> a wooden bridge, later upgraded to stone,<sup id="cite_ref-memotransfront_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-memotransfront-5">[4]</a></sup> connected <i>vicus Saravus</i> with the south-western bank of the Saar, today's St&#160;Arnual, where at least one <a href="/wiki/Roman_villa" title="Roman villa">Roman villa</a> was located.<sup id="cite_ref-Arnual_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Arnual-10">[9]</a></sup> In the 3rd century AD, a <a href="/wiki/Mithraeum" title="Mithraeum">Mithras shrine</a> was built in a cave in <i>Halberg</i> hill, on the eastern bank of the Saar river, next to today's old "Osthafen" harbor,<sup id="cite_ref-mithras_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mithras-11">[10]</a></sup> and a small <a href="/wiki/Castra" title="Castra">Roman camp</a> was constructed at the foot of Halberg hill<sup id="cite_ref-Saarlandbilder_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Saarlandbilder-9">[8]</a></sup> next to the river.<sup id="cite_ref-kastell_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kastell-8">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Toward the end of the 4th century, the <a href="/wiki/Alemanni" title="Alemanni">Alemanni</a> destroyed the castra and <i>vicus Saravus</i>, removing permanent human presence from the Saarbrücken area for almost a century.<sup id="cite_ref-Stadtluft8_9_6-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Stadtluft8_9-6">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Middle_Ages_to_18th_century">Middle Ages to 18th century</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Saarbr%C3%BCcken&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Middle Ages to 18th century">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Saarbr%C3%BCcken_Castle#History" title="Saarbrücken Castle">History of Saarbrücken Castle</a></div>
<p>The Saar area came under the control of the <a href="/wiki/Franks" title="Franks">Franks</a> towards the end of the 5th century. In the 6th century, the <a href="/wiki/Merovingian_dynasty" title="Merovingian dynasty">Merovingians</a> gave the village <i>Merkingen</i>, which had formed on the ruins of the villa on the south-western end of the (in those times still usable) Roman bridge, to the <a href="/wiki/Bishopric_of_Metz" title="Bishopric of Metz">Bishopric of Metz</a>. Between 601 and 609, Bishop Arnual founded a community of clerics, a <i><a href="/wiki/Stift#Stift_as_a_collegiate_body_or_building" title="Stift">Stift</a></i>, there. Centuries later the <i>Stift</i>, and in 1046 <i>Merkingen</i>, took on his name, giving birth to <i>St&#160;Arnual</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-Stadtluft8_9_6-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Stadtluft8_9-6">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>The oldest documentary reference to Saarbrücken is a deed of donation from 999, which documents that <a href="/wiki/Otto_III,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Otto III, Holy Roman Emperor">Emperor Otto III</a> gave the "castellum Sarabrucca" (Saarbrücken castle) to the Bishops of Metz. The Bishops gave the area to the <a href="/wiki/Count" title="Count">Counts</a> of <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=County_of_Saargau&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="County of Saargau (page does not exist)">Saargau</a></i> as a <a href="/wiki/Fee_(feudal_tenure)" class="mw-redirect" title="Fee (feudal tenure)">fief</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Stadtluft8_9_6-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Stadtluft8_9-6">[5]</a></sup> By 1120, the county of Saarbrücken had been formed and a small settlement around the castle developed. In 1168, <a href="/wiki/Frederick_I,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Frederick I, Holy Roman Emperor">Emperor Barbarossa</a> ordered the <a href="/wiki/Slighting" title="Slighting">slighting</a> of Saarbrücken because of a <a href="/wiki/Feud" title="Feud">feud</a> with Count Simon I. The damage cannot have been grave, as the castle continued to exist.<sup id="cite_ref-GdS_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GdS-12">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1321/1322<sup id="cite_ref-memotransfront_5-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-memotransfront-5">[4]</a></sup> Count Johann I of <a href="/w/index.php?title=County_of_Saarbr%C3%BCcken-Commercy&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="County of Saarbrücken-Commercy (page does not exist)">Saarbrücken-Commercy</a> gave city status to the settlement of Saarbrücken and the fishing village of St&#160;Johann on the opposite bank of the Saar, introducing a joint administration and emancipating the inhabitants from <a href="/wiki/Serfdom" title="Serfdom">serfdom</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-LHSSB_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-LHSSB-7">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>From 1381 to 1793 the counts of <a href="/wiki/County_of_Nassau-Saarbr%C3%BCcken" class="mw-redirect" title="County of Nassau-Saarbrücken">Nassau-Saarbrücken</a> were the main local rulers.<br />
In 1549, <a href="/wiki/Charles_V,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Charles V, Holy Roman Emperor">Emperor Charles V</a> prompted the construction of the <i>Alte Brücke</i> (old bridge) connecting Saarbrücken and St&#160;Johann. At the beginning of the 17th century, <a href="/wiki/Louis_II,_Count_of_Nassau-Weilburg" title="Louis II, Count of Nassau-Weilburg">Count Ludwig II</a> ordered the construction of a new Renaissance-style castle on the site of the old castle, and founded Saarbrücken's oldest <a href="/wiki/Gymnasium_(Germany)" title="Gymnasium (Germany)">secondary school</a>, the <i>Ludwigsgymnasium</i>. During the <a href="/wiki/Thirty_Years%27_War" title="Thirty Years' War">Thirty Years' War</a>, the population of Saarbrücken was reduced to just 70 by 1637, down from 4500 in 1628. During the <a href="/wiki/Franco-Dutch_War" title="Franco-Dutch War">Franco-Dutch War</a>, <a href="/wiki/Louis_XIV_of_France" title="Louis XIV of France">King Louis XIV's</a> troops burned down Saarbrücken in 1677, almost completely destroying the city such that just 8 houses remained standing.<sup id="cite_ref-LHSSB_7-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-LHSSB-7">[6]</a></sup><br />
The area was incorporated into <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a> for the first time in the 1680s. In 1697 France was forced to relinquish the Saar province, but from 1793 to 1815 regained control of the region.</p>

<p>During the reign of <a href="/wiki/William_Henry,_Prince_of_Nassau-Saarbr%C3%BCcken" title="William Henry, Prince of Nassau-Saarbrücken">Prince William Henry</a> from 1741 to 1768, the coal mines were nationalized and his policies created a <a href="/wiki/Proto-industrialization" title="Proto-industrialization">proto-industrialized</a> economy, laying the foundation for <a href="/wiki/Saarland#Economy" title="Saarland">Saarland's</a> later highly industrialized economy. Saarbrücken was booming, and Prince William Henry spent on building and on infrastructure like the <i>Saarkran</i> river <a href="/wiki/Treadwheel_crane" title="Treadwheel crane">crane</a> (1761), far beyond his financial means. However, the famous <a href="/wiki/Baroque_architecture" title="Baroque architecture">baroque</a> architect Friedrich Joachim Stengel created not only the <i>Saarkran</i>, but many iconic buildings that still shape Saarbrücken's face today, like the <i>Friedenskirche</i> (Peace Church), which was finished in 1745, the <i>Old City Hall</i> (1750), the catholic <i>St.&#160;John's</i> <i><a href="/wiki/Minor_basilica" title="Minor basilica">Basilica</a></i> (1754), and the famous <i><a href="/wiki/Ludwigskirche" title="Ludwigskirche">Ludwigskirche</a></i> (1775), Saarbrücken's landmark.<sup id="cite_ref-LHSSB_7-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-LHSSB-7">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="19th_century">19th century</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Saarbr%C3%BCcken&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: 19th century">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>In 1793, Saarbrücken was captured by <a href="/wiki/French_Revolution" title="French Revolution">French revolutionary</a> troops and in the treaties of <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Campo_Formio" title="Treaty of Campo Formio">Campo Formio</a> and <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Lun%C3%A9ville" title="Treaty of Lunéville">Lunéville</a>, the county of Saarbrücken was ceded to France.<sup id="cite_ref-LHSSB_7-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-LHSSB-7">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>After 1815 Saarbrücken became part of the <a href="/wiki/Prussia" title="Prussia">Prussian</a> <a href="/wiki/Rhine_Province" title="Rhine Province">Rhine Province</a>. The office of mayor Saarbrücken administrated the urban municipalities Saarbrücken and St&#160;Johann, and the rural municipalities <a href="/wiki/Malstatt-Burbach" title="Malstatt-Burbach"><i>Malstatt</i>, <i>Burbach</i></a>, <i>Brebach</i>, and <i>Rußhütte</i>. The <a href="/wiki/Saar-Warndt_coal_mining_basin" title="Saar-Warndt coal mining basin">coal and iron resources of the region</a> were developed: in 1852, a railway connecting the <a href="/wiki/Palatine_Ludwig_Railway_Company" title="Palatine Ludwig Railway Company">Palatine Ludwig Railway</a> with the <a href="/wiki/Chemins_de_fer_de_l%27Est" title="Chemins de fer de l'Est">French Eastern Railway</a> was constructed, the Burbach ironworks started production in 1856, beginning in 1860 the Saar up to <a href="/wiki/Ensdorf,_Saarland" title="Ensdorf, Saarland">Ensdorf</a> was channeled, and Saarbrücken was connected to the <a href="/wiki/List_of_canals_in_France" title="List of canals in France">French canal network</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-LHSSB_7-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-LHSSB-7">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>At the start of the <a href="/wiki/Franco-Prussian_War#Occupation_of_Saarbr.C3.BCcken" title="Franco-Prussian War">Franco-Prussian War</a>, Saarbrücken was the first target of the French invasion force which drove off the Prussian vanguard and occupied Alt-Saarbrücken on 2&#160;August 1870. Oral tradition has it that 14-year-old French Prince <a href="/wiki/Napol%C3%A9on,_Prince_Imperial" title="Napoléon, Prince Imperial">Napoléon Eugène Louis Bonaparte</a> fired his first cannon in this battle, an event commemorated by the <i>Lulustein</i> memorial in Alt-Saarbrücken. On 4&#160;August 1870 the French left Saarbrückenand, driven away towards Metz in the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Spicheren" title="Battle of Spicheren">Battle of Spicheren</a> on 6&#160;August 1870.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="20th_century">20th century</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Saarbr%C3%BCcken&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: 20th century">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1909 the cities of Saarbrücken, St Johann und Malstatt-Burbach merged and formed the major city of Saarbrücken with a population of over 100,000.</p>
<p>During <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a>, factories and railways in Saarbrücken were bombed. The <a href="/wiki/Royal_Naval_Air_Service" title="Royal Naval Air Service">Royal Naval Air Service</a> raided Saarbrücken with 11&#160;<a href="/wiki/Airco_DH.4" title="Airco DH.4">DH4s</a> on October 17, 1917, and a week later with 9&#160;<a href="/wiki/Handley_Page_Type_O" title="Handley Page Type O">HP11s</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[12]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Royal_Flying_Corps" title="Royal Flying Corps">Royal Flying Corps</a> raided Saarbrücken's railway station with 5&#160;<a href="/wiki/Airco_DH.9" title="Airco DH.9">DH9s</a> on July&#160;31, 1918, on which occasion one DH9 crashed near the town centre.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>Saarbrücken became capital of the <a href="/wiki/Saar_(League_of_Nations)" class="mw-redirect" title="Saar (League of Nations)">Saar territory</a> established in 1920. Under the <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Versailles" title="Treaty of Versailles">Treaty of Versailles</a> (1919), the Saar coal mines were made the exclusive property of France for a period of 15 years as compensation for the destruction of French mines during the <a href="/wiki/First_World_War" class="mw-redirect" title="First World War">First World War</a>. The treaty also provided for a <a href="/wiki/Plebiscite" class="mw-redirect" title="Plebiscite">plebiscite</a>, at the end of the 15-year period, to determine the territory's future status, and in 1935 more than 90% of the electorate voted for reunification with Germany, while only 0.8% voted for unification with France. The remainder wanted to rejoin Germany but not while the Nazis were in power. This "status quo" group voted for maintenance of the League of Nations' administration. In 1935, the Saar territory rejoined <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a> and formed a district under the name Saarland.</p>

<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="World_War_II">World War II</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Saarbr%C3%BCcken&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: World War II">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Saarbrücken was heavily <a href="/wiki/Strategic_bombing_during_World_War_II" title="Strategic bombing during World War II">bombed</a> in <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-luftangriffe_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-luftangriffe-16">[15]</a></sup> In total 1,234 people (1.1 percent of the population) in Saarbrücken were killed in bombing raids 1942-45.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[16]</a></sup> 11,000 homes were destroyed and 75 percent of the city left in ruins.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/RAF_Bomber_Command" title="RAF Bomber Command">Royal Air Force</a> raided Saarbrücken at least 10 times. Often employing <a href="/wiki/Area_bombing_directive" title="Area bombing directive">area bombing</a>, the Royal Air Force used total of at least 1495 planes to attack Saarbrücken, killing a minimum of 635 people and heavily damaging more than 8400 buildings, of which more than 7700 were completely destroyed, thus <a href="/wiki/Dehousing" title="Dehousing">dehousing</a> more than 50,000 people.<sup id="cite_ref-luftangriffe_16-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-luftangriffe-16">[15]</a></sup> The first major raid on Saarbrücken was done by 291&#160;aircraft of the Royal Air Force on July&#160;29, 1942, targeting industrial facilities. Losing 9&#160;aircraft, the bombers destroyed almost 400&#160;buildings, damaging more than 300 others, and killed more than 150 people. <sup id="cite_ref-diary_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-diary-18">[17]</a></sup> On August&#160;28, 1942, 113 Royal Air Force planes raided Saarbrücken doing comparably little damage due to widely scattered bombing.<sup id="cite_ref-diary_18-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-diary-18">[17]</a></sup> After the Royal Air Force mistakenly bombed <a href="/wiki/Saarlouis" title="Saarlouis">Saarlouis</a> instead of Saarbrücken on September&#160;1, 1942, it raided Saarbrücken with 118&#160;planes on September&#160;19, 1942, causing comparably little damage as the bombing scattered to the west of Saarbrücken due to ground haze.<sup id="cite_ref-diary_18-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-diary-18">[17]</a></sup> There were small raids with 28&#160;<a href="/wiki/De_Havilland_Mosquito" title="De Havilland Mosquito">Mosquitos</a><sup id="cite_ref-diary_18-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-diary-18">[17]</a></sup> on April&#160;30, 1944, with 33&#160;Mosquitos<sup id="cite_ref-diary_18-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-diary-18">[17]</a></sup> on June&#160;29, 1944, and with just 2&#160;Mosquitos<sup id="cite_ref-diary_18-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-diary-18">[17]</a></sup> on July&#160;26, 1944. At the request of the American Third Army, the Royal Air Force massively raided Saarbrücken on October&#160;5, 1944, in order to destroy supply lines, especially the railway. The 531&#160;<a href="/wiki/Avro_Lancaster" title="Avro Lancaster">Lancasters</a> and 20&#160;Mosquitos achieved these goals, losing 3&#160;Lancasters but destroying large parts of Malstatt and nearly all of Alt-Saarbrücken.<sup id="cite_ref-diary_18-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-diary-18">[17]</a></sup> From January&#160;13 to January&#160;14, the Royal Air Force raided Saarbrücken three times, targeting the railway yard. The attacks with 158, 274, and 134 planes, respectively, were very effective.<sup id="cite_ref-diary_18-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-diary-18">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Eighth_Air_Force" title="Eighth Air Force">8th US Air Force</a> raided Saarbrücken at least 16 times, from October&#160;4, 1943, to November&#160;9, 1944. Targeting mostly the marshalling yards, a total of at least 2387 planes of the 8th.&#160;USAF killed a minimum of 543 people and heavily damaged more than 4400 buildings, of which more than 700 were completely destroyed, thus depriving more than 2300 people of shelter.<sup id="cite_ref-luftangriffe_16-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-luftangriffe-16">[15]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Donald_J._Gott" title="Donald J. Gott">Donald J. Gott</a> and <a href="/wiki/William_E._Metzger,_Jr." class="mw-redirect" title="William E. Metzger, Jr.">William E. Metzger, Jr.</a> were posthumously awarded the <a href="/wiki/Medal_of_Honor" title="Medal of Honor">Medal of Honor</a> for their actions during the bombing run on November&#160;9, 1944.</p>

<p>On the ground, Saarbrücken was defended by the 347. Infanterie-Division commanded by <a href="/wiki/Wolf-G%C3%BCnther_Trierenberg" title="Wolf-Günther Trierenberg">Wolf-Günther Trierenberg</a> in 1945.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[18]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/70th_Infantry_Division_(United_States)" title="70th Infantry Division (United States)">US 70th Infantry Division</a> was tasked with punching through the <a href="/wiki/Siegfried_Line" title="Siegfried Line">Siegfried Line</a> and taking Saarbrücken. As the fortifications were unusually strong, it first had to take the Siegfried Line fortifications on the French heights near <a href="/wiki/Spicheren" title="Spicheren">Spicheren</a> overlooking Saarbrücken. This <i>Spichern-Stellung</i> had been constructed in 1940 after the French had fallen back on the <a href="/wiki/Maginot_Line" title="Maginot Line">Maginot Line</a> during the <a href="/wiki/Phoney_War" title="Phoney War">Phoney War</a>. The 276th Infantry Regiment attacked <a href="/wiki/Forbach" title="Forbach">Forbach</a> on February&#160;19, 1945, and a fierce battle ensued, halting the American advance at the rail-road tracks cutting through Forbach on February&#160;22, 1945.<sup id="cite_ref-70th_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-70th-20">[19]</a></sup> The 274th and 275th Infantry Regiments took Spicheren on February&#160;20, 1945.<sup id="cite_ref-70th_20-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-70th-20">[19]</a></sup> When the 274th Infantry Regiment captured the Spicheren Heights<sup id="cite_ref-70th_20-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-70th-20">[19]</a></sup> on February&#160;23, 1945, after a heavy battle on the previous day, the Germans counter-attacked for days, but by February&#160;27, 1945, the heights were fully under American control.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[20]</a></sup> A renewed attack on March&#160;3, 1945, allowed units of the 70th Infantry Division to enter <a href="/wiki/Stiring-Wendel" title="Stiring-Wendel">Stiring-Wendel</a> and the remainder of Forbach. By March&#160;5, 1945, all of Forbach and major parts of Stiring-Wendel had been taken. However, fighting for Stiring-Wendel, especially for the <i>Simon</i> mine, continued for days.<sup id="cite_ref-70th_20-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-70th-20">[19]</a></sup> After the German defenders of Stiring-Wendel fell back to Saarbrücken on March&#160;12 and 13, 1945,<sup id="cite_ref-274th_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-274th-22">[21]</a></sup> the 70th Infantry Division still faced a strong segment of the Siegfried Line, which had been reinforced<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[22]</a></sup> around Saarbrücken as late as 1940. After having the German troops south of the Saar fall back across the Saar at night, the German defenders of Saarbrücken retreated early on March&#160;20, 1945. The 70th Infantry Division flanked Saarbrücken by crossing the Saar north-west of Saarbrücken. The 274th Infantry Regiment entered Saarbrücken on March&#160;20, 1945, fully occupying it the following day, thus ending the war for Saarbrücken.<sup id="cite_ref-274th_22-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-274th-22">[21]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="After_World_War_II">After World War II</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Saarbr%C3%BCcken&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: After World War II">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In 1945, Saarbrücken temporarily became part of the <a href="/wiki/Allied_Occupation_Zones_in_Germany" class="mw-redirect" title="Allied Occupation Zones in Germany">French Zone of Occupation</a>. In 1947, France created the nominally politically independent <a href="/wiki/Saar_(protectorate)" class="mw-redirect" title="Saar (protectorate)">Saar Protectorate</a> and merged it economically with France in order to exploit the area's vast coal reserves. Saarbrücken became capital of the new Saar state. A <a href="/wiki/Saar_Statute_referendum,_1955" title="Saar Statute referendum, 1955">referendum in 1955</a> came out with over two thirds of the voters rejecting an independent Saar state. The area rejoined the <a href="/wiki/Federal_Republic_of_Germany" class="mw-redirect" title="Federal Republic of Germany">Federal Republic of Germany</a> on 1 January 1957, sometimes called <i>Kleine Wiedervereinigung</i> (little reunification). Economic reintegration would, however, take many more years. Saarbrücken became capital of the <i><a href="/wiki/States_of_Germany" title="States of Germany">Bundesland</a></i> (federal state) Saarland. After the administrative reform of 1974, the city had a population of more than 200,000.</p>
<p>From 1990 to 1993, students and an arts professor from the town first secretly, then officially, created <a href="/wiki/Platz_des_Unsichtbaren_Mahnmals" title="Platz des Unsichtbaren Mahnmals">an invisible memorial to Jewish cemeteries</a>. It is located on the fore-court of the Saarbrücken Castle.</p>
<p>On March 9, 1999 at 4:40am, there was a bomb attack on the controversial <a href="/wiki/Wehrmachtsausstellung" title="Wehrmachtsausstellung">Wehrmachtsausstellung</a> exhibition next to Saarbrücken Castle, resulting in minor damage to the <a href="/wiki/Folk_high_school#Germany_and_Austria" title="Folk high school">Volkshochschule</a> building housing the exhibition and the adjoining <i><a href="/wiki/Saarland_Museum#Museum_in_the_Palace_Church" title="Saarland Museum">Schlosskirche</a></i> church; this attack, fortunately, did not cause any injuries.<sup id="cite_ref-BerlinerZeitung_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BerlinerZeitung-24">[23]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Infrastructure">Infrastructure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Saarbr%C3%BCcken&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Infrastructure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The city is served by <a href="/wiki/Saarbr%C3%BCcken_Airport" title="Saarbrücken Airport">Saarbrücken Airport</a> (SCN), and since June 2007 <a href="/wiki/InterCityExpress" class="mw-redirect" title="InterCityExpress">ICE</a> high speed train services along the <a href="/wiki/LGV_Est" title="LGV Est">LGV Est</a> line provide high speed connections to <a href="/wiki/Paris" title="Paris">Paris</a> from <a href="/wiki/Saarbr%C3%BCcken_Hauptbahnhof" title="Saarbrücken Hauptbahnhof">Saarbrücken Hauptbahnhof</a>. Saarbrücken's <a href="/wiki/Saarbahn" title="Saarbahn">Saarbahn</a> (modelled on the <a href="/wiki/Karlsruhe_model" title="Karlsruhe model">Karlsruhe model</a> <a href="/wiki/Light_rail" title="Light rail">light rail</a>) crosses the French–German border, connecting to the French city of <a href="/wiki/Sarreguemines" title="Sarreguemines">Sarreguemines</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Science_and_Education">Science and Education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Saarbr%C3%BCcken&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Science and Education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Saarbrücken is also the home of the main campus of <a href="/wiki/Saarland_University" title="Saarland University">Saarland University</a> (<i>Universität des Saarlandes</i>). Co-located with the University are several research centres including:</p>
<ul>
<li>the <a href="/wiki/Max_Planck_Institute_for_Informatics" title="Max Planck Institute for Informatics">Max Planck Institute for Informatics</a>,</li>
<li>the <a href="/wiki/Max_Planck_Institute_for_Software_Systems" title="Max Planck Institute for Software Systems">Max Planck Institute for Software Systems</a>,</li>
<li>the <a href="/wiki/Helmholtz_Association_of_German_Research_Centres" title="Helmholtz Association of German Research Centres">Helmholtz</a> Institute for Pharmaceutical Research Saarland (HIPS),<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[24]</a></sup></li>
<li>the <a href="/wiki/Fraunhofer_Society" title="Fraunhofer Society">Fraunhofer</a> Institute for Non-destructive Testing,</li>
<li>the <a href="/wiki/German_Research_Centre_for_Artificial_Intelligence" title="German Research Centre for Artificial Intelligence">German Research Centre for Artificial Intelligence</a>,</li>
<li>the Center for <a href="/wiki/Bioinformatics" title="Bioinformatics">Bioinformatics</a>,</li>
<li>the <a href="/wiki/Europa-Institut" title="Europa-Institut">Europa-Institut</a>,</li>
<li>the <a href="/wiki/Korea_Institute_of_Science_and_Technology#KIST_Europe" title="Korea Institute of Science and Technology">Korea Institute of Science and Technology Europe</a> Research Society,</li>
<li>the <a href="/wiki/Gottfried_Wilhelm_Leibniz_Scientific_Community#D_-_Mathematics.2C_Natural_Sciences_.26_Engineering" class="mw-redirect" title="Gottfried Wilhelm Leibniz Scientific Community">Leibniz Institute</a> for New Materials (INM), and</li>
<li>the <a href="/wiki/Intel" title="Intel">Intel</a> Visual Computing Institute,<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[25]</a></sup></li>
<li>the <i>Center for IT-Security, Privacy, and Accountability (CISPA)</i>,<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[26]</a></sup></li>
<li>the Society for Environmentally Compatible Process Technology,</li>
<li>the <i>Institut für Angewandte Informationsforschung</i> for applied linguistics,</li>
</ul>
<p>several institutes focusing on transfer of technology between academia and companies, and the <i>Science Park Saar</i> startup incubator. The <a href="/wiki/Botanischer_Garten_der_Universit%C3%A4t_des_Saarlandes" title="Botanischer Garten der Universität des Saarlandes">Botanischer Garten der Universität des Saarlandes</a> (a <a href="/wiki/Botanical_garden" title="Botanical garden">botanical garden</a>) was closed in 2016 due to budget cuts.</p>
<p>Furthermore, Saarbrücken houses the administration of the <i>Deutsch-Französische Hochschule</i>, a French-German cooperation of 180 institutions of <a href="/wiki/Tertiary_education" title="Tertiary education">tertiary education</a> mainly from France and Germany but also from Bulgaria, Canada, Spain, Luxembourg, Netherlands, Poland, Great Britain, Russia and Switzerland, which offers bi-national French-German degree programs and <a href="/wiki/Doctorate" title="Doctorate">doctorates</a> as well as tri-national degree programs. The Saarland University also has a <i>Centre Juridique Franco-Allemand</i>, offering a French and a German law degree program.</p>
<p>Saarbrücken houses several other institutions of tertiary education as well:</p>
<ul>
<li>the <a href="/wiki/Fachhochschule" title="Fachhochschule">University of Applied Sciences</a> <i>Hochschule für Technik und Wirtschaft des Saarlandes</i>,</li>
<li>the <a href="/wiki/Art_school" title="Art school">University of Arts</a> <i>Hochschule der Bildenden Künste Saar</i>,</li>
<li>the <a href="/wiki/Music_school" title="Music school">University of Music</a> <i>Hochschule für Musik Saar</i>, and</li>
<li>the private <a href="/wiki/Fachhochschule" title="Fachhochschule">Fachhochschule</a> for <a href="/wiki/Health_promotion" title="Health promotion">health promotion</a> and <a href="/wiki/Physical_fitness" title="Physical fitness">physical fitness</a> <i>Deutsche Hochschule für Prävention und Gesundheitsmanagement</i></li>
</ul>
<p>Saarbrücken also a houses a <a href="/wiki/Folk_high_school#Germany_and_Austria.23Germany_and_Austria" title="Folk high school">Volkshochschule</a>.</p>
<p>With the end of coal mining in the Saar region, Saarbrücken's <a href="/wiki/Fachhochschule" title="Fachhochschule">Fachhochschule</a> for mining, the <i>Fachhochschule für Bergbau Saar</i>, was closed at the beginning of the 21st century. The <a href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic_Diocese_of_Trier" title="Roman Catholic Diocese of Trier">Roman Catholic Diocese of Trier</a>'s <i>Katholische Hochschule für Soziale Arbeit</i>, a Fachhochschule for <a href="/wiki/Social_work" title="Social work">social work</a>, was closed in 2008 for cost cutting reasons. The Saarland's Fachhochschule for administrative personnel working for the government, the <i>Fachhochschule für Verwaltung des Saarlandes</i>, was moved from Saarbrücken to <i>Göttelborn</i> in 2012.</p>
<p>Saarbrücken houses several institutions of <a href="/wiki/Primary_education" title="Primary education">primary</a> and <a href="/wiki/Secondary_education" title="Secondary education">secondary education</a>. Notable is the Saarland's oldest <a href="/wiki/Gymnasium_(Germany)" title="Gymnasium (Germany)">grammar school</a>, the <i>Ludwigsgymnasium</i>, which was founded in 1604 as a <a href="/wiki/Latin_school" title="Latin school">latin school</a>. The building of Saarbrücken's bi-lingual French-German <i>Deutsch-Französisches Gymnasium</i>, founded in 1961 and operating as a <a href="/wiki/Laboratory_school" title="Laboratory school">laboratory school</a> under the <a href="/wiki/%C3%89lys%C3%A9e_Treaty" title="Élysée Treaty">Élysée Treaty</a>, also houses the <i>École française de Sarrebruck et Dilling</i>, a French primary school which offers bi-lingual German elements. Together with several <a href="/wiki/Kindergarten" title="Kindergarten">Kindergartens</a> offering bi-lingual French-German education, Saarbrücken thus offers a full bi-lingual French-German formal education.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geography">Geography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Saarbr%C3%BCcken&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Geography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Climate">Climate</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Saarbr%C3%BCcken&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Climate">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Region">Region</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Saarbr%C3%BCcken&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Region">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Some of the closest cities are <a href="/wiki/Trier" title="Trier">Trier</a>, <a href="/wiki/Luxembourg_(city)" class="mw-redirect" title="Luxembourg (city)">Luxembourg</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nancy,_France" title="Nancy, France">Nancy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Metz" title="Metz">Metz</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kaiserslautern" title="Kaiserslautern">Kaiserslautern</a>, <a href="/wiki/Karlsruhe" title="Karlsruhe">Karlsruhe</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mannheim" title="Mannheim">Mannheim</a>. Saarbrücken is connected by the city's <a href="/wiki/Public_transport" title="Public transport">public transport</a> network to the town of <a href="/wiki/Sarreguemines" title="Sarreguemines">Sarreguemines</a> in France, and to the neighboring town of <a href="/wiki/V%C3%B6lklingen" title="Völklingen">Völklingen</a>, where the old steel works were the first industrial <a href="/wiki/Monument" title="Monument">monument</a> to be declared a <a href="/wiki/World_Heritage_Site" title="World Heritage Site">World Heritage Site</a> by <a href="/wiki/UNESCO" title="UNESCO">UNESCO</a> in 1994 — the <a href="/wiki/V%C3%B6lklinger_H%C3%BCtte" class="mw-redirect" title="Völklinger Hütte">Völklinger Hütte</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sport">Sport</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Saarbr%C3%BCcken&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Sport">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The city is home to several different teams, most notable of which is <a href="/wiki/Association_football" title="Association football">association football</a> team based at the <a href="/wiki/Ludwigsparkstadion" title="Ludwigsparkstadion">Ludwigsparkstadion</a>, <a href="/wiki/1._FC_Saarbr%C3%BCcken" title="1. FC Saarbrücken">1. FC Saarbrücken</a>, which also has a <a href="/wiki/1._FC_Saarbr%C3%BCcken_II" title="1. FC Saarbrücken II">reserve team</a> and a <a href="/wiki/1._FC_Saarbr%C3%BCcken_(women)" title="1. FC Saarbrücken (women)">women's section</a>. In the past a <a href="/wiki/Bundesliga" title="Bundesliga">top-flight</a> team, twice the country's vice-champions, and participant in European competitions, the club draws supporters from across the <a href="/wiki/Saarland" title="Saarland">region</a>.</p>
<p>Lower league <a href="/wiki/SV_Saar_05_Saarbr%C3%BCcken" title="SV Saar 05 Saarbrücken">SV Saar 05 Saarbrücken</a> is the other football team in the city.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Saarland_Hurricanes" title="Saarland Hurricanes">Saarland Hurricanes</a> are one of the top <a href="/wiki/American_football" title="American football">American football</a> teams in the country, with its junior team winning the <a href="/wiki/German_Junior_Bowl" title="German Junior Bowl">German Junior Bowl</a> in 2013.</p>
<p>Various sporting events are held at the <a href="/wiki/Saarlandhalle" title="Saarlandhalle">Saarlandhalle</a>, most notable of which was the <a href="/wiki/Badminton" title="Badminton">badminton</a> <a href="/w/index.php?title=Bitburger_Open_Grand_Prix_Gold&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Bitburger Open Grand Prix Gold (page does not exist)">Bitburger Open Grand Prix Gold</a>, part of the <a href="/wiki/BWF_Grand_Prix_Gold_and_Grand_Prix" title="BWF Grand Prix Gold and Grand Prix">BWF Grand Prix Gold and Grand Prix</a> tournaments, held in <a href="/wiki/2013_Bitburger_Open_Grand_Prix_Gold" title="2013 Bitburger Open Grand Prix Gold">2013</a> and <a href="/wiki/2012_Bitburger_Open_Grand_Prix_Gold" title="2012 Bitburger Open Grand Prix Gold">2012</a>.</p>
