<p><b>Albrecht Wenzel Eusebius von Wallenstein</b> (<small><span class="unicode haudio"><span class="fn"><span style="white-space:nowrap"><a href="/wiki/File:De-Albrecht_Wenzel_Eusebius_von_Wallenstein.ogg" title="About this sound"><img alt="About this sound" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/11px-Loudspeaker.svg.png" width="11" height="11" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/17px-Loudspeaker.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/22px-Loudspeaker.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="20" data-file-height="20" /></a>&#160;</span><a href="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/1/14/De-Albrecht_Wenzel_Eusebius_von_Wallenstein.ogg" class="internal" title="De-Albrecht Wenzel Eusebius von Wallenstein.ogg">pronunciation</a></span>&#160;<small class="metadata audiolinkinfo" style="cursor:help;">(<a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Media_help" title="Wikipedia:Media help"><span style="cursor:help;">help</span></a>·<a href="/wiki/File:De-Albrecht_Wenzel_Eusebius_von_Wallenstein.ogg" title="File:De-Albrecht Wenzel Eusebius von Wallenstein.ogg"><span style="cursor:help;">info</span></a>)</small></span></small>; <a href="/wiki/Czech_language" title="Czech language">Czech</a>: <span lang="cs" xml:lang="cs"><i>Albrecht Václav Eusebius z Valdštejna</i></span>;<sup id="cite_ref-Encyclop.C3.A6dia_Britannica_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Encyclop.C3.A6dia_Britannica-1">[1]</a></sup> 24 September 1583&#160;– 25 February 1634),<sup id="cite_ref-Schiller-1_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Schiller-1-2">[2]</a></sup> also <b>von Waldstein</b>,<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[a]</a></sup> was a <a href="/wiki/Bohemia" title="Bohemia">Bohemian</a><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[b]</a></sup> military leader and Holy Roman Empire <a href="/wiki/Count_palatine" title="Count palatine">Count palatine</a>, who offered his services, an army of 30,000 to 100,000 men, during the <a href="/wiki/Thirty_Years%27_War" title="Thirty Years' War">Thirty Years' War</a> (1618–48), to emperor <a href="/wiki/Ferdinand_II,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Ferdinand II, Holy Roman Emperor">Ferdinand II</a>. He became the <a href="/wiki/Commander-in-chief" title="Commander-in-chief">supreme commander</a> of the armies of the <a href="/wiki/Habsburg_Monarchy" title="Habsburg Monarchy">Habsburg Monarchy</a> and was a major figure of the Thirty Years' War.</p>
<p>An imperial <a href="/wiki/Generalissimo" title="Generalissimo">generalissimo</a><sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[3]</a></sup> by land, and Admiral of the Baltic Sea from 21 April 1628, who had made himself ruler of the lands of the <a href="/wiki/Duchy_of_Friedland" title="Duchy of Friedland">Duchy of Friedland</a> in northern <a href="/wiki/Bohemia" title="Bohemia">Bohemia</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-Coxe_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Coxe-6">[4]</a></sup> Wallenstein found himself released from service on 13 August 1630 after Ferdinand grew wary of his ambition.<sup id="cite_ref-Fortune_City_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Fortune_City-7">[5]</a></sup> Several <a href="/wiki/Protestant" class="mw-redirect" title="Protestant">Protestant</a> victories over <a href="/wiki/Catholic_Church" title="Catholic Church">Catholic</a> armies induced Ferdinand to recall Wallenstein, who again turned the war in favor of the Imperial cause. Dissatisfied with the Emperor's treatment of him, Wallenstein considered allying with the Protestants. However, he was assassinated at <a href="/wiki/Cheb" title="Cheb">Eger/Cheb</a> in <a href="/wiki/Bohemia" title="Bohemia">Bohemia</a> by one of the army's officials, an Irish general, Walter Devereux, with the emperor's approval.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Albrecht_von_Wallenstein&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:162px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Isabelle_von_Harrach.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1e/Isabelle_von_Harrach.jpg/160px-Isabelle_von_Harrach.jpg" width="160" height="240" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/1/1e/Isabelle_von_Harrach.jpg 1.5x" data-file-width="200" data-file-height="300" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Isabelle_von_Harrach.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Isabelle von Harrach, Wallenstein's second wife</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Wallenstein was born on 24 September 1583 in <a href="/wiki/He%C5%99manice_(N%C3%A1chod_District)" title="Heřmanice (Náchod District)">Heřmanice</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bohemia" title="Bohemia">Bohemia</a>, which is the easternmost and largest region in what was then the <a href="/wiki/Holy_Roman_Empire" title="Holy Roman Empire">Holy Roman Empire</a>, the present-day <a href="/wiki/Czech_Republic" title="Czech Republic">Czech Republic</a>, into a poor <a href="/wiki/Protestantism" title="Protestantism">Protestant</a> branch of the <a href="/wiki/Waldstein_family" title="Waldstein family">Waldstein (Wallenstein, Valdštejn) family</a> who owned Heřmanice castle and seven surrounding villages.<sup id="cite_ref-Schiller-1_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Schiller-1-2">[2]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[6]</a></sup> His mother, Markéta Smiřická of Smiřice, died in 1593; his father, Vilém, died in 1595.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>They had raised him bilingually – the father spoke German while his mother preferred Czech – yet Wallenstein in his childhood had a better command of <a href="/wiki/Czech_language" title="Czech language">Czech</a> than of <a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Rebitsch23_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rebitsch23-10">[8]</a></sup> His parents' religious affiliations were <a href="/wiki/Lutheranism" title="Lutheranism">Lutheranism</a> and <a href="/wiki/Utraquism" title="Utraquism">Utraquism</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Rebitsch23_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rebitsch23-10">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>After their deaths, Albrecht for two years lived with his maternal uncle Heinrich (Jindřich) Slavata of Chlum and Košumberk, a member of the <a href="/wiki/Unity_of_the_Brethren" title="Unity of the Brethren">Unity of the Brethren</a> (Bohemian Brethren), and adopted his uncle's religious affiliation.<sup id="cite_ref-Rebitsch23_10-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rebitsch23-10">[8]</a></sup> His uncle sent him to the brethren's school at <a href="/wiki/Lu%C5%BEe_(Chrudim_District)#Ko.C5.A1umberk_castle" title="Luže (Chrudim District)">Košumberk Castle</a> in Eastern Bohemia.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>In 1597, Albrecht was sent to the Protestant Latin school at <a href="/wiki/Z%C5%82otoryja" title="Złotoryja">Goldberg (now Złotoryja)</a> in <a href="/wiki/Silesia" title="Silesia">Silesia</a>, where the then-German environment led him to hone his German language skills.<sup id="cite_ref-Rebitsch23_10-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rebitsch23-10">[8]</a></sup> While German became Wallenstein's lingua franca, he is said to have continued to curse in Czech.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[9]</a></sup> On 29 August 1599, Wallenstein continued his education at the Protestant <a href="/wiki/University_of_Altdorf" title="University of Altdorf">University of Altdorf</a> near <a href="/wiki/Nuremberg" title="Nuremberg">Nuremberg</a>, <a href="/wiki/Franconia" title="Franconia">Franconia</a>, where he was often engaged in brawls and épée fights, leading to his imprisonment in the town prison.<sup id="cite_ref-Rebitsch23_10-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rebitsch23-10">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>In February 1600,<sup id="cite_ref-Rebitsch23_10-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rebitsch23-10">[8]</a></sup> Albrecht left <a href="/wiki/Altdorf_bei_N%C3%BCrnberg" title="Altdorf bei Nürnberg">Altdorf</a> and travelled around the <a href="/wiki/Holy_Roman_Empire" title="Holy Roman Empire">Holy Roman Empire</a>, <a href="/wiki/Early_modern_France" title="Early modern France">France</a> and <a href="/wiki/History_of_Italy_(1559%E2%80%931814)" title="History of Italy (1559–1814)">Italy</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-Rebitsch24_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rebitsch24-12">[10]</a></sup> where he studied at the universities of <a href="/wiki/University_of_Bologna" title="University of Bologna">Bologna</a> and <a href="/wiki/University_of_Padua" title="University of Padua">Padua</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Ripley_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ripley-13">[11]</a></sup> By this time, Wallenstein was fluent in German, Czech, <a href="/wiki/Latin_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Latin language">Latin</a> and <a href="/wiki/Italian_language" title="Italian language">Italian</a>, was able to understand <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">Spanish</a>, and spoke some <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Rebitsch23_10-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rebitsch23-10">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>Wallenstein then joined the army of the Emperor <a href="/wiki/Rudolf_II,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Rudolf II, Holy Roman Emperor">Rudolf II</a> in <a href="/wiki/Royal_Hungary" class="mw-redirect" title="Royal Hungary">Hungary</a>, where, under the command of <a href="/wiki/Giorgio_Basta" title="Giorgio Basta">Giorgio Basta</a>, he saw two years of armed service (1604–1606) against the <a href="/wiki/Long_War_(Ottoman_wars)" class="mw-redirect" title="Long War (Ottoman wars)">Ottoman Turks</a> and <a href="/wiki/Stephen_Bocskay" class="mw-redirect" title="Stephen Bocskay">Hungarian</a> rebels.<sup id="cite_ref-Ripley_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ripley-13">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1604, his sister Kateřina Anna married the leader of the Moravian Protestants, Karel the Older <a href="/wiki/House_of_Zierotin" class="mw-redirect" title="House of Zierotin">of Zierotin</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[12]</a></sup> He then studied at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Olomouc" class="mw-redirect" title="University of Olomouc">University of Olomouc</a> (matriculating in 1606). His contact with the Olomouc <a href="/wiki/Jesuit_order" class="mw-redirect" title="Jesuit order">Jesuits</a> was partly responsible for his conversion to <a href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic_Church" class="mw-redirect" title="Roman Catholic Church">Catholicism</a> in the same year.<sup id="cite_ref-Rebitsch24_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rebitsch24-12">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>The contributory factor to his conversion may have been the <a href="/wiki/Counter-Reformation" title="Counter-Reformation">Counter-Reformation</a> policy of the <a href="/wiki/Habsburg" class="mw-redirect" title="Habsburg">Habsburgs</a> that effectively barred Protestants from being appointed to higher offices at court in Bohemia and in Moravia, and the impressions he gathered in Catholic Italy.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[13]</a></sup> However, there are no sources clearly indicating the reasons for Wallenstein's conversion, except for a subjunctive anecdote by his contemporary Franz Christoph von Khevenhüller about the Virgin Mary saving Wallenstein's life when he fell from a window in <a href="/wiki/Innsbruck" title="Innsbruck">Innsbruck</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Rebitsch24_12-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rebitsch24-12">[10]</a></sup> Wallenstein was later made a member of the <a href="/wiki/List_of_Knights_of_the_Golden_Fleece" title="List of Knights of the Golden Fleece">Order of the Golden Fleece</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>In 1607, based on recommendations by his brother-in-law, Zierotin, and another relative, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Adam_of_Waldstein&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Adam of Waldstein (page does not exist)">Adam of Waldstein</a>, often mistakenly referred to as his uncle, Wallenstein was made <a href="/wiki/Chamberlain_(office)" title="Chamberlain (office)">chamberlain</a> at the court of <a href="/wiki/Matthias,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Matthias, Holy Roman Emperor">Matthias</a>, and later also chamberlain to archdukes Ferdinand and Maximilian.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1609, Wallenstein married Czech Lucretia of Víckov, née Nekšová, of Landek,<sup id="cite_ref-Encyclop.C3.A6dia_Britannica_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Encyclop.C3.A6dia_Britannica-1">[1]</a></sup> the wealthy widow of Arkleb of Víckov<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[15]</a></sup> who owned the towns of <a href="/wiki/Vset%C3%ADn" title="Vsetín">Vsetín</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lukov_(Zl%C3%ADn_District)" title="Lukov (Zlín District)">Lukov</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rymice" title="Rymice">Rymice</a> and Všetuly/<a href="/wiki/Hole%C5%A1ov" title="Holešov">Holešov</a> (all in eastern Moravia).<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[16]</a></sup> She was three years older than Wallenstein, and he inherited her estates after her death in 1614.<sup id="cite_ref-Ripley_13-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ripley-13">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>He used his wealth to win favour, offering and commanding 200 horses for Archduke <a href="/wiki/Ferdinand_II,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Ferdinand II, Holy Roman Emperor">Ferdinand of Styria</a> for his war with <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Venice" title="Republic of Venice">Venice</a> in 1617, thereby relieving the fortress of <a href="/wiki/Gradisca" class="mw-redirect" title="Gradisca">Gradisca</a> from the Venetian siege.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[17]</a></sup> He later endowed a monastery in his dead wife's name and had her reburied there.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>In 1623, Wallenstein married Isabella Katharina, daughter of Count <a href="/wiki/Harrach" title="Harrach">Harrach</a>. She bore him two children, a son who died in infancy and a surviving daughter.<sup id="cite_ref-Ripley_13-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ripley-13">[11]</a></sup> Examples of the couple's correspondence survive. The two marriages made him one of the wealthiest men in the <a href="/wiki/Crown_of_Bohemia" class="mw-redirect" title="Crown of Bohemia">Bohemian Crown</a>.</p>
<h2><span id="Thirty_Years'_War"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Thirty_Years.27_War">Thirty Years' War</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Albrecht_von_Wallenstein&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Thirty Years' War">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p>The <a href="/wiki/Thirty_Years%27_War" title="Thirty Years' War">Thirty Years' War</a> began in 1618 when the <a href="/wiki/The_Estates" title="The Estates">estates</a> of Bohemia rebelled against Ferdinand of Styria and elected <a href="/wiki/Frederick_V,_Elector_Palatine" class="mw-redirect" title="Frederick V, Elector Palatine">Frederick V, Elector Palatine</a>, the leader of the <a href="/wiki/Protestant_Union" title="Protestant Union">Protestant Union</a>, as their new king. Wallenstein associated himself with the cause of the <a href="/wiki/Catholics" class="mw-redirect" title="Catholics">Catholics</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Habsburg_dynasty" class="mw-redirect" title="Habsburg dynasty">Habsburg dynasty</a>.</p>
<p>Sympathizing with the Bohemians, he used his position as commander of the troops of the Moravian estates to escape with the Moravian treasure-chest to <a href="/wiki/Vienna" title="Vienna">Vienna</a>. There, however, the authorities told him that the money would go back to the Moravians — but he had shown his loyalty to Ferdinand, the future Emperor.</p>
<p>Wallenstein equipped a regiment of <a href="/wiki/Cuirassier" title="Cuirassier">cuirassiers</a> and won great distinction under <a href="/wiki/Charles_Bonaventure_de_Longueval,_Count_of_Bucquoy" title="Charles Bonaventure de Longueval, Count of Bucquoy">Charles Bonaventure de Longueval, Count of Bucquoy</a> in the wars against <a href="/wiki/Ernst_von_Mansfeld" title="Ernst von Mansfeld">Ernst von Mansfeld</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gabriel_Bethlen" title="Gabriel Bethlen">Gabriel Bethlen</a> (both supporters of the Bohemian revolt) in <a href="/wiki/Moravia" title="Moravia">Moravia</a>. Wallenstein recovered his lands (which the rebels had seized in 1619) and after the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_White_Mountain" title="Battle of White Mountain">Battle of White Mountain</a> (8 November 1620) he secured the estates belonging to his mother's family and confiscated tracts of Protestant lands.</p>
<p>He grouped his new possessions into a territory called <a href="/wiki/Fr%C3%BDdlant_v_%C4%8Cechach" class="mw-redirect" title="Frýdlant v Čechach">Friedland (Frýdlant)</a> in northern Bohemia. A series of successes in battle led to Wallenstein becoming in 1622 an imperial <a href="/wiki/Count_palatine" title="Count palatine">count palatine</a>, in 1623 a prince, and in 1625 <a href="/wiki/Duke" title="Duke">Duke</a> of <a href="/wiki/Duchy_of_Friedland" title="Duchy of Friedland">Friedland</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Schiller_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Schiller-20">[18]</a></sup> Wallenstein proved an able administrator of the duchy<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[19]</a></sup> and sent a large representation to Prague to emphasize his nobility.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (June 2017)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>

<p>In order to aid Ferdinand (elected <a href="/wiki/Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Holy Roman Emperor">Holy Roman Emperor</a> in 1619) against the Northern Protestants and to produce a balance in the Army of the <a href="/wiki/Catholic_League_(German)" title="Catholic League (German)">Catholic League</a> under <a href="/wiki/Johann_Tserclaes,_Count_of_Tilly" title="Johann Tserclaes, Count of Tilly">Johann Tserclaes, Count of Tilly</a>, Wallenstein offered to raise a whole army for the imperial service following the <i><a href="/wiki/Bellum_se_ipsum_alet" title="Bellum se ipsum alet">bellum se ipsum alet</a></i> principle, and received his final commission on 25 July 1625. Wallenstein's successes as a military commander brought him fiscal credit, which in turn enabled him to receive loans to buy lands, many of them being the former estates of conquered Bohemian nobles. He used his credit to grant loans to Ferdinand II, which were repaid through lands and titles.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[20]</a></sup> Wallenstein's popularity soon recruited 30,000 (not long afterwards 50,000) men.<sup id="cite_ref-Eggenberger_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Eggenberger-23">[21]</a></sup> The two armies worked together over 1625–27, at first against Mansfeld.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Having beaten Mansfeld at <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Dessau_Bridge" title="Battle of Dessau Bridge">Dessau</a> (25 April 1626), Wallenstein cleared <a href="/wiki/Silesia" title="Silesia">Silesia</a> of the remnants of Mansfeld's army in 1627.<sup id="cite_ref-Eggenberger_23-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Eggenberger-23">[21]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Fuller_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Fuller-24">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>At this time he bought from the emperor the <a href="/wiki/Duchy_of_Sagan" class="mw-redirect" title="Duchy of Sagan">Duchy of Sagan</a> (in Silesia). He then joined Tilly in the struggle against <a href="/wiki/Christian_IV_of_Denmark" title="Christian IV of Denmark">Christian IV of Denmark</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[23]</a></sup> and afterwards gained as a reward the Duchies of <a href="/wiki/Mecklenburg" title="Mecklenburg">Mecklenburg</a>, whose hereditary dukes suffered expulsion for having helped the <a href="/wiki/Denmark" title="Denmark">Danish</a> king. This awarding of a major territory to someone of the lower nobility shocked the high-born rulers of many other <a href="/wiki/List_of_states_in_the_Holy_Roman_Empire" title="List of states in the Holy Roman Empire">German states</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Wedgwood_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wedgwood-26">[24]</a></sup></p>
<p>Wallenstein assumed the title of "<a href="/wiki/Admiral" title="Admiral">Admiral</a> of the <a href="/wiki/North_Sea" title="North Sea">North</a> and <a href="/wiki/Baltic_Sea" title="Baltic Sea">Baltic Seas</a>". However, in 1628 he failed to capture <a href="/wiki/Stralsund" title="Stralsund">Stralsund</a>, which resisted the <a href="/wiki/Capitulation_of_Franzburg" title="Capitulation of Franzburg">Capitulation of Franzburg</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Stralsund_(1628)" class="mw-redirect" title="Battle of Stralsund (1628)">subsequent siege</a> with assistance of Danish, Scottish and <a href="/wiki/Sweden" title="Sweden">Swedish</a> troops, a blow that denied him access to the Baltic and the chance of challenging the naval power of the <a href="/wiki/Scandinavia" title="Scandinavia">Scandinavian</a> kingdoms and of the <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Netherlands</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Fuller_24-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Fuller-24">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>Although he succeeded in defeating <a href="/wiki/Christian_IV_of_Denmark" title="Christian IV of Denmark">Christian IV of Denmark</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Wolgast" title="Battle of Wolgast">Battle of Wolgast</a> and neutralizing <a href="/wiki/Denmark" title="Denmark">Denmark</a> in the subsequent <a href="/wiki/Peace_of_L%C3%BCbeck" class="mw-redirect" title="Peace of Lübeck">Peace of Lübeck</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[25]</a></sup> the situation further deteriorated when the presence of Imperial Catholic troops on the Baltic and the Emperor's "<a href="/wiki/Edict_of_Restitution" title="Edict of Restitution">Edict of Restitution</a>" brought King <a href="/wiki/Gustavus_Adolphus_of_Sweden" title="Gustavus Adolphus of Sweden">Gustavus Adolphus of Sweden</a> into the conflict.<sup id="cite_ref-Fuller_24-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Fuller-24">[22]</a></sup> Wallenstein attempted to aid the forces of the <a href="/wiki/Polish-Lithuanian_Commonwealth" class="mw-redirect" title="Polish-Lithuanian Commonwealth">Polish-Lithuanian Commonwealth</a> under <a href="/wiki/Hetman" title="Hetman">Hetman</a> <a href="/wiki/Stanis%C5%82aw_Koniecpolski" title="Stanisław Koniecpolski">Stanisław Koniecpolski</a>, which were <a href="/wiki/Polish-Swedish_War" class="mw-redirect" title="Polish-Swedish War">fighting Sweden</a> in 1629; however, Wallenstein failed to engage any major Swedish forces and this significantly affected the outcome of the conflict.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[26]</a></sup></p>

<p>Over the course of the war Wallenstein's ambitions and the abuses of his forces had earned him a host of enemies, both Catholic and Protestant, princes and non-princes alike. Ferdinand suspected Wallenstein of planning a coup to take control of the <a href="/wiki/Holy_Roman_Empire" title="Holy Roman Empire">Holy Roman Empire</a>. The Emperor's advisors advocated dismissing him, and in September 1630 envoys were sent to Wallenstein to announce his removal.<sup id="cite_ref-Schiller_20-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Schiller-20">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>Wallenstein gave over his army to General Tilly, and retired to <a href="/wiki/Ji%C4%8D%C3%ADn" title="Jičín">Jičín</a>, the capital of his Duchy of Friedland. There he lived in an atmosphere of "mysterious magnificence".<sup id="cite_ref-Ingrao_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ingrao-29">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p>However, circumstances forced Ferdinand to call Wallenstein into the field again.<sup id="cite_ref-Schiller_20-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Schiller-20">[18]</a></sup> The successes of Gustavus Adolphus over General Tilly at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Breitenfeld_(1631)" title="Battle of Breitenfeld (1631)">Battle of Breitenfeld</a> and on the <a href="/wiki/Lech_river" class="mw-redirect" title="Lech river">Lech</a> (1632), where Tilly was killed, and his advance to <a href="/wiki/Munich" title="Munich">Munich</a> and occupation of Bohemia, demanded action.<sup id="cite_ref-Ingrao_29-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ingrao-29">[27]</a></sup> In mid-1632, Wallenstein raised a fresh army within a few weeks and took to the field. He drove the Saxon army from Bohemia and then advanced against Gustavus Adolphus, whom he opposed near <a href="/wiki/Nuremberg" title="Nuremberg">Nuremberg</a>, and, after the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_the_Alte_Veste" title="Battle of the Alte Veste">Battle of the Alte Veste</a>, dislodged. In November, came the great <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_L%C3%BCtzen_(1632)" title="Battle of Lützen (1632)">Battle of Lützen</a>, in which Wallenstein was forced to retreat but in the confused melee, Gustavus Adolphus was killed. Wallenstein withdrew to winter quarters in Bohemia.<sup id="cite_ref-Ingrao_29-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ingrao-29">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the campaigning of 1633, Wallenstein's apparent unwillingness to attack the enemy caused much concern in Vienna and in Spain. At this time the dimensions of the war grew more European. Wallenstein had begun preparing to desert the Emperor. He expressed anger at Ferdinand's refusal to revoke the <a href="/wiki/Edict_of_Restitution" title="Edict of Restitution">Edict of Restitution</a>. Historic records tell little about his secret negotiations but some sources <sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (June 2017)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup> indicated he was preparing to force a "just peace" on the Emperor "in the interests of united <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a>". With this apparent "plan" he entered into negotiations with <a href="/wiki/Electorate_of_Saxony" title="Electorate of Saxony">Saxony</a>, <a href="/wiki/Margraviate_of_Brandenburg" title="Margraviate of Brandenburg">Brandenburg</a>, <a href="/wiki/Swedish_Empire" title="Swedish Empire">Sweden</a>, and <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a>. But, apparently, the Habsburgs' enemies tried to draw him to their side. In any case, he gained little support. Anxious to make his power felt, he resumed the offensive against the Swedes and Saxons, winning his last victory at <a href="/wiki/%C5%9Acinawa" title="Ścinawa">Steinau</a> on the <a href="/wiki/Oder" title="Oder">Oder</a> in October. He then resumed negotiations.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEChisholm1911281_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEChisholm1911281-30">[28]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Assassination">Assassination</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Albrecht_von_Wallenstein&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Assassination">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Ermordung-wallensteins-in-eger-anonymer-kupferstich_1-640x370.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7a/Ermordung-wallensteins-in-eger-anonymer-kupferstich_1-640x370.jpg/300px-Ermordung-wallensteins-in-eger-anonymer-kupferstich_1-640x370.jpg" width="300" height="173" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7a/Ermordung-wallensteins-in-eger-anonymer-kupferstich_1-640x370.jpg/450px-Ermordung-wallensteins-in-eger-anonymer-kupferstich_1-640x370.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7a/Ermordung-wallensteins-in-eger-anonymer-kupferstich_1-640x370.jpg/600px-Ermordung-wallensteins-in-eger-anonymer-kupferstich_1-640x370.jpg 2x" data-file-width="640" data-file-height="370" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Ermordung-wallensteins-in-eger-anonymer-kupferstich_1-640x370.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The Killing of Wallenstein in <a href="/wiki/Cheb" title="Cheb">Eger/Cheb</a>.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>In December Wallenstein retired with his army to Bohemia, around Pilsen (now <a href="/wiki/Plze%C5%88" title="Plzeň">Plzeň</a>). Vienna soon definitely convinced itself of his treachery, a <a href="/wiki/Secret_court" class="mw-redirect" title="Secret court">secret court</a> found him guilty, and the Emperor looked seriously for a means of getting rid of him (a successor in command, the later emperor <a href="/wiki/Ferdinand_III,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Ferdinand III, Holy Roman Emperor">Ferdinand III</a>, was already waiting). Wallenstein was aware of the plan to replace him, but felt confident that when the army came to decide between him and the Emperor the decision would be in his favour.<sup id="cite_ref-Ingrao_29-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ingrao-29">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p>On 24 January 1634 the Emperor signed a secret patent (shown only to certain officers of Wallenstein's army) removing him from his command. Finally an open <a href="/wiki/Letters_patent" title="Letters patent">patent</a> charging Wallenstein with <a href="/wiki/High_treason" title="High treason">high treason</a> was signed on 18 February and published in <a href="/wiki/Prague" title="Prague">Prague</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Schiller_20-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Schiller-20">[18]</a></sup> In the patent, Ferdinand II ordered to have Wallenstein brought under arrest to Vienna, dead or alive.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[29]</a></sup> Losing the support of his army, Wallenstein now realized the extent of his peril, and on 23 February with a company of some hundred men, he went from Plzeň to <a href="/wiki/Cheb" title="Cheb">Eger/Cheb</a>, hoping to meet the Swedes under <a href="/wiki/Bernhard_of_Saxe-Weimar" class="mw-redirect" title="Bernhard of Saxe-Weimar">Duke Bernhard</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEChisholm1911282_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEChisholm1911282-32">[30]</a></sup></p>
<p>After his arrival at Cheb, however, certain senior Scottish and Irish officers in his force assassinated him on the night of February 25.<sup id="cite_ref-Ingrao_29-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ingrao-29">[27]</a></sup> To carry out the assassination, a regiment of dragoons under the command of an Irish colonel, Walter Butler<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">[31]</a></sup> and the Scots colonels <a href="/wiki/Walter_Leslie_(1607-1667)" class="mw-redirect" title="Walter Leslie (1607-1667)">Walter Leslie and John Gordon</a><sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">[32]</a></sup> first fell upon Wallenstein's trusted officers <a href="https://de.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adam_Erdmann_Tr%C4%8Dka_von_L%C3%ADpa" class="extiw" title="de:Adam Erdmann Trčka von Lípa">Adam Trczka</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vil%C3%A9m_Kinsk%C3%BD" title="Vilém Kinský">Vilém Kinsky</a>, Christian Illov and Henry Neumann while the latter attended a feast at Cheb Castle (which had come under the command of Gordon himself), and massacred them. Trczka alone managed to fight his way out into the courtyard, only to be shot down by a group of musketeers.<sup id="cite_ref-Wedgwood_26-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wedgwood-26">[24]</a></sup> A few hours later, an Irish captain, Walter Devereux, together with a few companions, broke into the burgomaster's house at the main square where Wallenstein had his lodgings (again courtesy of Gordon), and kicked open the bedroom door. Devereux then ran his <a href="/wiki/Halberd" title="Halberd">halberd</a> through the unarmed Wallenstein, who, roused from sleep, is said to have asked in vain for <a href="/wiki/No_quarter" title="No quarter">quarter</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Holy Roman Emperor">Holy Roman Emperor</a> had given free rein to the party who he knew wished <i>"to bring in Wallenstein, alive or dead"</i>. After the assassination, he rewarded the murderers with honour and riches.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">[33]</a></sup></p>
<p>Wallenstein was buried at <a href="/wiki/Ji%C4%8D%C3%ADn" title="Jičín">Jičín</a>. In 1785, his remains were moved to <a href="/wiki/Mnichovo_Hradi%C5%A1t%C4%9B" title="Mnichovo Hradiště">Mnichovo Hradiště</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legacy">Legacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Albrecht_von_Wallenstein&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Legacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Czech National Museum produced a large exhibition about Wallenstein at the <a href="/wiki/Wallenstein_Palace" title="Wallenstein Palace">Wallenstein Palace</a> in Prague (current seat of Senate) from 15 November 2007 till 15 February 2008. He is also the subject of Calderón de la Barca's play <i><a href="/wiki/Wallenstein_(play)" class="mw-redirect" title="Wallenstein (play)">El Prodigio de Alemania</a></i><sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">[34]</a></sup> and Schiller's play trilogy <i><a href="/wiki/Wallenstein_(play)" class="mw-redirect" title="Wallenstein (play)">Wallenstein</a></i>.</p>
<p>One of the episodes in <a href="/wiki/Erich_K%C3%A4stner" title="Erich Kästner">Erich Kästner</a>'s "<a href="/wiki/The_35th_of_May,_or_Conrad%27s_Ride_to_the_South_Seas" title="The 35th of May, or Conrad's Ride to the South Seas">The 35 May</a>" depicts Wallenstein in his afterlife being engaged in a fierce war with <a href="/wiki/Hannibal" title="Hannibal">Hannibal</a> and emphasizes both generals' callous disregard for the lives of their soldiers - underlining Kästner's <a href="/wiki/Pacifist" class="mw-redirect" title="Pacifist">pacifist</a> views. Wallenstein is also a main figure in <a href="/wiki/Alfred_D%C3%B6blin" title="Alfred Döblin">Alfred Döblin</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Wallenstein_(novel)" title="Wallenstein (novel)">eponymous novel</a> from 1920.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Wallenstein's particular genius lay in recognizing a new way for funding war: instead of merely plundering enemies, he called for a new method of systematic "war taxes". Even a city or a prince on the side of the Emperor had to pay taxes towards the war.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Wallenstein understood the enormous wastage of resources that resulted from tax exactions on princes and cities of defeated enemies only, and desired to replace this with a "balanced" system of taxation; wherein both sides bore the cost of a war. He was unable to fully realize this ambition. His idea led to the random exploitation of whole populations on either side, until finally, almost fifteen years after his death, the war had become so expensive that the warring parties were forced to make peace.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Wallenstein's idea reportedly inspired many, among them, <a href="/wiki/Jean-Baptiste_Colbert" title="Jean-Baptiste Colbert">Jean-Baptiste Colbert</a>, to "pluck the goose with a minimum of screeching".<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Composer <a href="/wiki/Bed%C5%99ich_Smetana" title="Bedřich Smetana">Bedřich Smetana</a> honored Wallenstein in his 1859 symphonic poem <i><a href="/wiki/Wallenstein%27s_Camp" class="mw-redirect" title="Wallenstein's Camp">Wallenstein's Camp</a></i>, which was originally intended as an overture to a play by <a href="/wiki/Schiller" class="mw-redirect" title="Schiller">Schiller</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">[35]</a></sup></p>
<p>Wallenstein has also been examined by such economists as <a href="/wiki/Arthur_Salz" title="Arthur Salz">Arthur Salz</a> in his book <i>Wallenstein als Merkantalist</i> (<i>Wallenstein as <a href="/wiki/Mercantilist" class="mw-redirect" title="Mercantilist">Mercantilist</a></i>).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
