<p><b>Kevin Coyne</b> (27 January 1944 – 2 December 2004) was an English musician, singer, composer, film-maker, and a writer of lyrics, stories and poems. The "anti-star"<sup id="cite_ref-Clayson_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Clayson-1">[1]</a></sup> was born in <a href="/wiki/Derby" title="Derby">Derby</a>, England, and died in his adopted home of <a href="/wiki/Nuremberg" title="Nuremberg">Nuremberg</a>, Germany.</p>
<p>Coyne is notable for his unorthodox style of blues-influenced guitar composition, the intense quality of his vocal delivery, and his bold treatment of injustice to the mentally ill in his lyrics. Many influential musicians have described themselves as Coyne fans, among them <a href="/wiki/Sting_(musician)" title="Sting (musician)">Sting</a> and <a href="/wiki/John_Lydon" title="John Lydon">John Lydon</a>. In the mid-1970s, prior to the formation of <a href="/wiki/The_Police" title="The Police">the Police</a>, Coyne's band included guitarist <a href="/wiki/Andy_Summers" title="Andy Summers">Andy Summers</a>. Prominent <a href="/wiki/BBC" title="BBC">BBC</a> disc jockey and <a href="/wiki/World_music" title="World music">world music</a> authority <a href="/wiki/Andy_Kershaw" title="Andy Kershaw">Andy Kershaw</a> has described Coyne as "a national treasure who keeps getting better" and as one of the great <a href="/wiki/British_blues" title="British blues">British blues</a> voices.</p>
<p>Over many years Coyne produced the distinctive art work for many of his own album covers but his move to Germany, in the 1980s, saw his work on full-size paintings blossom in its own right.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_days">Early days</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kevin_Coyne&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early days">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>As a teenager and young adult Coyne studied at the <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Wright_of_Derby" title="Joseph Wright of Derby">Joseph Wright School of Art</a> from 1957 to 1961 and then studied graphics and painting at Derby School of Art from 1961 to 1965. There he met Nick Cudworth (piano, acoustic guitar).<sup id="cite_ref-kevincoynepage_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kevincoynepage-3">[3]</a></sup> His love of American <a href="/wiki/Blues" title="Blues">bluesmen</a> developed, as did his song-craft and his guitar and vocal talents.</p>
<p>At the conclusion of his arts training, Coyne began the work that would change him forever – he spent the three years, from 1965 to 1968, working as a social therapist and <a href="/wiki/Psychiatric_nurse" class="mw-redirect" title="Psychiatric nurse">psychiatric nurse</a> at <a href="/wiki/Whittingham_Hospital" title="Whittingham Hospital">Whittingham Hospital</a> near <a href="/wiki/Preston,_Lancashire" title="Preston, Lancashire">Preston</a> in Lancashire and then for "The Soho Project" in London as a drugs counsellor. During this period of working with the mentally ill he performed regularly. Subsequently, his musical aspirations took precedence and he signed a record deal in 1969.<sup id="cite_ref-kevincoynepage_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kevincoynepage-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Joined by Dave Clague (bass, acoustic guitar, ex-<a href="/wiki/Bonzo_Dog_Doo-Dah_Band" title="Bonzo Dog Doo-Dah Band">Bonzo Dog Doo-Dah Band</a>), Coyne's band got an early break as a result of a demo heard by <a href="/wiki/John_Peel" title="John Peel">John Peel</a>, who in 1969 signed them to his <a href="/wiki/Dandelion_Records" title="Dandelion Records">Dandelion Records</a> label. At first billed as Coyne-Clague (an early Dandelion release erroneously named them just "Clague"), the band soon altered its name to Siren.<sup id="cite_ref-kevincoynepage_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kevincoynepage-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="An_established_artist">An established artist</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kevin_Coyne&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: An established artist">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1973 he appeared on the BBC's <i><a href="/wiki/The_Old_Grey_Whistle_Test" title="The Old Grey Whistle Test">The Old Grey Whistle Test</a></i>, performing "I Want My Crown" and "House on the Hill" with guitarist Gordon Smith and percussionist Chilli Charles.</p>
<p>In late 1975 and 1976 Coyne completed the musical <i>England, England</i>, written with playwright <a href="/wiki/Snoo_Wilson" title="Snoo Wilson">Snoo Wilson</a>, and described as "an evocation of the <a href="/wiki/Kray_Twins" class="mw-redirect" title="Kray Twins">Kray Twins</a>". The musical, directed by <a href="/wiki/Dusty_Hughes_(playwright)" title="Dusty Hughes (playwright)">Dusty Hughes</a>, was performed on stage in August 1977 at the <a href="/wiki/Jeannetta_Cochrane_Theatre" class="mw-redirect" title="Jeannetta Cochrane Theatre">Jeannetta Cochrane Theatre</a>, in <a href="/wiki/Holborn" title="Holborn">Holborn</a>, London. It was one of the first theatre pieces to reference the fascist associations of a kind of British nationalism that later became more prevalent with the rise of the <a href="/wiki/National_Front_(UK)" title="National Front (UK)">National Front</a> and the election of <a href="/wiki/Margaret_Thatcher" title="Margaret Thatcher">Margaret Thatcher</a>. From 18 August to 24 September 1977 it played at the <a href="/wiki/Bush_Theatre" title="Bush Theatre">Bush Theatre</a> in <a href="/wiki/Shepherds_Bush" class="mw-redirect" title="Shepherds Bush">Shepherds Bush</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1978 Coyne collaborated with fellow Derby Art School graduate <a href="/wiki/Ian_Breakwell" title="Ian Breakwell">Ian Breakwell</a> to produce the film <i>The Institution</i> based on Breakwell's <a href="/wiki/Artist_Placement_Group" title="Artist Placement Group">Artist Placement Group</a> work at <a href="/wiki/Rampton_Secure_Hospital" title="Rampton Secure Hospital">Rampton Secure Hospital</a> in Nottinghamshire.</p>
<p>Early in his career, Coyne turned down a meeting with founder of <a href="/wiki/Elektra_Records" title="Elektra Records">Elektra Records</a> <a href="/wiki/Jac_Holzman" title="Jac Holzman">Jac Holzman</a> (Coyne's band Siren were on Elektra in America) to discuss replacing <a href="/wiki/Jim_Morrison" title="Jim Morrison">Jim Morrison</a> in <a href="/wiki/The_Doors" title="The Doors">the Doors</a>. "I didn't like the leather trousers!" was Coynes' alleged reason.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>The uncompromising stance continued even when he was one of the first artists signed to <a href="/wiki/Virgin_Records" title="Virgin Records">Virgin Records</a> and it was this attitude that endeared him to label-mates such as <a href="/wiki/John_Lydon" title="John Lydon">John Lydon</a>, who played "Eastbourne Ladies" on a Desert Island Discs–type show, and <a href="/wiki/The_Mekons" title="The Mekons">the Mekons</a>, who recorded his "Having a Party", a scathing attack on <a href="/wiki/Richard_Branson" title="Richard Branson">Richard Branson</a>.</p>
<p>Coyne's first solo album <i>Case History</i> (1972), primarily with just his own voice and guitar, was powerful and direct, was recorded for Peel's Dandelion label. When Dandelion ceased to exist, the album largely sank into obscurity. But not before it had come to the attention of Virgin Records, who were sufficiently impressed to sign Coyne and release his 1973 album <i><a href="/wiki/Marjory_Razorblade" title="Marjory Razorblade">Marjory Razorblade</a></i>.</p>
<p>Described as being musically "... a mixture of blues and music hall comedy, with a punk edge", the 1973 album contained many notable songs, such as the bitter and irreverent "Eastbourne Ladies" and the plaintive "House on the Hill" about life in a psychiatric institution. It was the record that was to be largely responsible for putting Coyne on the map of mainstream rock.</p>
<p>Another Virgin album release, <i>Babble</i>, by Coyne and singer <a href="/wiki/Dagmar_Krause" title="Dagmar Krause">Dagmar Krause</a>, courted controversy when Kevin suggested, in the theatre presentation of the piece, that the destructive relationship between the two lovers could have been based on <a href="/wiki/Moors_murders" title="Moors murders">The Moors Murderers</a>. Two performances at the <a href="/wiki/Theatre_Royal_Stratford_East" title="Theatre Royal Stratford East">Theatre Royal in Stratford, London</a> were cancelled at short notice by <a href="/wiki/Newham_Council" class="mw-redirect" title="Newham Council">Newham Council</a> following negative press reports in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Sun_(United_Kingdom)" title="The Sun (United Kingdom)">The Sun</a></i> and the <i><a href="/wiki/London_Evening_Standard" title="London Evening Standard">Evening Standard</a></i>. The show was eventually staged, for four nights, at the Oval House in <a href="/wiki/Kennington" title="Kennington">Kennington</a>. Reviewing the show for the <i><a href="/wiki/New_Musical_Express" class="mw-redirect" title="New Musical Express">NME</a></i>, <a href="/wiki/Paul_Du_Noyer" title="Paul Du Noyer">Paul Du Noyer</a> wrote:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>`Babble' is a particularly thorough, painstaking exploration of the reality of one relationship, stripped of romance and artifice. The format employed is correspondingly stark. Against a stage-set of light-bulb, table and chairs Coyne and his partner <a href="/wiki/Dagmar_Krause" title="Dagmar Krause">Dagmar Krause</a> stand at either side; the only accompaniment comes from Bob Ward and Brian Godding, playing electric and acoustic guitar in the gloom behind.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>American singer/songwriter <a href="/wiki/Will_Oldham" title="Will Oldham">Will Oldham</a> claimed that the <i>Babble</i> album had "changed my life" and he recorded two of the songs himself. Oldham also went on to form a side project called The Babblers – who strictly played covers of songs from Babble. Extracts from a performance of <i>Babble</i>, in Berlin, were included in the short German film <i>Herz Aus Feuer</i> (1979) by Claudia Strauven and Wolfgang Kraesze.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>The album <i><a href="/wiki/Politicz" title="Politicz">Politicz</a></i>, featuring Peter Kirtley on guitar and Steve Bull on keyboards, was released in 1982. <a href="/wiki/AllMusic" title="AllMusic">AllMusic</a>'s reviewer Dean McFarlane described the album as "One of the British singer/songwriter's more outwardly experimental records, this album contains some of his most intimate work, deeply personal songs and techniques which were taking him further and further away from tradition... strictly a post-punk album with a humorous political agenda".<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> The same year Coyne appeared in concert with his band (Kirtley and Bull augmented by Steve Lamb on bass and Dave Wilson on drums), live in front of the <a href="/wiki/Berlin_Wall" title="Berlin Wall">Berlin Wall</a> at the Tempodrom. The concert was later issued on the 2008 DVD <i>At the Last Wall</i> (Dockland Productions, Meyer Records).<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Nuremberg">Nuremberg</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kevin_Coyne&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Nuremberg">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Following a <a href="/wiki/Nervous_breakdown" class="mw-redirect" title="Nervous breakdown">nervous breakdown</a> and increasing difficulties with drink, Coyne left the UK in 1985. He settled in <a href="/wiki/Nuremberg" title="Nuremberg">Nuremberg</a>, Germany and having given up alcohol, never stopped recording and touring, as well as writing books and exhibiting his paintings. A selection of Coyne's writings, including many of his poems, can be viewed on the internet.<sup id="cite_ref-CoyneArt_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CoyneArt-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>Coyne's move to Germany saw his writing and painting career truly blossom. He published four books, two of which, <i>Showbusiness</i> and <i>Party Dress</i>, were published by <a href="/wiki/Serpent%27s_Tail" title="Serpent's Tail">Serpent's Tail</a> in London.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> There were numerous exhibition of his visual work throughout Europe and the response was reassuringly strong. Those in Berlin, <a href="/wiki/Amsterdam" title="Amsterdam">Amsterdam</a> and <a href="/wiki/Z%C3%BCrich" title="Zürich">Zürich</a> being particularly well reviewed and attended.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> The paintings gained some notoriety<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> and still attract commercial attention today.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the late 1980s Coyne acted on stage, playing the small part of a rock star in <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Linie_Eins&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Linie Eins (page does not exist)">Linie Eins</a></i> (<i>Line One</i>), a German musical, at the <a href="/wiki/Staatstheater_N%C3%BCrnberg" title="Staatstheater Nürnberg">Nuremberg Opera House</a>, but appearing only at the very end of the play.<sup id="cite_ref-kevincoynepage_3-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kevincoynepage-3">[3]</a></sup> His 1995 album, <i>The Adventures of Crazy Frank</i>, was based on a stage musical about English comedian <a href="/wiki/Frank_Randle" title="Frank Randle">Frank Randle</a> – with Coyne in the title role. It also starred the singer Julia Kempken who was erroneously listed in the <i><a href="/wiki/The_Guardian" title="The Guardian">Guardian</a></i> obituary as Kevin's wife.<sup id="cite_ref-Clayson_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Clayson-1">[1]</a></sup> Kempken later wrote fondly of this mistake, suggesting that her performance on stage as Randle's wife had been so strong as to transform her, in the eyes of the press, into Kevin's actual wife. In reality Kevin married only twice, first to Lesley and second to Helmi, having another relationship between the two which saw the birth of his son Nico.</p>
<p>In Germany his sons from his first marriage, Eugene and <a href="/wiki/Robert_Coyne" title="Robert Coyne">Robert</a>, appeared on recordings such as <i>Tough And Sweet</i> (1993) and <i>Sugar Candy Taxi</i> (1999), with guitarist and multi-instrumentalist Robert joining his band. His later German recordings, including <i>Knocking on Your Brain</i> (1997) often featured the "Paradise Band". In later years he also collaborated with <a href="/wiki/Brendan_Croker" title="Brendan Croker">Brendan Croker</a> on <i>Life Is Almost Wonderful</i>, with <a href="/wiki/Jon_Langford" title="Jon Langford">Jon Langford</a> of <a href="/wiki/The_Mekons" title="The Mekons">the Mekons</a> (on <i>One Day in Chicago</i>) and with <a href="/wiki/Gary_Lucas" title="Gary Lucas">Gary Lucas</a> once of Captain Beefheart's <a href="/wiki/Captain_Beefheart#The_Magic_Band" title="Captain Beefheart">Magic Band</a> (on <i>Knocking on Your Brain</i>). A reunion with original Siren members Dave Clague and Nick Cudworth happened for a John Peel's Dandelion Records DVD, alongside solo performances by Coyne. Siren performed all material for the film without any prior rehearsals.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Death">Death</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kevin_Coyne&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Death">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Diagnosed with <a href="/wiki/Lung_fibrosis" class="mw-redirect" title="Lung fibrosis">lung fibrosis</a> in 2002, Coyne died peacefully at his home. He is survived by his wife Helmi and his sons Eugene, Robert and Nico.</p>
<p>His wife Helmi intends to continue releasing recordings Kevin made in his last years on Kevin's own Turpentine Records label. The first was <i>Underground</i> (2006).</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Influences">Influences</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kevin_Coyne&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Influences">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In a 2004 interview with Frank Bangay, Coyne named his favourite blues musicians as <a href="/wiki/Robert_Johnson_(musician)" class="mw-redirect" title="Robert Johnson (musician)">Robert Johnson</a>, <a href="/wiki/Leroy_Carr" title="Leroy Carr">Leroy Carr</a>, <a href="/wiki/Peetie_Wheatstraw" title="Peetie Wheatstraw">Peetie Wheatstraw</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tommy_McClennan" title="Tommy McClennan">Tommy McClennan</a><sup id="cite_ref-influences_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-influences-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Critical_appraisal">Critical appraisal</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kevin_Coyne&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Critical appraisal">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Although Coyne has been neglected by popular music historians and academics, George McKay's 2013 book <i>Shakin' All Over: Popular Music and Disability</i>, features a critical discussion of Coyne's work. The book opens with an epigraph from Coyne, from one of the pre-recorded dialogues featured on his 1977 album <i>In Living Black and White</i>: 'anything that rhymes with "me"'. Describing him as 'the great lost English singer-songwriter' with his 'social-work approach to pop', McKay discusses the 1978 song "Having a Party" in the context of songs about the destructive economy of the pop industry. He also notes Coyne's 'anti-star' status and his innovative 'anti'-guitar playing: "Not being able, or electing not, to play the instrument "properly", and hearing other voices while singing: there is something culturally disabling about each of these artistic choices, quite apart from the lyrical terrain".<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>On 15 June 2017 Coyne was commemorated with the unveiling of a <a href="/wiki/Blue_plaque" title="Blue plaque">blue plaque</a> at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Derby" title="University of Derby">University of Derby Art School</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
