<p><b>Haverholme Priory</b> was a <a href="/wiki/Monastery" title="Monastery">monastery</a> in <a href="/wiki/Lincolnshire" title="Lincolnshire">Lincolnshire</a>, England. Its remains are situated 4 miles (6&#160;km) north-east from the town of <a href="/wiki/Sleaford" title="Sleaford">Sleaford</a> and less than 1 mile (1.6&#160;km) south-west from the village of <a href="/wiki/Anwick" title="Anwick">Anwick</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Foundation">Foundation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Haverholme_Priory&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Foundation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1137 <a href="/wiki/Alexander,_Bishop_of_Lincoln" class="mw-redirect" title="Alexander, Bishop of Lincoln">Alexander, Bishop of Lincoln</a> offered the site of Haverholme Priory to the <a href="/wiki/Cistercian_monks" class="mw-redirect" title="Cistercian monks">Cistercian monks</a> of <a href="/wiki/Fountains_Abbey" title="Fountains Abbey">Fountains Abbey</a>. After two years of construction the order rejected the site and instead established <a href="/wiki/Louth_Park_Abbey" title="Louth Park Abbey">Louth Park Abbey</a>. Haverholme was offered to <a href="/wiki/Gilbert_of_Sempringham" title="Gilbert of Sempringham">Gilbert of Sempringham</a> and his <a href="/wiki/Gilbertine" class="mw-redirect" title="Gilbertine">Gilbertine</a> order who sent nuns and brothers from <a href="/wiki/Sempringham_Priory" title="Sempringham Priory">Sempringham</a> to inhabit the new buildings of what was to be a <a href="/wiki/Double_monastery" title="Double monastery">double monastery</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-WPage_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WPage-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Gilbertine_operation">Gilbertine operation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Haverholme_Priory&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Gilbertine operation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Haverholme_Priory,_1826.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6c/Haverholme_Priory%2C_1826.jpg/220px-Haverholme_Priory%2C_1826.jpg" width="220" height="111" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6c/Haverholme_Priory%2C_1826.jpg/330px-Haverholme_Priory%2C_1826.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6c/Haverholme_Priory%2C_1826.jpg/440px-Haverholme_Priory%2C_1826.jpg 2x" data-file-width="600" data-file-height="302" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Haverholme_Priory,_1826.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Haverholme Priory in an 1826 sketch in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Gentleman%27s_Magazine" title="The Gentleman's Magazine">The Gentleman's Magazine</a></i></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Gilbertine" class="mw-redirect" title="Gilbertine">Gilbertines</a> also inherited the responsibility for keeping the neighbouring fens drained, and to maintain a foot ferry to <a href="/wiki/Sleaford" title="Sleaford">Sleaford</a> across the <a href="/wiki/River_Slea" title="River Slea">River Slea</a> at <a href="/w/index.php?title=Ewerby_Waith&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Ewerby Waith (page does not exist)">Ewerby Waith</a>. They were however summoned to account in 1316 when it fell into disrepair. They were summoned again in 1360 when Alice Everingham, daughter of <a href="/w/index.php?title=John_de_Everingham&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="John de Everingham (page does not exist)">John de Everingham</a>, who was supposed to have taken vows, fled from the Priory, only to be hunted down and recaptured. She complained to the Bishop of the time that she had never taken vows and she was being held against her will, so he ordered her to be released.</p>
<p>It is rumoured that in 1164 <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Becket" title="Thomas Becket">Thomas Becket</a> hid at Haverholme during one of his arguments with the King.<sup id="cite_ref-WPage_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WPage-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<div style="clear:left;"></div>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Dissolution_and_subsequent_history">Dissolution and subsequent history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Haverholme_Priory&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Dissolution and subsequent history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Haverholme_Priory_(geograph_2181031).jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9f/Haverholme_Priory_%28geograph_2181031%29.jpg/220px-Haverholme_Priory_%28geograph_2181031%29.jpg" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9f/Haverholme_Priory_%28geograph_2181031%29.jpg/330px-Haverholme_Priory_%28geograph_2181031%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9f/Haverholme_Priory_%28geograph_2181031%29.jpg/440px-Haverholme_Priory_%28geograph_2181031%29.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3072" data-file-height="2304" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Haverholme_Priory_(geograph_2181031).jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Haverholme Priory to-day</div>
</div>
</div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Henry_VIII_of_England" title="Henry VIII of England">Henry VIII</a> dissolved the Priory in 1538<sup id="cite_ref-WPage_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WPage-2">[2]</a></sup> and it had various owners for the next two and a half centuries. It was inherited by the Finch-Hatton family. <a href="/wiki/George_Finch-Hatton,_10th_Earl_of_Winchilsea" title="George Finch-Hatton, 10th Earl of Winchilsea">George Finch-Hatton, 10th Earl of Winchilsea</a> and 5th Earl of Nottingham rebuilt it in 1830. It was used as a family home by the Finch-Hatton family for almost a century but by the early 1920s it was up for sale.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Haverholme was sold in 1926 to an American woman who had most of it dismantled, stone by stone, to be rebuilt in America. The cargo was on the dock in Liverpool when the buyer became a victim in a train crash. Eventually the stones, never shipped to America, were used to build new docks.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>One casualty of this<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (June 2013)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup> was the <a href="/wiki/Sleaford_Navigation" title="Sleaford Navigation">Sleaford Canal</a> locks, which also fell into disuse, effectively closing the canal.</p>
<p>The present ruin is the remains of a Gothic building built around 1835 to designs by the architect <a href="/wiki/Henry_Edward_Kendall" title="Henry Edward Kendall">Henry Edward Kendall</a>. This was a rebuild of an earlier house dating from 1780. The ruins are now a Grade II <a href="/wiki/Listed_building" title="Listed building">listed building</a><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> and designated <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Monument" class="mw-redirect" title="Ancient Monument">Ancient Monument</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
