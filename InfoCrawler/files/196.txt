<p><b>Liliane Henriette Charlotte Bettencourt</b> <small>French pronunciation:&#160;​</small><span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/French" title="Help:IPA/French">[lil.jan be.tɑ̃.kuːʁ]</a></span> (nee <b>Schueller</b>; 21 October 1922 – 21 September 2017) was a French <a href="/wiki/Beneficiary" title="Beneficiary">heiress</a>, <a href="/wiki/Socialite" title="Socialite">socialite</a> and businesswoman. She was one of the principal <a href="/wiki/Shareholder" title="Shareholder">shareholders</a> of <a href="/wiki/L%27Or%C3%A9al" title="L'Oréal">L'Oréal</a>.</p>
<p>At the time of her death, she was the <a href="/wiki/List_of_female_billionaires" title="List of female billionaires">richest woman</a> in the world (and the 14th richest person), with a net worth of US$44.3 billion, according to the 2017 version of "<a href="/wiki/The_World%27s_Billionaires" title="The World's Billionaires">The World's Billionaires</a>."<sup id="cite_ref-forbes.com_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-forbes.com-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biography">Biography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Liliane_Bettencourt&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Biography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Bettencourt was born in Paris, the only child of Louise Madeleine Berthe (<i>née</i> Doncieux) and <a href="/wiki/Eug%C3%A8ne_Schueller" title="Eugène Schueller">Eugène Schueller</a>, the founder of L'Oréal, one of the world's largest cosmetics and beauty companies. Her mother died when Liliane was five years old, and she formed a close bond with her father, who later married Liliane's British <a href="/wiki/Governess" title="Governess">governess</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-EvenStan1_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EvenStan1-5">[5]</a></sup> At the age of 15, she joined her father's company as an apprentice, mixing cosmetics and labelling bottles of shampoo.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1950, she married French politician <a href="/wiki/Andr%C3%A9_Bettencourt" title="André Bettencourt">André Bettencourt</a>, who served as a cabinet minister in <a href="/wiki/French_government" class="mw-redirect" title="French government">French governments</a> of the 1960s and 1970s and rose to become deputy chairman of L'Oréal. Mr. Bettencourt had been a member of <a href="/wiki/La_Cagoule" title="La Cagoule">La Cagoule</a>, a violent French <a href="/wiki/Fascism" title="Fascism">fascist</a> pro-Nazi group that Liliane's father, a Nazi sympathizer, had funded and supported in the 1930s and whose members were arrested in 1937. After the war, her husband, like other members of La Cagoule, was given refuge at L'Oréal despite his politically inconvenient past.<sup id="cite_ref-Telegraph1_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Telegraph1-8">[8]</a></sup> Eventually the Bettencourts settled in an <a href="/wiki/Art_Moderne" class="mw-redirect" title="Art Moderne">Art Moderne</a> mansion built in 1951 on rue de Delabordère in <a href="/wiki/Neuilly-sur-Seine" title="Neuilly-sur-Seine">Neuilly-sur-Seine</a>, France.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> They had one daughter, <a href="/wiki/Fran%C3%A7oise_Bettencourt_Meyers" title="Françoise Bettencourt Meyers">Françoise</a>, who was born in 1953.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1957, Bettencourt inherited the L'Oréal fortune when her father died, becoming the principal <a href="/wiki/Shareholder" title="Shareholder">shareholder</a>. In 1963 the company <a href="/wiki/IPO" class="mw-redirect" title="IPO">went public</a>, although Bettencourt continued to own a majority stake. In 1974, in fear that the company would be nationalised after the French elections, she exchanged almost half of her stake for a three percent (3%) stake in <a href="/wiki/Nestl%C3%A9" title="Nestlé">Nestlé</a> S.A.<sup id="cite_ref-Telegraph2_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Telegraph2-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>As of December 2012, Bettencourt owned 185,661,879 (30.5%) of the outstanding shares of L'Oréal, of which 76,441,389 (12.56%) shares are effectively held in trust (for her daughter). The remainder is owned as follows: 178,381,021 (29.78%) shares owned by Nestlé, 229,933,941 (37.76%) shares are publicly held, and the remainder are held as treasury stock or in the company savings plan. The Bettencourt family and Nestlé act in concert pursuant to a shareholders' agreement.<sup id="cite_ref-2012RegistrationDocument1_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2012RegistrationDocument1-12">[12]</a></sup> Bettencourt ended her Board Director tenure on 13 February 2012 and her grandson, Jean-Victor, was appointed as Board Director.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> Bettencourt's daughter and her daughter's husband (Jean-Pierre Meyers) are also members of the board of directors.<sup id="cite_ref-2012RegistrationDocument_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2012RegistrationDocument-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>Bettencourt generally shunned media attention and granted few interviews. From 2007 she faced intense media scrutiny and publicity over her relationship with <a href="/wiki/Fran%C3%A7ois-Marie_Banier" title="François-Marie Banier">François-Marie Banier</a>, the estrangement with her daughter Françoise and her alleged funding of conservative French politicians, including former French president <a href="/wiki/Nicolas_Sarkozy" title="Nicolas Sarkozy">Nicolas Sarkozy</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> In August 2012, she sold her private island, <a href="/wiki/D%27Arros_Island" title="D'Arros Island">D'Arros Island</a>, to a Seychelles-registered conservation business linked to the Swiss Save our Seas Foundation, for £60 million.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>Bettencourt died on 21 September 2017.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Philanthropy">Philanthropy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Liliane_Bettencourt&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Philanthropy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1987 Bettencourt, together with her husband and daughter, founded the Bettencourt Schueller Foundation (la Fondation Bettencourt Schueller) to support and develop medical, cultural and humanitarian projects. The Foundation is based in <a href="/wiki/Neuilly-sur-Seine" title="Neuilly-sur-Seine">Neuilly-sur-Seine, France</a>. The Foundation, which benefits from assets of <a href="/wiki/Euro" title="Euro">€</a>150 million and an annual budget of approx €15 million<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> devotes approximately 55% of its funds to scientific education and research, 33% to humanitarian and social projects and 12% to culture and arts.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2008 the Foundation helped fund the new Monet wing at the <a href="/wiki/Mus%C3%A9e_Marmottan_Monet" title="Musée Marmottan Monet">Musée Marmottan Monet</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Telegraph3_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Telegraph3-20">[20]</a></sup> Conversely, in 2007 Bettencourt was jointly "awarded" a <a href="/wiki/Ethecon_Foundation" title="Ethecon Foundation">Black Planet Award</a>, an award given for destroying the planet, along with <a href="/wiki/Peter_Brabeck-Letmathe" title="Peter Brabeck-Letmathe">Peter Brabeck-Letmathe</a> for proliferating contaminated baby food, monopolising water resources, and tolerating child labour.<sup id="cite_ref-ifban_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ifban-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span id="&quot;L'affaire_Bettencourt&quot;"></span><span class="mw-headline" id=".22L.27affaire_Bettencourt.22">"L'affaire Bettencourt"</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Liliane_Bettencourt&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: &quot;L'affaire Bettencourt&quot;">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>By most accounts Bettencourt met <a href="/wiki/Fran%C3%A7ois-Marie_Banier" title="François-Marie Banier">François-Marie Banier</a>, a French writer, artist and celebrity photographer, in 1987 when he was commissioned to photograph her for the French magazine <i>Egoiste</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-EvenStan1_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EvenStan1-5">[5]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-nyt1a_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nyt1a-23">[23]</a></sup> Over the ensuing years, Banier and Bettencourt became friends and she became his benefactor, bestowing gifts upon him estimated to be worth as much as €1.3&#160;billion. These gifts include, amongst other things, a life insurance policy worth €253&#160;million in 2003, another life insurance policy worth €262&#160;million in 2006,<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> 11 works of art in 2001 valued at €20&#160;million, including paintings by <a href="/wiki/Picasso" class="mw-redirect" title="Picasso">Picasso</a>, <a href="/wiki/Matisse" class="mw-redirect" title="Matisse">Matisse</a>, <a href="/wiki/Piet_Mondrian" title="Piet Mondrian">Mondrian</a>, <a href="/wiki/Delaunay" title="Delaunay">Delaunay</a> and <a href="/wiki/L%C3%A9ger" title="Léger">Léger</a>, a photograph by surrealist <a href="/wiki/Man_Ray" title="Man Ray">Man Ray</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-EvenStan1_5-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EvenStan1-5">[5]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Times1_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Times1-25">[25]</a></sup> and cash. The life insurance policies were allegedly signed over to Banier after Bettencourt was recovering from two hospital stays in 2003 and 2006.<sup id="cite_ref-nyt1_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nyt1-26">[26]</a></sup> In April 2013, Forbes magazine listed Liliane Bettencourt as the world's richest woman in 1999 at a value of $30 billion.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p>In December 2007, just a month after the death of her father, <a href="/wiki/Fran%C3%A7oise_Bettencourt_Meyers" title="Françoise Bettencourt Meyers">Françoise Bettencourt Meyers</a> lodged a criminal complaint against Banier, accusing him of <i>abus de faiblesse</i> (or the exploitation of a physical or psychological weakness for personal gain) over Bettencourt.<sup id="cite_ref-Forbes2_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Forbes2-28">[28]</a></sup> As a result of her complaint, the Brigade Financière, the financial investigative arm of the French national police, opened an investigation and, after interviewing members of Bettencourt's staff, determined to present the case to a court in <a href="/wiki/Nanterre" title="Nanterre">Nanterre</a> for trial in September 2009.<sup id="cite_ref-nyt1_26-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nyt1-26">[26]</a></sup> In December 2009, the court delayed ruling on the case until April 2010 (later extended until July 2010) pending the results of a medical examination of Bettencourt's mental state.<sup id="cite_ref-nyt1_26-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nyt1-26">[26]</a></sup> However, Bettencourt refused to submit to these examinations.<sup id="cite_ref-wsj1_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wsj1-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<p>In July 2010 the trial was adjourned again until autumn 2010, at the earliest, after details of tape recordings made by Bettencourt's butler, Pascal Bonnefoy, became public. The tapes, which were turned over to police, consisted of over 21 hours of conversation and were made because the butler had feared that Bettencourt was suffering from <a href="/wiki/Alzheimer%27s_disease" title="Alzheimer's disease">Alzheimer's disease</a> and was being duped.<sup id="cite_ref-alderman_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-alderman-30">[30]</a></sup> The tapes allegedly reveal that Bettencourt had made Banier her "sole heir", excluding the L'Oréal shares which make up the bulk of Bettencourt's estate and which had already been signed over to her daughter and two grandsons.<sup id="cite_ref-Independent2_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Independent2-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<p>On 6 December 2010 Bettencourt reconciled with her daughter, ending a series of lawsuits. It is reported that Bettencourt and Banier had separated, and he was eventually written out of Bettencourt's will.<sup id="cite_ref-BBC_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BBC-32">[32]</a></sup> The spat reignited over the following summer, however, when Bettencourt said her daughter needed to seek psychological help,<sup id="cite_ref-bbcguardianship_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bbcguardianship-33">[33]</a></sup> which renewed their estrangement.<sup id="cite_ref-bbcguardianship_33-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bbcguardianship-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Guardianship">Guardianship</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Liliane_Bettencourt&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Guardianship">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On 8 June 2011, it was reported daughter Meyers had filed an application with the court to make Bettencourt a <a href="/wiki/Ward_(law)" title="Ward (law)">ward</a> of the state for her health and being incapable of the management of her fortune.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> On 17 October 2011 a French judge ruled that she was to be placed under the <a href="/wiki/Legal_guardian" title="Legal guardian">guardianship</a> of members of her family on concerns about Bettencourt's declining mental health. Francoise Bettencourt-Meyers, along with Bettencourt's two grandsons, gained control of her wealth and property.<sup id="cite_ref-bbcguardianship_33-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bbcguardianship-33">[33]</a></sup> One of the grandsons was additionally named as her personal guardian.<sup id="cite_ref-bbcguardianship_33-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bbcguardianship-33">[33]</a></sup> Liliane Bettencourt's lawyer said he would appeal, and told <i>Le Monde</i> newspaper that "Mrs. Bettencourt was ready for 'nuclear war' with her daughter."<sup id="cite_ref-bbcguardianship_33-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bbcguardianship-33">[33]</a></sup> As of 2014, Francoise Bettencourt-Meyers is guardian of the fortune,<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> while Francoise's son, L’Oréal Board member and member of the Supervisory Board of the Bettencourt family holding company, Tethys, Jean-Victor Meyers oversees her health and personal life after a judge determined he was the only person able to "ward off all conflict between Liliane Bettencourt and Françoise Bettencourt-Meyers."<sup id="cite_ref-alderman_30-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-alderman-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Madoff_victim">Madoff victim</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Liliane_Bettencourt&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Madoff victim">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Bettencourt was reported to be one of the most high-profile victims of <a href="/wiki/Bernard_Madoff" title="Bernard Madoff">Bernard Madoff</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Ponzi_scheme" title="Ponzi scheme">Ponzi scheme</a>, losing €22&#160;million. She was the first investor in a fund managed by <a href="/wiki/Access_International_Advisors" title="Access International Advisors">Access International Advisors</a>, which was co-founded by <a href="/wiki/Ren%C3%A9-Thierry_Magon_de_la_Villehuchet" class="mw-redirect" title="René-Thierry Magon de la Villehuchet">René-Thierry Magon de la Villehuchet</a>. De la Villehuchet committed suicide on 22 December 2008 after it became known that his funds had invested a substantial amount of their capital with Madoff.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Political_scandal">Political scandal</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Liliane_Bettencourt&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Political scandal">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In June 2010, during the <a href="/wiki/Bettencourt_affair" title="Bettencourt affair">Bettencourt affair</a>, Bettencourt became embroiled in a high-level French political scandal after other details of the tape recordings made by her butler became public. The tapes allegedly picked up conversations between Bettencourt and her financial adviser, Patrice de Maistre, which indicate that Bettencourt may have avoided paying taxes by keeping a substantial amount of cash in undeclared Swiss bank accounts. The tapes also allegedly captured a conversation between Bettencourt and <a href="/wiki/%C3%89ric_Woerth" title="Éric Woerth">Éric Woerth</a>, who was soliciting a job for his wife managing Bettencourt's wealth, while he was acting as budget minister and running a high-profile campaign to catch wealthy tax evaders.<sup id="cite_ref-Globe1_37-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Globe1-37">[37]</a></sup> Moreover, Bettencourt received a €30&#160;million tax rebate while Woerth was budget minister.<sup id="cite_ref-Vanity1_38-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Vanity1-38">[38]</a></sup></p>
<p>In July 2010, the scandal appeared to widen after Bettencourt's former accountant, Claire Thibout, alleged in an interview with the French investigative website <a href="/wiki/Mediapart" title="Mediapart">Mediapart</a>, that conservative French politicians were frequently given envelopes stuffed with cash at Bettencourt's mansion in Neuilly-sur-Seine. She alleged that Woerth, while acting as treasurer for the <a href="/wiki/Union_for_a_Popular_Movement" title="Union for a Popular Movement">Union for a Popular Movement (UMP)</a>, was given an envelope containing €150,000 in cash in March 2007 towards the presidential campaign of <a href="/wiki/Nicolas_Sarkozy" title="Nicolas Sarkozy">Nicolas Sarkozy</a>. She also made, then retracted, a claim that Sarkozy was a frequent visitor to the Bettencourt's home while he was mayor of Neuilly-sur-Seine from 1983 to 2002 and received envelopes containing cash. A few days later, she declared she had been pressured by French police to retract her testimony about Nicolas Sarkozy's illegal cash.<sup id="cite_ref-mediapartdossier_39-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mediapartdossier-39">[39]</a></sup> Mr Sarkozy and Mr Woerth both deny wrongdoing.<sup id="cite_ref-Globe1_37-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Globe1-37">[37]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-cnn1_40-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cnn1-40">[40]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> Following these allegations, French police raided the home and office of de Maistre, who heads Clymène, the company owned by Bettencourt to manage her wealth.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> French law limits political donations to €7,500 for political parties and €4,600 for individuals. Contributions above €150 must be paid by cheque with the donor clearly identified.<sup id="cite_ref-cnn1_40-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cnn1-40">[40]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Telegraph4_43-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Telegraph4-43">[43]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span id="Forbes'_rankings"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Forbes.27_rankings">Forbes' rankings</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Liliane_Bettencourt&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Forbes' rankings">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Forbes ranked Bettencourt in its <a href="/wiki/List_of_billionaires" class="mw-redirect" title="List of billionaires">list of the world's wealthiest persons</a> with an estimated fortune of US$38.8 billion (as of 28 June 2016). As of 2016, she was the top <a href="/wiki/List_of_female_billionaires" title="List of female billionaires">female billionaire</a> in the world, and was also the <a href="/wiki/List_of_French_people_by_net_worth" title="List of French people by net worth">richest person in France</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Forbes1_44-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Forbes1-44">[44]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2005, Forbes judged her to be the 39th most powerful woman in the world.<sup id="cite_ref-Forbes3_45-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Forbes3-45">[45]</a></sup></p>
