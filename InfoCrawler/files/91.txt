<h2 style="font-family:sans-serif; font-size:100%; font-weight:bold; border: none; margin: 0; padding:0; padding-bottom:.1em; color:#f9f9ff;"><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Arts_Portal"><big>The Arts Portal</big></span></h2>
</div>
<div style="border: 1px solid #aaaaaa; border-top-width: 1px; vertical-align: top;background: white;opacity: 1; color: black; text-align: left; margin: 0 0 10px; padding: 1em;padding-top: .3em;">
<p><br /></p>
<div style="float:right;margin-left:1em">
<center>The <a href="/wiki/Parthenon" title="Parthenon">Parthenon</a></center>
<p><a href="/wiki/File:The_Parthenon_in_Athens.jpg" class="image" title="The Parthenon on top of the Acropolis, Athens, Greece"><img alt="The Parthenon on top of the Acropolis, Athens, Greece" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/da/The_Parthenon_in_Athens.jpg/180px-The_Parthenon_in_Athens.jpg" width="180" height="127" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/da/The_Parthenon_in_Athens.jpg/270px-The_Parthenon_in_Athens.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/da/The_Parthenon_in_Athens.jpg/360px-The_Parthenon_in_Athens.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1144" data-file-height="804" /></a><br /></p>
</div>
<p><b><a href="/wiki/The_arts" title="The arts">The arts</a></b> is a vast subdivision of <a href="/wiki/Culture" title="Culture">culture</a>, composed of many creative endeavors and disciplines. It is a broader term than "<i><a href="/wiki/Art" title="Art">art</a></i>", which, as a description of a field, usually means only the <a href="/wiki/Visual_arts" title="Visual arts">visual arts</a>. <i>The arts</i> encompass the <a href="/wiki/Visual_arts" title="Visual arts">visual arts</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Literary_arts" class="mw-redirect" title="Literary arts">literary arts</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Performing_arts" title="Performing arts">performing arts</a> – <a href="/wiki/Music" title="Music">music</a>, <a href="/wiki/Theatre" title="Theatre">theatre</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dance" title="Dance">dance</a> and <a href="/wiki/Film" title="Film">film</a>, among others. This list is by no means comprehensive, but only meant to introduce the concept of the arts. For all intents and purposes, the history of the arts begins with the <i><a href="/wiki/History_of_art" title="History of art">history of art</a></i>. The arts might have origins in early human evolutionary prehistory.</p>
<p>Ancient Greek art saw the veneration of the animal form and the development of equivalent skills to show musculature, poise, beauty and anatomically correct proportions. <a href="/wiki/Roman_Empire" title="Roman Empire">Ancient Roman</a> art depicted gods as idealized humans, shown with characteristic distinguishing features (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Jupiter_(mythology)" title="Jupiter (mythology)">Jupiter</a>'s thunderbolt). In <a href="/wiki/Byzantine_art" title="Byzantine art">Byzantine</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gothic_art" title="Gothic art">Gothic art</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">Middle Ages</a>, the dominance of the church insisted on the expression of biblical and not material truths. Eastern art has generally worked in a style akin to Western medieval art, namely a concentration on surface patterning and local colour (meaning the plain colour of an object, such as basic red for a red robe, rather than the modulations of that colour brought about by light, shade and reflection). A characteristic of this style is that the local colour is often defined by an outline (a contemporary equivalent is the cartoon). This is evident in, for example, the art of <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tibet" title="Tibet">Tibet</a> and <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a>. Religious <a href="/wiki/Islam" title="Islam">Islamic</a> art forbids iconography, and expresses religious ideas through geometry instead. The physical and rational certainties depicted by the 19th-century Enlightenment were shattered not only by new discoveries of relativity by <a href="/wiki/Albert_Einstein" title="Albert Einstein">Einstein</a> and of unseen psychology by <a href="/wiki/Sigmund_Freud" title="Sigmund Freud">Freud</a>, but also by unprecedented technological development. Paradoxically the expressions of new technologies were greatly influenced by the ancient tribal arts of Africa and Oceania, through the works of Paul Gauguin and the Post-Impressionists, Pablo Picasso and the Cubists, as well as the Futurists and others.</p>

<div style="border: 1px solid #aaaaaa; border-top-width: 1px; vertical-align: top;background: white;opacity: 1; color: black; text-align: left; margin: 0 0 10px; padding: 1em;padding-top: .3em;">
<div style="float:left;margin-right:1em"><a href="/wiki/File:Old_Kannada_inscription_dated_1115_AD_in_the_Lakshmi_Devi_temple_at_Doddagaddavalli.jpg" class="image" title="Old-Kannada inscription, 1114 CE at Doddagaddavalli"><img alt="Old-Kannada inscription, 1114 CE at Doddagaddavalli" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b1/Old_Kannada_inscription_dated_1115_AD_in_the_Lakshmi_Devi_temple_at_Doddagaddavalli.jpg/120px-Old_Kannada_inscription_dated_1115_AD_in_the_Lakshmi_Devi_temple_at_Doddagaddavalli.jpg" width="120" height="187" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b1/Old_Kannada_inscription_dated_1115_AD_in_the_Lakshmi_Devi_temple_at_Doddagaddavalli.jpg/180px-Old_Kannada_inscription_dated_1115_AD_in_the_Lakshmi_Devi_temple_at_Doddagaddavalli.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b1/Old_Kannada_inscription_dated_1115_AD_in_the_Lakshmi_Devi_temple_at_Doddagaddavalli.jpg/240px-Old_Kannada_inscription_dated_1115_AD_in_the_Lakshmi_Devi_temple_at_Doddagaddavalli.jpg 2x" data-file-width="740" data-file-height="1156" /></a></div>
<b><a href="/wiki/Hoysala_literature" title="Hoysala literature">Hoysala literature</a></b> is the large body of literature in the <a href="/wiki/Kannada_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Kannada language">Kannada</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sanskrit_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Sanskrit language">Sanskrit languages</a> produced by the <a href="/wiki/Hoysala_Empire" title="Hoysala Empire">Hoysala Empire</a> (1025–1343) in what is now southern India. <a href="/wiki/Kannada_literature" title="Kannada literature">Kannada literature</a> during this period consisted of writings relating to the socio-religious developments of the <a href="/wiki/Jain" class="mw-redirect" title="Jain">Jain</a> and <a href="/wiki/Veerashaiva" title="Veerashaiva">Veerashaiva</a> faiths, and to a lesser extent that of the <a href="/wiki/Vaishnavism" title="Vaishnavism">Vaishnava</a> faith. The earliest well-known <a href="/wiki/Brahmin" title="Brahmin">brahmin</a> writers in Kannada were from the Hoysala court. While most of the courtly textual production was in Kannada, an important corpus of <a href="/wiki/Monastery" title="Monastery">monastic</a> <a href="/wiki/Vaishnava" class="mw-redirect" title="Vaishnava">Vaishnava</a> literature relating to <i><a href="/wiki/Dvaita" class="mw-redirect" title="Dvaita">Dvaita</a></i> (dualistic) philosophy was written by the renowned philosopher <a href="/wiki/Madhvacharya" title="Madhvacharya">Madhvacharya</a> in Sanskrit. Writing Kannada literature in native <a href="/wiki/Meter_(poetry)" class="mw-redirect" title="Meter (poetry)">metres</a> was first popularised by the court poets. These metres were the <i>sangatya</i>, compositions sung to the accompaniment of a musical instrument; <i>shatpadi</i>, six-line verses; <i>ragale</i>, lyrical compositions in blank verse; and <i><a href="/wiki/Tripadi" title="Tripadi">tripadi</a></i>, three-line verses. However, Jain writers continued to use the traditional <i><a href="/wiki/Champu" title="Champu">champu</a></i>, composed of prose and verse. Important literary contributions in Kannada were made not only by court poets but also by noblemen, commanders, ministers, ascetics and saints associated with monasteries.

<p><i>Zhou Maoshu Appreciating Lotuses</i>, a designated <a href="/wiki/National_Treasures_of_Japan" class="mw-redirect" title="National Treasures of Japan">National Treasure of Japan</a>, is a 15th century painting mounted as a <a href="/wiki/Kakemono" title="Kakemono">hanging scroll</a> by <b><a href="/wiki/Kan%C5%8D_Masanobu" title="Kanō Masanobu">Kanō Masanobu</a></b> that depicts the 11th century <a href="/wiki/Confucianism" title="Confucianism">Confucian</a> scholar <a href="/wiki/Zhou_Maoshu" class="mw-redirect" title="Zhou Maoshu">Zhou Maoshu</a> in a boat floating on a lake with <a href="/wiki/Lotus_(plant)" class="mw-redirect" title="Lotus (plant)">lotuses</a>. Kanō was the chief painter of the <a href="/wiki/Ashikaga_shogunate" title="Ashikaga shogunate">Ashikaga shogunate</a> and is generally considered the founder of the <a href="/wiki/Kan%C5%8D_school" title="Kanō school">Kanō school</a> of painting, which would become the dominant style of painting until the <a href="/wiki/Meiji_period" title="Meiji period">Meiji period</a>.</p>

<h2 style="font-family:sans-serif; font-size:100%; font-weight:bold; border: none; margin: 0; padding:0; padding-bottom:.1em; color:#f9f9ff;"><span class="mw-headline" id="Featured_biography">Featured biography</span></h2>

<div style="border: 1px solid #aaaaaa; border-top-width: 1px; vertical-align: top;background: white;opacity: 1; color: black; text-align: left; margin: 0 0 10px; padding: 1em;padding-top: .3em;">
<div style="float:right;margin-left:0.2em"><a href="/wiki/File:Bronwyn_Bancroft_Feb_2010.JPG" class="image" title="Bronwyn Bancroft"><img alt="Bronwyn Bancroft" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/68/Bronwyn_Bancroft_Feb_2010.JPG/120px-Bronwyn_Bancroft_Feb_2010.JPG" width="120" height="90" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/68/Bronwyn_Bancroft_Feb_2010.JPG/180px-Bronwyn_Bancroft_Feb_2010.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/68/Bronwyn_Bancroft_Feb_2010.JPG/240px-Bronwyn_Bancroft_Feb_2010.JPG 2x" data-file-width="2592" data-file-height="1944" /></a></div>
<b><a href="/wiki/Bronwyn_Bancroft" title="Bronwyn Bancroft">Bronwyn Bancroft</a></b> (born 1958) is an <a href="/wiki/Indigenous_Australians" title="Indigenous Australians">Indigenous Australian</a> artist, notable for being the first Australian fashion designer invited to show her work in Paris. Born in <a href="/wiki/Tenterfield,_New_South_Wales" title="Tenterfield, New South Wales">Tenterfield, New South Wales</a>, and trained in <a href="/wiki/Canberra" title="Canberra">Canberra</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sydney" title="Sydney">Sydney</a>, Bancroft worked as a fashion designer, and is an artist, illustrator, and arts administrator. In 1985 Bancroft established a shop called Designer Aboriginals, selling fabrics made by Indigenous artists including herself. She was a founding member of Boomalli Aboriginal Artists Co-operative. Artwork by Bancroft is held by the <a href="/wiki/National_Gallery_of_Australia" title="National Gallery of Australia">National Gallery of Australia</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Art_Gallery_of_New_South_Wales" title="Art Gallery of New South Wales">Art Gallery of New South Wales</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Art_Gallery_of_Western_Australia" title="Art Gallery of Western Australia">Art Gallery of Western Australia</a>. She has provided artwork for over 20&#160;children's books, including <i>Stradbroke Dreaming</i> by writer and activist <a href="/wiki/Oodgeroo_Noonuccal" title="Oodgeroo Noonuccal">Oodgeroo Noonuccal</a> and books by artist and writer <a href="/wiki/Sally_Morgan_(artist)" title="Sally Morgan (artist)">Sally Morgan</a>. She has also received design commissions, including one for the exterior of a sports centre in Sydney. With a long history of involvement in community activism and arts administration, Bancroft has served as a board member for the National Gallery of Australia. Her painting <i>Prevention of AIDS</i> (1992) was used in a campaign to raise awareness of <a href="/wiki/HIV/AIDS" title="HIV/AIDS">HIV/AIDS</a> in Australia. As of 2010, Bancroft sits on the boards of copyright collection agency Viscopy and Tranby Aboriginal College, as well as being on the Artists Board at the <a href="/wiki/Museum_of_Contemporary_Art,_Sydney" class="mw-redirect" title="Museum of Contemporary Art, Sydney">Museum of Contemporary Art, Sydney</a>.
