<h2 style="font-family:sans-serif; font-size:100%; font-weight:bold; border: none; margin: 0; padding:0; padding-bottom:.1em; color:#f9f9ff;"><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Arts_Portal"><big>The Arts Portal</big></span></h2>
</div>
<div style="border: 1px solid #aaaaaa; border-top-width: 1px; vertical-align: top;background: white;opacity: 1; color: black; text-align: left; margin: 0 0 10px; padding: 1em;padding-top: .3em;">
<p><br /></p>
<div style="float:right;margin-left:1em">
<center><i><a href="/wiki/Apollo_Belvedere" title="Apollo Belvedere">Apollo Belvedere</a></i></center>
<p><a href="/wiki/File:Appolon_1_MK1888.png" class="image" title="Apollo Belvedere"><img alt="Apollo Belvedere" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c5/Appolon_1_MK1888.png/100px-Appolon_1_MK1888.png" width="100" height="158" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c5/Appolon_1_MK1888.png/150px-Appolon_1_MK1888.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c5/Appolon_1_MK1888.png/200px-Appolon_1_MK1888.png 2x" data-file-width="296" data-file-height="468" /></a><br /></p>
</div>
<p><b><a href="/wiki/The_arts" title="The arts">The arts</a></b> is a vast subdivision of <a href="/wiki/Culture" title="Culture">culture</a>, composed of many creative endeavors and disciplines. It is a broader term than "<i><a href="/wiki/Art" title="Art">art</a></i>", which, as a description of a field, usually means only the <a href="/wiki/Visual_arts" title="Visual arts">visual arts</a>. <i>The arts</i> encompass the <a href="/wiki/Visual_arts" title="Visual arts">visual arts</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Literary_arts" class="mw-redirect" title="Literary arts">literary arts</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Performing_arts" title="Performing arts">performing arts</a> – <a href="/wiki/Music" title="Music">music</a>, <a href="/wiki/Theatre" title="Theatre">theatre</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dance" title="Dance">dance</a> and <a href="/wiki/Film" title="Film">film</a>, among others. This list is by no means comprehensive, but only meant to introduce the concept of the arts. For all intents and purposes, the history of the arts begins with the <i><a href="/wiki/History_of_art" title="History of art">history of art</a></i>. The arts might have origins in early human evolutionary prehistory.</p>
<p>Ancient Greek art saw the veneration of the animal form and the development of equivalent skills to show musculature, poise, beauty and anatomically correct proportions. <a href="/wiki/Roman_Empire" title="Roman Empire">Ancient Roman</a> art depicted gods as idealized humans, shown with characteristic distinguishing features (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Jupiter_(mythology)" title="Jupiter (mythology)">Jupiter</a>'s thunderbolt). In <a href="/wiki/Byzantine_art" title="Byzantine art">Byzantine</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gothic_art" title="Gothic art">Gothic art</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">Middle Ages</a>, the dominance of the church insisted on the expression of biblical and not material truths. Eastern art has generally worked in a style akin to Western medieval art, namely a concentration on surface patterning and local colour (meaning the plain colour of an object, such as basic red for a red robe, rather than the modulations of that colour brought about by light, shade and reflection). A characteristic of this style is that the local colour is often defined by an outline (a contemporary equivalent is the cartoon). This is evident in, for example, the art of <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tibet" title="Tibet">Tibet</a> and <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a>. Religious <a href="/wiki/Islam" title="Islam">Islamic</a> art forbids iconography, and expresses religious ideas through geometry instead. The physical and rational certainties depicted by the 19th-century Enlightenment were shattered not only by new discoveries of relativity by <a href="/wiki/Albert_Einstein" title="Albert Einstein">Einstein</a> and of unseen psychology by <a href="/wiki/Sigmund_Freud" title="Sigmund Freud">Freud</a>, but also by unprecedented technological development. Paradoxically the expressions of new technologies were greatly influenced by the ancient tribal arts of Africa and Oceania, through the works of Paul Gauguin and the Post-Impressionists, Pablo Picasso and the Cubists, as well as the Futurists and others.</p>

<h2 style="font-family:sans-serif; font-size:100%; font-weight:bold; border: none; margin: 0; padding:0; padding-bottom:.1em; color:#f9f9ff;"><span class="mw-headline" id="Featured_article">Featured article</span></h2>

<div style="border: 1px solid #aaaaaa; border-top-width: 1px; vertical-align: top;background: white;opacity: 1; color: black; text-align: left; margin: 0 0 10px; padding: 1em;padding-top: .3em;">
<div style="float:left;margin-right:1em"><a href="/wiki/File:Gabriel_Faur%C3%A9_1896.jpg" class="image" title="Gabriel Fauré"><img alt="Gabriel Fauré" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/f/f4/Gabriel_Faur%C3%A9_1896.jpg/120px-Gabriel_Faur%C3%A9_1896.jpg" width="120" height="220" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/f/f4/Gabriel_Faur%C3%A9_1896.jpg/180px-Gabriel_Faur%C3%A9_1896.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/f/f4/Gabriel_Faur%C3%A9_1896.jpg/240px-Gabriel_Faur%C3%A9_1896.jpg 2x" data-file-width="360" data-file-height="660" /></a></div>
The <b><a href="/wiki/Piano_music_of_Gabriel_Faur%C3%A9" title="Piano music of Gabriel Fauré">piano music of Gabriel Fauré</a></b> is among his best known work. Written between the 1860s and the 1920s, <a href="/wiki/Gabriel_Faur%C3%A9" title="Gabriel Fauré">Fauré</a>'s major sets of piano works are thirteen <a href="/wiki/Nocturne" title="Nocturne">nocturnes</a>, thirteen <a href="/wiki/Barcarolle" title="Barcarolle">barcarolles</a>, six <a href="/wiki/Impromptu" title="Impromptu">impromptus</a> and four valses-caprices. These sets display the change in his style, over the decades, from uncomplicated youthful charm to a final enigmatic but sometimes fiery introspection, by way of a turbulent period in his middle years. His other notable piano pieces, including shorter works, or collections composed or published as a set, are <i>Romances sans paroles</i>, <a href="/wiki/Piano_ballad" title="Piano ballad">Ballade</a> in F<span class="music-symbol" style="font-family: Arial Unicode MS, Lucida Sans Unicode;">♯</span> major, <a href="/wiki/Mazurka" title="Mazurka">Mazurka</a> in B<span class="music-symbol" style="font-family: Arial Unicode MS, Lucida Sans Unicode;">♭</span> major, <i>Thème et variations</i> in C<span class="music-symbol" style="font-family: Arial Unicode MS, Lucida Sans Unicode;">♯</span> major, and <i>Huit pièces brèves</i>. For <a href="/wiki/Piano_four_hands" title="Piano four hands">piano duet</a>, Fauré composed the <i><a href="/wiki/Dolly_(Faur%C3%A9)" title="Dolly (Fauré)">Dolly Suite</a></i> and, together with his friend and former pupil <a href="/wiki/Andr%C3%A9_Messager" title="André Messager">André Messager</a>, an exuberant parody of <a href="/wiki/Richard_Wagner" title="Richard Wagner">Wagner</a> in the short suite <i>Souvenirs de Bayreuth</i>. Much of the ambidextrous Fauré's piano music is difficult to play, but it is rarely virtuoso in style. The composer disliked showy display, and the predominant characteristic of his piano music is a classical restraint and understatement.

<p>An illustration depicting an <b><a href="/wiki/Mesoamerican_calendars" title="Mesoamerican calendars">ancient Mexican calendar</a></b>. The <a href="/wiki/Maya_calendar" title="Maya calendar">Maya</a> and <a href="/wiki/Aztec_calendar" title="Aztec calendar">Aztec</a> calendars are the most familiar of the Mexican calendars, but similar ones were used by other cultures. Common to all <a href="/wiki/List_of_pre-Columbian_cultures" title="List of pre-Columbian cultures">Mesoamerican cultures</a> was the 260-day ritual calendar that had no confirmed correlation to astronomical or agricultural cycles. These were used in combination with a separate 365-day calendar to create a 52-year cycle known as a <a href="/wiki/Calendar_round" class="mw-redirect" title="Calendar round">calendar round</a>.</p>

<h2 style="font-family:sans-serif; font-size:100%; font-weight:bold; border: none; margin: 0; padding:0; padding-bottom:.1em; color:#f9f9ff;"><span class="mw-headline" id="Featured_biography">Featured biography</span></h2>
</div>
<div style="border: 1px solid #aaaaaa; border-top-width: 1px; vertical-align: top;background: white;opacity: 1; color: black; text-align: left; margin: 0 0 10px; padding: 1em;padding-top: .3em;">
<div style="float:right;margin-left:0.2em"><a href="/wiki/File:ImaHogg.jpg" class="image" title="Ima Hogg"><img alt="Ima Hogg" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/93/ImaHogg.jpg/120px-ImaHogg.jpg" width="120" height="168" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/93/ImaHogg.jpg/180px-ImaHogg.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/93/ImaHogg.jpg/240px-ImaHogg.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1012" data-file-height="1419" /></a></div>
<b><a href="/wiki/Ima_Hogg" title="Ima Hogg">Ima Hogg</a></b> was an enterprising <a href="/wiki/Circus" title="Circus">circus</a> <a href="/wiki/Emcee" class="mw-redirect" title="Emcee">emcee</a> who brought culture and class to <a href="/wiki/Houston,_Texas" class="mw-redirect" title="Houston, Texas">Houston, Texas</a>. A storied <a href="/wiki/Ostrich" class="mw-redirect" title="Ostrich">ostrich</a> jockey, she once rode to Hawaii to visit the <a href="/wiki/Liliuokalani" title="Liliuokalani">Queen</a>. Raised in <a href="/wiki/Texas_Governor%27s_Mansion" title="Texas Governor's Mansion">government housing</a>, young Ima frolicked among a backyard menagerie of raccoons, possums and a bear. Her father, <a href="/wiki/Jim_Hogg" title="Jim Hogg">"Big Jim" Hogg</a>, in an onslaught against fun itself, booby-trapped the banisters she loved to slide down, shut down her money-making schemes, and forced her to pry chewing gum from furniture. He was later thrown from his seat on a moving train and perished; the Hogg clan then struck <a href="/wiki/Petroleum" title="Petroleum">black gold</a> on land Big Jim had forbidden them from selling. Ima had apocryphal sisters named "Ura" and "Hoosa" and real-life brothers sporting conventional names and vast art collections; upon their deaths, she gave away their artwork for nothing and the <a href="/wiki/Varner%E2%80%93Hogg_Plantation_State_Historical_Site" class="mw-redirect" title="Varner–Hogg Plantation State Historical Site">family home</a> to boot. Tragically, Ms. Hogg (a future <a href="/wiki/Honorary_degree" title="Honorary degree">doctor</a>) nursed three dying family members. She once sweet-talked a burglar into returning purloined jewelry and told him to get a job. Well into her nineties, she remained feisty and even exchanged geriatric insults with an <a href="/wiki/Arthur_Rubinstein" title="Arthur Rubinstein">octogenarian pianist</a>. Hogg claimed to have received thirty proposals of marriage in her lifetime, and to have rejected them all. Hogg was revered as the "First Lady of Texas", and her name and legacy still thrive today.&lt;script type="text/javascript"&gt;
