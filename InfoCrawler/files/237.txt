			<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Prophet</h1>
									<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
									<div id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
								<div id="contentSub"></div>

<p>In <a href="/wiki/Religion" title="Religion">religion</a>, a <b>prophet</b> is an individual who has been contacted by a <a href="/wiki/Divinity" title="Divinity">divine</a> being in order to speak on the entity's behalf, serving as an intermediary with humanity by delivering messages or teachings from the supernatural source to other people.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> The message that the prophet conveys is called a <a href="/wiki/Prophecy" title="Prophecy">prophecy</a>, which transports—at least in Judaism—a message beyond mere pagan <a href="/wiki/Soothsaying" class="mw-redirect" title="Soothsaying">soothsaying</a>, <a href="/wiki/Augury" title="Augury">augury</a>, <a href="/wiki/Divination" title="Divination">divination</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Forecasting" title="Forecasting">forecasting</a>, and, most prominently in the <a href="/wiki/Neviim" class="mw-redirect" title="Neviim">neviim</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Tanakh" title="Tanakh">Tanakh</a>, often comprises issues of <a href="/wiki/Social_justice" title="Social justice">social justice</a>.</p>
<p>Claims of prophethood have existed in many cultures through history, including <a href="/wiki/Judaism" title="Judaism">Judaism</a>, <a href="/wiki/Christianity" title="Christianity">Christianity</a>, <a href="/wiki/Islam" title="Islam">Islam</a>, in <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek_Philosophy" class="mw-redirect" title="Ancient Greek Philosophy">Ancient Greek Philosophy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Zoroastrianism" title="Zoroastrianism">Zoroastrianism</a>, <a href="/wiki/Manichaeism" title="Manichaeism">Manichaeism</a>, and many others. Prophets are traditionally regarded as having a role in society that promotes change due to their messages and actions which often convey God's displeasure concerning the behavior of the people.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Abrahamic_religions">Abrahamic religions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Prophet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Abrahamic religions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Judaism">Judaism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Prophet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Judaism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Nevi%27im" title="Nevi'im">Nevi'im</a> and <a href="/wiki/Prophets_in_Judaism" title="Prophets in Judaism">Prophets in Judaism</a></div>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Hebrew_language" title="Hebrew language">Hebrew</a>, the word נָבִיא (<i>nāvî</i>), "spokesperson", traditionally translates as "prophet".<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> The second subdivision of the <a href="/wiki/Hebrew_Bible" title="Hebrew Bible">Hebrew Bible</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tanakh" title="Tanakh">TaNaKh</a> (for "Torah, Nevi'im, Ketuvim"), is devoted to the Hebrew prophets. The meaning of <i>navi</i> is perhaps described in <a href="/wiki/Book_of_Deuteronomy" title="Book of Deuteronomy">Deuteronomy</a> 18:18,<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> where <a href="/wiki/God" title="God">God</a> said, "...and I will put My words in his mouth, and he shall speak unto them all that I shall command him." Thus, the <i>navi</i> was thought to be the "mouth" of God. The root <a href="/wiki/Nun_(letter)" title="Nun (letter)">nun</a>-<a href="/wiki/Bet_(letter)" title="Bet (letter)">bet</a>-<a href="/wiki/Aleph" title="Aleph">alef</a> ("navi") is based on the two-letter root nun-bet which denotes hollowness or openness; to receive transcendental wisdom, one must make oneself "open". Cf. <a href="/wiki/Rashbam" title="Rashbam">Rashbam</a>'s comment to <a href="/wiki/Book_of_Genesis" title="Book of Genesis">Genesis</a> 20:7.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Duccio_di_Buoninsegna_066.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/39/Duccio_di_Buoninsegna_066.jpg/220px-Duccio_di_Buoninsegna_066.jpg" width="220" height="604" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/39/Duccio_di_Buoninsegna_066.jpg/330px-Duccio_di_Buoninsegna_066.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/39/Duccio_di_Buoninsegna_066.jpg/440px-Duccio_di_Buoninsegna_066.jpg 2x" data-file-width="776" data-file-height="2129" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Duccio_di_Buoninsegna_066.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Malachi" title="Malachi">Malachi</a>, one of the last prophets of Israel, painting by Duccio di Buoninsegna, c. 1310 (Museo dell'Opera del Duomo, <a href="/wiki/Siena_Cathedral" title="Siena Cathedral">Siena Cathedral</a>). <i>“He [<a href="/wiki/Mashiach" class="mw-redirect" title="Mashiach">Mashiach</a>] will turn the hearts of the parents to their children, and the hearts of the children to their parents” (Malachi 4:6)</i><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></div>

<p>In addition to writing and speaking messages from God, <a href="/wiki/Israelites" title="Israelites">Israelite</a> or <a href="/wiki/Jews" title="Jews">Jewish</a> nevi'im ("spokespersons", "prophets") often acted out prophetic parables in their life.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> For example, in order to contrast the people’s disobedience with the obedience of the <a href="/wiki/Rechabite" title="Rechabite">Rechabites</a>, God has <a href="/wiki/Jeremiah" title="Jeremiah">Jeremiah</a> invite the Rechabites to drink wine, in disobedience to their ancestor’s command. The Rechabites refuse, wherefore God commends them.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Other prophetic parables acted out by Jeremiah include burying a linen belt so that it gets ruined to illustrate how God intends to ruin Judah's pride.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-cj13_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cj13-11">[11]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-cj13_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cj13-11">[11]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Likewise, Jeremiah buys a clay jar and smashes it in the <a href="/wiki/Gehenna" title="Gehenna">Valley of Ben Hinnom</a> in front of elders and priests to illustrate that God will smash the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Judah" title="Kingdom of Judah">nation of Judah</a> and the city of Judah beyond repair.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> God instructs Jeremiah to make a yoke from wood and leather straps and to put it on his own neck to demonstrate how God will put the nation under the yoke of <a href="/wiki/Nebuchadnezzar_II" title="Nebuchadnezzar II">Nebuchadnezzar</a>, king of <a href="/wiki/Babylon" title="Babylon">Babylon</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> In a similar way, the prophet <a href="/wiki/Isaiah" title="Isaiah">Isaiah</a> had to walk stripped and barefoot for three years to illustrate the coming captivity,<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> and the prophet <a href="/wiki/Ezekiel" title="Ezekiel">Ezekiel</a> had to lie on his side for 390 days and eat measured food to illustrate the coming siege.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>The prophetic assignment is not always portrayed as positive in the Hebrew Bible,<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-18">[18]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> and prophets were often the target of persecution and opposition.<sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceB_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceB-20">[20]</a></sup> God’s personal prediction for Jeremiah, "Attack you they will, overcome you they can't,"<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> was performed many times in the <a href="/wiki/Bible_prophecy" title="Bible prophecy">biblical narrative</a> as Jeremiah warned of destruction of those who continued to refuse repentance and accept more moderate consequences.<sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceB_20-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceB-20">[20]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> In return for his adherence to God’s discipline and speaking God’s words, Jeremiah was attacked by his own brothers,<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> beaten and put into the stocks by a priest and <a href="/wiki/False_prophet" title="False prophet">false prophet</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> imprisoned by the king,<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> threatened with death,<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> thrown into a cistern by Judah’s officials,<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> and opposed by a false prophet.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> Likewise, Isaiah was told by his hearers who rejected his message, "Leave the way! Get off the path! Let us hear no more about the <a href="/wiki/Book_of_Isaiah#.22Holy_One_of_Israel.22" title="Book of Isaiah">Holy One of Israel</a>!"<sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_18-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-18">[18]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> The life of Moses being threatened by <a href="/wiki/Pharaoh_of_the_Exodus" class="mw-redirect" title="Pharaoh of the Exodus">Pharaoh</a> is another example.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<p>According to I <a href="/wiki/Books_of_Samuel" title="Books of Samuel">Samuel</a> 9:9,<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> the old name for navi is <i>ro'eh</i>, רֹאֶה, which literally means "Seer". That could document an ancient shift, from viewing prophets as seers for hire to viewing them as moral teachers. Allen (1971) comments that in the <a href="/wiki/Solomon%27s_Temple" title="Solomon's Temple">First Temple</a> Era, there were essentially seer-priests, who formed a guild, <a href="/wiki/Divination" title="Divination">divined</a>, performed rituals and sacrifices, and were scribes, and then there were canonical prophets, who did none of these (and were against divination) and had instead a message to deliver.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The seer-priests were usually attached to a local shrine or temple, such as <a href="/wiki/Shiloh_(biblical_city)" title="Shiloh (biblical city)">Shiloh</a>, and initiated others as priests in that priesthood: it was a mystical craft-guild with apprentices and recruitment. Canonical prophets were not organised this way. The similar term <i>ben-navi</i> ("son of the prophet") means "member of a seer-priest guild".<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Some examples of prophets in the Tanakh include <a href="/wiki/Abraham" title="Abraham">Abraham</a>, Moses, <a href="/wiki/Miriam" title="Miriam">Miriam</a>, Isaiah, <a href="/wiki/Samuel" title="Samuel">Samuel</a>, Ezekiel, <a href="/wiki/Malachi" title="Malachi">Malachi</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Job_(biblical_figure)" title="Job (biblical figure)">Job</a>. In Jewish tradition <a href="/wiki/Daniel_(biblical_figure)" title="Daniel (biblical figure)">Daniel</a> is not counted in the list of prophets.</p>
<p>A Jewish tradition suggests that there were twice as many prophets as the number which left Egypt, which would make 1,200,000 prophets.<sup id="cite_ref-jfaq_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-jfaq-33">[33]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Talmud" title="Talmud">Talmud</a> recognizes the existence of <a href="/wiki/Prophets_in_Judaism#The_48_Prophets" title="Prophets in Judaism">48 male prophets</a> who bequeathed permanent messages to mankind.<sup id="cite_ref-jfaq_33-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-jfaq-33">[33]</a></sup> According to the Talmud there were also seven women who are counted as prophets whose message bears relevance for all generations: <a href="/wiki/Sarah" title="Sarah">Sarah</a>, <a href="/wiki/Miriam" title="Miriam">Miriam</a>, <a href="/wiki/Deborah" title="Deborah">Devorah</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hannah_(Bible)" class="mw-redirect" title="Hannah (Bible)">Hannah</a> (mother of the prophet Samuel), <a href="/wiki/Abigail" title="Abigail">Abigail</a> (a wife of <a href="/wiki/David" title="David">King David</a>), <a href="/wiki/Huldah" title="Huldah">Huldah</a> (from the time of Jeremiah), and <a href="/wiki/Esther" title="Esther">Esther</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-jfaq_33-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-jfaq-33">[33]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Rashi" title="Rashi">Rashi</a> points out that <a href="/wiki/Rebecca" title="Rebecca">Rebecca</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rachel" title="Rachel">Rachel</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Leah" title="Leah">Leah</a> were also prophets.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
<p>Prophets in Judaism are not always Jews.<sup id="cite_ref-jfaq_33-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-jfaq-33">[33]</a></sup> The story of <a href="/wiki/Balaam" title="Balaam">Balaam</a> in <a href="/wiki/Book_of_Numbers" title="Book of Numbers">Numbers</a> 22 describes a non-Jewish prophet.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> According to the Talmud, <a href="/wiki/Obadiah" title="Obadiah">Obadiah</a> is said to have been a convert to Judaism.</p>
<p>The last nevi'im ("spokespersons", "prophets") mentioned in the <a href="/wiki/Jewish_Bible" class="mw-redirect" title="Jewish Bible">Jewish Bible</a> are <a href="/wiki/Haggai" title="Haggai">Haggai</a>, <a href="/wiki/Zechariah_(Hebrew_prophet)" title="Zechariah (Hebrew prophet)">Zechariah</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Malachi" title="Malachi">Malachi</a>, all of whom lived at the end of the 70-year Babylonian exile. The <a href="/wiki/Talmud" title="Talmud">Talmud</a> (<a href="/wiki/Sanhedrin_(tractate)" title="Sanhedrin (tractate)">Sanhedrin</a> 11a) states that Haggai, Zachariah, and Malachi were the last prophets, and nowadays only the "<a href="/wiki/Bath_Kol" class="mw-redirect" title="Bath Kol">Bath Kol</a>" (בת קול, lit. <i>daughter of a voice</i>, "voice of God") exists.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Christianity">Christianity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Prophet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Christianity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Prophets_of_Christianity" title="Prophets of Christianity">Prophets of Christianity</a></div>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Christianity" title="Christianity">Christianity</a> a prophet (or seer)<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> is one inspired by God through the <a href="/wiki/Holy_Spirit_(Christianity)" class="mw-redirect" title="Holy Spirit (Christianity)">Holy Spirit</a> to deliver a message for a specific purpose. God's calling as a prophet is not to elevate an individual for their own glory, but for the glory of God and to turn people to him. Some <a href="/wiki/Christian" title="Christian">Christian</a> <a href="/wiki/Religious_denomination" title="Religious denomination">denominations</a> limit a prophet's message to those only to the entire church congregation and exclude personal messages not intended for the body of believers, but in the <a href="/wiki/Bible" title="Bible">Bible</a> on a number of occasions prophets were called to deliver personal messages.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> The reception of a message is termed <a href="/wiki/Revelation" title="Revelation">revelation</a> and the delivery of the message is termed prophecy.</p>
<p>James Jordan argues that the office of prophet involves more than delivering the direct revelations of God. He writes, "The full meaning of prophet is council member, a member of God's Divine Council . . . Moses, who is an exemplary prophet of the Old Covenant (Numbers 12:6–8) . . . not only received information from the Counsel and passed its decisions onto the people . . . he also actively argued before the Council when he felt it necessary, even 'changing God's mind' on occasion (Exodus 32:7–14, 30–35; Numbers 14:13–19)." <sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> In this way, Christ is executing a prophetic office when he intercedes for Christians and ordinary Christians are executing a prophetic office when they reason with God on the behalf of others in prayer, just as Moses interceded and persuaded God on behalf of Israel as part of his prophetic function.</p>
<p>The term prophet is applied to those who receive public or private revelation. Public Revelation, in Catholicism, is part of the <a href="/wiki/Deposit_of_faith" class="mw-redirect" title="Deposit of faith">Deposit of faith</a>, the revelation of which was completed by Jesus; whereas Private Revelation does not add to the Deposit. The term "deposit of faith" refers to the entirety of Jesus Christ's revelation, and is passed to successive generations in two different forms, sacred scripture (the Bible) and sacred tradition.</p>
<p>Anyone who claims to speak God's words or teach in his name and is not a prophet the Bible terms a <a href="/wiki/False_prophet" title="False prophet">false prophet</a>. One test given in the <a href="/wiki/Old_Testament" title="Old Testament">Old Testament</a> in <a href="/wiki/Book_of_Deuteronomy" title="Book of Deuteronomy">Deuteronomy</a><sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> contains a warning of those who prophecy events which do not come to pass and said they should be put to death. Elsewhere a false prophet may be someone who is purposely trying to deceive, is delusional, under the influence of <a href="/wiki/Satan" title="Satan">Satan</a> or is speaking from his own spirit.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup></p>

<p>Some Christians who believe in <a href="/wiki/Dispensationalism" title="Dispensationalism">dispensationalism</a> believe prophecy ended along with the rest of the sign gifts shortly after the coming of <a href="/wiki/Jesus_in_Christianity" title="Jesus in Christianity">Jesus</a>, who delivered the "<a href="/wiki/Biblical_law_in_Christianity" class="mw-redirect" title="Biblical law in Christianity">fullness of the law</a>". Within this group, many Protestants believe that prophecy ended with the last of the Hebrew prophets of the <a href="/wiki/Hebrew_Bible" title="Hebrew Bible">Hebrew Bible</a>, leaving a gap of about 400 years between then and the life of Jesus. The majority, including the <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Orthodox_Church" title="Eastern Orthodox Church">Eastern Orthodox</a>, allow an exception for <a href="/wiki/John_the_Baptist" title="John the Baptist">John the Baptist</a> as a prophet contemporary with Jesus.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/New_Testament" title="New Testament">New Testament</a> passages that explicitly discuss prophets existing after the death and resurrection of <a href="/wiki/Christ" class="mw-redirect" title="Christ">Christ</a> include <a href="/wiki/Book_of_Revelation" title="Book of Revelation">Revelation</a> 11:10,<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Gospel_of_Matthew" title="Gospel of Matthew">Matthew</a> 10:40–41 and 23:34,<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Gospel_of_John" title="Gospel of John">John</a> 13:20 and 15:20<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Acts_of_the_Apostles" title="Acts of the Apostles">Acts</a> 11:25–30, 13:1 and 15:32.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> Christians believe that the Holy Spirit leads people to faith in Jesus and gives them the ability to lead a <a href="/wiki/Christian_worship" title="Christian worship">Christian life</a> and to give gifts (i.e. abilities) to Christians. These may include the <a href="/wiki/Charism" class="mw-redirect" title="Charism">charismatic</a> gifts such as prophecy, <a href="/wiki/Glossolalia" title="Glossolalia">tongues</a>, <a href="/wiki/Faith_healing" title="Faith healing">miraculous healing</a> ability, and <a href="/wiki/Clairsentience" class="mw-redirect" title="Clairsentience">clairsentience</a>. Christians holding a view known as <a href="/wiki/Cessationism" title="Cessationism">cessationism</a> believe these gifts were given only in New Testament times and ceased after the last <a href="/wiki/Apostle_(Christian)" class="mw-redirect" title="Apostle (Christian)">apostle died</a>.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Didache" title="Didache">Didache</a> gives extensive instruction in how to distinguish between true and false prophets, as well as commands regarding tithes to prophets in the church.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Irenaeus" title="Irenaeus">Irenaeus</a>, wrote of 2nd-century believers with the gift of prophecy,<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> while <a href="/wiki/Justin_Martyr" title="Justin Martyr">Justin Martyr</a> argued in his <i>Dialogue with Trypho</i> that prophets were not found among the Jews in his time, but that the church had prophets.<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/The_Shepherd_of_Hermas" title="The Shepherd of Hermas">The Shepherd of Hermas</a> describes revelation in a vision regarding the proper operation of prophecy in the church.<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Eusebius_of_Caesarea" class="mw-redirect" title="Eusebius of Caesarea">Eusebius</a> mentions that <a href="/wiki/Quadratus_of_Athens" title="Quadratus of Athens">Quadratus</a> and Ammia of <a href="/wiki/Ala%C5%9Fehir" title="Alaşehir">Philadelphia</a> were both prominent prophets following the age of the Twelve Apostles.<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Tertullian" title="Tertullian">Tertullian</a>, writing of the church meetings of the <a href="/wiki/Montanism" title="Montanism">Montanists</a> (to whom he belonged), described in detail the practice of prophecy in the 2nd-century church.<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup></p>
<p>A number of later Christian saints were claimed to have powers of prophecy, such as <a href="/wiki/Columba_of_Iona" class="mw-redirect" title="Columba of Iona">Columba of Iona</a>, <a href="/wiki/Saint_Malachy" title="Saint Malachy">Saint Malachy</a> or <a href="/wiki/Padre_Pio" title="Padre Pio">Padre Pio</a>. <a href="/wiki/Marian_apparition" title="Marian apparition">Marian apparitions</a> like those at Fatima or Kibeho often included prophecies regarding the future of the world as well as of the local areas they occurred in <sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup></p>
<p>Prophetic movements in particular can be traced throughout the Christian Church's history, in expressions such as <a href="/wiki/Montanism" title="Montanism">Montanism</a>, <a href="/wiki/Novatianism" title="Novatianism">Novatianism</a>, <a href="/wiki/Donatism" title="Donatism">Donatism</a>, <a href="/wiki/Franciscan" class="mw-redirect" title="Franciscan">Franciscanism</a>, <a href="/wiki/Anabaptist" class="mw-redirect" title="Anabaptist">Anabaptism</a>, <a href="/wiki/Camisard" title="Camisard">Camisard</a> enthusiasm, <a href="/wiki/Puritan" class="mw-redirect" title="Puritan">Puritanism</a>, <a href="/wiki/Religious_Society_of_Friends" class="mw-redirect" title="Religious Society of Friends">Quakerism</a>, <a href="/wiki/Quietism_(Christian_philosophy)" title="Quietism (Christian philosophy)">Quietism</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Pietism" title="Pietism">Pietism</a>. Modern Pentecostals and Charismatics, movements which together make up approximately half a billion people, believe in the contemporary function of the gift of prophecy, and some in these movements allow for idea that God may continue to gift the church with some individuals who are prophets.</p>
<p>Some Christians also believe that the title "prophet" encompasses others than those who receive visions from God. A more modern definition of prophet is someone who spreads God's truths. These can be revealed in a number of ways, not only visions.</p>
<p>Some Christian sects recognize the existence of a "modern-day" prophet. One such denomination is <a href="/wiki/The_Church_of_Jesus_Christ_of_Latter-day_Saints" title="The Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints">The Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints</a> (LDS Church), which teaches that God still communicates with mankind through prophecy.<sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup></p>

<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Prophets_and_messengers_in_Islam" title="Prophets and messengers in Islam">Prophets and messengers in Islam</a></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Quran" title="Quran">Quran</a> identifies a number of men as "Prophets of Islam" (<a href="/wiki/Arabic_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Arabic language">Arabic</a>: <span lang="ar" dir="rtl" xml:lang="ar">nabiyy نبي</span>‎‎; pl. anbiyaa' أنبياء). <a href="/wiki/Muslim" title="Muslim">Muslims</a> believe such individuals were assigned a special mission by <a href="/wiki/God_in_Islam" title="God in Islam">God</a> (<a href="/wiki/Arabic_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Arabic language">Arabic</a>: <i><a href="/wiki/Allah" title="Allah">Allah</a></i>) to guide humanity. Besides <a href="/wiki/Muhammad_in_Islam" title="Muhammad in Islam">Muhammad</a>, this includes prophets such as <a href="/wiki/Abraham_in_Islam" title="Abraham in Islam">Ibrahim</a> (Abraham), <a href="/wiki/Moses_in_Islam" title="Moses in Islam">Musa</a> (Moses) and <a href="/wiki/Jesus_in_Islam" title="Jesus in Islam">Isa</a> (Jesus).</p>

<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Mohammed_receiving_revelation_from_the_angel_Gabriel.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/20/Mohammed_receiving_revelation_from_the_angel_Gabriel.jpg/220px-Mohammed_receiving_revelation_from_the_angel_Gabriel.jpg" width="220" height="166" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/20/Mohammed_receiving_revelation_from_the_angel_Gabriel.jpg/330px-Mohammed_receiving_revelation_from_the_angel_Gabriel.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/20/Mohammed_receiving_revelation_from_the_angel_Gabriel.jpg/440px-Mohammed_receiving_revelation_from_the_angel_Gabriel.jpg 2x" data-file-width="720" data-file-height="542" /></a>

<p>Although only twenty-five prophets<sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup> are <a href="/wiki/Prophets_in_the_Qur%27an" class="mw-redirect" title="Prophets in the Qur'an">mentioned by name in the Qur'an</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Hadith" title="Hadith">hadith</a> (no. 21257 in <i><a href="/wiki/Musnad_Ahmad_ibn_Hanbal" title="Musnad Ahmad ibn Hanbal">Musnad Ibn Hanbal</a></i>)<sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup> mentions that there were (more or less) 124,000 prophets in total throughout history. Other traditions place the number of prophets at 224,000. Some scholars hold that there are an even greater number in the history of mankind, and only God knows. The Qur'an says that God has sent a prophet to every group of people throughout time, and that Muhammad is the last of the prophets, sent for the whole of humankind.<sup id="cite_ref-56" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup> The message of all the prophets is believed to be the same. In Islam, all prophetic messengers are prophets (such as <a href="/wiki/Adam" title="Adam">Adam</a>, <a href="/wiki/Noah" title="Noah">Noah</a>, <a href="/wiki/Abraham" title="Abraham">Abraham</a>, <a href="/wiki/Moses" title="Moses">Moses</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jesus" title="Jesus">Jesus</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Muhammad" title="Muhammad">Muhammad</a>) though not all prophets are prophetic messengers. The primary distinction is that a prophet is required to demonstrate God's law through his actions, character, and behavior without necessarily calling people to follow him, while a prophetic messenger is required to pronounce God's law (i.e. revelation) and call his people to submit and follow him. Muhammad is distinguished from the rest of the prophetic messengers and prophets in that he was commissioned by God to be the prophetic messenger to all of mankind. Many of these prophets are also found in the texts of Judaism (The Torah, the Prophets, and the Writings; collectively known as the Old Testament to Christians) and Christianity.<sup id="cite_ref-57" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup></p>
<p>While <a href="/wiki/Islam" title="Islam">Islam</a> shares the Jewish tradition that the first prophet is <a href="/wiki/Adam_in_Islam" title="Adam in Islam">Adem</a> (<a href="/wiki/Adam" title="Adam">Adam</a>), it differs in that the <a href="/wiki/Last_prophet" title="Last prophet">last prophet</a> is Muhammad, who in Islam is called Seal of the prophets or <i><a href="/wiki/Khatam_an-Nabiyyin" title="Khatam an-Nabiyyin">Khatam an-Nabiyyin</a></i>. Muslims often refer to Muhammad as <i>the prophet,</i> in the form of a noun.<sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-59" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-60" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-61" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup> Isa (Jesus) is the result of a <a href="/wiki/Virgin_birth_of_Jesus" title="Virgin birth of Jesus">virgin birth</a> in Islam as in Christianity, and is regarded as a prophet.<sup id="cite_ref-62" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup></p>
<p>Traditionally, four prophets are believed to have been sent <a href="/wiki/Islamic_holy_books" title="Islamic holy books">holy books</a>: the <a href="/wiki/Torah_in_Islam" title="Torah in Islam">Tawrat</a> (<a href="/wiki/Torah" title="Torah">Torah</a>) to Moses, the <a href="/wiki/Zabur" title="Zabur">Zabur</a> (<a href="/wiki/Psalms" title="Psalms">Psalms</a>) to David, the <a href="/wiki/Gospel_in_Islam" title="Gospel in Islam">Injil</a> (<a href="/wiki/The_gospel" title="The gospel">Gospel</a>) to Jesus, and the Qur'an to Muhammad; those prophets are considered "messengers" or <i><a href="/wiki/Messengers_of_Islam" class="mw-redirect" title="Messengers of Islam">rasul</a></i> (<i>Ule al A'zm men al Rusul</i> أولي العزم من الرسل). Other main prophets are considered messengers or <i>Nabi</i>, even if they didn't receive a Book from God. Examples include the messenger-prophet <a href="/wiki/Aaron" title="Aaron">Aaron</a> (<i><a href="/wiki/Aaron#Islam" title="Aaron">Harun</a></i>), the messenger-prophet <a href="/wiki/Ishmael" title="Ishmael">Ishmael</a> (<i><a href="/wiki/Ishmael_in_Islam" title="Ishmael in Islam">Isma'il</a></i>) and the messenger-prophet <a href="/wiki/Joseph_in_Islam" title="Joseph in Islam">Yusuf</a> (<a href="/wiki/Joseph_(son_of_Jacob)" class="mw-redirect" title="Joseph (son of Jacob)">Joseph</a>).</p>
<p>Although it offers many incidents from the lives of many prophets, the Qur'an focuses with special narrative and rhetorical emphasis on the careers of the first four of these five major prophets. Of all the figures before Muhammad, Moses is referred to most frequently in the Qur'an. As for the fifth, the Qur'an is frequently addressed directly to Muhammad, and it often discusses situations encountered by him. Direct use of his name in the text, however, is rare. Rarer still is the mention of Muhammad's contemporaries.</p>
<h3><span id="Bahá'í"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Bah.C3.A1.27.C3.AD">Bahá'í</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Prophet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Bahá'í">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Manifestation_of_God" title="Manifestation of God">Manifestation of God</a></div>

<p>The <a href="/wiki/Bah%C3%A1%27%C3%AD_Faith" title="Bahá'í Faith">Bahá'í Faith</a> refers to what are commonly called prophets as "Manifestations of God" who are directly linked with the concept of <a href="/wiki/Progressive_revelation_(Bah%C3%A1%27%C3%AD)" title="Progressive revelation (Bahá'í)">Progressive revelation</a>. Bahá'ís believe that God expresses this will at all times and in many ways, including through a series of divine messengers referred to as "Manifestations of God" or "divine educators".<sup id="cite_ref-eor_63-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-eor-63">[63]</a></sup> In expressing God's intent, these Manifestations are seen to establish religion in the world. Thus they are seen as an intermediary between God and humanity.<sup id="cite_ref-manifestation_64-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-manifestation-64">[64]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Manifestations of God are not seen as incarnations of God, and are also not seen as ordinary mortals. Instead, the Bahá'í concept of the Manifestation of God emphasizes simultaneously the humanity of that intermediary and the divinity in the way they show forth the will, knowledge and attributes of God; thus they have both human and divine stations.<sup id="cite_ref-manifestation_64-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-manifestation-64">[64]</a></sup></p>
<p>In addition to the Manifestations of God, there are also minor prophets. While the Manifestations of God, or major prophets, are compared to the Sun (which produces its own heat and light), minor prophets are compared to the Moon (which receives its light from the sun). Moses, for example, is taught as having been a Manifestation of God and his brother Aaron a minor prophet. Moses spoke on behalf of God, and Aaron spoke on behalf of Moses (<a href="/wiki/Book_of_Exodus" title="Book of Exodus">Exodus</a> 4:14-17).<sup id="cite_ref-65" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup> Other Jewish prophets are considered minor prophets, as they are considered to have come in the shadow of the dispensation of Moses to develop and consolidate the process he set in motion.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Prophetic_claims_in_movements_deriving_from_Abrahamic_religions">Prophetic claims in movements deriving from Abrahamic religions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Prophet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Prophetic claims in movements deriving from Abrahamic religions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In modern times the term "prophet" can be somewhat controversial. Many Christians with <a href="/wiki/Pentecostalism" title="Pentecostalism">Pentecostal</a> or <a href="/wiki/Charismatic_Movement" title="Charismatic Movement">charismatic beliefs</a> believe in the continuation of the gift of prophecy and the continuation of the role of prophet as taught in <a href="/wiki/Epistle_to_the_Ephesians" title="Epistle to the Ephesians">Ephesians</a> 4.<sup id="cite_ref-66" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup> The content of prophecies can vary widely. Prophecies are often spoken as quotes from God. They may contain quotes from scripture, statements about the past or current situation, or predictions of the future. Prophecies can also 'make manifest the secrets' of the hearts of other people, telling about the details of their lives. Sometimes, more than one person in a congregation will receive the same message in prophecy, with one giving it before another.</p>
<p>Other movements claim to have prophets. In France, Michel Potay says he received a revelation, called <i>The Revelation of Arès</i>, dictated by Jesus in 1974, then by God in 1977. He is considered a prophet by his followers, the <a href="/wiki/Pilgrims_of_Ar%C3%A8s" title="Pilgrims of Arès">Pilgrims of Arès</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Catholicism">Catholicism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Prophet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Catholicism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A number of modern catholic saints have been claimed to have powers of prophecy, such as <a href="/wiki/Padre_Pio" title="Padre Pio">Padre Pio</a> <sup id="cite_ref-67" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Alexandrina_Maria_da_Costa" title="Alexandrina Maria da Costa">Alexandrina Maria da Costa</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-68" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup></p>
<p>In addition to this many modern <a href="/wiki/Marian_apparition" title="Marian apparition">Marian apparitions</a> included prophecies in them about the world and about the local areas. The <a href="/wiki/Three_Secrets_of_F%C3%A1tima" title="Three Secrets of Fátima">Fátima apparition</a> in 1917 included a prophecy given by Mary to three children, that on October 13, 1917 there would be a great miracle for all to see at <a href="/wiki/F%C3%A1tima,_Portugal" title="Fátima, Portugal">Fátima</a>, Portugal, and on that day tens of thousands of people headed to Fátima to see what would happen including newspaper journalists. Many witnesses, including journalists, claimed to see the sun "dance" in the sky in the afternoon of that day, exactly as the visionaries had predicted several months before.<sup id="cite_ref-69" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-69">[69]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Our_Lady_of_Kibeho" title="Our Lady of Kibeho">Kibeho apparition</a> in <a href="/wiki/Rwanda" title="Rwanda">Rwanda</a> in the 1980s included many prophecies about great violence and destruction that was coming, and the <a href="/wiki/Rwandan_genocide" title="Rwandan genocide">Rwandan genocide</a> only ten years later was interpreted by the visionaries as the fulfilment of these prophecies <sup id="cite_ref-70" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-70">[70]</a></sup></p>
<p>Several miracles and a vision of the <a href="/wiki/Prophecy_of_the_Popes" title="Prophecy of the Popes">identity of the last 112 Popes</a> were attributed to <a href="/wiki/Saint_Malachy" title="Saint Malachy">Saint Malachy</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Archbishop_of_Armagh" title="Archbishop of Armagh">Archbishop of Armagh</a> (1095 – 1148).</p>
<h3><span id="Jehovah's_Witnesses"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Jehovah.27s_Witnesses">Jehovah's Witnesses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Prophet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Jehovah's Witnesses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Watch_Tower_Society_unfulfilled_predictions" title="Watch Tower Society unfulfilled predictions">Watch Tower Society unfulfilled predictions</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Jehovah%27s_Witnesses" title="Jehovah's Witnesses">Jehovah's Witnesses</a> do not consider any single person in their modern-day organization to be a prophet.<sup id="cite_ref-71" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-71">[71]</a></sup> Their literature has referred to their organization collectively as God's "prophet" on earth; this is understood, however, in the sense of declaring their interpretation of God's judgments from the Bible along with God's guidance of His Holy Spirit.<sup id="cite_ref-72" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-72">[72]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-73" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-73">[73]</a></sup> Their publishing company, <a href="/wiki/Watch_Tower_Bible_and_Tract_Society_of_Pennsylvania" title="Watch Tower Bible and Tract Society of Pennsylvania">Watch Tower</a>, and official position magazine, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Watchtower" title="The Watchtower">The&#160;Watchtower</a></i>, have asserted: "Ever since <i>The&#160;Watchtower</i> began to be published in July 1879 it has looked ahead into the future... No, <i>The&#160;Watchtower</i> is no inspired prophet, but it follows and explains a Book of prophecy the predictions in which have proved to be unerring and unfailing till now. <i>The&#160;Watchtower</i> is therefore under safe guidance. It may be read with confidence, for its statements may be checked against that prophetic Book."<sup id="cite_ref-74" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-74">[74]</a></sup> They also claim that they are God's one and only true channel to mankind on earth, and used by God for this purpose.</p>
<p>They have made many <a href="/wiki/Eschatology_of_Jehovah%27s_Witnesses" title="Eschatology of Jehovah's Witnesses">eschatological forecasts</a>, some of which have led people (including followers) to incorrect assumptions. One example is <i>The&#160;Watchtower's</i> assertions that the end of the "<a href="/wiki/Gentile#LDS_Church_usage" title="Gentile">Gentile</a> times" or "times of the nations" would occur in 1914; even prominent Watch Tower representatives such as <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Hugh_Macmillan#Macmillan.27s_proclamation_of_1914" title="Alexander Hugh Macmillan">A. H. Macmillan</a> incorrectly concluded and overstated their expectations. As a result, <i>The Watchtower</i> has acknowledged that Jehovah's Witnesses "have made mistakes in their understanding of what would occur at the end of certain time periods."<sup id="cite_ref-75" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-75">[75]</a></sup> Concurrently with these exceptions, Jehovah's Witnesses in their literature and assemblies have taught their leadership was personally chosen by Jesus Christ in 1919 (a prophetic year in Jehovah's Witnesses eschatology) and that they are "God's sole channel on earth," and "Jehovah's spirit directed organization".</p>

<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Prophet,_seer,_and_revelator" title="Prophet, seer, and revelator">Prophet, seer, and revelator</a>; <a href="/wiki/President_of_the_Church" title="President of the Church">President of the Church</a>; and <a href="/wiki/Revelation_(Latter_Day_Saints)" title="Revelation (Latter Day Saints)">Revelation (Latter Day Saints)</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Joseph_Smith" title="Joseph Smith">Joseph Smith</a>, who established the <a href="/wiki/Church_of_Christ_(Latter_Day_Saints)" title="Church of Christ (Latter Day Saints)">Church of Christ</a> in 1830, is considered a prophet by members of the <a href="/wiki/Latter_Day_Saint_movement" title="Latter Day Saint movement">Latter Day Saint movement</a>, of which <a href="/wiki/The_Church_of_Jesus_Christ_of_Latter-day_Saints" title="The Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints">The Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints</a> (LDS Church) is the largest denomination. Additionally, many churches within the movement believe in a succession of modern prophets (accepted by Latter Day Saints as "<a href="/wiki/Prophet,_seer,_and_revelator" title="Prophet, seer, and revelator">prophets, seers, and revelators</a>") since the time of Joseph Smith. <a href="/wiki/Thomas_S._Monson" title="Thomas S. Monson">Thomas S. Monson</a> is the current president and prophet of <a href="/wiki/The_Church_of_Jesus_Christ_of_Latter-day_Saints" title="The Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints">The Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints</a>.</p>

He announced a <a href="/wiki/Second_Coming" title="Second Coming">Second Coming</a>, resulting in the <a href="/wiki/Great_Disappointment" title="Great Disappointment">Great Disappointment</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_religions">Other religions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Prophet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Other religions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Hinduism">Hinduism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Prophet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Hinduism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Hinduism" title="Hinduism">Hindu</a> concept of <a href="/wiki/Rishis" class="mw-redirect" title="Rishis">rishis</a> is similar to the concept of prophets. The Sanskrit word <i>rishi</i> is loosely translated into English as "seer" (a prophet, a man who can foresee the future). Hinduism recognizes and reveres thousands of rishis, who can be thought of as the collective founders of the Hindu religion over many millennia. Of these, special importance is given to the <a href="/wiki/Saptarshi" class="mw-redirect" title="Saptarshi">Saptarshi</a> (the Seven Sages), widely regarded as patriarchs of the Hindu religion, whose listing is different according to different texts. The Saptarshi and their clans are believed to have composed the hymns of the four <a href="/wiki/Vedas" title="Vedas">Vedas</a> by entering into communion with the Supreme Cosmic Spirit through meditation. For instance, <a href="/wiki/Rigveda" title="Rigveda">Rigveda</a> 1.1 is attributed to Rishi Madhucchandā Vaishwāmitra (i.e. Madhucchandā of the clan of <a href="/wiki/Vishwamitra" class="mw-redirect" title="Vishwamitra">Vishwamitra</a>). Most rishikās were male, but some were female too. <a href="/wiki/Lopamudra" title="Lopamudra">Lopamudra</a> is the author of one hymn in the Rigveda, and <a href="/wiki/Gargi_Vachaknavi" title="Gargi Vachaknavi">Gargi Vachaknavi</a> is described in the <a href="/wiki/Brihadaranyaka_Upanishad" title="Brihadaranyaka Upanishad">Brihadaranyaka Upanishad</a> as a highly respected woman in the field of Brahmajñāna. Apart from the Vedas, various rishis are also credited with composing the several <a href="/wiki/Smriti" title="Smriti">Smriti</a> texts, like <a href="/wiki/Veda_Vyasa" class="mw-redirect" title="Veda Vyasa">Veda Vyasa</a> who composed the <i><a href="/wiki/Mah%C4%81bh%C4%81rata" class="mw-redirect" title="Mahābhārata">Mahābhārata</a></i>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ifa_and_other_African_traditional_religions">Ifa and other African traditional religions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Prophet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Ifa and other African traditional religions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><i>Main article:</i> <a href="/wiki/Traditional_African_religion" class="mw-redirect" title="Traditional African religion">Traditional African religion</a>, <a href="/wiki/If%C3%A1" title="Ifá">Ifá</a>, <a href="/wiki/Religion_in_Africa" title="Religion in Africa">Religion in Africa</a></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Divination" title="Divination">Divination</a> remains an important aspect of the lives of the people of contemporary <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a>, especially amongst the usually rural, socially traditionalistic segments of its population. In arguably its most influential manifestation, the system of prophecy practiced by the <a href="/wiki/Babalawo" title="Babalawo">Babalawos</a> and <a href="/wiki/Iyanifa" class="mw-redirect" title="Iyanifa">Iyanifas</a> of the historically <a href="/wiki/Yoruba_people" title="Yoruba people">Yoruba</a> regions of <a href="/wiki/West_Africa" title="West Africa">West Africa</a> have bequeathed to the world a corpus of <a href="/wiki/Fortune-telling" title="Fortune-telling">fortune-telling</a> poetic methodologies so intricate that they have been added by <a href="/wiki/UNESCO" title="UNESCO">UNESCO</a> to its official <i><a href="/wiki/UNESCO_Intangible_Cultural_Heritage_Lists" title="UNESCO Intangible Cultural Heritage Lists">intangible cultural heritage of the World list</a></i>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Native_Americans">Native Americans</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Prophet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Native Americans">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Native_Americans_in_the_United_States" title="Native Americans in the United States">Native American</a> <a href="/wiki/Great_Peacemaker" title="Great Peacemaker">Great Peacemaker</a> (Deganawidah) co-founded the <a href="/wiki/Iroquois#Iroquois_Confederacy" title="Iroquois">Haudenosaunee league</a> in <a href="/wiki/Pre-Columbian" class="mw-redirect" title="Pre-Columbian">pre-Columbian</a> times. In retrospect, his <a href="/wiki/Great_Peacemaker#Prophecy_of_the_boy_seer" title="Great Peacemaker">prophecy of the boy seer</a> could appear to refer to the conflict between natives and Europeans (white serpent).</p>
<p>From 1805 until the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Tippecanoe" title="Battle of Tippecanoe">Battle of Tippecanoe</a> that falsified his predictions in 1811, the "<a href="/wiki/Shawnee" title="Shawnee">Shawnee</a> prophet" <a href="/wiki/Tenskwatawa" title="Tenskwatawa">Tenskwatawa</a> lead an Indian alliance to stop Europeans to take more and more land going west. He reported <a href="/wiki/Vision_(spirituality)" title="Vision (spirituality)">visions</a> he had. He is said to have accurately predicted a <a href="/wiki/Solar_eclipse" title="Solar eclipse">solar eclipse</a>. His brother <a href="/wiki/Tecumseh" title="Tecumseh">Tecumseh</a> re-established the alliance for <a href="/wiki/Tecumseh%27s_War" title="Tecumseh's War">Tecumseh's War</a>, that ended with the latter's death in 1813. Tecumseh fought together with British forces that, in the area of the <a href="/wiki/Great_Lakes" title="Great Lakes">Great Lakes</a>, occupied essentially today's territory of <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a>.</p>
<p>20 years later (1832), <a href="/wiki/Wabokieshiek" title="Wabokieshiek">Wabokieshiek</a>, the "<a href="/wiki/Ho-Chunk" title="Ho-Chunk">Winnebago</a> Prophet", after whom <a href="/wiki/Prophetstown,_Illinois" title="Prophetstown, Illinois">Prophetstown</a> has been named, (also called "White Cloud") claimed that British forces would support the Indians in the <a href="/wiki/Black_Hawk_War" title="Black Hawk War">Black Hawk War</a> against the United States as 20 years earlier (based on "visions"). They did not, and no longer he was considered a "prophet".</p>
<p>In 1869, the <a href="/wiki/Paiute" title="Paiute">Paiute</a> <a href="/wiki/Wodziwob" title="Wodziwob">Wodziwob</a> founded the <a href="/wiki/Ghost_Dance" title="Ghost Dance">Ghost Dance</a> movement. The dance rituals were an occasion to announce his visions of an earthquake that would swallow the whites. He seems to have died in 1872.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Northern_Paiute" class="mw-redirect" title="Northern Paiute">Northern Paiute</a> <a href="/wiki/Wovoka" title="Wovoka">Wovoka</a> claimed he had a vision during the <a href="/wiki/Solar_eclipse" title="Solar eclipse">solar eclipse</a> of January 1, 1889, that the Paiute dead would come back and the whites would vanish from America, provided the natives performed <a href="/wiki/Ghost_Dance" title="Ghost Dance">Ghost Dances</a>. This idea spread among other Native American peoples. The government were worried about a rebellion and sent troops, which lead to the death of <a href="/wiki/Sitting_Bull" title="Sitting Bull">Sitting Bull</a> and to the <a href="/wiki/Wounded_Knee_massacre" class="mw-redirect" title="Wounded Knee massacre">Wounded Knee massacre</a> in 1890.</p>
