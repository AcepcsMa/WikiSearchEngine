<p>The <b>New Zealand National Party</b> (<a href="/wiki/M%C4%81ori_language" title="Māori language">Māori</a>: <span lang="mi" xml:lang="mi"><i>Rōpū Nāhinara o Aotearoa</i></span>),<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> shortened to <b>National</b> (<span lang="mi" xml:lang="mi"><i>Nāhinara</i></span>) or the <b>Nats</b>,<sup id="cite_ref-:0_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-6">[6]</a></sup> is a <a href="/wiki/Centre-right" class="mw-redirect" title="Centre-right">centre-right</a> <a href="/wiki/List_of_political_parties_in_New_Zealand" title="List of political parties in New Zealand">political party in New Zealand</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-centre-right_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-centre-right-3">[3]</a></sup> It is one of two <a href="/wiki/Major_party" title="Major party">major parties</a> in contemporary <a href="/wiki/Politics_of_New_Zealand" title="Politics of New Zealand">New Zealand politics</a>, alongside its historic rival, the <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand_Labour_Party" title="New Zealand Labour Party">New Zealand Labour Party</a>.</p>
<p>National is New Zealand's second-oldest extant political party,<sup id="cite_ref-Raymond_2005_32_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Raymond_2005_32-8">[8]</a></sup> originating in 1936 with the merger of the two main conservative parties, <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand_Reform_Party" title="New Zealand Reform Party">Reform</a> and <a href="/wiki/United_Party_(New_Zealand)" title="United Party (New Zealand)">United</a>. They had previously formed <a href="/wiki/United%E2%80%93Reform_Coalition" title="United–Reform Coalition">a coalition</a> against the growing <a href="/wiki/Labour_movement" title="Labour movement">labour movement</a>. National has governed for five periods in the course of the 20th and 21st centuries—it has held office in New Zealand for more years than any other party.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Historically, the party's policy platform had shifted from moderate <a href="/wiki/Economic_liberalism" title="Economic liberalism">economic liberalism</a> to increased emphasis on state interventionism under the <a href="/wiki/Third_National_Government_of_New_Zealand" title="Third National Government of New Zealand">Third National Government</a> (1975 to 1984).<sup id="cite_ref-Scharpf-Schmidt_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Scharpf-Schmidt-11">[11]</a></sup> Following the <a href="/wiki/Rogernomics" title="Rogernomics">free-market reforms</a> of the rival <a href="/wiki/Fourth_Labour_Government_of_New_Zealand" title="Fourth Labour Government of New Zealand">Labour Government</a> in the 1980s, National has again advocated <a href="/wiki/Free_enterprise" class="mw-redirect" title="Free enterprise">free enterprise</a>, reduction of taxes, and individual rights.</p>
<p>Since <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand_general_election,_2008" title="New Zealand general election, 2008">November 2008</a>, National has been the largest party in <a href="/wiki/Minority_government" title="Minority government">minority governments</a> with support from the centrist <a href="/wiki/United_Future" title="United Future">United Future</a>, the classical-liberal <a href="/wiki/ACT_New_Zealand" title="ACT New Zealand">ACT Party</a> and the indigenous-rights-based <a href="/wiki/M%C4%81ori_Party" title="Māori Party">Māori Party</a>. With 47% of votes in the <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand_general_election,_2014" title="New Zealand general election, 2014">2014 general election</a>, the party won 59 of the 121 seats in the <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand_House_of_Representatives" title="New Zealand House of Representatives">House of Representatives</a>. <a href="/wiki/Bill_English" title="Bill English">Bill English</a> has been the party leader and <a href="/wiki/Prime_Minister_of_New_Zealand" title="Prime Minister of New Zealand">Prime Minister of New Zealand</a> since 12 December 2016.</p>

<p>The New Zealand National Party's principles has been characterised as encompassing both <a href="/wiki/Conservatism" title="Conservatism">conservative</a> and <a href="/wiki/Liberalism" title="Liberalism">liberal</a> tendencies (with the two in tension), and outlying <a href="/wiki/Populist" class="mw-redirect" title="Populist">populist</a> and <a href="/wiki/Libertarian" class="mw-redirect" title="Libertarian">libertarian</a> tendencies.<sup id="cite_ref-teara_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-teara-1">[1]</a></sup> The party's principles, last revised in 2003, seek "a safe, prosperous and successful New Zealand that creates opportunities for all New Zealanders to reach their personal goals and dreams".<sup id="cite_ref-teara_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-teara-1">[1]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Palffy_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Palffy-12">[12]</a></sup> It supports a limited <a href="/wiki/Welfare_state" title="Welfare state">welfare state</a> but says that work, merit, innovation and personal initiative must be encouraged to reduce unemployment and boost <a href="/wiki/Economic_growth" title="Economic growth">economic growth</a>. Historically, the party has supported a higher degree of <a href="/wiki/Protectionism" title="Protectionism">protectionism</a> and interventionism than it has in recent decades.<sup id="cite_ref-Gustafson_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gustafson-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>In a 1959 speech, party leader and Prime Minister <a href="/wiki/Keith_Holyoake" title="Keith Holyoake">Keith Holyoake</a> encapsulated the conservative and liberal principles of the National Party:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>We believe in the maximum degree of personal freedom and the maximum degree of individual choice for our people. We believe in the least interference necessary with individual rights, and the least possible degree of state interference.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/First_National_Government_of_New_Zealand" title="First National Government of New Zealand">First</a>, <a href="/wiki/Second_National_Government_of_New_Zealand" title="Second National Government of New Zealand">Second</a> and <a href="/wiki/Third_National_Government_of_New_Zealand" title="Third National Government of New Zealand">Third</a> National governments generally sought to preserve the economic and social stability of New Zealand, mainly keeping intact the high degree of protectionism and the strong welfare state built up by the <a href="/wiki/First_Labour_Government_of_New_Zealand" title="First Labour Government of New Zealand">First Labour Government</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Gustafson_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gustafson-13">[13]</a></sup> The last major interventionist policy was Prime Minister <a href="/wiki/Robert_Muldoon" title="Robert Muldoon">Robert Muldoon</a>'s massive infrastructure projects designed to ensure New Zealand's energy independence after the 1973 oil shock, "<a href="/wiki/Think_Big" title="Think Big">Think Big</a>".<sup id="cite_ref-Palffy_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Palffy-12">[12]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Fourth_National_Government_of_New_Zealand" title="Fourth National Government of New Zealand">Fourth National Government</a> mostly carried on the sweeping <a href="/wiki/Free-market" class="mw-redirect" title="Free-market">free-market</a> reforms of the <a href="/wiki/Fourth_Labour_Government_of_New_Zealand" title="Fourth Labour Government of New Zealand">Fourth Labour Government</a> known as "<a href="/wiki/Rogernomics" title="Rogernomics">Rogernomics</a>", after Labour's finance minister Sir <a href="/wiki/Roger_Douglas" title="Roger Douglas">Roger Douglas</a>. The corporatisation and sale of numerous state-owned enterprises, the abolishment of collective bargaining and major government spending cuts were introduced under the Fourth National Government, policies that were popularly known as "<a href="/wiki/Ruthanasia" title="Ruthanasia">Ruthanasia</a>" (National's finance minister at the time was <a href="/wiki/Ruth_Richardson" title="Ruth Richardson">Ruth Richardson</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>Following the moderate <a href="/wiki/Fifth_Labour_Government_of_New_Zealand" title="Fifth Labour Government of New Zealand">Fifth Labour Government</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Fifth_National_Government_of_New_Zealand" title="Fifth National Government of New Zealand">Fifth National Government</a> took power in 2008 under <a href="/wiki/John_Key" title="John Key">John Key</a>. Historically, the National Party has tended to take a <a href="/wiki/Social_conservative" class="mw-redirect" title="Social conservative">social conservative</a> position on most political issues, though more recently in certain <a href="/wiki/Hot-button_issue" class="mw-redirect" title="Hot-button issue">hot-button issues</a> the party has taken a more <a href="/wiki/Social_liberal" class="mw-redirect" title="Social liberal">social liberal</a> stance. For instance, they extended free <a href="/wiki/General_practitioner" title="General practitioner">general practitioner</a> visits to children under 13 as part of their 2014 election package, as well as extending paid parental leave by two weeks to 16 weeks.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-6">[6]</a></sup> The National parliamentary caucus was split on the issue of <a href="/wiki/Same-sex_marriage_in_New_Zealand" title="Same-sex marriage in New Zealand">same-sex marriage</a> in 2014, with Key publicly supporting gay rights.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the most recent general election, in 2014, the National Party ran a campaign focusing on stability.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_6-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-6">[6]</a></sup> They promised to limit new spending every year (to $1.5 billion), to not introduce any new taxes, and to improve the performance of public service organisations by setting performance-based 'Public Service Targets'. They also campaigned on the possibility of moderate tax cuts within the next few years and are generally in favour of free-trade agreements such as the <a href="/wiki/Trans-Pacific_Partnership" title="Trans-Pacific Partnership">Trans-Pacific Partnership</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Formation">Formation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=New_Zealand_National_Party&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Formation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The National Party was formed in May 1936, but its roots go considerably further back. The party came about as the result of a merger between the <a href="/wiki/United_Party_(New_Zealand)" title="United Party (New Zealand)">United Party</a> (known as the <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand_Liberal_Party" title="New Zealand Liberal Party">Liberal Party</a> until 1927, except for a short period between 1925 and 1927 when it used the name "National Party") and the <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand_Reform_Party" title="New Zealand Reform Party">Reform Party</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Raymond_2005_32_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Raymond_2005_32-8">[8]</a></sup> The United Party gained its main support from the cities, and drew upon <a href="/wiki/Business" title="Business">businesses</a> for money and upon <a href="/wiki/Middle_class" title="Middle class">middle class</a> electors for votes,<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> while the Reform Party had a <a href="/wiki/Rural" class="mw-redirect" title="Rural">rural</a> base and received substantial support from <a href="/wiki/Farmer" title="Farmer">farmers</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> who then formed a substantial proportion of the population.</p>

<p>Historically, the Liberal and Reform parties had competed against each other, but from 1931 until 1935 a <a href="/wiki/United%E2%80%93Reform_Coalition" title="United–Reform Coalition">United–Reform Coalition</a> held power in New Zealand.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> The coalition went into the <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand_general_election,_1935" title="New Zealand general election, 1935">1935 election</a> under the title of the "National Political Federation", a name adopted to indicate that the grouping intended to represent New Zealanders from all backgrounds (in contrast to the previous situation, where United served city-dwellers and Reform served farmers). However, because of the effects of the <a href="/wiki/Great_Depression" title="Great Depression">Great Depression</a> and a perception that the existing coalition government had handled the situation poorly, the National Political Federation lost heavily in 1935 to the <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand_Labour_Party" title="New Zealand Labour Party">Labour Party</a>, the rise of which had prompted the alliance. The two parties were cut down to 19 seats between them. Another factor was a third party, the <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand_Democrat_Party_(1934)" title="New Zealand Democrat Party (1934)">Democrat Party</a> formed by <a href="/wiki/Albert_Davy" title="Albert Davy">Albert Davy</a>, a former organiser for the coalition who disapproved of the "socialist" measures that the coalition had introduced. The new party split the conservative vote and aided Labour's victory.</p>
<p>In hopes of countering Labour's rise, United and Reform decided to turn their alliance into a single party. This party, the New Zealand National Party, was formed at a meeting held in <a href="/wiki/Wellington" title="Wellington">Wellington</a> on 13 and 14 May 1936. Erstwhile members of the United and Reform parties made up the bulk of the new party. The United Party's last leader, <a href="/wiki/George_Forbes_(New_Zealand_politician)" title="George Forbes (New Zealand politician)">George Forbes</a>, Prime Minister from 1930 until 1935, opened the conference; he served as Leader of the Opposition from May until November, when former Reform MP <a href="/wiki/Adam_Hamilton" title="Adam Hamilton">Adam Hamilton</a> was elected the first leader. Hamilton led the party into its first election in 1938. He got the top job primarily because of a compromise between Forbes and Reform leader <a href="/wiki/Gordon_Coates" title="Gordon Coates">Gordon Coates</a>, neither of whom wished to serve under the other. Hamilton, however, failed to counter Labour's popular <a href="/wiki/Prime_Minister_of_New_Zealand" title="Prime Minister of New Zealand">Prime Minister</a>, <a href="/wiki/Michael_Joseph_Savage" title="Michael Joseph Savage">Michael Joseph Savage</a> effectively. Because of this, perceptions that he remained too much under the control of Coates and because he lacked real support from his party colleagues, Hamilton failed to prevent Labour's re-election in <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand_general_election,_1938" title="New Zealand general election, 1938">1938</a>. In 1940 <a href="/wiki/Sidney_Holland" title="Sidney Holland">Sidney Holland</a> replaced Hamilton. <a href="/wiki/William_Polson" title="William Polson">William Polson</a> "acted effectively as Holland's deputy" (Gustafson). One former Reform MP <a href="/wiki/Herbert_Kyle" title="Herbert Kyle">Herbert Kyle</a> resigned in 1942 in protest at the "autocratic" behaviour of Holland and the new party organisation.</p>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand_general_election,_1943" title="New Zealand general election, 1943">1943 election</a> Labour's majority was reduced, but it remained in power. In the <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand_general_election,_1946" title="New Zealand general election, 1946">1946 election</a>, National also failed to unseat Labour. However, in the <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand_general_election,_1949" title="New Zealand general election, 1949">1949 election</a>, thirteen years after the party's foundation, National finally won power, and Holland became Prime Minister.</p>
<h3><span id="First_National_Government_(1949–1957)"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="First_National_Government_.281949.E2.80.931957.29">First National Government (1949–1957)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=New_Zealand_National_Party&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: First National Government (1949–1957)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/First_National_Government_of_New_Zealand" title="First National Government of New Zealand">First National Government of New Zealand</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Sidney_George_Holland_(1953).jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1c/Sidney_George_Holland_%281953%29.jpg/150px-Sidney_George_Holland_%281953%29.jpg" width="150" height="196" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1c/Sidney_George_Holland_%281953%29.jpg/225px-Sidney_George_Holland_%281953%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1c/Sidney_George_Holland_%281953%29.jpg/300px-Sidney_George_Holland_%281953%29.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2636" data-file-height="3445" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Sidney_George_Holland_(1953).jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Sidney Holland was the first National Prime Minister, 1949–1957</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>In 1949 National had campaigned on "the private ownership of production, distribution and exchange". Once in power the new Holland Government proved decidedly administratively conservative, retaining, for instance, the <a href="/wiki/Welfare_state" title="Welfare state">welfare state</a> set up by the previous Labour Government; though National gained, and has largely kept, a reputation for showing more favour to farmers and to <a href="/wiki/Business" title="Business">business</a> than did the Labour Party.</p>
<p>In 1951 the <a href="/wiki/1951_New_Zealand_waterfront_dispute" title="1951 New Zealand waterfront dispute">Waterfront Dispute</a> broke out, lasting 151 days. The National government stepped into the conflict, acting in opposition to the maritime <a href="/wiki/Trade_union" title="Trade union">unions</a>. Holland also used this opportunity to call the 1951 <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand_general_election,_1951" title="New Zealand general election, 1951">snap election</a>. Campaigning on an <a href="/wiki/Anti-communism" title="Anti-communism">anti-Communist</a> platform and exploiting the Labour Opposition's apparent indecisiveness, National returned with an increased majority, gaining 54 parliamentary seats out of 80.</p>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand_general_election,_1954" title="New Zealand general election, 1954">1954 election</a>, National was elected to a third term, though losing some of its seats. Towards the end of his third term, however, Holland became increasingly ill, and stepped down from the leadership shortly before the general election in 1957. <a href="/wiki/Keith_Holyoake" title="Keith Holyoake">Keith Holyoake</a>, the party's long-standing deputy leader, took Holland's place. Holyoake, however, had insufficient time to establish himself in the public mind as Prime Minister, and lost in the <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand_general_election,_1957" title="New Zealand general election, 1957">election later that year</a> to Labour, then led by <a href="/wiki/Walter_Nash" title="Walter Nash">Walter Nash</a>.</p>
<h3><span id="Second_National_Government_(1960–1972)"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Second_National_Government_.281960.E2.80.931972.29">Second National Government (1960–1972)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=New_Zealand_National_Party&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Second National Government (1960–1972)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Second_National_Government_of_New_Zealand" title="Second National Government of New Zealand">Second National Government of New Zealand</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Keith_Holyoake.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/44/Keith_Holyoake.jpg/150px-Keith_Holyoake.jpg" width="150" height="196" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/44/Keith_Holyoake.jpg/225px-Keith_Holyoake.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/44/Keith_Holyoake.jpg/300px-Keith_Holyoake.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3829" data-file-height="5000" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Keith_Holyoake.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Keith Holyoake, Prime Minister 1957 and 1960–1972</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Nash's government became very unpopular as Labour acquired a reputation for poor economic management, and much of the public saw its <a href="/wiki/Black_Budget_(New_Zealand)" title="Black Budget (New Zealand)">1958 Budget</a>, known since as the "Black Budget", as miserly.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> After only one term in office, Labour suffered defeat at the hands of Holyoake and the National Party in the <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand_general_election,_1960" title="New Zealand general election, 1960">elections of 1960</a>.</p>
<p>Holyoake's government lasted twelve years, the party gaining re-election three times (in <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand_general_election,_1963" title="New Zealand general election, 1963">1963</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand_general_election,_1966" title="New Zealand general election, 1966">1966</a>, and <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand_general_election,_1969" title="New Zealand general election, 1969">1969</a>). However, this period <a href="/wiki/Social_Credit_Party_(New_Zealand)" title="Social Credit Party (New Zealand)">Social Credit</a> arose, which broke the National/Labour duopoly in parliament, winning former National seats from 1966. Holyoake retired from the premiership and from the party leadership at the beginning of 1972, and his deputy, <a href="/wiki/Jack_Marshall" title="Jack Marshall">Jack Marshall</a>, replaced him.</p>
<p>Marshall suffered the same fate as Holyoake. Having succeeded an experienced leader in an election-year, he failed to establish himself in time. Marshall had an added disadvantage; he had to compete against the much more popular and charismatic <a href="/wiki/Norman_Kirk" title="Norman Kirk">Norman Kirk</a>, then leader of the Labour Party, and lost the ensuing <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand_general_election,_1972" title="New Zealand general election, 1972">election</a>. Unpopular policies, including initiating clear felling of parts of the Warawara kauri forest, also needlessly alienated voters.{Adams 1980}</p>
<h3><span id="Third_National_Government_(1975–1984)"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Third_National_Government_.281975.E2.80.931984.29">Third National Government (1975–1984)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=New_Zealand_National_Party&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Third National Government (1975–1984)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Third_National_Government_of_New_Zealand" title="Third National Government of New Zealand">Third National Government of New Zealand</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Muldoon_26_June_1969.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/89/Muldoon_26_June_1969.jpg/150px-Muldoon_26_June_1969.jpg" width="150" height="198" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/89/Muldoon_26_June_1969.jpg/225px-Muldoon_26_June_1969.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/89/Muldoon_26_June_1969.jpg/300px-Muldoon_26_June_1969.jpg 2x" data-file-width="562" data-file-height="743" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Muldoon_26_June_1969.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Sir Robert Muldoon, Prime Minister 1975–1984</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Within two years the National Party removed Marshall as its parliamentary leader and replaced him with <a href="/wiki/Robert_Muldoon" title="Robert Muldoon">Robert Muldoon</a>, who had previously served as <a href="/wiki/Minister_of_Finance_(New_Zealand)" title="Minister of Finance (New Zealand)">Minister of Finance</a>. An intense contest between Kirk and Muldoon followed. Kirk became ill and died in office (1974); his successor, <a href="/wiki/Bill_Rowling" title="Bill Rowling">Bill Rowling</a>, proved no match for Muldoon, and in the <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand_general_election,_1975" title="New Zealand general election, 1975">1975 election</a>, National under Muldoon returned comfortably to power.</p>
<p>The Muldoon administration, which favoured <a href="/wiki/Economic_interventionism" title="Economic interventionism">interventionist</a> economic policies, arouses mixed opinions amongst the <a href="/wiki/Free-market" class="mw-redirect" title="Free-market">free-market</a> adherents of the modern National. <a href="/wiki/Bill_Birch" title="Bill Birch">Bill Birch</a>'s "<a href="/wiki/Think_Big" title="Think Big">Think Big</a>" initiatives, designed to invest public money in energy self-sufficiency, stand in contrast to the party's contemporary<sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=New_Zealand_National_Party&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup> views.<sup id="cite_ref-Scharpf-Schmidt_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Scharpf-Schmidt-11">[11]</a></sup> Muldoon's autocratic leadership style became increasingly unpopular with both the public and the party, and together with disgruntlement over economic policy led to an attempted leadership change in 1980. Led by ministers <a href="/wiki/Derek_Quigley" title="Derek Quigley">Derek Quigley</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jim_McLay" title="Jim McLay">Jim McLay</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Jim_Bolger" title="Jim Bolger">Jim Bolger</a>, the challenge (dubbed the "colonels' coup") against Muldoon aimed to replace him with <a href="/wiki/Brian_Talboys" title="Brian Talboys">Brian Talboys</a>, his deputy. However, the plan collapsed as the result of Talboys' unwillingness, and Muldoon kept his position.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:NewZealandNationalPartyOldLogo.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/9e/NewZealandNationalPartyOldLogo.png/170px-NewZealandNationalPartyOldLogo.png" width="170" height="117" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/9/9e/NewZealandNationalPartyOldLogo.png 1.5x" data-file-width="193" data-file-height="133" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:NewZealandNationalPartyOldLogo.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A former National Party logo</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Under Muldoon, National won three consecutive general elections in 1975, <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand_general_election,_1978" title="New Zealand general election, 1978">1978</a> and <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand_general_election,_1981" title="New Zealand general election, 1981">1981</a>. However, public dissatisfaction grew, and Muldoon's controlling and belligerent style of <a href="/wiki/Leadership" title="Leadership">leadership</a> became less and less appealing. In both the 1978 and 1981 elections, National gained fewer votes than the Labour opposition, but could command a small majority in Parliament because of the then-used <a href="/wiki/First_Past_the_Post" class="mw-redirect" title="First Past the Post">First Past the Post</a> electoral system.</p>
<p>Dissent within the National Party continued to grow, however, with rebel National MPs <a href="/wiki/Marilyn_Waring" title="Marilyn Waring">Marilyn Waring</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mike_Minogue" title="Mike Minogue">Mike Minogue</a> causing particular concern to the leadership, threatening National's thin majority in parliament. When, in 1984, Marilyn Waring refused to support Muldoon's policies on visits by nuclear-powered and nuclear-armed ships, Muldoon called a snap <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand_general_election,_1984" title="New Zealand general election, 1984">election</a>. Muldoon made the television announcement of this election while visibly <a href="/wiki/Drunkenness" class="mw-redirect" title="Drunkenness">inebriated</a>, and some believe<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2007)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> that he later regretted the decision to "go to the country". National lost the election to Labour under <a href="/wiki/David_Lange" title="David Lange">David Lange</a>.</p>
<h3><span id="Fourth_National_Government_(1990–1999)"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Fourth_National_Government_.281990.E2.80.931999.29">Fourth National Government (1990–1999)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=New_Zealand_National_Party&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Fourth National Government (1990–1999)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Jim_Bolger_at_press_conference_cropped.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bb/Jim_Bolger_at_press_conference_cropped.jpg/150px-Jim_Bolger_at_press_conference_cropped.jpg" width="150" height="201" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bb/Jim_Bolger_at_press_conference_cropped.jpg/225px-Jim_Bolger_at_press_conference_cropped.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bb/Jim_Bolger_at_press_conference_cropped.jpg/300px-Jim_Bolger_at_press_conference_cropped.jpg 2x" data-file-width="819" data-file-height="1099" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Jim_Bolger_at_press_conference_cropped.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Jim_Bolger" title="Jim Bolger">Jim Bolger</a>, Prime Minister 1990–1997</div>
</div>
</div>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Fourth_National_Government_of_New_Zealand" title="Fourth National Government of New Zealand">Fourth National Government of New Zealand</a></div>
<p>Shortly after this loss, the National Party removed Muldoon from the leadership. <a href="/wiki/Jim_McLay" title="Jim McLay">Jim McLay</a>, who had replaced Brian Talboys as deputy leader shortly before the election, became the new leader. McLay, an urban liberal with right wing views on economics, however, failed to restore the party's fortunes. In 1986 <a href="/wiki/Jim_Bolger" title="Jim Bolger">Jim Bolger</a> took over the leadership with the support of centrists within the party.</p>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand_general_election,_1990" title="New Zealand general election, 1990">1990 election</a> National defeated Labour in an electoral landslide and formed a new government under <a href="/wiki/Jim_Bolger" title="Jim Bolger">Jim Bolger</a>. However, the party lost some support from Muldoon era policy based conservatives when it continued the economic reforms which had ultimately led to the defeat of the previous Labour government—these policies, started by Labour Party Finance Minister <a href="/wiki/Roger_Douglas" title="Roger Douglas">Roger Douglas</a> and popularly known as <a href="/wiki/Rogernomics" title="Rogernomics">Rogernomics</a>, centred on the <a href="/wiki/Privatization" title="Privatization">privatization</a> of state assets and on the removal of <a href="/wiki/Tariff" title="Tariff">tariffs</a> and <a href="/wiki/Subsidy" title="Subsidy">subsidies</a>. These policies alienated traditional Labour supporters, who saw them as a betrayal of the party's social service based character, but did not appear to appease the membership base of the non-parliamentary party either, which still had a significant supporter base for the <a href="/wiki/Statism" title="Statism">statist</a> intervention style policies of the Muldoon Government.</p>
<p>Many more conservative and centrist National supporters preferred Muldoon's more authoritarian and interventionist policies over the free-market liberalism promoted by Douglas. However, the new National Party Finance Minister, <a href="/wiki/Ruth_Richardson" title="Ruth Richardson">Ruth Richardson</a>, strongly supported Rogernomics, believing that Douglas had not gone far enough. Her policies—dubbed "<a href="/wiki/Ruthanasia" title="Ruthanasia">Ruthanasia</a>"— encouraged two MPs to leave the National Party and form the <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand_Liberal_Party_(1992)" title="New Zealand Liberal Party (1992)">New Zealand Liberal Party (1992)</a>. Richardson's views also met with considerable opposition within the National Party Parliamentary Caucus and for a time caused damage to the party's membership base.</p>
<p>At the <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand_general_election,_1993" title="New Zealand general election, 1993">1993 election</a>, National was narrowly able to secure its position in government due partly to a strongly recovering economy, after its large majority disappeared and the country faced an election night <a href="/wiki/Hung_parliament" title="Hung parliament">hung parliament</a>—National one seat short of the required 50 seats to govern. With <a href="/wiki/Special_vote" title="Special vote">special votes</a> counted in the following days, National won <a href="/wiki/Waitaki_(New_Zealand_electorate)" title="Waitaki (New Zealand electorate)">Waitaki</a>, allowing it to form a government but requiring the election of a Speaker from the opposition benches (<a href="/wiki/Peter_Tapsell_(New_Zealand_politician)" title="Peter Tapsell (New Zealand politician)">Peter Tapsell</a> of the Labour Party) to hold a working majority in the House. At the same time as the election, however, a referendum took place which established the <a href="/wiki/Mixed-member_proportional_representation" title="Mixed-member proportional representation">MMP</a> electoral system for future use in New Zealand general elections. This would have a significant impact on New Zealand politics. Some National Party MPs defected to a new grouping, <a href="/wiki/United_New_Zealand" title="United New Zealand">United New Zealand</a> in mid-1995. And as a result of the new electoral mechanics, the <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand_First" title="New Zealand First">New Zealand First</a> party, led by former National MP and former Cabinet minister <a href="/wiki/Winston_Peters" title="Winston Peters">Winston Peters</a>, held the balance of power after the <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand_general_election,_1996" title="New Zealand general election, 1996">1996 election</a>. After a prolonged period of negotiation lasting nearly two months, in which New Zealand First played National and Labour off against each other (both parties negotiated complete coalition agreements), New Zealand First entered into a coalition with National.</p>
<p>Under the coalition agreement, Peters became <a href="/wiki/Deputy_Prime_Minister_of_New_Zealand" title="Deputy Prime Minister of New Zealand">Deputy Prime Minister</a> and had the post of <a href="/wiki/Treasurer" title="Treasurer">Treasurer</a> especially created by the Crown for him. New Zealand First extracted a number of other concessions from National in exchange for its support. The influence of New Zealand First angered many National MPs, particularly <a href="/wiki/Jenny_Shipley" title="Jenny Shipley">Jenny Shipley</a>.</p>

<div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Jenny_Shipley_2013_(crop).jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fe/Jenny_Shipley_2013_%28crop%29.jpg/150px-Jenny_Shipley_2013_%28crop%29.jpg" width="150" height="197" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fe/Jenny_Shipley_2013_%28crop%29.jpg/225px-Jenny_Shipley_2013_%28crop%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fe/Jenny_Shipley_2013_%28crop%29.jpg/300px-Jenny_Shipley_2013_%28crop%29.jpg 2x" data-file-width="375" data-file-height="492" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Jenny_Shipley_2013_(crop).jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Jenny_Shipley" title="Jenny Shipley">Jenny Shipley</a>, Prime Minister 1997–1999</div>
</div>

<p>When, in 1997, Shipley toppled <a href="/wiki/Jim_Bolger" title="Jim Bolger">Bolger</a> to become National's new leader, relations between National and its coalition partner deteriorated. After Shipley sacked Peters from <a href="/wiki/Cabinet_of_New_Zealand" title="Cabinet of New Zealand">Cabinet</a> in 1998, the New Zealand First party split into two groups and half the MPs followed Peters out of the coalition but the remainder broke away, establishing themselves as independents or as members of new parties of which none survived the 1999 election. From the latter group National gained enough support to continue in government with additional confidence support of <a href="/wiki/Alamein_Kopu" title="Alamein Kopu">Alamein Kopu</a> a defect Alliance List MP. The visibly damaged National Government managed to survive the parliamentary term, but lost the <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand_general_election,_1999" title="New Zealand general election, 1999">election</a> to Labour's <a href="/wiki/Helen_Clark" title="Helen Clark">Helen Clark</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Alliance_(New_Zealand_political_party)" title="Alliance (New Zealand political party)">Alliance</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Jim_Anderton" title="Jim Anderton">Jim Anderton</a>, who formed a coalition government.</p>
<h3><span id="Opposition_(1999–2008)"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Opposition_.281999.E2.80.932008.29">Opposition (1999–2008)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=New_Zealand_National_Party&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Opposition (1999–2008)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Shipley continued to lead the National Party until 2001, when <a href="/wiki/Bill_English" title="Bill English">Bill English</a> replaced her. English, however, proved unable to gain traction against Clark, and National suffered its worst-ever electoral defeat in the <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand_general_election,_2002" title="New Zealand general election, 2002">2002 election</a>, gaining only 27 of 120 seats.<sup id="cite_ref-2002_election_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2002_election-24">[24]</a></sup> Many hoped that English would succeed in rebuilding the party, given time, but a year later polling showed the party performing only slightly better than in the election. In October 2003 English gave way as leader to <a href="/wiki/Don_Brash" title="Don Brash">Don Brash</a>, a former governor of the <a href="/wiki/Reserve_Bank_of_New_Zealand" title="Reserve Bank of New Zealand">Reserve Bank</a> who had joined the National Parliamentary caucus in the 2002 election.</p>
<p>Under Brash, the National Party's overall popularity with voters improved markedly. Mostly, however, the party achieved this by "reclaiming" support from electors who voted for other centre-right parties in 2002. National's campaigning on race relations, amid claims of preferential treatment of <a href="/wiki/M%C4%81ori_people" title="Māori people">Māori</a>, and amid their opposition to Labour Party policy during the <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand_foreshore_and_seabed_controversy" title="New Zealand foreshore and seabed controversy">foreshore-and-seabed controversy</a>, generated considerable publicity and much controversy. Strong campaigning on a tax-cuts theme in the lead-up to the <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand_general_election,_2005" title="New Zealand general election, 2005">2005 election</a>, together with a consolidation of centre-right support, may have contributed to the National Party's winning 48 out of 121 seats in Parliament. National, however, remained the second-largest party in Parliament (marginally behind Labour, which gained 50 seats), and had fewer options for forming a coalition government. With the formation of a new Labour-dominated Government, National remained the major Opposition party. During 2004-2005, the National Party had made renewed efforts to attract social conservative voters, through adoption of <a href="/wiki/Pro-life" class="mw-redirect" title="Pro-life">pro-life</a> and anti-<a href="/wiki/Same-sex_marriage" title="Same-sex marriage">same-sex marriage</a> voting records</p>
<p>After the 2005 election defeat Don Brash's leadership of National came under scrutiny from the media, and political watchers speculated on the prospect of a leadership-challenge before the next <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand_general_election,_2008" title="New Zealand general election, 2008">general election due in 2008</a>. Don Brash resigned on 23 November 2006, immediately before the release of <a href="/wiki/Nicky_Hager" title="Nicky Hager">Nicky Hager</a>'s book <i><a href="/wiki/The_Hollow_Men_(book)" title="The Hollow Men (book)">The Hollow Men</a></i>, which contained damaging revelations obtained from private emails. <a href="/wiki/John_Key" title="John Key">John Key</a> became the leader of the National caucus on 27 November 2006. Key fostered a more "centrist" image, discussing issues such as <a href="/wiki/Child_poverty" title="Child poverty">child poverty</a>.</p>
<h3><span id="Fifth_National_Government_(2008–present)"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Fifth_National_Government_.282008.E2.80.93present.29">Fifth National Government (2008–present)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=New_Zealand_National_Party&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Fifth National Government (2008–present)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Fifth_National_Government_of_New_Zealand" title="Fifth National Government of New Zealand">Fifth National Government of New Zealand</a></div>

<p>On 8 November 2008 the National Party won 58 seats in the <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand_general_election,_2008" title="New Zealand general election, 2008">general election</a>. The Labour Party, which had spent three terms in power, conceded the election and Prime Minister <a href="/wiki/Helen_Clark" title="Helen Clark">Helen Clark</a> stepped down. Subsequently, John Key entered into talks with the <a href="/wiki/ACT_New_Zealand" title="ACT New Zealand">ACT Party</a> (5 seats), the <a href="/wiki/M%C4%81ori_Party" title="Māori Party">Māori Party</a> (5 seats) and <a href="/wiki/United_Future" title="United Future">United Future</a> (1 seat), in order to achieve majority support in Parliament. On 19 November the Governor-General swore in the new National-led government.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> In Key's first Cabinet he gave the ACT Party's <a href="/wiki/Rodney_Hide" title="Rodney Hide">Rodney Hide</a> and <a href="/wiki/Heather_Roy" title="Heather Roy">Heather Roy</a> ministerial portfolios outside Cabinet, and the Māori Party's <a href="/wiki/Tariana_Turia" title="Tariana Turia">Tariana Turia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pita_Sharples" title="Pita Sharples">Pita Sharples</a> the same. United Future leader <a href="/wiki/Peter_Dunne" title="Peter Dunne">Peter Dunne</a> retained his ministerial post outside Cabinet which he had held within the immediately preceding <a href="/wiki/Fifth_Labour_Government_of_New_Zealand" title="Fifth Labour Government of New Zealand">Labour Government</a>.</p>
<p>The government <a href="/wiki/Taxation_in_New_Zealand" title="Taxation in New Zealand">cut taxes</a> on all income; the top personal tax rate was lowered from 39% to 38% and then 33% in 2010.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>

<p>At the 26 November <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand_general_election,_2011" title="New Zealand general election, 2011">2011 general election</a>, National gained 47.31% of the party vote, the highest percentage gained by any political party since MMP was introduced, helped by a lower voter turnout and the misfortunes of its traditional support parties. However, a reduced wasted vote enabled the party to gain 59 seats in Parliament, only one more than 2008. National re-entered <a href="/wiki/Confidence_and_supply" title="Confidence and supply">confidence and supply</a> agreements with ACT and with United Future's Peter Dunne to form a minority government with 61 seats in the new 121-seat Parliament. National also re-entered a coalition agreement with the Māori Party for extra reinsurance, despite both parties having differing view on National's contentious plans to partially sell (or "extend the mixed ownership model to") four state-owned enterprises. This nearly led to a cancellation of the agreement in February 2012 over <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Waitangi_claims_and_settlements" title="Treaty of Waitangi claims and settlements">Treaty of Waitangi obligations</a> for the mixed ownership companies, and again in July 2012 over water rights. At the start of its second term, Key's government consisted of National (58 seats), with support from ACT Party (5), the Māori Party (5) and United Future's Peter Dunne.</p>
<p>The government introduced the "mixed ownership model" plan, in which the Government planned to reduce its share in <a href="/wiki/Genesis_Energy_Limited" title="Genesis Energy Limited">Genesis Energy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Meridian_Energy" title="Meridian Energy">Meridian Energy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mercury_Energy" title="Mercury Energy">Mighty River Power</a> and <a href="/wiki/Solid_Energy" title="Solid Energy">Solid Energy</a> from 100% to 51% and <a href="/wiki/Air_New_Zealand" title="Air New Zealand">Air New Zealand</a> from 74% to 51%, and <a href="/wiki/Privatization" title="Privatization">sell off</a> the remainder. The plans to sell down Solid Energy were later axed due to the company's poor financial position. <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand_asset_sales_referendum,_2013" title="New Zealand asset sales referendum, 2013">A citizens-initiated referendum</a> on the sell-downs returned a 67.3% vote in opposition (on a turnout of 45.1%).</p>
<p>The National Government won a third term at the <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand_general_election,_2014" title="New Zealand general election, 2014">2014 general election</a>. The National Party won 47.04% of the party vote, and increased its seats to 60. National resumed its confidence and supply agreements with ACT and United Future.</p>

<p>After serving Prime Minister for nine years, Key announced his resignation as the party leader on 5 December 2016. He stepped down as Prime Minister on 12 December.<sup id="cite_ref-resignation_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-resignation-27">[27]</a></sup> Key's deputy <a href="/wiki/Bill_English" title="Bill English">Bill English</a> was acclaimed as the party's new leader on 12 December 2016 after Health Minister <a href="/wiki/Jonathan_Coleman_(politician)" title="Jonathan Coleman (politician)">Jonathan Coleman</a> and Minister of Police <a href="/wiki/Judith_Collins" title="Judith Collins">Judith Collins</a> withdrew from the <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand_National_Party_leadership_election,_2016" title="New Zealand National Party leadership election, 2016">leadership election</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Organisation">Organisation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=New_Zealand_National_Party&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Organisation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>National features both regional and electorate-level organisational structures. National traditionally had a strongly decentralised organisation, designed to allow electorates and the five regions to appeal to the unique voter base in their area. However, in light of the <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand_general_election,_2002" title="New Zealand general election, 2002">2002 election result</a>, a review of the party organisation resulted in decisions to weaken the regional structure and to implement a more centralised structure in order to make the structure more 'appropriate' for the new <a href="/wiki/Mixed-member_proportional_representation" title="Mixed-member proportional representation">mixed member proportional</a> electoral system.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> The Party President (currently<sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=New_Zealand_National_Party&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup> Peter Goodfellow) heads the administration outside of National's <a href="/wiki/Caucus" title="Caucus">current sitting MPs</a>.</p>
<p>The party's youth wing, the <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand_Young_Nationals" title="New Zealand Young Nationals">Young Nationals</a>, commonly known as the Young Nats, has provided much political impetus as a <a href="/wiki/Ginger_group" title="Ginger group">ginger group</a>.</p>
<p>The Bluegreens are a group within National who help formulate <a href="/wiki/Green_politics" title="Green politics">green</a> environmental policy.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
