			<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Albrecht Dürer's House</h1>
									<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">

<p><b>Albrecht Dürer's House</b><sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> (<a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a>: <b>Albrecht-Dürer-Haus</b>) is a <a href="/wiki/Nuremberg" title="Nuremberg">Nuremberg</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Fachwerkhaus" class="mw-redirect" title="Fachwerkhaus">Fachwerkhaus</a></i> that was the home of <a href="/wiki/Northern_Renaissance" title="Northern Renaissance">German Renaissance</a> artist <a href="/wiki/Albrecht_D%C3%BCrer" title="Albrecht Dürer">Albrecht Dürer</a> from 1509 to his death in 1528. The House lies in the extreme north-west of Nuremberg's <a href="/wiki/Altstadt" title="Altstadt">Altstadt</a>, near the <i>Kaiserburg</i> section of the <a href="/wiki/Nuremberg_Castle" title="Nuremberg Castle">Nuremberg Castle</a> and the <i>Tiergärtnertor</i> of <a href="/wiki/City_walls_of_Nuremberg" title="City walls of Nuremberg">Nuremberg's city walls</a>.</p>
<p>The house was built around 1420. It has five stories; the bottom two have sandstone walls, while the upper stories are <a href="/wiki/Timber_framing" title="Timber framing">timber framed</a>; the entire structure is topped by a <a href="/wiki/Half-hip_roof" class="mw-redirect" title="Half-hip roof">half-hip roof</a>. In 1501, it was purchased by <a href="/wiki/Bernhard_Walther" title="Bernhard Walther">Bernhard Walther</a>, a merchant and prominent astronomer. Walter remodeled the house, adding small windows to the roof so that it could function as an observatory. Walther died in 1504, and Dürer purchased the house in 1509.</p>
<p>Since 1871 the Albrecht-Dürer-Haus has been a museum dedicated to Dürer's life and work. In a restoration of 1909, the large <a href="/wiki/Dormer" title="Dormer">dormer</a> on the east-facing roof was replaced. (Compare <a class="external text" href="http://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:ADH_Delsenbach.Det.jpg">the earliest known engraving showing the house, from 1714</a> with <a class="external text" href="http://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:N%C3%BCrnberg_Albrecht-D%C3%BCrer-Haus_002.jpg">this photograph taken between 1860 and 1875</a> with <a class="external text" href="http://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:Nuernberg_Ansichtskarte_016.jpg">this postcard from 1909.</a> ) In October 1944, it took <a href="/wiki/File:Bundesarchiv_Bild_183-J27844,_N%C3%BCrnberg,_zerst%C3%B6rtes_Albrecht-D%C3%BCrer-Haus.jpg" title="File:Bundesarchiv Bild 183-J27844, Nürnberg, zerstörtes Albrecht-Dürer-Haus.jpg">significant damage from Allied bombing</a>. It was rebuilt by 1949, but did not reopen as a museum until 1971, Dürer's 500th birthday.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>The museum features installations of period furnishings, a re-creation of Dürer's workshop in which visitors can view demonstrations of <a href="/wiki/Printmaking" title="Printmaking">printmaking</a> techniques, and rotating exhibitions of drawings and prints by Dürer from the City of Nuremberg's Graphic Collection. Visitors can also receive a guided tour of the house from an actress playing Agnes Dürer, the wife of the artist.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
