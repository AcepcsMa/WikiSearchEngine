<p>The <b>Fleisch Bridge</b> (<a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a>: <span lang="de" xml:lang="de"><i>Fleischbrücke</i></span> or "Meat Bridge") or <b>Pegnitz Bridge</b> (<i>Pegnitzbrücke</i>) is a late <a href="/wiki/Renaissance" title="Renaissance">Renaissance</a> bridge in <a href="/wiki/Nuremberg" title="Nuremberg">Nuremberg</a>, <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a>. The bridge crosses the river <a href="/wiki/Pegnitz_(river)" title="Pegnitz (river)">Pegnitz</a> in the center of the old town, linking the districts St. Sebald and St. Lorenz along the axis of the main market. The single-arch bridge was built between 1596 and 1598 and replaced an earlier mixed construction of stone and wood which had been repeatedly destroyed by flood.</p>
<p>The Fleisch Bridge is notable for several technical features that were advanced for its time. These include an unusual large width of 15.3 m, and a clear span of 27 m which made it the largest masonry bridge arch in Germany at the time of its construction. With a rise of only 4.2 m, the arch features a span-to-rise ratio of 6.4 to 1, giving the bridge an almost unprecedented flat profile.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>This, however, came at the cost of high lateral thrusts even for a segmental arch bridge. This problem was solved by a particularly innovative construction of the <a href="/wiki/Abutment" title="Abutment">abutments</a> which were built onto 2000 wooden piles, 400 of which were rammed obliquely into the grounds. A very similar arrangement of the abutments had also been implemented slightly earlier at the <a href="/wiki/Rialto_Bridge" title="Rialto Bridge">Rialto bridge</a>, leading to speculations about a technology transfer from <a href="/wiki/Venice" title="Venice">Venice</a>, with which Nuremberg shared close trade links. A recent in-depth research, however, stresses the originality of the Fleisch Bridge on grounds of technical differences between the two bridges.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Fleisch Bridge has practically remained unchanged since the addition of a portal in 1599 and survived <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a> almost unscathed. A <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> inscription at the portal reads: <i>Omnia habent ortus suaque in crementa sed ecce quem cernis nunquam bos fuit hic Vitulus.</i> ("All things have a beginning and grow, but the ox upon whom you now look was never a calf.")</p>
