<p>The <b>Frauenkirche</b> ("Church of Our Lady") is a church in <a href="/wiki/Nuremberg" title="Nuremberg">Nuremberg</a>, <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a>. It stands on the eastern side of the main market. An example of <a href="/wiki/Brick_Gothic" title="Brick Gothic">brick Gothic</a> architecture, it was built on the initiative of <a href="/wiki/Charles_IV,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Charles IV, Holy Roman Emperor">Charles IV, Holy Roman Emperor</a> between 1352 and 1362. The church contains many sculptures, some of them heavily restored. Numerous works of art from the <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">Middle Ages</a> are kept in the church, such as the so-called Tucher Altar (c. 1440, originally the high altar of the <a href="/wiki/Augustinian" class="mw-redirect" title="Augustinian">Augustinian</a> church of <a href="/wiki/St._Vitus" class="mw-redirect" title="St. Vitus">St. Vitus</a>), and two monuments by <a href="/wiki/Adam_Kraft" title="Adam Kraft">Adam Kraft</a> (c. 1498).</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Frauenkirche,_Nuremberg&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Iglesia_de_Nuestra_Se%C3%B1ora,_N%C3%BAremberg,_Alemania,_2013-03-16,_DD_05.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/18/Iglesia_de_Nuestra_Se%C3%B1ora%2C_N%C3%BAremberg%2C_Alemania%2C_2013-03-16%2C_DD_05.jpg/220px-Iglesia_de_Nuestra_Se%C3%B1ora%2C_N%C3%BAremberg%2C_Alemania%2C_2013-03-16%2C_DD_05.jpg" width="220" height="330" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/18/Iglesia_de_Nuestra_Se%C3%B1ora%2C_N%C3%BAremberg%2C_Alemania%2C_2013-03-16%2C_DD_05.jpg/330px-Iglesia_de_Nuestra_Se%C3%B1ora%2C_N%C3%BAremberg%2C_Alemania%2C_2013-03-16%2C_DD_05.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/18/Iglesia_de_Nuestra_Se%C3%B1ora%2C_N%C3%BAremberg%2C_Alemania%2C_2013-03-16%2C_DD_05.jpg/440px-Iglesia_de_Nuestra_Se%C3%B1ora%2C_N%C3%BAremberg%2C_Alemania%2C_2013-03-16%2C_DD_05.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3292" data-file-height="4938" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Iglesia_de_Nuestra_Se%C3%B1ora,_N%C3%BAremberg,_Alemania,_2013-03-16,_DD_05.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Interior of the church</div>

<p>The church was built in the grand market, in place of the former <a href="/wiki/Jewish" class="mw-redirect" title="Jewish">Jewish</a> <a href="/wiki/Synagogue" title="Synagogue">synagogue</a>, which was destroyed during the <a href="/wiki/Pogrom" title="Pogrom">pogrom</a> of 1349 (which followed an outbreak of <a href="/wiki/Black_Death" title="Black Death">Black Death</a>). The architect was probably <a href="/wiki/Peter_Parler" title="Peter Parler">Peter Parler</a>. Charles IV wanted to use the Frauenkirche for imperial ceremonies, which is reflected in the porch with the balcony, and in the fact that the church is relatively unadorned except for the <a href="/wiki/Coats_of_arms" class="mw-redirect" title="Coats of arms">coats of arms</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Holy_Roman_Empire" title="Holy Roman Empire">Holy Roman Empire</a>, the seven <a href="/wiki/Prince-elector" title="Prince-elector">Electors</a>, the town of Nuremberg, and the city of <a href="/wiki/Rome" title="Rome">Rome</a>, where the <a href="/wiki/Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Holy Roman Emperor">Holy Roman Emperors</a> were <a href="/wiki/Coronation_of_the_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Coronation of the Holy Roman Emperor">crowned</a>.</p>
<p>Charles IV's son <a href="/wiki/Wenceslaus_IV_of_Bohemia" title="Wenceslaus IV of Bohemia">Wenceslas</a> was <a href="/wiki/Baptism" title="Baptism">baptized</a> in the church in 1361, on which occasion the <a href="/wiki/Imperial_Regalia" title="Imperial Regalia">Imperial Regalia</a>, including the imperial <a href="/wiki/Reliquaries" class="mw-redirect" title="Reliquaries">reliquaries</a>, were displayed to the people. Beginning in 1423, the Imperial Regalia was kept permanently in Nuremberg and displayed to the people once a year on a special wooden platform constructed for that purpose.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Architecture">Architecture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Frauenkirche,_Nuremberg&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Architecture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Frauenkirche is a <a href="/wiki/Hall_church" title="Hall church">hall church</a> with two <a href="/wiki/Aisle#Cathedral_architecture" title="Aisle">aisles</a> and a <a href="/wiki/Tribune_(architecture)" title="Tribune (architecture)">tribune</a> for the emperor. The church contains nine <a href="/wiki/Bay_(architecture)" title="Bay (architecture)">bays</a> supported by four <a href="/wiki/Columns" class="mw-redirect" title="Columns">columns</a>.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Triforium" title="Triforium">triforium</a>, named the Imperial Loft or <a href="/wiki/Michael_(archangel)" title="Michael (archangel)">St. Michael's</a> Loft, opens on to the <a href="/wiki/Nave" title="Nave">nave</a> by means of an <a href="/wiki/Arcade_(architecture)" title="Arcade (architecture)">arcade</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Arch" title="Arch">arches</a> of which are filled with floating <a href="/wiki/Tracery" title="Tracery">tracery</a>, consisting of three <a href="/wiki/Rosette_(design)" title="Rosette (design)">rosettes</a> supported by a segmental arch.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Narthex" title="Narthex">narthex</a> of the church contains <a href="/wiki/Tracery" title="Tracery">tracery</a>. All three sides of the narthex have <a href="/wiki/Portal_(architecture)" title="Portal (architecture)">portals</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Jambs" class="mw-redirect" title="Jambs">jambs</a> and <a href="/wiki/Archivolts" class="mw-redirect" title="Archivolts">archivolts</a> of which are decorated with sculptures. The <a href="/wiki/Gable" title="Gable">gable</a> contains many <a href="/wiki/Niche_(architecture)" title="Niche (architecture)">niches</a>, which used to house sculptures.</p>
<h2><span id="Männleinlaufen"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="M.C3.A4nnleinlaufen">Männleinlaufen</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Frauenkirche,_Nuremberg&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Männleinlaufen">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Uhrfrauenkirche.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/06/Uhrfrauenkirche.jpg/220px-Uhrfrauenkirche.jpg" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/06/Uhrfrauenkirche.jpg/330px-Uhrfrauenkirche.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/06/Uhrfrauenkirche.jpg/440px-Uhrfrauenkirche.jpg 2x" data-file-width="478" data-file-height="359" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Uhrfrauenkirche.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The Männleinlaufen.</div>
</div>

<p>One of the most notable features of the church is the <i>Männleinlaufen</i>, a mechanical clock that commemorates the <a href="/wiki/Golden_Bull_of_1356" title="Golden Bull of 1356">Golden Bull of 1356</a>. The clock was installed in the church in 1506. The <a href="/wiki/Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Holy Roman Emperor">Holy Roman Emperor</a> is shown seated with the <a href="/wiki/Prince-elector" title="Prince-elector">prince-electors</a> surrounding him.</p>
<p>The clock mechanism is activated at midday, when a bell is rung to start the sequence and is followed by the trumpeters and drummer. Then there is a procession of the electors around the figure of the Holy Roman Emperor.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Modern_history">Modern history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Frauenkirche,_Nuremberg&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Modern history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In <i><a href="/wiki/Triumph_of_the_Will" title="Triumph of the Will">Triumph of the Will</a></i>, <a href="/wiki/Leni_Riefenstahl" title="Leni Riefenstahl">Leni Riefenstahl</a>'s 1935 <a href="/wiki/Propaganda_film" title="Propaganda film">propaganda film</a> about the 1934 <a href="/wiki/Nuremberg_Rally" title="Nuremberg Rally">Nuremberg Rally</a>, the final scene consists of a <a href="/wiki/Military_parade" title="Military parade">military parade</a> through downtown Nuremberg, with <a href="/wiki/Adolf_Hitler" title="Adolf Hitler">Adolf Hitler</a> shown receiving salutes from <a href="/wiki/Nazi" class="mw-redirect" title="Nazi">Nazi</a> troops with the Frauenkirche in the background.</p>
<p>Since 1948, the balcony of the church, below the Männleinlaufen, is used for the opening ceremony of the <a href="/wiki/Christkindlesmarkt,_Nuremberg" title="Christkindlesmarkt, Nuremberg">Christkindlesmarkt</a>.</p>
<p>The church parish and the neighboring <a href="/wiki/St._Elizabeth,_Nuremberg" title="St. Elizabeth, Nuremberg">Parish of St. Elizabeth</a> are known collectively as the "Katholische Innenstadtkirche Nürnberg" (Catholic Downtown churches of Nuremberg).</p>
