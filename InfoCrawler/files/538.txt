<p>The <b>Nazi party rally grounds</b> (<a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a>: <span lang="de" xml:lang="de"><i>Reichsparteitagsgelände</i></span>, Literally: <i><a href="/wiki/Reich" title="Reich">Reich</a> Party Congress Grounds</i>) covered about 11 square kilometres in the southeast of <a href="/wiki/Nuremberg" title="Nuremberg">Nuremberg</a>, Germany. Six <a href="/wiki/Nuremberg_Rally" title="Nuremberg Rally">Nazi party rallies</a> were held there between 1933 and 1938.</p>

<p>The grounds included:</p>
<ul>
<li>the <i>Luitpoldarena</i>, a deployment area</li>
<li>the Luitpold Hall or "Old Congress Hall" (damaged during World War II, later demolished)</li>
<li>the <i>Kongresshalle</i> (Congress Hall) or <i>Neue Kongresshalle</i> (New Congress Hall) (unfinished)</li>
<li>the <i>Zeppelinfeld</i> (Zeppelin Field), another deployment area</li>
<li>the <i>Märzfeld</i> (March Field) (unfinished, later demolished), a deployment area for the <i>Wehrmacht</i> (army)</li>
<li>the <i><a href="/wiki/Deutsches_Stadion" title="Deutsches Stadion">Deutsche Stadion</a></i> (German stadium) (only foundations were built), which was to be the largest sports stadium in the world</li>
<li>the former <i>Stadion der Hitlerjugend</i> ("stadium of the <a href="/wiki/Hitler_Youth" title="Hitler Youth">Hitler Youth</a>", today <a href="/wiki/Frankenstadion" class="mw-redirect" title="Frankenstadion">Frankenstadion</a>)</li>
<li>the <i>Große Straße</i> ("Great Road"), a (never-used) parade road</li>
</ul>
<p>A "Haus der Kultur" (House of Culture) and a representative entrance portal towards the "Great Road" were planned at the northwestern end of the "Great Road", near the (new) Congress Hall.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Only <i>Zeppelinfeld</i>, <i>Luitpoldarena</i> and <i>Große Straße</i> were finished. The <i>Kongresshalle</i>, <i>Zeppelinfeld</i> and the <i>Große Straße</i> have been under monument protection since 1973 as significant examples of NS architecture.</p>
<p>The grounds were all designed by <a href="/wiki/Adolf_Hitler" title="Adolf Hitler">Hitler's</a> architect <a href="/wiki/Albert_Speer" title="Albert Speer">Albert Speer</a>, except for the Congress Hall, which was designed by <a href="/wiki/Ludwig_Ruff" title="Ludwig Ruff">Ludwig</a> and <a href="/wiki/Franz_Ruff" title="Franz Ruff">Franz Ruff</a>.</p>
<p>Today the whole site is a memorial, and parts are used as the <a href="/wiki/Norisring" title="Norisring">Norisring</a> motor racing track.</p>
<p>On 30 August 1933 Hitler declared Nuremberg the "City of the <i>Reichsparteitage</i> (Reich Party Congresses)".<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> The Party Congresses (<i>Reichsparteitage</i>) were a self-portrayal of the NS-state and had no programmatic task, simply demonstrating the unity of the nation. In a propagandistic way a relation was to be drawn between the NS movement and the glory of the medieval emperors and the meetings of the <a href="/wiki/Imperial_State" class="mw-redirect" title="Imperial State">Imperial States</a> which were held in Nuremberg.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Buildings">Buildings</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nazi_party_rally_grounds&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Buildings">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Luitpoldarena">Luitpoldarena</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nazi_party_rally_grounds&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Luitpoldarena">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Bundesarchiv_Bild_102-04062A,_N%C3%BCrnberg,_Reichsparteitag,_SA-_und_SS-Appell.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/eb/Bundesarchiv_Bild_102-04062A%2C_N%C3%BCrnberg%2C_Reichsparteitag%2C_SA-_und_SS-Appell.jpg/220px-Bundesarchiv_Bild_102-04062A%2C_N%C3%BCrnberg%2C_Reichsparteitag%2C_SA-_und_SS-Appell.jpg" width="220" height="316" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/eb/Bundesarchiv_Bild_102-04062A%2C_N%C3%BCrnberg%2C_Reichsparteitag%2C_SA-_und_SS-Appell.jpg/330px-Bundesarchiv_Bild_102-04062A%2C_N%C3%BCrnberg%2C_Reichsparteitag%2C_SA-_und_SS-Appell.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/eb/Bundesarchiv_Bild_102-04062A%2C_N%C3%BCrnberg%2C_Reichsparteitag%2C_SA-_und_SS-Appell.jpg/440px-Bundesarchiv_Bild_102-04062A%2C_N%C3%BCrnberg%2C_Reichsparteitag%2C_SA-_und_SS-Appell.jpg 2x" data-file-width="553" data-file-height="795" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Bundesarchiv_Bild_102-04062A,_N%C3%BCrnberg,_Reichsparteitag,_SA-_und_SS-Appell.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Reichsparteitag 1934, Luitpoldarena, "Totenehrung" (honouring of dead): SS-leader Heinrich Himmler, Adolf Hitler and SA-leader Viktor Lutze on the terrace in front of the "Ehrenhalle" (Hall of Honour); in the background: the crescent-shaped "Ehrentribüne" (literally: tribune of honour)</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Since 1906 a park named "Luitpoldhain" (literally translated: "Luitpold grove", named after <a href="/wiki/Luitpold,_Prince_Regent_of_Bavaria" title="Luitpold, Prince Regent of Bavaria">Luitpold, Prince Regent of Bavaria</a>) existed here. During the <a href="/wiki/Weimar_Republic" title="Weimar Republic">Weimar Republic</a> (1919–1933) the "Ehrenhalle" (Hall of Honour) was built in the park.</p>
<p>In 1933 Hitler replaced the park with a strictly-structured deployment area, the so-called "Luitpoldarena" with an area of 84,000 m².</p>
<p>During the party rallies, deployments of the SA and the SS with up to 150,000 people took place in this area. The central "relic" here was the "<a href="/wiki/Blutfahne" title="Blutfahne">Blutfahne</a>" (Blood flag), which was allegedly carried by the Beer Hall Putsch rebels and was soaked with the blood of one of them. At the "Blutfahnenweihe" (Blood flag consecration), new "Standarten" (flags) of SA- and SS-units were "consecrated" by touching their guidons with the "Blutfahne".</p>
<p>After 1945 the city of Nuremberg redesigned the area into a park again.<sup id="cite_ref-kubiss.de_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kubiss.de-5">[5]</a></sup> All buildings from the NS era were demolished. Only the half-round of the terraces of the main grandstand is recognizable.</p>
<h4><span id="Ehrenhalle_(Hall_of_Honour)"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Ehrenhalle_.28Hall_of_Honour.29">Ehrenhalle (Hall of Honour)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nazi_party_rally_grounds&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Ehrenhalle (Hall of Honour)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Ehrenhalle_nuremberg_2006.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c9/Ehrenhalle_nuremberg_2006.jpg/220px-Ehrenhalle_nuremberg_2006.jpg" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c9/Ehrenhalle_nuremberg_2006.jpg/330px-Ehrenhalle_nuremberg_2006.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c9/Ehrenhalle_nuremberg_2006.jpg/440px-Ehrenhalle_nuremberg_2006.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1536" data-file-height="1152" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Ehrenhalle_nuremberg_2006.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
"Ehrenhalle" (Hall of Honour) in the "Luitpoldhain" (park)</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The "Ehrenhalle" was built by the city of Nuremberg according to a plan of German architect Fritz Mayer. It was inaugurated in 1930, before the Hitler era during the Weimar Republic.<sup id="cite_ref-kubiss.de_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kubiss.de-5">[5]</a></sup> It is an arcaded hall with an adjacent cobbled stone terrace with two rows of pedestals for fire bowls. All fourteen pylons remain virtually intact and have not been ignited since the final Nazi party rally in September 1938. Originally the hall was to be a memorial site for the 9,855 soldiers from Nuremberg who were fallen in World War I.</p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Bundesarchiv_Bild_183-1987-0410-501,_N%C3%BCrnberg,_Reichsparteitag,_SA-Aufmarsch.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3e/Bundesarchiv_Bild_183-1987-0410-501%2C_N%C3%BCrnberg%2C_Reichsparteitag%2C_SA-Aufmarsch.jpg/220px-Bundesarchiv_Bild_183-1987-0410-501%2C_N%C3%BCrnberg%2C_Reichsparteitag%2C_SA-Aufmarsch.jpg" width="220" height="156" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3e/Bundesarchiv_Bild_183-1987-0410-501%2C_N%C3%BCrnberg%2C_Reichsparteitag%2C_SA-Aufmarsch.jpg/330px-Bundesarchiv_Bild_183-1987-0410-501%2C_N%C3%BCrnberg%2C_Reichsparteitag%2C_SA-Aufmarsch.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3e/Bundesarchiv_Bild_183-1987-0410-501%2C_N%C3%BCrnberg%2C_Reichsparteitag%2C_SA-Aufmarsch.jpg/440px-Bundesarchiv_Bild_183-1987-0410-501%2C_N%C3%BCrnberg%2C_Reichsparteitag%2C_SA-Aufmarsch.jpg 2x" data-file-width="799" data-file-height="565" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Bundesarchiv_Bild_183-1987-0410-501,_N%C3%BCrnberg,_Reichsparteitag,_SA-Aufmarsch.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
SA-deployment, Luitpoldarena, Reichsparteitag 1933; in the background: the "Ehrenhalle" (Hall of Honour)</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>During the Party Congress of 1929 the then unfinished "Hall of Honour" was used for the enactment of a cult of the dead by the National Socialists the first time. During the <a href="/wiki/Third_Reich" class="mw-redirect" title="Third Reich">Third Reich</a> the Nazis used the site primarily as a commemoration for the fallen soldiers of World War I and commemoration of the 16 dead of the "Hitlerputsch" (the so-called "Martyrs of the NS Movement") (<a href="/wiki/Beer_Hall_Putsch" title="Beer Hall Putsch">Beer Hall Putsch</a>) on 9 November 1923 in Munich. Hitler, accompanied by SS-leader Heinrich Himmler and SA-leader Viktor Lutze, strode through the arena over the 240 meters long granite path from the main grandstand to the terrace of the <i>Ehrenhalle</i> and showed the Nazi salute there. The ritual was the climax of the celebration.</p>
<h4><span id="Ehrentribüne_(Tribune_of_Honour)"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Ehrentrib.C3.BCne_.28Tribune_of_Honour.29">Ehrentribüne (Tribune of Honour)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nazi_party_rally_grounds&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Ehrentribüne (Tribune of Honour)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Opposite the "Ehrenhalle" the crescent-shaped "Ehrentribüne" (literally: tribune of honour) or main grandstand which measured 150&#160;m (490&#160;ft) long with 6&#160;m (20&#160;ft) gold eagles on each end was built. This structure, built by architect <a href="/wiki/Albert_Speer" title="Albert Speer">Albert Speer</a>, could seat 500 dignitaries and represented the first permanent structure built by the Nazis in Nuremberg. The "Ehrenhalle" and the "Ehrentribüne" were connected by a wide granite path. The "Ehrentribüne" was blown up in 1959/60.</p>
<h3><span id="Luitpoldhalle_(Luitpold_Hall)"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Luitpoldhalle_.28Luitpold_Hall.29">Luitpoldhalle (Luitpold Hall)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nazi_party_rally_grounds&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Luitpoldhalle (Luitpold Hall)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Nuernberg_Postkarte_Bayerische_Landesausstellung_1906_001.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/11/Nuernberg_Postkarte_Bayerische_Landesausstellung_1906_001.JPG/220px-Nuernberg_Postkarte_Bayerische_Landesausstellung_1906_001.JPG" width="220" height="140" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/11/Nuernberg_Postkarte_Bayerische_Landesausstellung_1906_001.JPG/330px-Nuernberg_Postkarte_Bayerische_Landesausstellung_1906_001.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/11/Nuernberg_Postkarte_Bayerische_Landesausstellung_1906_001.JPG/440px-Nuernberg_Postkarte_Bayerische_Landesausstellung_1906_001.JPG 2x" data-file-width="664" data-file-height="422" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Nuernberg_Postkarte_Bayerische_Landesausstellung_1906_001.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The later Luitpold Hall as machine hall, postcard from 1906</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The <i>Luitpold Hall</i> (Luitpoldhalle) (built 1906) had an outline of 180&#160;m ×&#160;50&#160;m (590&#160;ft ×&#160;160&#160;ft) featured 76 loudspeakers, 42 spotlights, the largest pipe organ in Germany and could seat 16,000 people. Dating back to the Bavarian Exposition, the former machine hall was renovated and first used by the Nazis for the party convention <i>party congress</i> of 1934. Its monumental neo classic facade featured a shell limestone facing with three enormous entrance portals. It was in this building during the <i>party congress</i> of 1935, that the <a href="/wiki/Nuremberg_laws" class="mw-redirect" title="Nuremberg laws">Nuremberg laws</a> were adapted which deprived <i>German Jews</i> and other minorities of their citizenship. The structure was severely damaged by allied bombs in early 1945 and a few years later replaced by a parking lot. The granite staircase leading to the building remains intact today.</p>
<h3><span id="Kongresshalle_(Congress_Hall)"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Kongresshalle_.28Congress_Hall.29">Kongresshalle (Congress Hall)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nazi_party_rally_grounds&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Kongresshalle (Congress Hall)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <b>Congress Hall</b> (Kongresshalle) is the biggest preserved national socialist monumental building and is landmarked. It was planned by the Nuremberg architects Ludwig and Franz Ruff. It was intended to serve as a congress centre for the NSDAP with a self-supporting roof and should have provided 50,000 seats. It was located on the shore of and in the pond <i>Dutzendteich</i> and marked the entrance of the rally grounds. The building reached a height of 39&#160;m (128&#160;ft) (a height of <i>70 m</i> was planned) and a diameter of 250&#160;m (820&#160;ft). The building is mostly built out of <a href="/wiki/Clinker_brick" title="Clinker brick">clinker</a> with a facade of granite panels. The design (especially the outer facade, among other features) is inspired by the <a href="/wiki/Colosseum" title="Colosseum">Colosseum</a> in Rome. The foundation stone was laid in 1935, but the building remained unfinished and without a roof. The building with an outline of an "U" ends with two head-buildings. Since 2001, the <i><a href="/wiki/Dokumentationszentrum_Reichsparteitagsgel%C3%A4nde" class="mw-redirect" title="Dokumentationszentrum Reichsparteitagsgelände">Dokumentationszentrum Reichsparteitagsgelände</a></i> (Documentation Center Nazi Party Rally Grounds), with the permanent exhibition <i>Faszination und Gewalt</i> (Fascination and Terror), has been located in the northern wing.<sup id="cite_ref-Documentation_Center_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Documentation_Center-6">[6]</a></sup> In the southern building, the <i>Serenadenhof</i>, the <i><a href="/wiki/Nuremberg_Symphony_Orchestra" title="Nuremberg Symphony Orchestra">Nuremberg Symphony Orchestra</a></i> have their domicile.</p>

<i>Große Straße</i> (Congress Hall in the background)</div>
</div>

<p>The great road is almost 2&#160;km (1.2&#160;mi) long and 40&#160;m (130&#160;ft) wide. It was intended to be the central <a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/axis" class="extiw" title="wiktionary:axis">axis</a> of the site and a <a href="/wiki/Parade" title="Parade">parade</a> road for the <a href="/wiki/Wehrmacht" title="Wehrmacht">Wehrmacht</a>. In its northwestern prolongation the road points towards <a href="/wiki/Nuremberg_Castle" title="Nuremberg Castle">Nuremberg Castle</a>. This was to create a relation between the role of Nuremberg during the Third Reich and its role during medieval times.</p>
<p>The road reached from the Congress Hall to the Märzfeld, the construction work started in 1935 and was finished in 1939 (it has never been used as a parade road, as due to the beginning of <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>, the last rally was held in 1938). The <a href="/wiki/Pavement_(material)" class="mw-redirect" title="Pavement (material)">pavement</a> was made of <a href="/wiki/Granite" title="Granite">granite</a> pavers in black and gray with edges of exactly 1.2&#160;m (3.9&#160;ft). A representative entrance portal and two pylons were planned at the northwestern end of the Great Road. Near the entrance area of the <i>German Stadion</i> a grandstand with a hall of pillars was planned for the government leaders and generals which were to take the salute on Wehrmacht formations which were to march in direction of the parade ground <i>Märzfeld</i>.</p>
<p>After the war, the road was used as a temporary <a href="/wiki/Airfield" class="mw-redirect" title="Airfield">airfield</a> for the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Army" title="United States Army">US Army</a>. Nowadays, it is used by the nearby Nuremberg fair and exhibition company as an occasional parking area for highly frequented fairs.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Zeppelinfeld">Zeppelinfeld</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nazi_party_rally_grounds&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Zeppelinfeld">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <b>Zeppelinfeld</b> (in English: <i>Zeppelin Field</i>) is located east of the Great Road. It consists of a large <a href="/wiki/Grandstand" title="Grandstand">grandstand</a> (<i>Zeppelinhaupttribüne</i>) with a width of 360 metres (390 yards) and a smaller stand. It was one of <a href="/wiki/Albert_Speer" title="Albert Speer">Albert Speer</a>'s first works for the <a href="/wiki/Nazi_party" class="mw-redirect" title="Nazi party">Nazi party</a> and was based upon the <a href="/wiki/Pergamon_Altar" title="Pergamon Altar">Pergamon Altar</a>. Its square piers are inspired by the work of Franco-American architect <a href="/wiki/Paul_Philippe_Cret" title="Paul Philippe Cret">Paul Philippe Cret</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> The grandstand is famous as the building that had the <a href="/wiki/Swastika" title="Swastika">swastika</a> blown from atop it in 1945, after Germany's fall in <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>. The name "Zeppelinfeld" or "Zeppelinwiese" refers to the fact that in August 1909 <a href="/wiki/Ferdinand_von_Zeppelin" title="Ferdinand von Zeppelin">Ferdinand Graf von Zeppelin</a> landed with one of his <a href="/wiki/Zeppelin" title="Zeppelin">airships</a> (LZ6) in this location.</p>
<p>From 1947 to 1995 the <a href="/wiki/Nurnberg_American_High_School" title="Nurnberg American High School">Nurnberg American High School</a>, a <a href="/wiki/DoDDS" class="mw-redirect" title="DoDDS">DoDDS</a> facility, used the field (called 'Soldiers Field') for high school football and American football practice. In the 1970s, the pillars were removed for safety reasons. Years of neglect had taken their toll. The rest of the stand is intact and used as the centerpiece of the <a href="/wiki/Norisring" title="Norisring">Norisring</a> motor racing track. The German leg of the traveling <a href="/wiki/Heavy_metal_(music)" class="mw-redirect" title="Heavy metal (music)">heavy metal</a> festival <a href="/wiki/Monsters_of_Rock#Germany" title="Monsters of Rock">Monsters of Rock</a> was held here twice during the 1980s. Another part of the grounds is home to a campground.</p>

<h3><span id="Deutsches_Stadion_(German_Stadium)"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Deutsches_Stadion_.28German_Stadium.29">Deutsches Stadion (German Stadium)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nazi_party_rally_grounds&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Deutsches Stadion (German Stadium)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Deutsches_Stadion" title="Deutsches Stadion">Deutsches Stadion</a></div>
<p>Along with his plans for the <i><a href="/wiki/Welthauptstadt_Germania" title="Welthauptstadt Germania">Welthauptstadt Germania</a></i> ("world capital Germania"), Albert Speer made the plans for the world's largest stadium which was to be located on the rally grounds. Derived from the <a href="/wiki/Panathenaic_Stadium" title="Panathenaic Stadium">Panathenaic Stadium</a> of <a href="/wiki/Athens" title="Athens">Athens</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> it would have offered 400,000 seats. It was to get the shape of a horseshoe; planned dimensions: length: 800 m, width: 450 m, height: 100 m, building area 350,000 m².</p>
<p>The laying of the foundation stone was on 9 July 1937. It was to be finished for the party congress in 1945. In 1938, the construction began with the excavation. It was stopped in 1939, but during the whole war, the casting pit had to be kept dry from entering ground water. After the war, the northern half of the pit filled up with the water and is today called <i>Silbersee</i> (silver lake), the southern half was used to deposit the debris of the destroyed downtown Nuremberg.</p>
<h3><span id="Märzfeld"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="M.C3.A4rzfeld">Märzfeld</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nazi_party_rally_grounds&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Märzfeld">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Märzfeld (literally: March Field, relating to the Roman <a href="/wiki/God_of_war" class="mw-redirect" title="God of war">god of war</a>, <i>Mars</i>) was to be a representation and parade ground for the <a href="/wiki/Wehrmacht" title="Wehrmacht">Wehrmacht</a>. It was located at the southern end of the "Große Straße" (Great road). Its dimensions were 955 by 610 meters (1,044&#160;yd ×&#160;667&#160;yd) or bigger than 80 football fields. The name of the huge deployment area was supposed to recall the recovery of military sovereignty of the <a href="/wiki/German_Reich" title="German Reich">German Reich</a> in March 1935. ("März" is the German name for the month "March." As in English, the name derives from the Roman warrior god <a href="/wiki/Mars_(mythology)" title="Mars (mythology)">Mars</a>. The name Märzfeld thus also alludes to the <a href="/wiki/Campus_Martius" title="Campus Martius">Campus Martius</a>, in Rome. The <a href="/wiki/Champs_de_Mars" class="mw-redirect" title="Champs de Mars">Champs de Mars</a> in <a href="/wiki/Paris" title="Paris">Paris</a> take their name from the Roman landmark as well.)</p>
<p>The construction, never completed, began in 1938 with plans calling for 24 granite towers each about 40 meters (130 feet) in height. Only eleven were ever completed and were demolished in 1966. Tribunes for about 160,000 people were planned around the field. On the central grandstand a group of colossal statures was planned: a goddess of victory and warriors.</p>
<p>Today the site is occupied by a residential district, <a href="/wiki/Langwasser" title="Langwasser">Langwasser</a>, which also extends to the south, into the area previously used for tent cities during the party rallies.</p>
<h3><span id="KdF-Stadt_(KdF-City)"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="KdF-Stadt_.28KdF-City.29">KdF-Stadt (KdF-City)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nazi_party_rally_grounds&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: KdF-Stadt (KdF-City)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the north-east of the rally grounds the <i>KdF-Stadt</i> (KdF-City) was built. In wooden exhibition halls regional products were presented and recreational events were accomplished during the party congresses. The NS-organisation <a href="/wiki/Strength_Through_Joy" title="Strength Through Joy">KdF</a> looked after workers during their free time.</p>
