<p><b>Amitabh Kumar "Amit" Singhal</b> (born September 1968) is the former <a href="/wiki/Vice_president#Hierarchy_of_vice_presidents" title="Vice president">senior vice president</a> of <a href="/wiki/Software_engineering" title="Software engineering">engineering</a> at <a href="/wiki/Uber_(company)" title="Uber (company)">Uber</a>, a position he took up in 2017, reporting to CEO <a href="/wiki/Travis_Kalanick" title="Travis Kalanick">Travis Kalanick</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Prior to this, he was senior vice president and software engineer at <a href="/wiki/Google_Inc." class="mw-redirect" title="Google Inc.">Google Inc.</a>. He was a Google Fellow, and was the head of Google's core ranking team.<sup id="cite_ref-bloomberg-interview_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bloomberg-interview-7">[7]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-singhal-knowledge-graph_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-singhal-knowledge-graph-8">[8]</a></sup> He left Google on 26 February 2016 as the Head of Google Search.<sup id="cite_ref-retirement_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-retirement-9">[9]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biography">Biography</span></h2>
<p>Born in <a href="/wiki/Jhansi" title="Jhansi">Jhansi</a>, a city in the state of <a href="/wiki/Uttar_Pradesh" title="Uttar Pradesh">Uttar Pradesh</a>, <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-thesis_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-thesis-11">[11]</a></sup> Singhal received a Bachelor of Engineering degree in <a href="/wiki/Computer_science" title="Computer science">computer science</a> from <a href="/wiki/IIT_Roorkee" class="mw-redirect" title="IIT Roorkee">IIT Roorkee</a> in 1989.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> He continued his computer science education in the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>, and received an <a href="/wiki/Master%27s_degree" title="Master's degree">M.S.</a> degree from <a href="/wiki/University_of_Minnesota_Duluth" title="University of Minnesota Duluth">University of Minnesota Duluth</a> in 1991.<sup id="cite_ref-umd_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-umd-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>Singhal continued his studies at <a href="/wiki/Cornell_University" title="Cornell University">Cornell University</a> in <a href="/wiki/Ithaca,_New_York" title="Ithaca, New York">Ithaca, New York</a> and received a <a href="/wiki/Ph.D." class="mw-redirect" title="Ph.D.">Ph.D.</a> degree in 1996.<sup id="cite_ref-umd_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-umd-13">[13]</a></sup> At Cornell, Singhal studied with <a href="/wiki/Gerard_Salton" title="Gerard Salton">Gerard Salton</a>, a pioneer in the field of <a href="/wiki/Information_retrieval" title="Information retrieval">information retrieval</a>, the academic discipline which forms the foundation of modern search. John Battelle, in his book "The Search" calls Gerard Salton "the father of digital search." He got interested in the problem of search in 1990 at the University of Minnesota Duluth. After getting a Ph.D. in 1996, Singhal joined <a href="/wiki/AT%26T_Labs" title="AT&amp;T Labs">AT&amp;T Labs</a> (previously a part of <a href="/wiki/Bell_Labs" title="Bell Labs">Bell Labs</a>), where he continued his research in information retrieval, speech retrieval and other related fields.<sup id="cite_ref-umd_13-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-umd-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Career">Career</span></h2>
<p>In 2000, he was recruited by friend <a href="/wiki/Krishna_Bharat" title="Krishna Bharat">Krishna Bharat</a> to join Google.<sup id="cite_ref-umd_13-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-umd-13">[13]</a></sup> Singhal ran Google's core search quality department where he and his team were responsible for the Google search algorithms. According to <i><a href="/wiki/New_York_Times" class="mw-redirect" title="New York Times">New York Times</a></i>, Singhal was the "master" of Google's ranking algorithm — the formulas that decide which Web pages best answer each user's question.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> As a reward for his rewrite of the search engine in 2001, Singhal was named a "Google Fellow".<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> Singhal served as the head of Google's core search ranking team<sup id="cite_ref-bloomberg-interview_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bloomberg-interview-7">[7]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-singhal-knowledge-graph_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-singhal-knowledge-graph-8">[8]</a></sup> until his retirement announced on 26 February 2016.<sup id="cite_ref-retirement_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-retirement-9">[9]</a></sup> It would later transpire that Singhal's departure from Google had occurred after a sexual harassment complaint against him, that an internal review had found "credible".<sup id="cite_ref-Swisher_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Swisher-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2017, he joined <a href="/wiki/Uber_(company)" title="Uber (company)">Uber</a> as SVP of Engineering, reporting to CEO Travis Kalanick, and with his fellow Google alum <a href="/w/index.php?title=Kevin_Thompson_(Engineer)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Kevin Thompson (Engineer) (page does not exist)">Kevin Thompson</a> operating as SVP of marketplace engineering.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> He was asked to leave in February 2017 after it emerged that he had not disclosed to Uber the sexual harassment complaint against him that had been responsible for him leaving Google.<sup id="cite_ref-Swisher_16-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Swisher-16">[16]</a></sup> Singhal's departure from Uber came at a time when Uber was receiving criticism and media attention after allegations by former employee Susan Fowler of a dysfunctional culture of sexual harassment at the company.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Honors_and_awards">Honors and awards</span></h2>
<p>In 2011 he was inducted as a Fellow of the <a href="/wiki/Association_for_Computing_Machinery" title="Association for Computing Machinery">Association for Computing Machinery</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-top50_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-top50-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>Fortune named Singhal one of the smartest people in tech.<sup id="cite_ref-fortunetop50_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fortunetop50-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2011, Singhal was given the Outstanding Achievement in Science and Technology Award at <a href="/wiki/The_Asian_Awards" title="The Asian Awards">The Asian Awards</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>He was elected Member of the <a href="/wiki/National_Academy_of_Engineering" title="National Academy of Engineering">National Academy of Engineering</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Quotes">Quotes</span></h2>
<p>He writes about the University of Minnesota Duluth:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>UMD was the turning point in my life. Studying Information Retrieval with Don Crouch and then Don recommending that I move to Cornell to study with Gerard Salton, is the main reason behind my success today. Don gave me the love for search, I have just followed my passion ever since.<sup id="cite_ref-umd_13-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-umd-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<div class="templatequotecite"><cite>— Amit Singhal</cite></div>
</blockquote>
