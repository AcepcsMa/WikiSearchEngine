<p>The two <b>Books of Kings</b>, originally a single book, are the eleventh and twelfth books of the <a href="/wiki/Hebrew_Bible" title="Hebrew Bible">Hebrew Bible</a> or <a href="/wiki/Old_Testament" title="Old Testament">Old Testament</a>. They conclude the <a href="/wiki/Deuteronomistic_history" class="mw-redirect" title="Deuteronomistic history">Deuteronomistic history</a>, a history of Israel also comprising the books of <a href="/wiki/Book_of_Joshua" title="Book of Joshua">Joshua</a> and <a href="/wiki/Book_of_Judges" title="Book of Judges">Judges</a> and the two <a href="/wiki/Books_of_Samuel" title="Books of Samuel">Books of Samuel</a>, which <a href="/wiki/Biblical_commentator" class="mw-redirect" title="Biblical commentator">biblical commentators</a> believe was written to provide a <a href="/wiki/Theology" title="Theology">theological</a> explanation for the <a href="/wiki/Siege_of_Jerusalem_(587_BC)" title="Siege of Jerusalem (587 BC)">destruction of the Kingdom of Judah</a> by Babylon in c. 586&#160;BCE and a foundation for a return from <a href="/wiki/Babylonian_captivity" title="Babylonian captivity">exile</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Sweeney_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sweeney-1">[1]</a></sup> The two books of Kings present a <a href="/wiki/History_of_ancient_Israel_and_Judah" title="History of ancient Israel and Judah">history of ancient Israel and Judah</a> from the death of King <a href="/wiki/David" title="David">David</a> to the release of <a href="/wiki/Jehoiachin" class="mw-redirect" title="Jehoiachin">Jehoiachin</a> from imprisonment in Babylon, a period of some 400 years (<abbr title="circa">c.</abbr><span style="white-space:nowrap;"> 960</span>&#160;– c.<span style="white-space:nowrap;"> 560&#160;BCE</span>).<sup id="cite_ref-Sweeney_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sweeney-1">[1]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Contents">Contents</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Books_of_Kings&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Contents">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p>In David's old age, <a href="/wiki/Adonijah" title="Adonijah">Adonijah</a> proclaims himself his successor but <a href="/wiki/Solomon" title="Solomon">Solomon</a>'s supporters arrange for David to proclaim Solomon as his successor, and so he comes to the throne after David's death.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> At the beginning of his reign he assumes God's promises to David and brings splendour to Israel and peace and prosperity to his people.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> The centrepiece of Solomon's reign is the building of the <a href="/wiki/Temple_of_Solomon" class="mw-redirect" title="Temple of Solomon">First Temple</a>: the claim that this took place 480 years after <a href="/wiki/The_Exodus" title="The Exodus">the Exodus</a> from Egypt marks it as a key event in Israel's history.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> At the end, however, he follows other gods and oppresses Israel.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>As a consequence of Solomon's failure to stamp out the worship of gods other than <a href="/wiki/Yahweh" title="Yahweh">Yahweh</a>, the kingdom of David is split in two in the reign of his own son <a href="/wiki/Rehoboam" title="Rehoboam">Rehoboam</a>, who becomes the first to reign over the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Judah" title="Kingdom of Judah">kingdom of Judah</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> The kings who follow Rehoboam in Jerusalem continue the royal line of David (i.e., they inherit the promise to David); in the north, however, dynasties follow each other in rapid succession, and the kings are uniformly bad (meaning that they fail to follow Yahweh alone). At length God brings the <a href="/wiki/Assyria" title="Assyria">Assyrians</a> to destroy the northern kingdom, leaving Judah as the sole custodian of the promise.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Hezekiah" title="Hezekiah">Hezekiah</a>, the 14th king of Judah, does "what was right in the eyes of the Lord" and institutes a far reaching religious reform, centralising sacrifice at the temple at Jerusalem and destroying the images of other gods. Yahweh saves Jerusalem and the kingdom from an invasion by Assyria. But <a href="/wiki/Manasseh_of_Judah" title="Manasseh of Judah">Manasseh</a>, the next king, reverses the reforms, and God announces that he will destroy Jerusalem because of this apostasy by the king. Manasseh's righteous grandson <a href="/wiki/Josiah" title="Josiah">Josiah</a> reinstitutes the reforms of Hezekiah, but it is too late: God, speaking through the prophetess <a href="/wiki/Huldah" title="Huldah">Huldah</a>, affirms that Jerusalem is to be destroyed.</p>
<p>God brings the <a href="/wiki/Neo-Babylonian_Empire" title="Neo-Babylonian Empire">Babylonians</a> against Jerusalem; Yahweh deserts his people, Jerusalem is razed and the Temple destroyed, and the priests, prophets and royal court are led into captivity. The final verses record how <a href="/wiki/Jehoiachin" class="mw-redirect" title="Jehoiachin">Jehoiachin</a>, the last king, is set free and given honour by the king of Babylon.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Textual_history">Textual history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Books_of_Kings&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Textual history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the original Hebrew Bible (the Bible used by Jews), First and Second Kings are a single book, as are the First and Second <a href="/wiki/Books_of_Samuel" title="Books of Samuel">Books of Samuel</a>. When this was translated into <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a> in the last few centuries BCE, Kings was joined with Samuel in a four-part work called the <a href="/wiki/Books_of_the_Kingdoms" title="Books of the Kingdoms">Book of Kingdoms</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Greek_Orthodox" class="mw-redirect" title="Greek Orthodox">Greek Orthodox</a> branch of Christianity continues to use the Greek translation (the <a href="/wiki/Septuagint" title="Septuagint">Septuagint</a>), but when a Latin translation (called the <a href="/wiki/Vulgate" title="Vulgate">Vulgate</a>) was made for the Western church, Kingdoms was first retitled the Book of Kings, parts One to Four, and eventually both Kings and Samuel were separated into two books each.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>Then, what it is now commonly known as <b>1 Samuel</b> and <b>2 Samuel</b> are called by <a href="/wiki/The_Vulgate" class="mw-redirect" title="The Vulgate">the Vulgate</a>, in imitation of <a href="/wiki/Septuagint" title="Septuagint">the Septuagint</a>, <b>1 Kings</b> and <b>2 Kings</b> respectively. What it is now commonly known as <b>1 Kings</b> and <b>2 Kings</b> would be <b>3 Kings</b> and <b>4 Kings</b> in old Bibles before the year 1516 such as the Vulgate and the Septuagint respectively.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> The division we know today, used by Protestant Bibles and adopted by Catholics, came into use in 1517. Some Bibles still preserve the old denomination, for example, <a href="/wiki/Douay_Rheims_bible" class="mw-redirect" title="Douay Rheims bible">Douay Rheims bible</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Deuteronomistic_history">The Deuteronomistic history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Books_of_Kings&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: The Deuteronomistic history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>According to Jewish tradition the author of Kings was <a href="/wiki/Jeremiah" title="Jeremiah">Jeremiah</a>, who would have been alive during the fall of Jerusalem in 586&#160;BCE.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> The most common view today accepts <a href="/wiki/Martin_Noth" title="Martin Noth">Martin Noth</a>'s thesis that Kings concludes a unified series of books which reflect the language and theology of the <a href="/wiki/Book_of_Deuteronomy" title="Book of Deuteronomy">Book of Deuteronomy</a>, and which biblical scholars therefore call the <a href="/wiki/Deuteronomistic_history" class="mw-redirect" title="Deuteronomistic history">Deuteronomistic history</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Noth argued that the History was the work of a single individual living in the 6th century BCE, but scholars today tend to treat it as made up of at least two layers,<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> a first edition from the time of <a href="/wiki/Josiah" title="Josiah">Josiah</a> (late 7th century BCE), promoting Josiah's religious reforms and the need for repentance, and (2) a second and final edition from the mid 6th century BCE.<sup id="cite_ref-Fretheim_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Fretheim-14">[14]</a></sup> Further levels of editing have also been proposed, including: a late 8th century BCE edition pointing to <a href="/wiki/Hezekiah" title="Hezekiah">Hezekiah</a> of Judah as the model for kingship; an earlier 8th century BCE version with a similar message but identifying <a href="/wiki/Jehu" title="Jehu">Jehu</a> of Israel as the ideal king; and an even earlier version promoting the House of David as the key to national well-being.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sources">Sources</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Books_of_Kings&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Sources">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The editors/authors of the Deuteronomistic history cite a number of sources, including (for example) a "<a href="/wiki/Acts_of_Solomon" title="Acts of Solomon">Book of the Acts of Solomon</a>" and, frequently, the "<a href="/wiki/Chronicles_of_the_Kings_of_Judah" title="Chronicles of the Kings of Judah">Annals of the Kings of Judah</a>" and a separate book, "<a href="/wiki/Chronicles_of_the_Kings_of_Israel" title="Chronicles of the Kings of Israel">Chronicles of the Kings of Israel</a>". The "Deuteronomic" perspective (that of the book of Deuteronomy) is particularly evident in prayers and speeches spoken by key figures at major transition points: Solomon's speech at the dedication of the Temple is a key example.<sup id="cite_ref-Fretheim_14-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Fretheim-14">[14]</a></sup> The sources have been heavily edited to meet the Deuteronomistic agenda,<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> but in the broadest sense they appear to have been:</p>
<ul>
<li>For the rest of Solomon's reign the text names its source as "the book of the acts of Solomon", but other sources were employed, and much was added by the redactor.</li>
<li>Israel and Judah: The two "chronicles" of Israel and Judah provided the chronological framework, but few details apart from the succession of monarchs and the account of how the <a href="/wiki/Temple_of_Solomon" class="mw-redirect" title="Temple of Solomon">Temple of Solomon</a> was progressively stripped as true religion declined. A third source, or set of sources, were cycles of stories about various prophets (<a href="/wiki/Elijah" title="Elijah">Elijah</a> and <a href="/wiki/Elisha" title="Elisha">Elisha</a>, <a href="/wiki/Isaiah" title="Isaiah">Isaiah</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ahijah_the_Shilonite" title="Ahijah the Shilonite">Ahijah</a> and <a href="/wiki/Micaiah" title="Micaiah">Micaiah</a>), plus a few smaller miscellaneous traditions. The conclusion of the book (2 Kings 25:18–21, 27–30) was probably based on personal knowledge.</li>
<li>A few sections were editorial additions not based on sources. These include various predictions of the downfall of the northern kingdom, the equivalent prediction of the downfall of Judah following the reign of <a href="/wiki/Manasseh_of_Judah" title="Manasseh of Judah">Manasseh</a>, the extension of <a href="/wiki/Josiah" title="Josiah">Josiah</a>'s reforms in accordance with the laws of <a href="/wiki/Deuteronomy" class="mw-redirect" title="Deuteronomy">Deuteronomy</a>, and the revision of the narrative from <a href="/wiki/Book_of_Jeremiah" title="Book of Jeremiah">Jeremiah</a> concerning Judah's last days.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Themes_and_genre">Themes and genre</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Books_of_Kings&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Themes and genre">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p>Kings is "history-like" rather than history in the modern sense, mixing legends, folktales, miracle stories and "fictional constructions" in with the annals, and its primary explanation for all that happens is God's offended sense of what is right; it is therefore more fruitful to read it as theological literature in the form of history.<sup id="cite_ref-Nelson.2C_pp.1-2_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Nelson.2C_pp.1-2-18">[18]</a></sup> The theological bias is seen in the way it judges each king of Israel on the basis of whether he recognises the authority of the <a href="/wiki/Temple_in_Jerusalem" title="Temple in Jerusalem">Temple in Jerusalem</a> (none do, and therefore all are "evil"), and each king of Judah on the basis of whether he destroys the "<a href="/wiki/High_place" title="High place">high places</a>" (rivals to the Temple in Jerusalem); it gives only passing mention to important and successful kings like <a href="/wiki/Omri" title="Omri">Omri</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jeroboam_II" title="Jeroboam II">Jeroboam II</a> and totally ignores one of the most significant events in ancient Israel's history, the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Qarqar" title="Battle of Qarqar">battle of Qarqar</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>The major themes of Kings are God's promise, the recurrent apostasy of the kings, and the judgement this brings on Israel:<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li>Promise: In return for Israel's promise to worship Yahweh alone, Yahweh makes promises to David and to Israel – to David, the promise that his line will rule Israel forever, to Israel, the promise of the land they will possess.</li>
<li>Apostasy: the great tragedy of Israel's history, meaning the destruction of the kingdom and the Temple, is due to the failure of the people, but more especially the kings, to worship Yahweh alone (Yahweh being the God of Israel).</li>
<li>Judgement: Apostasy leads to judgement. Judgement is not punishment, but simply the natural (or rather, God-ordained) consequence of Israel's failure to worship Yahweh alone.</li>
</ul>
<p>Another and related theme is that of prophecy. The main point of the prophetic stories is that God's prophecies are always fulfilled, so that any not yet fulfilled will be so in the future. The implication, the release of Jehoiachin and his restoration to a place of honour in Babylon in the closing scenes of the book, is that the promise of an eternal Davidic dynasty is still in effect, and that the Davidic line will be restored.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Chronology">Chronology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Books_of_Kings&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Chronology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Chronology_of_the_Bible" title="Chronology of the Bible">Chronology of the Bible</a></div>
<p>The standard Hebrew text of Kings presents an impossible chronology.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> To take just a single example, <a href="/wiki/Omri" title="Omri">Omri</a>'s accession to the throne of Israel in the 31st year of <a href="/wiki/Asa_of_Judah" title="Asa of Judah">Asa of Judah</a> (1 Kings 16:23) cannot follow the death of his predecessor <a href="/wiki/Zimri_(king)" title="Zimri (king)">Zimri</a> in the 27th year of Asa (1 Kings 16:15).<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> The Greek text corrects the impossibilities but does not seem to represent an earlier version.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> A large number of scholars have claimed to solve the difficulties, but the results differ, sometimes widely, and none has achieved consensus status.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Kings_and_2_Chronicles">Kings and 2 Chronicles</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Books_of_Kings&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Kings and 2 Chronicles">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/2_Chronicles" class="mw-redirect" title="2 Chronicles">2 Chronicles</a> covers much the same time-period as Kings, but it ignores the northern <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Israel_(Samaria)" title="Kingdom of Israel (Samaria)">Kingdom of Israel</a> almost completely, David is given a major role in planning the Temple, <a href="/wiki/Hezekiah" title="Hezekiah">Hezekiah</a> is given a much more far-reaching program of reform, and <a href="/wiki/Manasseh_of_Judah" title="Manasseh of Judah">Manasseh of Judah</a> is given an opportunity to repent of his sins, apparently to account for his long reign.<sup id="cite_ref-Sutherland_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sutherland-26">[26]</a></sup> It is usually assumed that the author of Chronicles used Kings as a source and re-wrote history as he would have liked it to have been.<sup id="cite_ref-Sutherland_26-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sutherland-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
