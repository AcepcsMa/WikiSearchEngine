<p><b>Federal elections</b> were held in Germany on Sunday, 24 September 2017, to elect the members of the 19th <a href="/wiki/Bundestag" title="Bundestag">Bundestag</a>. The new Bundestag will in turn elect a <a href="/wiki/Chancellor_of_Germany" title="Chancellor of Germany">Chancellor</a>, who must ordinarily command the support of an absolute majority of its members in order to form a new government.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Christian_Democratic_Union_(Germany)" class="mw-redirect" title="Christian Democratic Union (Germany)">Christian Democratic Union</a>/<a href="/wiki/Christian_Social_Union_of_Bavaria" class="mw-redirect" title="Christian Social Union of Bavaria">Christian Social Union</a> (<a href="/wiki/CDU/CSU" title="CDU/CSU">CDU/CSU</a>), led by <a href="/wiki/Angela_Merkel" title="Angela Merkel">Angela Merkel</a>, had maintained a double-digit lead over the <a href="/wiki/Social_Democratic_Party_of_Germany" title="Social Democratic Party of Germany">Social Democratic Party</a> (SPD) in <a href="/wiki/Opinion_polling_for_the_German_federal_election,_2017" title="Opinion polling for the German federal election, 2017">opinion polling</a> since the <a href="/wiki/German_federal_election,_2013" title="German federal election, 2013">2013 election</a>, aside from a period in early 2017 following the selection of <a href="/wiki/Martin_Schulz" title="Martin Schulz">Martin Schulz</a> as SPD leader.</p>
<p>The first exit poll after the polls closed at 6 p.m. showed the <a href="/wiki/Christian_Democratic_Union_(Germany)" class="mw-redirect" title="Christian Democratic Union (Germany)">Christian Democratic Union</a>/<a href="/wiki/Christian_Social_Union_of_Bavaria" class="mw-redirect" title="Christian Social Union of Bavaria">Christian Social Union</a> (<a href="/wiki/CDU/CSU" title="CDU/CSU">CDU/CSU</a>) to have 33% of the vote, the <a href="/wiki/Social_Democratic_Party_of_Germany" title="Social Democratic Party of Germany">Social Democratic Party</a> (SPD) to have achieved its worst result ever with just 20% of the vote, and <a href="/wiki/Alternative_for_Germany" title="Alternative for Germany">Alternative for Germany</a> (AfD)—who were previously unrepresented in the Bundestag—to have taken 12.6% of the vote and win <a href="/wiki/Plurality_(voting)" title="Plurality (voting)">plurality</a> in <a href="/wiki/Saxony" title="Saxony">Saxony</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Background">Background</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=German_federal_election,_2017&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>At the <a href="/wiki/German_federal_election,_2013" title="German federal election, 2013">previous federal election</a>, in 2013, the incumbent government—composed of the <a href="/wiki/Christian_Democratic_Union_(Germany)" class="mw-redirect" title="Christian Democratic Union (Germany)">Christian Democratic Union</a> (CDU), the <a href="/wiki/Christian_Social_Union_of_Bavaria" class="mw-redirect" title="Christian Social Union of Bavaria">Christian Social Union</a> (CSU), and the <a href="/wiki/Free_Democratic_Party_(Germany)" title="Free Democratic Party (Germany)">Free Democratic Party</a> (FDP)—failed to achieve a majority of seats. The FDP failed to get over 5% of the vote, denying the party seats in the Bundestag for the first time in its history. In contrast, the CDU/CSU obtained their best result since <a href="/wiki/German_federal_election,_1990" title="German federal election, 1990">1990</a>, with nearly 42% of the vote and just short of 50% of the seats. The CDU/CSU successfully negotiated with the <a href="/wiki/Social_Democratic_Party_of_Germany" title="Social Democratic Party of Germany">Social Democrats</a> (SPD) to form a <a href="/wiki/Grand_coalition_(Germany)" title="Grand coalition (Germany)">grand coalition</a> for the third time.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>In March 2017, the SPD chose <a href="/wiki/Martin_Schulz" title="Martin Schulz">Martin Schulz</a>, the former <a href="/wiki/President_of_the_European_Parliament" title="President of the European Parliament">President of the European Parliament</a>, as their leader and chancellor candidate. Support for the SPD initially increased; however, the CDU afterward regained its lead, with polls generally showing a 13–16% lead over the SPD. In May, the SPD lost control of the major state of <a href="/wiki/North_Rhine-Westphalia" title="North Rhine-Westphalia">North Rhine-Westphalia</a> in <a href="/wiki/North_Rhine-Westphalia_state_election,_2017" title="North Rhine-Westphalia state election, 2017">a state election</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Date">Date</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=German_federal_election,_2017&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Date">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>German law requires that a new Bundestag shall be elected on a Sunday or on a nationwide holiday between 46–48 months after the last Bundestag's first sitting (<i>Basic Law</i> Article 39 Section 1).<sup id="cite_ref-gesetze-im-internet.de_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gesetze-im-internet.de-3">[3]</a></sup> In January 2017, then President <a href="/wiki/Joachim_Gauck" title="Joachim Gauck">Joachim Gauck</a> scheduled the election for 24 September 2017.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>After the election, the 19th Bundestag has to hold its first sitting within 30 days. Until that first sitting, the members of the 18th Bundestag will remain in office (<i>Basic Law</i> Article 39 Section 1 and 2).<sup id="cite_ref-gesetze-im-internet.de_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gesetze-im-internet.de-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Electoral_system">Electoral system</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=German_federal_election,_2017&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Electoral system">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Germany uses the <a href="/wiki/Additional_member_system" class="mw-redirect" title="Additional member system">mixed-member proportional representation system</a>, a system of <a href="/wiki/Proportional_representation" title="Proportional representation">proportional representation</a> combined with elements of <a href="/wiki/First-past-the-post_voting" title="First-past-the-post voting">first-past-the-post voting</a>. The Bundestag has 598 nominal members, elected for a four-year term; these seats are distributed between the sixteen German states in proportion to the states' population eligible to vote.</p>
<p>Every elector has two votes; a constituency and a list vote. 299 members are elected in single-member constituencies by first-past-the-post, based just on the first votes. The second votes are used to produce an overall proportional result in the states and then in the Bundestag. Seats are allocated using the <a href="/wiki/Sainte-Lagu%C3%AB_method" class="mw-redirect" title="Sainte-Laguë method">Sainte-Laguë method</a>. If a party wins fewer constituency seats in a state than its second votes would entitle it to, it receives additional seats from the relevant state list. Parties can file lists in each single state under certain conditions; for example, a fixed number of supporting signatures. Parties can receive second votes only in those states in which they have successfully filed a state list.</p>
<p>If a party by winning single-member constituencies in one state receives more seats than it would be entitled to according to its second vote share in that state (so-called <a href="/wiki/Overhang_seat" title="Overhang seat">overhang seats</a>), the other parties receive compensation seats. Owing to this provision, the Bundestag usually has more than 598 members. The 18th and current Bundestag, for example, started with 631 seats: 598 regular and 33 overhang and compensation seats. Overhang seats are calculated at the state level, so many more seats are added to balance this out among the different states, adding more seats than would be needed to compensate for overhang at the national level in order to avoid <a href="/wiki/Negative_vote_weight" title="Negative vote weight">negative vote weight</a>.</p>
<p>In order to qualify for seats based on the party-list vote share, a party must either win three single-member constituencies <i>or</i> exceed a <a href="/wiki/Electoral_threshold" class="mw-redirect" title="Electoral threshold">threshold</a> of 5% of the second votes nationwide. If a party only wins one or two single-member constituencies and fails to get at least 5% of the second votes, it keeps the single-member seat(s), but other parties that accomplish at least <i>one</i> of the two threshold conditions receive compensation seats. (In the most recent example of this, during the <a href="/wiki/German_federal_election,_2002" title="German federal election, 2002">2002 election</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Party_of_Democratic_Socialism_(Germany)" title="Party of Democratic Socialism (Germany)">PDS</a> won only 4.0% of the party-list votes nationwide, but won two constituencies in the state of <a href="/wiki/Berlin" title="Berlin">Berlin</a>.) The same applies if an independent candidate wins a single-member constituency (which has not happened since <a href="/wiki/West_German_federal_election,_1949" title="West German federal election, 1949">1949</a>). In the <a href="/wiki/German_federal_election,_2013" title="German federal election, 2013">2013 election</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Free_Democratic_Party_(Germany)" title="Free Democratic Party (Germany)">FDP</a> only won 4.8% of party-list votes; this cost it all of its seats in the Bundestag.</p>
<p>If a voter has cast a first vote for a successful independent candidate or a successful candidate whose party failed to qualify for proportional representation, their second vote does not count to determine proportional representation. However, it does count to determine whether the elected party has exceeded the 5% threshold.</p>
<p>Parties representing recognized national minorities (currently <a href="/wiki/Danes#Danish_diaspora" title="Danes">Danes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Frisians" title="Frisians">Frisians</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sorbs" title="Sorbs">Sorbs</a> and <a href="/wiki/Romani_people" title="Romani people">Romani people</a>) are exempt from the 5% threshold, but normally only run in state elections.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>

<p>By tradition, the <a href="/wiki/Christian_Democratic_Union_of_Germany" title="Christian Democratic Union of Germany">Christian Democratic Union of Germany</a> (CDU) and <a href="/wiki/Christian_Social_Union_in_Bavaria" title="Christian Social Union in Bavaria">Christian Social Union in Bavaria</a> (CSU), which refer to each other as 'sister parties', do not compete against each other. The CSU has only filed a list in <a href="/wiki/Bavaria" title="Bavaria">Bavaria</a>, while the CDU has filed lists in the other fifteen states. This allows them to join in one parliamentary group after the election as the <a href="/wiki/CDU/CSU" title="CDU/CSU">CDU/CSU</a>, which they have always done in the past and which they are expected to do again after this election.</p>
<p>As the CDU/CSU and the <a href="/wiki/Social_Democratic_Party_of_Germany" title="Social Democratic Party of Germany">Social Democratic Party</a> (SPD) are likely to win the most seats in the election, their leading candidates are referred to as 'Chancellor candidates'. This does not, however, mean that the new Bundestag is legally bound to elect one of them as Chancellor.</p>

<p>Exit polls suggest that the <a href="/wiki/Christian_Democratic_Union_of_Germany" title="Christian Democratic Union of Germany">Christian Democratic Union</a> (CDU) and the <a href="/wiki/Christian_Social_Union_of_Bavaria" class="mw-redirect" title="Christian Social Union of Bavaria">Christian Social Union of Bavaria</a> (CSU) will remain the largest bloc, despite losing 8.5% percentage points from their last result. Their grand coalition partner and principal rival to lead the government, the <a href="/wiki/Social_Democratic_Party_of_Germany" title="Social Democratic Party of Germany">Social Democratic Party</a> (SPD), also fell in the popular vote, by about 5 percentage points. The right-wing, eurosceptic <a href="/wiki/Alternative_for_Germany" title="Alternative for Germany">Alternative for Germany</a> is set to enter the Bundestag for the first time as the third largest party, while the Free Democratic Party (FDP) is set to re-enter the Bundestag with 10% of the popular vote after being shut out in 2013. The Greens and the Left are set to remain in the Bundestag with about the same vote share as the previous election.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Results">Results</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=German_federal_election,_2017&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Results">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The CDU/CSU and the SPD remained the two largest parties in the Bundestag, but both received a significantly lower proportion of the vote than they did in the 2013 election.</p>
<p>The AfD received enough votes to enter the Bundestag for the first time, taking 12.6 percent of the vote--more than double the five percent threshold required to qualify for full parliamentary status. It also won three constituency seats, which would have qualified it for proportionally-elected seats in any event. Longstanding German electoral law stipulates that any party that wins at least three constituency seats qualifies for its share of MMP seats even if the party comes up short of the five percent threshold. This was the first federal election in which the AfD received enough votes to be represented in the Bundestag.</p>
<p>The FDP returned to the Bundestag with 10.7 percent of the vote.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Government_formation">Government formation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=German_federal_election,_2017&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Government formation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The SPD's deputy leader <a href="/wiki/Manuela_Schwesig" title="Manuela Schwesig">Manuela Schwesig</a> and parliamentary chairman <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Oppermann" title="Thomas Oppermann">Thomas Oppermann</a> have said that the SPD will leave the current <a href="/wiki/Grand_coalition" title="Grand coalition">grand coalition</a> government,<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[10]</a></sup> after unsatisfactory election results. The media speculated that <a href="/wiki/Chancellor_of_Germany" title="Chancellor of Germany">Chancellor</a> <a href="/wiki/Angela_Merkel" title="Angela Merkel">Angela Merkel</a> may need to form a <a href="/wiki/Jamaica_coalition_(politics)" title="Jamaica coalition (politics)">Jamaica coalition</a> (black-yellow-green) with the <a href="/wiki/Free_Democratic_Party_(Germany)" title="Free Democratic Party (Germany)">Free Democrats</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Alliance_90/The_Greens" title="Alliance 90/The Greens">Greens</a>, if the Social Democrats leave the government.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[11]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[12]</a></sup></p>
