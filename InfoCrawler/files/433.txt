<p><i><b>Triumph of the Will</b></i> (<a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a>: <span lang="de"><i><b>Triumph des Willens</b></i></span>) is a 1935 German <a href="/wiki/Propaganda_film" title="Propaganda film">propaganda film</a> directed, produced, edited, and co-written by <a href="/wiki/Leni_Riefenstahl" title="Leni Riefenstahl">Leni Riefenstahl</a>. It chronicles the 1934 <a href="/wiki/Nuremberg_Rally" title="Nuremberg Rally">Nazi Party Congress</a> in <a href="/wiki/Nuremberg" title="Nuremberg">Nuremberg</a>, which was attended by more than 700,000 Nazi supporters.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> The film contains excerpts from speeches given by <a href="/wiki/List_of_Nazi_Party_leaders_and_officials" title="List of Nazi Party leaders and officials">Nazi leaders</a> at the Congress, including <a href="/wiki/Adolf_Hitler" title="Adolf Hitler">Adolf Hitler</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rudolf_Hess" title="Rudolf Hess">Rudolf Hess</a> and <a href="/wiki/Julius_Streicher" title="Julius Streicher">Julius Streicher</a>, interspersed with footage of massed <a href="/wiki/Sturmabteilung" title="Sturmabteilung">Sturmabteilung</a> (SA) and <a href="/wiki/Schutzstaffel" title="Schutzstaffel">Schutzstaffel</a> (SS) troops and public reaction. Hitler commissioned the film and served as an unofficial <a href="/wiki/Executive_producer" title="Executive producer">executive producer</a>; his name appears in the opening titles. The film's overriding theme is the return of <a href="/wiki/Nazi_Germany" title="Nazi Germany">Germany</a> as a <a href="/wiki/Great_power" title="Great power">great power</a>, with Hitler as the leader who will bring glory to the nation. Because the film was made after the 1934 <a href="/wiki/Night_of_the_Long_Knives" title="Night of the Long Knives">Night of the Long Knives</a> (on 30 June) many prominent Sturmabteilung (SA) members are absent—they were murdered in that Party purge, organised and orchestrated by Hitler to replace the SA with the Schutzstaffel (SS) as his main paramilitary force.</p>
<p><i>Triumph of the Will</i> was released in 1935 and became a prominent example of propaganda in film history. Riefenstahl's techniques—such as moving cameras, <a href="/wiki/Aerial_photography" title="Aerial photography">aerial photography</a>, the use of <a href="/wiki/Long_focus_lens" class="mw-redirect" title="Long focus lens">long focus lenses</a> to create a distorted <a href="/wiki/Perspective_(graphical)" title="Perspective (graphical)">perspective</a>, and the revolutionary approach to the use of music and <a href="/wiki/Cinematography" title="Cinematography">cinematography</a>—have earned <i>Triumph of the Will</i> recognition as one of the <a href="/wiki/Films_considered_the_greatest_ever#Propaganda" class="mw-redirect" title="Films considered the greatest ever">greatest propaganda films in history</a>. Riefenstahl helped to stage the scenes, directing and rehearsing some of them at least fifty times. Riefenstahl won several awards, not only in Germany but also in the United States, France, Sweden and other countries. The film was popular in the <a href="/wiki/Third_Reich" class="mw-redirect" title="Third Reich">Third Reich</a>, and has continued to influence films, documentaries and commercials to this day.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> In Germany, the film is not censored but the courts commonly classify it as Nazi propaganda which requires an educational context to public screenings.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>An earlier film by Riefenstahl—<i><a href="/wiki/The_Victory_of_Faith" title="The Victory of Faith">The Victory of Faith</a> (Der Sieg des Glaubens)</i>—showed Hitler and SA leader <a href="/wiki/Ernst_R%C3%B6hm" title="Ernst Röhm">Ernst Röhm</a> together at the 1933 Nazi party congress. After Röhm's murder, the party attempted the destruction of all copies, leaving only one known to have survived in <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">Britain</a>. The direction and sequencing of images is almost the same as that Riefenstahl used in <i>Triumph of the Will</i> a year later.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Frank_Capra" title="Frank Capra">Frank Capra</a>'s seven-film series <i><a href="/wiki/Why_We_Fight" title="Why We Fight">Why We Fight</a></i> is said to have been <a href="/wiki/Why_We_Fight#Countering_enemy_propaganda_films" title="Why We Fight">directly inspired</a> by, and the United States' response to, <i>Triumph of the Will</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-Triumph_of_the_Will_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Triumph_of_the_Will-4">[4]</a></sup></p>

<p><b>Day 1</b>: The film opens with shots of the clouds above the city, and then moves through the clouds to float above the assembling masses below, with the intention of portraying beauty and majesty of the scene. The cruciform shadow of Hitler's plane is visible as it passes over the tiny figures marching below, accompanied by an orchestral arrangement of the <i><a href="/wiki/Horst-Wessel-Lied" title="Horst-Wessel-Lied">Horst-Wessel-Lied</a></i>. Upon arriving at the Nuremberg airport, Hitler and other Nazi leaders emerge from his plane to thunderous applause and a cheering crowd. He is then driven into Nuremberg, through equally enthusiastic people, to his hotel where a night rally is later held.</p>
<p><b>Day 2</b>: The second day begins with images of Nuremberg at dawn, accompanied by an extract from the Act III Prelude (<i>Wach Auf!</i>) of <a href="/wiki/Richard_Wagner" title="Richard Wagner">Richard Wagner</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Die_Meistersinger_von_N%C3%BCrnberg" title="Die Meistersinger von Nürnberg">Die Meistersinger von Nürnberg</a></i>. Following this is a <a href="/wiki/Montage_sequence" class="mw-redirect" title="Montage sequence">montage</a> of the attendees preparing for the opening of the Reich Party Congress, and footage of the top Nazi officials arriving at the <a href="/wiki/Nazi_party_rally_grounds#Luitpoldarena" title="Nazi party rally grounds">Luitpold Arena</a>. The film then cuts to the opening ceremony, where <a href="/wiki/Rudolf_Hess" title="Rudolf Hess">Rudolf Hess</a> announces the start of the Congress. The camera then introduces much of the Nazi hierarchy and covers their opening speeches, including <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Goebbels" title="Joseph Goebbels">Joseph Goebbels</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alfred_Rosenberg" title="Alfred Rosenberg">Alfred Rosenberg</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hans_Frank" title="Hans Frank">Hans Frank</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fritz_Todt" title="Fritz Todt">Fritz Todt</a>, <a href="/wiki/Robert_Ley" title="Robert Ley">Robert Ley</a> and <a href="/wiki/Julius_Streicher" title="Julius Streicher">Julius Streicher</a>. Then the film cuts to an outdoor rally for the <i><a href="/wiki/Reichsarbeitsdienst" class="mw-redirect" title="Reichsarbeitsdienst">Reichsarbeitsdienst</a></i> (Labor Service), which is primarily a series of quasi-military drills by men carrying spades. This is also where Hitler gives his first speech on the merits of the Labour Service and praising them for their work in rebuilding Germany. The day then ends with a torchlight <a href="/wiki/Sturmabteilung" title="Sturmabteilung">SA</a> parade in which <a href="/wiki/Viktor_Lutze" title="Viktor Lutze">Viktor Lutze</a> speaks to the crowds.</p>
<p><b>Day 3</b>: The third day starts with a <a href="/wiki/Hitler_Youth" title="Hitler Youth">Hitler Youth</a> rally on the parade ground. Again the camera covers the Nazi dignitaries arriving and the introduction of Hitler by <a href="/wiki/Baldur_von_Schirach" title="Baldur von Schirach">Baldur von Schirach</a>. Hitler then addresses the Youth, describing in <a href="/wiki/Militarism" title="Militarism">militaristic</a> terms how they must harden themselves and prepare for sacrifice. Everyone present, including General <a href="/wiki/Werner_von_Blomberg" title="Werner von Blomberg">Werner von Blomberg</a>, then assemble for a military pass and review, featuring <a href="/wiki/Wehrmacht" title="Wehrmacht">Wehrmacht</a> cavalry and various armored vehicles. That night Hitler delivers another speech to low-ranking party officials by torchlight, commemorating the first year since the Nazis took power and declaring that the party and state are one entity.</p>
<p><b>Day 4</b>: The fourth day is the <a href="/wiki/Climax_(narrative)" title="Climax (narrative)">climax</a> of the film, where the most memorable of the imagery is presented. Hitler, flanked by <a href="/wiki/Heinrich_Himmler" title="Heinrich Himmler">Heinrich Himmler</a> and Viktor Lutze, walks through a long wide expanse with over 150,000 <a href="/wiki/Sturmabteilung" title="Sturmabteilung">SA</a> and <a href="/wiki/Schutzstaffel" title="Schutzstaffel">SS</a> troops standing at attention, to lay a wreath at a First World War memorial. Hitler then reviews the parading SA and SS men, following which Hitler and Lutze deliver a speech where they discuss the <a href="/wiki/Night_of_the_Long_Knives" title="Night of the Long Knives">Night of the Long Knives</a> purge of the SA several months prior. Lutze reaffirms the SA's loyalty to the regime, and Hitler absolves the SA of any crimes committed by Ernst Röhm. New party flags are consecrated by letting them touch the <i><a href="/wiki/Blutfahne" title="Blutfahne">Blutfahne</a></i> (the same cloth flag said to have been carried by the fallen Nazis during the <a href="/wiki/Beer_Hall_Putsch" title="Beer Hall Putsch">Beer Hall Putsch</a>) and, following a final parade in front of the <a href="/wiki/Nuremberg_Frauenkirche" class="mw-redirect" title="Nuremberg Frauenkirche">Nuremberg Frauenkirche</a>, Hitler delivers his closing speech. In it he reaffirms the primacy of the Nazi Party in Germany, declaring, "All loyal Germans will become National Socialists. Only the best National Socialists are party comrades!" Hess then leads the assembled crowd in a final <i><a href="/wiki/Sieg_Heil" class="mw-redirect" title="Sieg Heil">Sieg Heil</a></i> salute for Hitler, marking the close of the party congress. The entire crowd sings the <i>Horst-Wessel-Lied</i> as the camera focuses on the giant Swastika banner, which fades into a line of silhouetted men in Nazi party uniforms, marching in formation as the lyrics "Comrades shot by the Red Front and the Reactionaries march in spirit together in our columns" are sung.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Origins">Origins</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Triumph_of_the_Will&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Origins">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>Shortly after he came to power Hitler called me to see him and explained that he wanted a film about a Party Congress, and wanted me to make it. My first reaction was to say that I did not know anything about the way such a thing worked or the organisation of the Party, so that I would obviously photograph all the wrong things and please nobody—even supposing that I could make a documentary, which I had never yet done. Hitler said that this was exactly why he wanted me to do it: because anyone who knew all about the relative importance of the various people and groups and so on might make a film that would be pedantically accurate, but this was not what he wanted. He wanted a film showing the Congress through a non-expert eye, selecting just what was most artistically satisfying—in terms of spectacle, I suppose you might say. He wanted a film which would move, appeal to, impress an audience which was not necessarily interested in politics.</p>
<div class="templatequotecite"><cite>— Leni Riefenstahl</cite></div>
</blockquote>
<p>Riefenstahl, a popular German actress, had directed her first film called <i><a href="/wiki/Das_blaue_Licht" class="mw-redirect" title="Das blaue Licht">Das blaue Licht</a></i> (<i>The Blue Light</i>) in 1932. Around the same time she first heard Hitler speak at a Nazi rally and, by her own admission, was impressed. She later began a correspondence with him that would last for years. Hitler, by turn, was equally impressed with <i>Das blaue Licht</i>, and in 1933 asked her to direct a film about the Nazis' annual <a href="/wiki/Nuremberg_Rally" title="Nuremberg Rally">Nuremberg Rally</a>. The Nazis had only recently taken power amid a period of <a href="/wiki/Weimar_Republic#The_end" title="Weimar Republic">political instability</a> (Hitler was the fourth <a href="/wiki/Chancellor_of_Germany_(German_Reich)" class="mw-redirect" title="Chancellor of Germany (German Reich)">Chancellor of Germany</a> in less than a year) and were considered an unknown quantity by many Germans, to say nothing of the world.</p>
<p>In <i><a href="/wiki/Mein_Kampf" title="Mein Kampf">Mein Kampf</a></i>,<sup id="cite_ref-PS2_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PS2-5">[5]</a></sup> Hitler talks of the success of British propaganda in World War I, believing people's ignorance meant simple repetition and an appeal to feelings over reason would suffice.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Hitler chose Riefenstahl as he wanted the film as "artistically satisfying"<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> as possible to appeal to a non-political audience, but he also believed that propaganda must admit no element of doubt.<sup id="cite_ref-PS2_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PS2-5">[5]</a></sup> As such, <i>Triumph of the Will</i> may be seen as a continuation of the unambiguous World War I-style propaganda, though heightened by the film's artistic or poetic nature.</p>
<p>Riefenstahl was initially reluctant, not because of any moral qualms, but because she wanted to continue making feature films.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Hitler persisted and Riefenstahl eventually agreed to make a film at the 1933 Nuremberg Rally called <i><a href="/wiki/Der_Sieg_des_Glaubens" class="mw-redirect" title="Der Sieg des Glaubens">Der Sieg des Glaubens</a></i> (<i>Victory of Faith</i>). However the film had numerous technical problems, including a lack of preparation (Riefenstahl reported having just a few days) and Hitler's apparent unease at being filmed. To make matters worse, Riefenstahl had to deal with infighting by party officials, in particular Joseph Goebbels who tried to have the film released by the Propaganda Ministry. Though <i>Der Sieg des Glaubens</i> apparently did well at the box office, it later became a serious embarrassment to the Nazis after SA Leader Ernst Röhm, who had a prominent role in the film, was executed during the Night of the Long Knives. All references to Röhm were ordered to be erased from German history, which included the destruction of all copies of <i>Der Sieg des Glaubens</i>. It was considered lost until a copy turned up in the 1990s in the United Kingdom.</p>
<p>In 1934, Riefenstahl had no wish to repeat the fiasco of <i>Der Sieg des Glaubens</i> and initially recommended fellow director <a href="/wiki/Walter_Ruttmann" title="Walter Ruttmann">Walter Ruttmann</a>. Ruttmann's film, which would have covered the rise of the Nazi Party from 1923 to 1934 and been more overtly propagandistic (the opening text of <i>Triumph of the Will</i> was his), did not appeal to Hitler. He again asked Riefenstahl, who finally relented (there is still debate over how willing she was) after Hitler guaranteed his personal support and promised to keep other Nazi organizations, specifically the Propaganda Ministry, from meddling with her film.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Production">Production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Triumph_of_the_Will&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The film follows a script similar to <i>Der Sieg des Glaubens</i>, which is evident when one sees both films side by side. For example, the city of Nuremberg scenes—even to the shot of a cat included in the city driving sequence in both films. Furthermore, <a href="/wiki/Herbert_Windt" title="Herbert Windt">Herbert Windt</a> reused much of his musical score for that film in <i>Triumph des Willens</i>, which he also scored. Riefenstahl shot <i>Triumph of the Will</i> on a budget of roughly 280,000RM (approx. $110K USD 1934, $1.54M 2015).<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> With that said, there were extensive preparations facilitated by the cooperation of party members, the military, and vital help from high-ranking Nazis like Goebbels. As <a href="/wiki/Susan_Sontag" title="Susan Sontag">Susan Sontag</a> observed, "The Rally was planned not only as a spectacular mass meeting, but as a spectacular propaganda film."<sup id="cite_ref-Sontag_1975_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sontag_1975-9">[9]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Albert_Speer" title="Albert Speer">Albert Speer</a>, Hitler's personal architect, designed the set in Nuremberg and did most of the coordination for the event. Pits were dug in front of the speakers' platform so Riefenstahl could get the camera angles she wanted, and tracks were laid so that her cameramen could get traveling shots of the crowd. When rough cuts weren't up to par, major party leaders and high-ranking public officials reenacted their speeches in a studio for her.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Riefenstahl also used a film crew that was extravagant by the standards of the day. Her crew consisted of 172 people, including 10 technical staff, 36 cameramen and assistants (operating in 16 teams with 30 cameras), nine aerial photographers, 17 newsreel men, 12 newsreel crew, 17 lighting men, two photographers, 26 drivers, 37 security personnel, four labor service workers, and two office assistants. Many of her cameramen also dressed in SA uniforms so they could blend into the crowds.</p>
<p>Riefenstahl had the difficult task of condensing an estimated 61 hours of film into two hours.<sup class="reference" id="ref_UCLAc"><a href="#endnote_UCLAc">[6]</a></sup> She labored to complete the film as fast as she could, going so far as to sleep in the editing room filled with hundreds of thousands of feet of film footage.<sup class="reference" id="ref_Triumph2001a"><a href="#endnote_Triumph2001a">[8]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Themes">Themes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Triumph_of_the_Will&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Themes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Religion">Religion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Triumph_of_the_Will&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Religion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>This morning's opening meeting... was more than a gorgeous show, it also had something of the <a href="/wiki/Mysticism" title="Mysticism">mysticism</a> and religious fervor of an <a href="/wiki/Easter" title="Easter">Easter</a> or <a href="/wiki/Christmas" title="Christmas">Christmas</a> <a href="/wiki/Mass_(liturgy)" title="Mass (liturgy)">Mass</a> in a great <a href="/wiki/Gothic_architecture" title="Gothic architecture">Gothic</a> cathedral.</p>
<div class="templatequotecite"><cite>— Reporter <a href="/wiki/William_Shirer" class="mw-redirect" title="William Shirer">William Shirer</a></cite></div>
</blockquote>
<p><i>Triumph of the Will</i> is sometimes seen as an example of Nazi <a href="/wiki/Political_religion" title="Political religion">political religion</a>. The primary religion in Germany before the Second World War was <a href="/wiki/Christianity" title="Christianity">Christianity</a>. With the primary sects being <a href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic" class="mw-redirect" title="Roman Catholic">Roman Catholic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Protestant" class="mw-redirect" title="Protestant">Protestant</a>, the Christian views in this movie are clearly meant to allow the movie to better connect with the intended audience.</p>
<p>Religion is a major theme in <i>Triumph of the Will</i>. The film opens with Hitler descending god-like out of the skies past twin cathedral spires. It contains many scenes of church bells ringing, and individuals in a state of near-religious fervor, as well as a prominent shot of Reich Protestant Bishop <a href="/wiki/Ludwig_M%C3%BCller_(theologian)" class="mw-redirect" title="Ludwig Müller (theologian)">Ludwig Müller</a> standing in his <a href="/wiki/Vestment" title="Vestment">vestments</a> among high-ranking Nazis. It is probably not a coincidence that the final parade of the film was held in front of the <a href="/wiki/Nuremberg_Frauenkirche" class="mw-redirect" title="Nuremberg Frauenkirche">Nuremberg Frauenkirche</a>. In his final speech in the film, Hitler also directly compares the Nazi party to a <a href="/wiki/Holy_Orders" class="mw-redirect" title="Holy Orders">holy order</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Consecration" title="Consecration">consecration</a> of new party flags by having Hitler touch them to the "blood banner" has obvious religious overtones. Hitler himself is portrayed in a <a href="/wiki/God_complex" title="God complex">messianic</a> manner, from the opening where he descends from the clouds in a plane, to his drive through Nuremberg where even a cat stops what it is doing to watch him, to the many scenes where the camera films from below and looks up at him: Hitler, standing on his podium, will issue a command to hundreds of thousands of followers. The audience happily complies in unison.<sup id="cite_ref-nysun_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nysun-11">[11]</a></sup> As <a href="/wiki/Frank_P._Tomasulo" title="Frank P. Tomasulo">Frank P. Tomasulo</a> comments, "Hitler is cast as a veritable German Messiah who will save the nation, if only the citizenry will put its destiny in his hands."<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Power">Power</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Triumph_of_the_Will&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Power">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>It is our <a href="/wiki/Will_(philosophy)" title="Will (philosophy)">will</a> that this state and this Reich shall endure through the coming millennia.</p>
<div class="templatequotecite"><cite>— Hitler</cite></div>
</blockquote>
<p>Germany had not seen images of military power and strength since the end of World War I, and the huge formations of men would remind the audience that Germany was becoming a great power once again. Though the Labor Service men carried spades, they handled them as if they were rifles. The <a href="/wiki/Aquila_(Roman)" title="Aquila (Roman)">Eagles</a> and <a href="/wiki/Swastika" title="Swastika">Swastikas</a> could be seen as a reference to the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Legion" class="mw-redirect" title="Roman Legion">Roman Legions</a> of antiquity. The large mass of well-drilled party members could be seen in a more ominous light, as a warning to <a href="/wiki/Dissident" title="Dissident">dissidents</a> thinking of challenging the regime.</p>
<p>Hitler's arrival in an airplane should also be viewed in this context. According to Kenneth Poferl, "Flying in an airplane was a luxury known only to a select few in the 1930s, but Hitler had made himself widely associated with the practice, having been the first politician to campaign via air travel. Victory reinforced this image and defined him as the top man in the movement, by showing him as the only one to arrive in a plane and receive an individual welcome from the crowd. Hitler's speech to the SA also contained an implied threat: if he could have Röhm, the commander of the hundreds of thousands of troops on the screen, shot, it was only logical to assume that Hitler could get away with having anyone executed."</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Unity">Unity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Triumph_of_the_Will&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Unity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>As soon as our own propaganda admits so much as a glimmer of right on the other side, the foundation for doubt in our own right has been laid.</p>
<div class="templatequotecite"><cite>— Hitler</cite></div>
</blockquote>
<p>It was very important to Adolf Hitler that his propaganda messages carry a unified theme. If a country isn't unified in saying the enemy is bad, the audience starts to have doubts. Unity is seen throughout this film, even in the camps where soldiers live. The camp outside of Nuremberg is very uniform and clean; the tents are aligned in perfect rows, each one the same as the next. The men there also make a point not to wear their shirts, because their shirts display their rankings and status. Shirtless they are all equals, unified. When they march, it is in unison and they all carry their weapons identically, one to another.</p>
<p>Hitler's message to the workers also includes the notion of unity:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>The concept of labor will no longer be a dividing one but a uniting one, and no longer will there be anybody in Germany who will regard manual labor any less highly than any other form of labor.</p>
<div class="templatequotecite"><cite>— Hitler</cite></div>
</blockquote>
<p>Children were also used to convey unity:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>We want to be a united nation, and you, my youth, are to become this nation. In the future, we do not wish to see classes and castes, and you must not allow them to develop among you. One day, we want to see one nation.</p>
<div class="templatequotecite"><cite>— Hitler</cite></div>
</blockquote>
<p><i>Triumph of the Will</i> has many scenes that blur the distinction between the Nazi Party, the German state, and the German people. Germans in peasant farmers' costumes and other traditional clothing greet Hitler in some scenes. The torchlight processions, though now associated by many with the Nazis, would remind the viewer of the <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">medieval</a> <a href="/wiki/Carnival" title="Carnival">Karneval</a> celebration. The old flag of <a href="/wiki/German_Empire" title="German Empire">Imperial Germany</a> is also shown several times flying alongside the Swastika, and there is a ceremony where Hitler pays his respects to soldiers who died in World War I (as well as to President <a href="/wiki/Paul_von_Hindenburg" title="Paul von Hindenburg">Paul von Hindenburg</a>, who had died a month before the convention). There is also a scene where the Labor Servicemen individually call out which town or area in Germany they are from, reminding the viewers that the Nazi Party had expanded from its stronghold in <a href="/wiki/Bavaria" title="Bavaria">Bavaria</a> to become a <a href="/wiki/Pan-Germanism" title="Pan-Germanism">pan-German</a> movement.</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>The Party is Hitler—and Hitler is Germany just as Germany is Hitler!</p>
<div class="templatequotecite"><cite>— Rudolf Hess</cite></div>
</blockquote>
<h2><span id="Hitler's_speeches"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Hitler.27s_speeches">Hitler's speeches</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Triumph_of_the_Will&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Hitler's speeches">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Among the themes presented, the desire for pride in Germany and the purification of the German people is well exemplified through the speeches and ideals of the <a href="/wiki/Nazi_Germany" title="Nazi Germany">Third Reich</a> in <i>Triumph of the Will</i>.</p>

<p>In every speech given and shown in <i>Triumph of the Will</i>, pride is one of the major focuses. Hitler advocates to the people that they should not be satisfied with their current state and they should not be satisfied with the descent from power and greatness Germany has endured since World War I. The German people should believe in themselves and the movement that is occurring in Germany. Hitler promotes pride in Germany through the unification of it. Unifying Germany would force the elimination of what does not amount to the standards of the Nazi regime.</p>
<p>To unify Germany, Hitler believes purification would have to take place. This meant not only eliminating the citizens of Germany who are not of the <a href="/wiki/Aryan_race" title="Aryan race">Aryan race</a>, but the sick, weak, handicapped, or any other citizens deemed unhealthy or impure. In <i>Triumph of the Will</i>, Hitler preaches to the people that Germany must take a look at itself and seek out that which does not belong: "[T]he elements that have become bad, and therefore do not belong with us!" Though within the context, he seems to be referring to the corrupt elements of the power structure, it later could seem in hindsight to imply that the elimination of the "inferior" people of Germany would, in theory, return Germany to its once prideful and powerful former self<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Accuracy_dispute#Disputed_statement" title="Wikipedia:Accuracy dispute"><span title="The material near this tag is possibly inaccurate or nonfactual. (March 2017)">dubious</span></a> <span class="metadata">– <a href="/wiki/Talk:Triumph_of_the_Will#Dubious" title="Talk:Triumph of the Will">discuss</a></span></i>]</sup>. <a href="/wiki/Julius_Streicher" title="Julius Streicher">Julius Streicher</a> stresses the importance of purification in his speech, a direct reference to his own virulent <a href="/wiki/Anti-semitism" class="mw-redirect" title="Anti-semitism">anti-semitism</a>. Hundreds of thousands mentally sick and disabled would be murdered in the <a href="/wiki/Action_T4" class="mw-redirect" title="Action T4">Action T4</a>, a programme run directly from <a href="/wiki/Hitler%27s_Chancellery" title="Hitler's Chancellery">Hitler's Chancellery</a> (<i>Kanzlei des Führers</i>).</p>
<p>Hitler preaches to the people in his speeches that they should believe in their country and themselves. The German people are better than what they have become because of the impurities in society. Hitler wants them to believe in him and believe what he wants to do for his people, and what he is doing is for the country's and people's benefit. Hess says in the last scene of <i>Triumph of the Will</i>, "Heil Hitler, hail victory, hail victory!" Everyone in attendance yells in support. This verbal sign represents their faith to their leader and his most trusted advisors that they believe in the Nazi cause. This is directly following Hitler's yell, "Long live the National Socialist Movement! Long live Germany!" and the crowd erupts with cheering and the fulfillment of pride for themselves and their political party.</p>
<p>In the closing speech of <i>Triumph of the Will</i>, Hitler enters the room from the back, appearing to emerge from the people. After a one sentence introduction, he tells his faithful Nazis how the German nation has subordinated itself to the <a href="/wiki/Nazi_Party" title="Nazi Party">Nazi Party</a> because its leaders are mostly of Germans. He promises that the new state that the Nazis have created will endure for thousands of years. Hitler says that the youth will carry on after the old have weakened. They close with a chant, "Hitler is the Party, Hitler." The camera focuses on the large Swastika above Hitler and the film ends with the images of this Swastika imposed on Nazis marching in a few columns. His speech brought attention to the rally and created a huge turnout in the following years. He attracted many people in the way that he addressed the issues and his people. He spoke to them as if it were a sermon and engaged the people. In 1934, over a million Germans participated in the <a href="/wiki/Nuremberg_Rally" title="Nuremberg Rally">Nuremberg Rally</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Response">Response</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Triumph_of_the_Will&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Response">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>Triumph of the Will</i> premiered on 28 March 1935 at the Berlin Ufa Palace Theater and was an instant success. Within two months the film had earned 815,000 <a href="/wiki/German_Reichsmark" class="mw-redirect" title="German Reichsmark">Reichsmark</a>, and Ufa considered it one of the three most profitable films of that year. Hitler praised the film as being an "incomparable glorification of the power and beauty of our Movement." For her efforts, Riefenstahl was rewarded with the German Film Prize (<i>Deutscher Filmpreis</i>), a gold medal at the 1935 <a href="/wiki/Venice_Biennale" title="Venice Biennale">Venice Biennale</a>, and the Grand Prix at the 1937 <a href="/wiki/World_Exhibition" class="mw-redirect" title="World Exhibition">World Exhibition</a> in <a href="/wiki/Paris" title="Paris">Paris</a>. However, there were few claims that the film would result in a mass influx of "<a href="/wiki/Converts" class="mw-redirect" title="Converts">converts</a>" to <a href="/wiki/Fascism" title="Fascism">fascism</a> and the Nazis apparently did not make a serious effort to promote the film outside of Germany. Film historian Richard Taylor also said that <i>Triumph of the Will</i> was not generally used for propaganda purposes inside the Third Reich. <i><a href="/wiki/The_Independent" title="The Independent">The Independent</a></i> wrote in 2003: "<i>Triumph of the Will</i> seduced many wise men and women, persuaded them to admire rather than to despise, and undoubtedly won the Nazis friends and allies all over the world."<sup id="cite_ref-the_independent_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-the_independent-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>The reception in other countries was not always as enthusiastic. British documentarian <a href="/wiki/Paul_Rotha" title="Paul Rotha">Paul Rotha</a> called it tedious, while others were repelled by its pro-Nazi sentiments. During World War II, <a href="/wiki/Frank_Capra" title="Frank Capra">Frank Capra</a> helped to create a direct response, through the film series called <i><a href="/wiki/Why_We_Fight" title="Why We Fight">Why We Fight</a></i>, a series of newsreels commissioned by the United States government that spliced in footage from <i>Triumph of the Will</i>, but recontextualized it so that it promoted the cause of the <a href="/wiki/Allies_of_World_War_II" title="Allies of World War II">Allies</a> instead. Capra later remarked that <i>Triumph of the Will</i> "fired no gun, dropped no bombs. But as a <a href="/wiki/Psychology" title="Psychology">psychological</a> weapon aimed at destroying the will to resist, it was just as lethal."<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> Clips from <i>Triumph of the Will</i> were also used in an Allied propaganda short called <i>General Adolph Takes Over</i>,<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> set to the British dance tune "<a href="/wiki/The_Lambeth_Walk" title="The Lambeth Walk">The Lambeth Walk</a>". The legions of marching soldiers, as well as Hitler giving his Nazi salute, were made to look like wind-up dolls, dancing to the music. The Danish resistance used to take over cinemas and force the projectionist to show <i>Swinging the Lambeth Walk</i> (as it was also known); Erik Barrow has said: "The extraordinary risks were apparently felt justified by a moment of savage anti-Hitler ridicule."<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> Also during World War II, the poet <a href="/wiki/Dylan_Thomas" title="Dylan Thomas">Dylan Thomas</a> wrote a screenplay for and narrated <i>These Are The Men</i>, a propaganda piece using <i>Triumph of the Will</i> footage to discredit Nazi leadership.</p>
<p>One of the best ways to gauge the response to <i>Triumph of the Will</i> was the instant and lasting international fame it gave Riefenstahl. <i><a href="/wiki/The_Economist" title="The Economist">The Economist</a></i> said it "sealed her reputation as the greatest female filmmaker of the 20th century."<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> For a director who made eight films, only two of which received significant coverage outside of Germany, Riefenstahl had unusually high name recognition for the remainder of her life, most of it stemming from <i>Triumph of the Will</i>. However, her career was also permanently damaged by this association. After the war, Riefenstahl was imprisoned by the Allies for four years for allegedly being a Nazi sympathizer and was permanently <a href="/wiki/Blacklist" class="mw-redirect" title="Blacklist">blacklisted</a> by the film industry. When she died in 2003–68 years after the film's premiere—her <a href="/wiki/Obituary" title="Obituary">obituary</a> received significant coverage in many major publications, including the <a href="/wiki/Associated_Press" title="Associated Press">Associated Press</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/The_Wall_Street_Journal" title="The Wall Street Journal">The Wall Street Journal</a></i>,<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_York_Times" title="The New York Times">The New York Times</a></i>,<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> and <i><a href="/wiki/The_Guardian" title="The Guardian">The Guardian</a></i>,<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> most of which reaffirmed the importance of <i>Triumph of the Will</i>. Though the actual effectiveness of <i>Triumph of the Will</i> is hard to measure in terms of numbers or statistics that actually state its effectiveness, its response from the people is well documented with the amount of views and the popularity of the movie during the time period.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Controversy">Controversy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Triumph_of_the_Will&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Controversy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Julius_Streicher_72-920_crop.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/03/Julius_Streicher_72-920_crop.jpg/220px-Julius_Streicher_72-920_crop.jpg" width="220" height="292" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/0/03/Julius_Streicher_72-920_crop.jpg 1.5x" data-file-width="267" data-file-height="354" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Julius_Streicher_72-920_crop.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Julius_Streicher" title="Julius Streicher">Julius Streicher</a> in custody in 1945</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Like American filmmaker <a href="/wiki/D._W._Griffith" title="D. W. Griffith">D. W. Griffith</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/The_Birth_of_a_Nation" title="The Birth of a Nation">The Birth of a Nation</a></i>, <i>Triumph of the Will</i> has been criticized as a use of spectacular filmmaking to promote a profoundly <a href="/wiki/Unethical" class="mw-redirect" title="Unethical">unethical</a> system. In her defense, Riefenstahl claimed that she was naïve about the Nazis when she made it and had no knowledge of Hitler's <a href="/wiki/Genocide" title="Genocide">genocidal</a> or <a href="/wiki/Anti-semitic" class="mw-redirect" title="Anti-semitic">anti-semitic</a> policies. She also pointed out that <i>Triumph of the Will</i> contains "not one single <a href="/wiki/Anti-semitic" class="mw-redirect" title="Anti-semitic">anti-semitic</a> word", although it does contain a veiled comment by <a href="/wiki/Julius_Streicher" title="Julius Streicher">Julius Streicher</a>, the notorious Jew-baiter (who was hanged after the <a href="/wiki/Nuremberg_trials" title="Nuremberg trials">Nuremberg trials</a>), that "a people that does not protect its racial purity will perish".</p>
<p>However, <a href="/wiki/Roger_Ebert" title="Roger Ebert">Roger Ebert</a> has observed that for some, "the very absence of anti-semitism in <i>Triumph of the Will</i> looks like a calculation; excluding the central motif of almost all of Hitler's public speeches must have been a deliberate decision to make the film more efficient as propaganda."<sup id="cite_ref-Ebert_1994_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ebert_1994-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>Riefenstahl also repeatedly defended herself against the charge that she was a Nazi propagandist, saying that <i>Triumph of the Will</i> focuses on images over ideas, and should therefore be viewed as a <i><a href="/wiki/Gesamtkunstwerk" title="Gesamtkunstwerk">Gesamtkunstwerk</a></i> (holistic work of art).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> In 1964, she returned to this topic, saying:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>If you see this film again today you ascertain that it doesn't contain a single reconstructed scene. Everything in it is true. And it contains no tendentious commentary at all. It is history. A pure historical film&#160;... it is <a href="/wiki/Cin%C3%A9ma_v%C3%A9rit%C3%A9" title="Cinéma vérité">film-vérité</a>. It reflects the truth that was then in 1934, history. It is therefore a documentary. Not a propaganda film. Oh! I know very well what propaganda is. That consists of recreating events in order to illustrate a <a href="/wiki/Dissertation" class="mw-redirect" title="Dissertation">thesis</a>, or, in the face of certain events, to let one thing go in order to accentuate another. I found myself, me, at the heart of an event which was the reality of a certain time and a certain place. My film is composed of what stemmed from that.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>However, Riefenstahl was an active participant in the rally, though in later years she downplayed her influence significantly, claiming, "I just observed and tried to film it well. The idea that I helped to plan it is downright absurd." Ebert states that <i>Triumph of the Will</i> is "by general consent [one] of the best documentaries ever made", but added that because it reflects the ideology of a movement regarded by many as <a href="/wiki/Evil" title="Evil">evil</a>, it poses "a classic question of the contest between art and morality: Is there such a thing as pure art, or does all art make a political statement?"<sup id="cite_ref-Ebert_1994_22-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ebert_1994-22">[22]</a></sup> When reviewing the film for his "Great Movies" collection, Ebert reversed his opinion, characterizing his earlier conclusion as "the received opinion that the film is great but evil" and calling it "a terrible film, paralyzingly dull, simpleminded, overlong and not even 'manipulative', because it is too clumsy to manipulate anyone but a true believer".<sup id="cite_ref-Ebert_2008_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ebert_2008-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Susan_Sontag" title="Susan Sontag">Susan Sontag</a> considers <i>Triumph of the Will</i> the "most successful, most purely propagandistic film ever made, whose very conception negates the possibility of the filmmaker's having an aesthetic or visual conception independent of propaganda." Sontag points to Riefenstahl's involvement in the planning and design of the Nuremberg ceremonies as evidence that Riefenstahl was working as a propagandist, rather than as an artist in any sense of the word. With some 30 cameras and a crew of 150, the marches, parades, speeches, and processions were orchestrated like a movie set for Riefenstahl's film. Further, this was not the first political film made by Riefenstahl for the Third Reich (there was <i>Victory of Faith</i>, 1933), nor was it the last (<i><a href="/wiki/Day_of_Freedom" class="mw-redirect" title="Day of Freedom">Day of Freedom</a></i>, 1935, and <i><a href="/wiki/Olympia_(1938_film)" title="Olympia (1938 film)">Olympia</a></i>, 1938). "Anyone who defends Riefenstahl's films as documentary", Sontag states, "if documentary is to be distinguished from propaganda, is being disingenuous. In <i>Triumph of Will</i>, the document (the image) is no longer simply the record of reality; 'reality' has been constructed to serve the image."<sup id="cite_ref-Sontag_1975_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sontag_1975-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Brian_Winston" title="Brian Winston">Brian Winston</a>'s essay on the film in <i>The Movies as History</i> is largely a critique of Sontag's analysis. Winston argues that any filmmaker could have made the film look impressive because the Nazis' <i><a href="/wiki/Mise_en_sc%C3%A8ne" class="mw-redirect" title="Mise en scène">mise en scène</a></i> was impressive, particularly when they were offering it for camera re-stagings. In form, the film alternates repetitively between marches and speeches. Winston asks the viewers to consider if such a film should be seen as anything more than a pedestrian effort. Like Rotha, he finds the film tedious, and believes anyone who takes the time to analyze its structure will quickly agree.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Wehrmacht_objections">Wehrmacht objections</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Triumph_of_the_Will&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Wehrmacht objections">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The first controversy over <i>Triumph of the Will</i> occurred even before its release, when several generals in the Wehrmacht protested over the minimal army presence in the film. Only one scene—the review of the German cavalry—actually involved the German military. The other formations were party organizations that were not part of the military.</p>
<p>The opposition of the generals, was not simply out of personalized pique or vanity. As produced by Riefenstahl, <i>Triumph of the Will</i>, posits Germany as a leaderless mass of lost souls without any organizing institutions, or antecedent institutional leaders. And that the "new order" embodied by the Nazi Party and Hitler, provides a both new, and a singular/saving leader and institutional framework for the whole of the German nation.</p>
<p>However, the Army had been, and had seen itself as being, an institution that held shared responsibility for the leadership of the nation and state since at least the time of Fredrick the Great. The leaders of that Army had also been viewed throughout the history of the German-speaking peoples as an integral part of the leadership cadre. By omitting the Army (along with other institutions, e.g., the nobility, the Church, academia, business), the film demonstrated that the Army, as well as its leaders, was "disappeared" from what the Army considered to be its shared leadership role in the state, National Socialist or otherwise. The Army's leaders vehemently disagreed with this implied assertion of the film.</p>
<p>Hitler proposed his own "artistic" compromise where <i>Triumph of the Will</i> would open with a camera slowly tracking down a row of all the "overlooked" generals (and placate each general's ego). According to her own testimony, Riefenstahl refused his suggestion and insisted on keeping artistic control over <i>Triumph of the Will</i>. She did agree to return to the 1935 rally to make a film exclusively about the Wehrmacht, which became <i><a href="/wiki/Tag_der_Freiheit:_Unsere_Wehrmacht" title="Tag der Freiheit: Unsere Wehrmacht">Tag der Freiheit: Unsere Wehrmacht</a></i> (<i>Day of Freedom: Our Armed Forces</i>).</p>

<p><i>Triumph of the Will</i> remains well known for its striking visuals. As one historian notes, "many of the most enduring images of the [Nazi] regime and its leader derive from Riefenstahl's film."<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<p>Extensive excerpts of the film were used in <a href="/wiki/Erwin_Leiser" title="Erwin Leiser">Erwin Leiser</a>'s documentary <i>Mein Kampf</i>, produced in Sweden in 1960. Riefenstahl unsuccessfully sued the Swedish production company Minerva-Film for copyright violation, although she did receive forty thousand marks in compensation from German and Austrian distributors of the film.<sup id="cite_ref-Trimborn240_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Trimborn240-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1942, Charles A. Ridley of the <a href="/wiki/British_Ministry_of_Information" class="mw-redirect" title="British Ministry of Information">British Ministry of Information</a> made a short propaganda film, <i>Lambeth Walk – Nazi Style</i>, which edited footage of Hitler and German soldiers from the film to make it appear they were marching and dancing to the song "<a href="/wiki/The_Lambeth_Walk" title="The Lambeth Walk">The Lambeth Walk</a>".<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[note 1]</a></sup> The targeted-at-Nazis parody of "The Lambeth Walk" (a British dance that had been popular in swing clubs in Germany which the Nazis denounced as "Jewish mischief and animalistic hopping"<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[27]</a></sup>) so enraged <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Goebbels" title="Joseph Goebbels">Joseph Goebbels</a> that reportedly he ran out of the screening room kicking chairs and screaming profanities.<sup id="cite_ref-Parody_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Parody-29">[28]</a></sup> The propaganda film was distributed uncredited to newsreel companies, who would supply their own narration.<sup id="cite_ref-Parody_29-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Parody-29">[28]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Charlie_Chaplin" title="Charlie Chaplin">Charlie Chaplin</a>'s satire <i><a href="/wiki/The_Great_Dictator" title="The Great Dictator">The Great Dictator</a></i> (1940) was inspired in large part by <i>Triumph of the Will</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-Trimborn123ff_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Trimborn123ff-30">[29]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Frank_Capra" title="Frank Capra">Frank Capra</a> used significant footage, with a mocking narration in the <a href="/wiki/Prelude_to_War" title="Prelude to War">first installment</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Propaganda" title="Propaganda">propagandistic</a> film produced by the United States Army <i><a href="/wiki/Why_We_Fight" title="Why We Fight">Why We Fight</a></i> as an exposure of Nazi militarism and totalitarianism to American soldiers and sailors.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[30]</a></sup> The film has been studied by many contemporary artists, including film directors <a href="/wiki/Peter_Jackson" title="Peter Jackson">Peter Jackson</a>, <a href="/wiki/George_Lucas" title="George Lucas">George Lucas</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ridley_Scott" title="Ridley Scott">Ridley Scott</a>.</p>
