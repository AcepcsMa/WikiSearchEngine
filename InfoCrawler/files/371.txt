<p><b>Charles IV</b> (<a href="/wiki/Czech_language" title="Czech language">Czech</a>: <span lang="cs" xml:lang="cs"><i>Karel IV.</i></span>, <a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a>: <span lang="de" xml:lang="de"><i>Karl IV.</i></span>, <a href="/wiki/Latin_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Latin language">Latin</a>: <span lang="la" xml:lang="la"><i>Carolus IV</i></span>; 14 May 1316 ‚Äì 29 November 1378<sup id="cite_ref-Herzfeld_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Herzfeld-1">[1]</a></sup>), born <b>Wenceslaus</b>,<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> was a <a href="/wiki/List_of_Bohemian_monarchs" title="List of Bohemian monarchs">King of Bohemia</a> and the first King of Bohemia to also become <a href="/wiki/Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Holy Roman Emperor">Holy Roman Emperor</a>. He was a member of the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Luxembourg" title="House of Luxembourg">House of Luxembourg</a> from his father's side and the <a href="/wiki/House_of_P%C5%99emyslid" class="mw-redirect" title="House of P≈ôemyslid">House of P≈ôemyslid</a> from his mother's side, which he emphasised, because it gave him two saints as direct ancestors.</p>
<p>He was the eldest son and heir of King <a href="/wiki/John_of_Bohemia" title="John of Bohemia">John of Bohemia</a>, who died at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Cr%C3%A9cy" title="Battle of Cr√©cy">Battle of Cr√©cy</a> on 26&#160;August 1346. Charles inherited the <a href="/wiki/Count_of_Luxembourg" class="mw-redirect" title="Count of Luxembourg">County of Luxembourg</a> from his father and was elected king of the <a href="/wiki/King_of_Bohemia" class="mw-redirect" title="King of Bohemia">Kingdom of Bohemia</a>. On 2&#160;September 1347, Charles was crowned King of Bohemia.</p>
<p>On 11 July 1346, the <a href="/wiki/Prince-electors" class="mw-redirect" title="Prince-electors">prince-electors</a> chose him as <a href="/wiki/King_of_the_Romans" title="King of the Romans">King of the Romans</a> (<i>rex Romanorum</i>) in opposition to <a href="/wiki/Louis_IV,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Louis IV, Holy Roman Emperor">Emperor Louis&#160;IV</a>. Charles was crowned on 26&#160;November 1346 in <a href="/wiki/Bonn" title="Bonn">Bonn</a>. After his opponent died, he was re-elected in 1349 and crowned King of the Romans. In 1355 he was crowned <a href="/wiki/King_of_Italy" title="King of Italy">King of Italy</a> and Holy Roman Emperor. With his coronation as <a href="/wiki/King_of_Burgundy" class="mw-redirect" title="King of Burgundy">King of Burgundy</a> in 1365, he became the personal ruler of all the kingdoms of the <a href="/wiki/Holy_Roman_Empire" title="Holy Roman Empire">Holy Roman Empire</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Life">Life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Charles_IV,_Holy_Roman_Emperor&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Charles IV was born to <a href="/wiki/John_the_Blind" class="mw-redirect" title="John the Blind">King John</a> of the Luxembourg dynasty and <a href="/wiki/Elisabeth_of_Bohemia_(1292%E2%80%931330)" class="mw-redirect" title="Elisabeth of Bohemia (1292‚Äì1330)">Queen Elizabeth of Bohemia</a> of the Czech <a href="/wiki/Premyslid_dynasty" class="mw-redirect" title="Premyslid dynasty">Premyslid Dynasty</a> in <a href="/wiki/Prague" title="Prague">Prague</a>. He was originally named Wenceslaus (<i>V√°clav</i>), the name of his maternal grandfather, <a href="/wiki/Wenceslaus_II_of_Bohemia" title="Wenceslaus II of Bohemia">King Wenceslaus II</a>. He chose the name Charles at his <a href="/wiki/Confirmation" title="Confirmation">confirmation</a> in honor of his uncle, <a href="/wiki/Charles_IV_of_France" title="Charles IV of France">King Charles IV of France</a>, at whose court he was resident for seven years.</p>
<p>He received <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">French</a> education and was literate and fluent in five languages: <a href="/wiki/Latin_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Latin language">Latin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Czech_language" title="Czech language">Czech</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a>, <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Italian_language" title="Italian language">Italian</a>. In 1331 he gained some experience of warfare in Italy with his father. At the beginning of 1333, Charles went to <a href="/wiki/Lucca" title="Lucca">Lucca</a> (<a href="/wiki/Tuscany" title="Tuscany">Tuscany</a>) to consolidate his rule there. In an effort to defend the city, Charles founded the nearby fortress and the town of <a href="/wiki/Montecarlo,_Tuscany" title="Montecarlo, Tuscany">Montecarlo</a> (Charles' Mountain).<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> From 1333 he administered the <a href="/wiki/Lands_of_the_Bohemian_Crown" title="Lands of the Bohemian Crown">lands of the Bohemian Crown</a> due to his father's frequent absence and deteriorating eyesight. In 1334, Charles was named <a href="/wiki/Margrave_of_Moravia" class="mw-redirect" title="Margrave of Moravia">Margrave of Moravia</a>, the traditional title for heirs to the throne. Two years later, he assumed the government of <a href="/wiki/Tyrol_(state)" title="Tyrol (state)">Tyrol</a> on behalf of his brother, <a href="/wiki/John_Henry,_Margrave_of_Moravia" title="John Henry, Margrave of Moravia">John Henry</a>, and was soon actively involved in a struggle for the possession of this county.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="King_of_the_Romans">King of the Romans</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Charles_IV,_Holy_Roman_Emperor&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: King of the Romans">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On 11 July 1346, in consequence of an alliance between his father and <a href="/wiki/Pope_Clement_VI" title="Pope Clement VI">Pope Clement VI</a>, relentless enemy of the emperor <a href="/wiki/Louis_IV,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Louis IV, Holy Roman Emperor">Louis IV</a>, Charles was chosen as Roman <a href="/wiki/Anti-king" title="Anti-king">king in opposition</a> to Louis by some of the <a href="/wiki/Prince-electors" class="mw-redirect" title="Prince-electors">prince-electors</a> at <a href="/wiki/Rhens" title="Rhens">Rhens</a>. As he had previously promised to be subservient to Clement, he made extensive concessions to the pope in 1347. Confirming the papacy in the possession of vast territories, he promised to annul the acts of Louis against Clement, to take no part in Italian affairs, and to defend and protect the church.</p>

<p>Charles IV was in a very weak position in Germany. Owing to the terms of his election, he was derisively referred to as a "Priests' King" (<i>Pfaffenk√∂nig</i>). Many bishops and nearly all of the <a href="/wiki/Imperial_State" class="mw-redirect" title="Imperial State">Imperial cities</a> remained loyal to Louis the Bavarian. Worse still, Charles backed the wrong side in the <a href="/wiki/Hundred_Years%27_War" title="Hundred Years' War">Hundred Years' War</a>, losing his father and many of his best knights at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Cr%C3%A9cy" title="Battle of Cr√©cy">Battle of Cr√©cy</a> in August 1346, with Charles himself escaping from the field wounded.</p>
<p>Civil war in Germany was prevented, however, when Louis IV died on 11 October 1347, after suffering a stroke during a bear hunt. In January 1349, <a href="/wiki/House_of_Wittelsbach" title="House of Wittelsbach">House of Wittelsbach</a> partisans attempted to secure the election of <a href="/wiki/G%C3%BCnther_von_Schwarzburg" title="G√ºnther von Schwarzburg">G√ºnther von Schwarzburg</a> as king, but he attracted few supporters and died unnoticed and unmourned after a few months. Thereafter, Charles faced no direct threat to his claim to the Imperial throne.</p>
<p>Charles initially worked to secure his power base. <a href="/wiki/Bohemia" title="Bohemia">Bohemia</a> had remained untouched by the plague. <a href="/wiki/Prague" title="Prague">Prague</a> became his capital, and he rebuilt the city on the model of Paris, establishing the <a href="/wiki/New_Town,_Prague" title="New Town, Prague">New Town</a> (<i>Nov√© Mƒõsto</i>). In 1348, he founded the <a href="/wiki/Charles_University_in_Prague" class="mw-redirect" title="Charles University in Prague">Charles University in Prague</a>, which was later named after him and was the first university in <a href="/wiki/Central_Europe" title="Central Europe">Central Europe</a>. This served as a training ground for bureaucrats and lawyers. Soon Prague emerged as the intellectual and cultural center of Central Europe.</p>

<p>Having made good use of the difficulties of his opponents, Charles was again elected in <a href="/wiki/Frankfurt" title="Frankfurt">Frankfurt</a> on 17 June 1349 and re-crowned at <a href="/wiki/Aachen" title="Aachen">Aachen</a> on 25 July 1349. He was soon the undisputed ruler of the Empire. Gifts or promises had won the support of the Rhenish and <a href="/wiki/Swabia" title="Swabia">Swabian</a> towns; a marriage alliance secured the friendship of the Habsburgs; and an alliance with <a href="/wiki/Rudolf_II,_Duke_of_Bavaria" class="mw-redirect" title="Rudolf II, Duke of Bavaria">Rudolf II of Bavaria</a>, <a href="/wiki/Count_Palatine_of_the_Rhine" class="mw-redirect" title="Count Palatine of the Rhine">Count Palatine of the Rhine</a>, was obtained when Charles, who had become a widower in 1348, married Rudolph's daughter <a href="/wiki/Anna_of_Bavaria" class="mw-redirect" title="Anna of Bavaria">Anna</a>.</p>
<p>In 1350 the king was visited at Prague by the Roman tribune <a href="/wiki/Cola_di_Rienzo" title="Cola di Rienzo">Cola di Rienzo</a>, who urged him to go to Italy, where the poet <a href="/wiki/Petrarch" title="Petrarch">Petrarch</a> and the citizens of <a href="/wiki/Florence" title="Florence">Florence</a> also implored his presence.<sup id="cite_ref-F.X.1_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-F.X.1-5">[5]</a></sup> Turning a deaf ear to these entreaties, Charles kept Cola in prison for a year, and then handed him as a prisoner to Clement at <a href="/wiki/Avignon" title="Avignon">Avignon</a>.</p>
<p>Outside Prague, Charles attempted to expand the Bohemian crown lands, using his imperial authority to acquire fiefs in <a href="/wiki/Silesia" title="Silesia">Silesia</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Upper_Palatinate" title="Upper Palatinate">Upper Palatinate</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Franconia" title="Franconia">Franconia</a>. The latter regions comprised "New Bohemia," a string of possessions intended to link Bohemia with the Luxemburg territories in the Rhineland. The Bohemian estates, however, were not willing to support Charles in these ventures. When Charles sought to codify Bohemian law in the <a href="/wiki/Maiestas_Carolina" title="Maiestas Carolina">Maiestas Carolina</a> of 1355, he met with sharp resistance. After that point, Charles found it expedient to scale back his efforts at centralization.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Holy_Roman_Emperor">Holy Roman Emperor</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Charles_IV,_Holy_Roman_Emperor&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Holy Roman Emperor">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Goldene-bulle_1c-480x475.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/91/Goldene-bulle_1c-480x475.jpg/220px-Goldene-bulle_1c-480x475.jpg" width="220" height="218" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/91/Goldene-bulle_1c-480x475.jpg/330px-Goldene-bulle_1c-480x475.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/91/Goldene-bulle_1c-480x475.jpg/440px-Goldene-bulle_1c-480x475.jpg 2x" data-file-width="480" data-file-height="475" /></a>

<p>In 1354 Charles crossed the <a href="/wiki/Alps" title="Alps">Alps</a> without an army, received the <a href="/wiki/Iron_Crown_of_Lombardy" title="Iron Crown of Lombardy">Lombard crown</a> in <a href="/wiki/Basilica_of_Sant%27Ambrogio" title="Basilica of Sant'Ambrogio">St. Ambrose Basilica</a>, <a href="/wiki/Milan" title="Milan">Milan</a>, on 5&#160;January&#160;1355, and was crowned emperor at <a href="/wiki/Rome" title="Rome">Rome</a> by a <a href="/wiki/Cardinal_(Catholicism)" class="mw-redirect" title="Cardinal (Catholicism)">cardinal</a> in April of the same year.<sup id="cite_ref-Palacky_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Palacky-6">[6]</a></sup> His sole object appears to have been to obtain the Imperial crown in peace, in accordance with a promise previously made to Pope Clement. He only remained in the city for a few hours, in spite of the expressed wishes of the Roman people. Having virtually abandoned all the Imperial rights in Italy, the emperor re-crossed the Alps, pursued by the scornful words of Petrarch, but laden with considerable wealth.<sup id="cite_ref-F.XIX.12_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-F.XIX.12-7">[7]</a></sup> On his return, Charles was occupied with the administration of the Empire, then just recovering from the <a href="/wiki/Black_Death" title="Black Death">Black Death</a>, and in 1356 he promulgated the famous <a href="/wiki/Golden_Bull_of_1356" title="Golden Bull of 1356">Golden Bull</a> to regulate the election of the king.</p>
<p>Having given <a href="/wiki/Moravia" title="Moravia">Moravia</a> to one brother, <a href="/wiki/John_Henry_of_Bohemia" class="mw-redirect" title="John Henry of Bohemia">John Henry</a>, and erected the <a href="/wiki/County_of_Luxembourg" class="mw-redirect" title="County of Luxembourg">county of Luxembourg</a> into a duchy for another, <a href="/wiki/Wenceslaus_I,_Duke_of_Luxembourg" title="Wenceslaus I, Duke of Luxembourg">Wenceslaus</a>, he was unremitting in his efforts to secure other territories as compensation and to strengthen the Bohemian monarchy. To this end he purchased part of the upper <a href="/wiki/Palatinate_of_the_Rhine" class="mw-redirect" title="Palatinate of the Rhine">Palatinate of the Rhine</a> in&#160;1353, and in 1367 annexed <a href="/wiki/Lower_Lusatia" title="Lower Lusatia">Lower Lusatia</a> to Bohemia and bought numerous estates in various parts of Germany. On the death of <a href="/wiki/Meinhard_III_of_Gorizia-Tyrol" class="mw-redirect" title="Meinhard III of Gorizia-Tyrol">Meinhard, Duke of Upper Bavaria</a> and <a href="/wiki/Count_of_Tyrol" class="mw-redirect" title="Count of Tyrol">Count of Tyrol</a>, in 1363, <a href="/wiki/Upper_Bavaria" title="Upper Bavaria">Upper Bavaria</a> was claimed by the sons of the emperor Louis&#160;IV, and <a href="/wiki/Tyrol_(state)" title="Tyrol (state)">Tyrol</a> by <a href="/wiki/Rudolf_IV,_Duke_of_Austria" title="Rudolf IV, Duke of Austria">Rudolf&#160;IV, Duke of Austria</a>. Both claims were admitted by Charles on the understanding that if these families died out both territories should pass to the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Luxembourg" title="House of Luxembourg">House of Luxembourg</a>. At about the same time he was promised the succession to the <a href="/wiki/Margravate_of_Brandenburg" class="mw-redirect" title="Margravate of Brandenburg">Margravate of Brandenburg</a>, which he actually obtained for his son Wenceslaus in&#160;1373.</p>

<p><a href="/wiki/Casimir_III_the_Great" title="Casimir III the Great">Casimir III of Poland</a> and <a href="/wiki/Louis_I_of_Hungary" title="Louis I of Hungary">Louis I of Hungary</a> entered a conspiracy against Charles and managed to persuade <a href="/wiki/Otto_V,_Duke_of_Bavaria" title="Otto V, Duke of Bavaria">Otto V of Bavaria</a> to join. After the repeal of the estate contract by margrave Otto, in early July 1371, Charles IV declared hostilities and invaded Brandenburg; after two years of conflict the <a href="/wiki/Margraviate_of_Brandenburg" title="Margraviate of Brandenburg">Margraviate of Brandenburg</a> in 1373 became part of the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Bohemia" title="Kingdom of Bohemia">Czech lands</a>. He also gained a considerable portion of <a href="/wiki/Silesia" title="Silesia">Silesian</a> territory, partly by inheritance through his third wife, <a href="/wiki/Anna_von_Schweidnitz" title="Anna von Schweidnitz">Anna von Schweidnitz</a>, daughter of <a href="/wiki/Henry_II,_Duke_of_%C5%9Awidnica" title="Henry II, Duke of ≈öwidnica">Henry II, Duke of ≈öwidnica</a> and <a href="/wiki/Catherine_of_Hungary_(d._1355)" class="mw-redirect" title="Catherine of Hungary (d. 1355)">Catherine of Hungary</a>. In 1365 Charles visited <a href="/wiki/Pope_Urban_V" title="Pope Urban V">Pope Urban&#160;V</a> at <a href="/wiki/Avignon" title="Avignon">Avignon</a> and undertook to escort him to Rome; on the same occasion he was crowned King of <a href="/wiki/Lower_Burgundy" title="Lower Burgundy">Burgundy</a> at <a href="/wiki/Arles" title="Arles">Arles</a>.</p>
<p>His second journey to Italy took place in 1368, when he had a meeting with Pope Urban&#160;V at <a href="/wiki/Viterbo" title="Viterbo">Viterbo</a>, was besieged in his palace at <a href="/wiki/Siena" title="Siena">Siena</a>, and left the country before the end of 1369. During his later years, the emperor took little part in German affairs beyond securing the election of his son Wenceslaus as king of the Romans in&#160;1376, and negotiating a peace between the <a href="/wiki/Swabian_League_of_Cities" title="Swabian League of Cities">Swabian League of Cities</a> and some nobles in&#160;1378. After dividing his lands between his three sons and his nephews,<sup id="cite_ref-Herzfeld_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Herzfeld-1">[1]</a></sup> he died in November 1378 at <a href="/wiki/Prague" title="Prague">Prague</a>, where he was buried, and where a statue was erected to his memory in&#160;1848.</p>
<p>Charles IV suffered from <a href="/wiki/Gout" title="Gout">gout</a> (metabolic arthritis), a painful disease quite common in that time.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Evaluation_and_legacy">Evaluation and legacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Charles_IV,_Holy_Roman_Emperor&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Evaluation and legacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The reign of Charles IV was characterised by a transformation in the nature of the Empire and is remembered as the <a href="/wiki/Golden_Age" title="Golden Age">Golden Age</a> of Bohemia. He promulgated the <a href="/wiki/Golden_Bull_of_1356" title="Golden Bull of 1356">Golden Bull of 1356</a> whereby the succession to the imperial title was laid down, which held for the next four centuries.</p>
<p>He also organized the states of the empire into peace-keeping <a href="/wiki/Confederations" class="mw-redirect" title="Confederations">confederations</a>. In these, the Imperial cities figured prominently. The Swabian Landfriede confederation of 1370 was made up almost entirely of <a href="/wiki/Free_imperial_city" title="Free imperial city">Imperial Cities</a>. At the same time, the leagues were organized and led by the crown and its agents. As with the electors, the cities that served in these leagues were given privileges to aid in their efforts to keep the peace.</p>
<p>He assured his dominance over the eastern borders of the Empire through succession treaties with the <a href="/wiki/Habsburg" class="mw-redirect" title="Habsburg">Habsburgs</a> and the purchase of Brandenburg. He also claimed imperial lordship over the crusader states of <a href="/wiki/Prussia" title="Prussia">Prussia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Livonia" title="Livonia">Livonia</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Patronage_of_culture_and_the_arts">Patronage of culture and the arts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Charles_IV,_Holy_Roman_Emperor&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Patronage of culture and the arts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Karl_IV_HRR.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/63/Karl_IV_HRR.jpg/170px-Karl_IV_HRR.jpg" width="170" height="254" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/63/Karl_IV_HRR.jpg/255px-Karl_IV_HRR.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/63/Karl_IV_HRR.jpg/340px-Karl_IV_HRR.jpg 2x" data-file-width="428" data-file-height="639" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Karl_IV_HRR.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Statue of Charles IV near <a href="/wiki/Charles_Bridge" title="Charles Bridge">Charles Bridge</a> (1848), Prague, by <a href="/wiki/Ernst_Julius_H%C3%A4hnel" title="Ernst Julius H√§hnel">Ernst Julius H√§hnel</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Prague" title="Prague">Prague</a> became the capital of the Holy Roman Empire during the reign of Charles IV. The name of the royal founder and patron remains on many monuments and institutions, for example <a href="/wiki/Charles_University" title="Charles University">Charles University</a>, <a href="/wiki/Charles_Bridge" title="Charles Bridge">Charles Bridge</a>, <a href="/wiki/Charles_Square" title="Charles Square">Charles Square</a>. <a href="/wiki/Czech_Gothic_architecture" title="Czech Gothic architecture">High Gothic</a> <a href="/wiki/Prague_Castle" title="Prague Castle">Prague Castle</a> and part of the <a href="/wiki/Saint_Vitus_Cathedral" class="mw-redirect" title="Saint Vitus Cathedral">cathedral of Saint Vitus</a> by <a href="/wiki/Peter_Parler" title="Peter Parler">Peter Parler</a> were also built under his patronage. Finally, the first flowering of manuscript painting in Prague dates from Charles' reign. In the present <a href="/wiki/Czech_Republic" title="Czech Republic">Czech Republic</a>, he is still regarded as <i><a href="/wiki/Pater_Patriae" title="Pater Patriae">Pater Patriae</a></i> (<a href="/wiki/Father_of_the_Nation" title="Father of the Nation">father of the country</a> or <i>otec vlasti</i>), a title first coined by <a href="/wiki/Adalbertus_Ranconis_de_Ericinio" title="Adalbertus Ranconis de Ericinio">Adalbertus Ranconis de Ericinio</a> at his funeral.</p>
<p>Charles also had strong ties to <a href="/wiki/Nuremberg" title="Nuremberg">Nuremberg</a>, staying within its city walls 52 times and thereby strengthening its reputation amongst German cities. Charles was the patron of the <a href="/wiki/Nuremberg_Frauenkirche" class="mw-redirect" title="Nuremberg Frauenkirche">Nuremberg Frauenkirche</a>, built between 1352 and 1362 (the architect was likely <a href="/wiki/Peter_Parler" title="Peter Parler">Peter Parler</a>), where the imperial court worshipped during its stays in Nuremberg.</p>
<p>Charles's imperial policy was focused on the dynastic sphere and abandoned the lofty ideal of the Empire as a universal monarchy of Christendom. In 1353 he granted the <a href="/wiki/Duchy_of_Luxembourg" class="mw-redirect" title="Duchy of Luxembourg">Duchy of Luxembourg</a> to his half-brother, <a href="/wiki/Wenceslaus_I,_Duke_of_Luxembourg" title="Wenceslaus I, Duke of Luxembourg">Wenceslaus</a>. He concentrated his energies chiefly on the economic and intellectual development of Bohemia, where he founded the university in 1348 and encouraged the <a href="/wiki/Renaissance_humanism" title="Renaissance humanism">early humanists</a>. He corresponded with <a href="/wiki/Petrarch" title="Petrarch">Petrarch</a> and invited him to visit the royal residence in Prague, whilst the Italian hoped ‚Äî to no avail ‚Äî to see Charles move his residence to Rome and reawaken tradition of the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Empire" title="Roman Empire">Roman Empire</a>.</p>
<p>Charles's sister <a href="/wiki/Bonne_of_Bohemia" class="mw-redirect" title="Bonne of Bohemia">Bona</a> married the eldest son of <a href="/wiki/Philip_VI_of_France" title="Philip VI of France">Philip&#160;VI of France</a>, the future <a href="/wiki/John_II_of_France" title="John II of France">John&#160;II of France</a>, in 1335. Thus, Charles was the maternal uncle of <a href="/wiki/Charles_V_of_France" title="Charles V of France">Charles&#160;V of France</a>, who solicited his relative's advice at <a href="/wiki/Metz" title="Metz">Metz</a> in 1356 during the <a href="/wiki/Parisian_Revolt" class="mw-redirect" title="Parisian Revolt">Parisian Revolt</a>. This family connection was celebrated publicly when Charles made a solemn visit to his nephew in 1378, just months before his death. A detailed account of the occasion, enriched by many splendid miniatures, can be found in Charles V's copy of the <i><a href="/wiki/Grandes_Chroniques_de_France" title="Grandes Chroniques de France">Grandes Chroniques de France</a></i>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Family_and_children">Family and children</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Charles_IV,_Holy_Roman_Emperor&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Family and children">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Karl_IV_Blanca_Valois.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/87/Karl_IV_Blanca_Valois.jpg/220px-Karl_IV_Blanca_Valois.jpg" width="220" height="283" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/87/Karl_IV_Blanca_Valois.jpg/330px-Karl_IV_Blanca_Valois.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/87/Karl_IV_Blanca_Valois.jpg/440px-Karl_IV_Blanca_Valois.jpg 2x" data-file-width="441" data-file-height="567" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Karl_IV_Blanca_Valois.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Charles and his first wife, Blanche</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Charles was married four times. His first wife was <a href="/wiki/Blanche_of_Valois" title="Blanche of Valois">Blanche of Valois</a>, (1316‚Äì48), daughter of <a href="/wiki/Charles_of_Valois" class="mw-redirect" title="Charles of Valois">Charles</a>, <a href="/wiki/Count_of_Valois" class="mw-redirect" title="Count of Valois">Count of Valois</a>, and a half-sister of <a href="/wiki/Philip_VI_of_France" title="Philip VI of France">Philip&#160;VI of France</a>. They had three children:</p>
<ul>
<li>son (b.1334), died young</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Margaret_of_Bohemia,_Queen_of_Hungary" title="Margaret of Bohemia, Queen of Hungary">Margaret of Bohemia</a> (1335 - 1349); married <a href="/wiki/Louis_I_of_Hungary" title="Louis I of Hungary">Louis&#160;I of Hungary</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Catherine_of_Bohemia" title="Catherine of Bohemia">Catherine of Bohemia</a> (1342‚Äì95); married <a href="/wiki/Rudolf_IV_of_Austria" class="mw-redirect" title="Rudolf IV of Austria">Rudolf&#160;IV of Austria</a><sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Otto_V,_Duke_of_Bavaria" title="Otto V, Duke of Bavaria">Otto&#160;V, Duke of Bavaria</a>, Elector of Brandenburg.</li>
</ul>
<p>He secondly married <a href="/wiki/Anna_of_Bavaria" class="mw-redirect" title="Anna of Bavaria">Anna of Bavaria</a>, (1329‚Äì53), daughter of <a href="/wiki/Rudolf_II,_Duke_of_Bavaria" class="mw-redirect" title="Rudolf II, Duke of Bavaria">Rudolf II, Duke of Bavaria</a>; they had one son:</p>
<ul>
<li>Wenceslaus (1350‚Äì51).</li>
</ul>
<p>His third wife was <a href="/wiki/Anna_von_Schweidnitz" title="Anna von Schweidnitz">Anna von Schweidnitz</a>, (1339‚Äì62), daughter of <a href="/wiki/Henry_II,_Duke_of_%C5%9Awidnica" title="Henry II, Duke of ≈öwidnica">Henry&#160;II, Duke of ≈öwidnica</a> and <a href="/wiki/Catherine_of_Hungary_(d._1355)" class="mw-redirect" title="Catherine of Hungary (d. 1355)">Katharina of Anjou</a> (daughter of <a href="/wiki/Charles_I_of_Hungary" title="Charles I of Hungary">Charles&#160;I Robert, King of Hungary</a>), by whom he had three children:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Elisabeth_of_Bohemia_(1358%E2%80%931373)" title="Elisabeth of Bohemia (1358‚Äì1373)">Elisabeth of Bohemia</a> (19 April 1358 ‚Äì 4 September 1373); married <a href="/wiki/Albert_III_of_Austria" class="mw-redirect" title="Albert III of Austria">Albert&#160;III of Austria</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Wenceslaus,_King_of_the_Romans" class="mw-redirect" title="Wenceslaus, King of the Romans">Wenceslaus</a> (1361‚Äì1419); later elected King of Germany (formally King of the Romans) and, on his father's death, became King of Bohemia (as Wenceslaus&#160;IV) and Emperor-elect of the Holy Roman Empire; married firstly to <a href="/wiki/Joanna_of_Bavaria" title="Joanna of Bavaria">Joanna of Bavaria</a> in 1370 and secondly to <a href="/wiki/Sophia_of_Bavaria" title="Sophia of Bavaria">Sophia of Bavaria</a> in 1389.</li>
<li>son (born and died 11&#160;July 1362).</li>
</ul>
<p>His fourth wife was <a href="/wiki/Elizabeth_of_Pomerania" title="Elizabeth of Pomerania">Elizabeth of Pomerania</a>, (1345 or 1347‚Äì1393), daughter of Duke <a href="/wiki/Bogislaw_V,_Duke_of_Pomerania" title="Bogislaw V, Duke of Pomerania">Bogislaw&#160;V, Duke of Pomerania</a> and <a href="/wiki/Elisabeth_of_Poland_(1326-1361)" class="mw-redirect" title="Elisabeth of Poland (1326-1361)">Elisabeth of Poland</a>, daughter of <a href="/wiki/Casimir_III_of_Poland" class="mw-redirect" title="Casimir III of Poland">Casimir&#160;III of Poland</a>. They had six children:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Anne_of_Bohemia" title="Anne of Bohemia">Anne of Bohemia</a> (1366‚Äì94); married <a href="/wiki/Richard_II_of_England" title="Richard II of England">Richard II of England</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Sigismund,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Sigismund, Holy Roman Emperor">Sigismund</a> (1368‚Äì1437); later Holy Roman Emperor, King of Hungary and Bohemia, and Margrave of Brandenburg; married firstly <a href="/wiki/Mary,_Queen_of_Hungary" title="Mary, Queen of Hungary">Mary of Hungary</a> in 1385, and secondly to <a href="/wiki/Barbara_of_Cilli" title="Barbara of Cilli">Barbara of Cilli</a> in 1405/1408.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/John_of_G%C3%B6rlitz" title="John of G√∂rlitz">John of G√∂rlitz</a> (1370‚Äì96); later Margrave of Moravia and Duke of G√∂rlitz; married <a href="/wiki/Richardis_Catherine_of_Sweden" class="mw-redirect" title="Richardis Catherine of Sweden">Richardis Catherine of Sweden</a>. His only daughter and heiress was <a href="/wiki/Duchess_of_Luxembourg" class="mw-redirect" title="Duchess of Luxembourg">Duchess of Luxembourg</a>.</li>
<li>Charles (13 March 1372 ‚Äì 24 July 1373).</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Margaret_of_Bohemia,_Burgravine_of_Nuremberg" title="Margaret of Bohemia, Burgravine of Nuremberg">Margaret of Bohemia</a> (1373‚Äì1410); married <a href="/wiki/John_III,_Burgrave_of_Nuremberg" title="John III, Burgrave of Nuremberg">John&#160;III</a>, <a href="/wiki/Burgraviate_of_Nuremberg" title="Burgraviate of Nuremberg">Burgrave of Nuremberg</a>.</li>
<li>Henry (1377‚Äì78)</li>
</ul>
