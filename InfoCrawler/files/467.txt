<p>The <b>subsequent Nuremberg trials</b> (formally the <b>Trials of War Criminals before the Nuremberg Military Tribunals</b>) were a series of twelve U.S. <a href="/wiki/Military_tribunal" class="mw-redirect" title="Military tribunal">military tribunals</a> for <a href="/wiki/War_crime" title="War crime">war crimes</a> against members of the leadership of <a href="/wiki/Nazi_Germany" title="Nazi Germany">Nazi Germany</a>, held in the <a href="/wiki/Palace_of_Justice_(Nuremberg)" class="mw-redirect" title="Palace of Justice (Nuremberg)">Palace of Justice</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nuremberg" title="Nuremberg">Nuremberg</a>, after <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a> from 1946 to 1949 following the <a href="/wiki/Nuremberg_Trials" class="mw-redirect" title="Nuremberg Trials">Trial of the Major War Criminals</a> before the <a href="/wiki/International_Military_Tribunal" class="mw-redirect" title="International Military Tribunal">International Military Tribunal</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Heller_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Heller-1">[1]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Background">Background</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Subsequent_Nuremberg_trials&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Although it had been initially planned to hold more than just one international trial at the IMT, the growing differences between the victorious allies (the United States, United Kingdom, France, and <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet Union</a>) made this impossible. However, the Control Council Law No. 10, which the <a href="/wiki/Allied_Control_Council" title="Allied Control Council">Allied Control Council</a> had issued on 20 December 1945, empowered any of the occupying authorities to try suspected war criminals in their respective occupation zones. Based on this law, the U.S. authorities proceeded after the end of the initial Nuremberg Trial against the major war criminals to hold another twelve trials in Nuremberg. The judges in all these trials were American, and so were the prosecutors; the Chief of Counsel for the Prosecution was <a href="/wiki/Brigadier_general_(United_States)" title="Brigadier general (United States)">Brigadier General</a> <a href="/wiki/Telford_Taylor" title="Telford Taylor">Telford Taylor</a>. In the other occupation zones similar trials took place.<sup id="cite_ref-Heller_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Heller-1">[1]</a></sup></p>

<p>The Nuremberg process initiated 3,887 cases of which 3400 were dropped. Less than 500 cases (489) went to trial that involved 1672 defendants. 1416 were found guilty, but less than 200 were executed. Another 279 defendants were sent to life in prison but by the 1950s almost all of them had been released.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Many of the longer prison sentences were reduced substantially by decree of high commissioner <a href="/wiki/John_J._McCloy" title="John J. McCloy">John J. McCloy</a> in 1951, and 10 outstanding death sentences from the <i>Einsatzgruppen</i> Trial were converted to prison terms. The same year, an <a href="/wiki/Amnesty" title="Amnesty">amnesty</a> released many of those who had received prison sentences.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Criticism">Criticism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Subsequent_Nuremberg_trials&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Criticism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Some of the NMTs have been criticised for their conclusion that "moral bombing" of civilians, including its <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_weapon" title="Nuclear weapon">nuclear variety</a>, was legal, and for their judgement that, in certain situations, executing civilians in reprisal was permissible.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Conduct_of_the_prosecution">Conduct of the prosecution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Subsequent_Nuremberg_trials&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Conduct of the prosecution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In a 2005 interview for the <i><a href="/wiki/Washington_Post" class="mw-redirect" title="Washington Post">Washington Post</a></i>, <a href="/wiki/Benjamin_B._Ferencz" class="mw-redirect" title="Benjamin B. Ferencz">Benjamin B. Ferencz</a>, Chief Prosecutor for the United States Army at the Einsatzgruppen Trial, revealed some of his activities during his period in Germany. "Someone who was not there could never really grasp how unreal the situation was," he said. The Americans delivered at least a dozen low-ranking German SS suspects to <a href="/wiki/Displaced_persons_camp" title="Displaced persons camp">displaced persons camps</a> for the purpose of having them executed by the DPs ("displaced persons"), without prior trial or sentencing. Under military law at that time, it was legal to hand over suspects to their victims for further questioning.<sup id="cite_ref-brz_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-brz-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<dl>
<dd>"I once saw DPs beat an SS man and then strap him to the steel gurney of a crematorium. They slid him in the oven, turned on the heat and took him back out. Beat him again, and put him back in until he was burnt alive. I did nothing to stop it. I suppose I could have brandished my weapon or shot in the air, but I was not inclined to do so. Does that make me an <a href="/wiki/Accomplice" title="Accomplice">accomplice</a> to murder?"<sup id="cite_ref-brz_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-brz-4">[4]</a></sup></dd>
</dl>
<p>In the interview, Ferencz also pointed out that the military legal norms at the time permitted actions that would not be possible today.</p>
<dl>
<dd>"You know how I got witness statements? I'd go into a village where, say, an American pilot had parachuted and been beaten to death and line everyone up against the wall. Then I'd say, 'Anyone who lies will be shot on the spot.' It never occurred to me that statements taken under duress would be invalid."<sup id="cite_ref-brz_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-brz-4">[4]</a></sup></dd>
</dl>
