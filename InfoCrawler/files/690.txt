<p><b>Gera</b> is the third-largest city in <a href="/wiki/Thuringia" title="Thuringia">Thuringia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a>, with 96,000 inhabitants, located 55 kilometres (34 miles) south of <a href="/wiki/Leipzig" title="Leipzig">Leipzig</a>, 75&#160;km (47 miles) east of <a href="/wiki/Erfurt" title="Erfurt">Erfurt</a> and 120&#160;km (75 miles) west of <a href="/wiki/Dresden" title="Dresden">Dresden</a>.</p>
<p>Gera was first mentioned in 995 and developed to a city during the 13th century. Later, it was the residence of several lines of the <a href="/wiki/Reuss_(state)" class="mw-redirect" title="Reuss (state)">Reussians</a> until the end of monarchy in Germany in 1918. Over the 19th century, Gera became a centre of the textile industry and saw a period of rapid growth. In 1952, the city also became an administrative centre in GDR as one of the capitals of <a href="/wiki/Gera_(Bezirk)" class="mw-redirect" title="Gera (Bezirk)">Gera administrative district (Bezirk)</a>. In 1990, Gera became part of re-established Thuringia. The loss of its administrative functions as well as its industrial core (caused both by structural change among the European textile industry and the economic system change after the German reunification) precipitated the city's slide into an enduring economic crisis.</p>
<p>Since 1990, many of Gera's buildings have been restored and big urban planning programmes like the <a href="/wiki/Bundesgartenschau" title="Bundesgartenschau">Bundesgartenschau</a> 2007 were implemented to stimulate Gera's economy. Sights include some retained buildings of the royal residence epoque and a large number of public and private buildings from the economic heyday between 1870 and 1930. The famous painter <a href="/wiki/Otto_Dix" title="Otto Dix">Otto Dix</a> was born in Gera in 1891.</p>
<p>Gera lies in a hilly landscape in the east of Thuringia, within the wide valley of the <a href="/wiki/Wei%C3%9Fe_Elster" class="mw-redirect" title="Weiße Elster">Weiße Elster</a> river.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Middle_Ages">Middle Ages</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gera&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Middle Ages">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The place name <i>Gera</i> originally referred to the area of the Elster river valley where the city stands now. The name most likely<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:No_original_research" title="Wikipedia:No original research"><span title="The material near this tag possibly contains original research. (March 2011)">original research?</span></a></i>]</sup> originated before the European <a href="/wiki/Migration_Period" title="Migration Period">Migration Period</a> – the Slavic people who first settled the area during the 8th century adopted the name. The first known documentary mention of Gera dates from 995.</p>
<p>In 999 Emperor <a href="/wiki/Otto_III,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Otto III, Holy Roman Emperor">Otto III</a> assigned the "province" of Gera to the <a href="/wiki/Quedlinburg_Abbey" title="Quedlinburg Abbey">Quedlinburg Abbey</a>. In turn, the church assigned the protectorship of this area in 1209 to the <a href="/wiki/Vogt" title="Vogt">Vogts</a> of <a href="/wiki/Weida,_Thuringia" title="Weida, Thuringia">Weida</a> (in German: <i>Vögte von Weida</i>) who served as its administrators. The Vogts of Weida were the ancestors of the <a href="/wiki/Reuss_(state)" class="mw-redirect" title="Reuss (state)">Reussians</a>, who ruled Gera until 1918. Gera was first mentioned as a town in 1237, though it is unclear in which year Gera got the municipal law. The small town got circumvallated in the 13th century on an area of 350 x 350 m and the Vogts' city castle was built in the south-western corner at today's Burgstraße. A municipal seal was first used in 1350, the council was first named in 1360 and the town hall was mentioned in 1426. The abbess of Quedlinburg remained the formal sovereign of Gera until 1358, as the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Wettin" title="House of Wettin">Wettins</a> followed her up. The Vogt's couldn't emancipate theirselfs from Wettin rule. In 1450, Gera was almost totally destroyed during the <a href="/wiki/Saxon_Fratricidal_War" title="Saxon Fratricidal War">Saxon Fratricidal War</a>, but it could recuperate quick because the starting textile manufacture brought wealth to the town.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_modern_period">Early modern period</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gera&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Early modern period">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Heinrich_Posthumus_Reu%C3%9F.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f3/Heinrich_Posthumus_Reu%C3%9F.jpg/220px-Heinrich_Posthumus_Reu%C3%9F.jpg" width="220" height="305" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f3/Heinrich_Posthumus_Reu%C3%9F.jpg/330px-Heinrich_Posthumus_Reu%C3%9F.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/f/f3/Heinrich_Posthumus_Reu%C3%9F.jpg 2x" data-file-width="425" data-file-height="589" /></a>

<p>The <a href="/wiki/German_Reformation" class="mw-redirect" title="German Reformation">Reformation</a> was introduced in Gera in 1533 against the will of the Vogts by the Wettins. After the <a href="/wiki/Schmalkaldic_War" title="Schmalkaldic War">Schmalkaldic War</a> in 1546, the Wettins lost Gera to the <a href="/wiki/Lands_of_the_Bohemian_Crown" title="Lands of the Bohemian Crown">Bohemian Crown</a>, who however had no influence on the city, so that the Vogts resp. the Reussians (which arose from the Vogts in the mid-16th century) as indeed rulers were strengthened. During the 16th century, some cloth-makers from the <a href="/wiki/Spanish_Netherlands" title="Spanish Netherlands">Spanish Netherlands</a> migrated to Gera as religious refugees at <a href="/wiki/Henry_II,_Count_of_Reuss-Gera" title="Henry II, Count of Reuss-Gera">Count Henry's</a> invitation and raised the textile business in Gera. In the 18th century, there was a first peak in this industry (<a href="/wiki/Putting-out_system" title="Putting-out system">Putting-out system</a>), which can be seen today at the large agents houses.</p>
<p>In 1673, the Reussians were raised to <a href="/wiki/Imperial_Count" title="Imperial Count">Imperial Counts</a> which granted them full sovereignty within the <a href="/wiki/Holy_Roman_Empire" title="Holy Roman Empire">Holy Roman Empire</a>; Gera became their royal residence. In 1686 and 1780, big town fires destroyed most buildings in Gera. The town was rebuilt relatively uniform during the 1780s in late-Baroque style, which marks the inner city until today.</p>
<p>In 1806 <a href="/wiki/Napoleon" title="Napoleon">Napoleon</a> established his Imperial Headquarters at Gera during the <a href="/wiki/War_of_the_Fourth_Coalition" title="War of the Fourth Coalition">War of the Fourth Coalition</a>. From here, on October 12, 1806, the French Emperor purposely sent an arrogant and threatening letter to King <a href="/wiki/Frederick_William_III_of_Prussia" title="Frederick William III of Prussia">Frederick William III of Prussia</a> – a letter that enticed <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Prussia" title="Kingdom of Prussia">Prussia</a> to war and a crushing defeat at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Jena" class="mw-redirect" title="Battle of Jena">Battle of Jena</a> a few days later.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Since_1815">Since 1815</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gera&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Since 1815">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>The Gera line of Reussians died out in 1802, so that it had no royal residence until the new one moved there from <a href="/wiki/Schleiz" title="Schleiz">Schleiz</a> in 1848. From 1848 to 1918 Gera served as the capital of the <a href="/wiki/Principality_of_Reuss-Gera" title="Principality of Reuss-Gera">Principality of Reuss-Gera</a>. With the industrial revolution in the mid-19th century, Gera grew rapidly, due largely to its textile industry, which saw the first <a href="/wiki/Power_loom" title="Power loom">Power loom</a> installed in 1836. In 1859, Gera was first connected by railway to <a href="/wiki/Halle_(Saale)" title="Halle (Saale)">Halle</a> via <a href="/wiki/Zeitz" title="Zeitz">Zeitz</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wei%C3%9Fenfels" title="Weißenfels">Weißenfels</a>. During the following decades, rail lines in all directions made Gera to a transport hub, and the town kept growing. The second electric tram in Germany was installed in Gera in 1892.</p>
<p>After World War I, during the <a href="/wiki/German_Revolution_of_1918%E2%80%9319" title="German Revolution of 1918–19">German Revolution of 1918–19</a>, the prince of Reuss was forced to abdicate and as the resultant "<a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Reuss" class="mw-redirect" title="Republic of Reuss">Republic of Reuss</a>" joined the newly founded state of <a href="/wiki/Thuringia" title="Thuringia">Thuringia</a> in 1920. After the incorporation of some suburbs in the 1910s and 1920s Gera, with some 80,000 inhabitants, was the largest city in Thuringia, although the more centrally located <a href="/wiki/Weimar" title="Weimar">Weimar</a> became its capital.</p>
<p>After the Nazi takeover of Germany, the Jewish community of Gera was destroyed, the synagogue burnt down in the <a href="/wiki/Kristallnacht" title="Kristallnacht">Kristallnacht</a> in 1938 and the city's Jews emigrated or were murdered in concentration camps. A month before the end of World War II, on 6 April 1945, U.S. bombing destroyed parts of the city and killed 514 residents. Some 300 buildings were hit, including the Osterstein castle and several historic buildings in city centre, many of which weren't rebuilt after the war. U.S. forces occupied Gera on 14 April 1945 but were replaced by the Soviets on 1 July 1945.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>

<p>Gera became part of the <a href="/wiki/GDR" class="mw-redirect" title="GDR">GDR</a> in 1949 and was a flash point in the <a href="/wiki/Uprising_of_1953_in_East_Germany" title="Uprising of 1953 in East Germany">Uprising of 1953 in East Germany</a> as thousands of workers – among them many employees of the Soviet-established <a href="/wiki/Wismut_(mining_company)" title="Wismut (mining company)">Wismut</a> uranium mining entity in the nearby <a href="/wiki/Ore_Mountains" title="Ore Mountains">Ore Mountains</a> (<i>Erzgebirge</i>) – demanded higher wages and free elections. The demonstrations were put down by Soviet military forces, including armored units.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>With governmental reorganization in East Germany, the city became the capital of the newly created <a href="/wiki/Gera_(Bezirk)" class="mw-redirect" title="Gera (Bezirk)">District of Gera</a> in 1952. The population rose during the GDR period, and massive <i><a href="/wiki/Plattenbau" title="Plattenbau">Plattenbau</a></i> prefabricated apartment blocks, built throughout East Germany, were constructed in Gera.</p>
<p>After <a href="/wiki/German_reunification" title="German reunification">German reunification</a> in 1990, Gera became part of the restored state of Thuringia. However, Gera lost most of its administrative functions after reunification, and nearly all the city's factories were closed. This led to a continuing economic crisis with significant unemployment, and the city's population declined from 135,000 in 1990 to less than 100,000 a decade later.</p>
<p>The city hosted the <a href="/wiki/Bundesgartenschau" title="Bundesgartenschau">Bundesgartenschau</a> (federal horticultural exhibition) in 2007.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Topography">Topography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gera&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Topography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Gera is located in a smooth-hilly landscape in eastern Thuringia at the <a href="/wiki/Wei%C3%9Fe_Elster" class="mw-redirect" title="Weiße Elster">Weiße Elster</a> river, between the <a href="/wiki/Thuringian_Highland" title="Thuringian Highland">Thuringian Highland</a> approx. 50&#160;km (31&#160;mi) in the south-west, the <a href="/wiki/Ore_Mountains" title="Ore Mountains">Ore Mountains</a> approx. 50&#160;km (31&#160;mi) in the south-east and the <a href="/wiki/Leipzig_Bay" title="Leipzig Bay">Leipzig Bay</a> approx. 50&#160;km (31&#160;mi) in the north. The municipal territory is marked by the Elster valley, crossing it in south-northern direction. West of the valley, the landscape is more hilly and forested (with the Gera Municipal Forest), whereas the eastern parts are more flat and in agricultural use. The elevation is between 180 metres (the level of the White Elster river) and 354&#160;m (1,161.42&#160;ft) (when measured at Gera-Falka at the furthest southeastern point). Usually the height <a href="/wiki/Above_mean_sea_level" class="mw-redirect" title="Above mean sea level">above sea level</a> for the city appears as 205&#160;m (673&#160;ft) when measured at the market place.</p>
<p>Small tributaries of the Weiße Elster river within the borders of Gera are the <i>Erlbach</i> on the western side and the <i>Wipsenbach</i>, <i>Gessenbach</i> and <i>Brahme</i> on the eastern side.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Climate">Climate</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gera&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Climate">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Gera has a <a href="/wiki/Humid_continental_climate" title="Humid continental climate">humid continental climate</a> (Dfb) or an <a href="/wiki/Oceanic_climate" title="Oceanic climate">oceanic climate</a> (<i>Cfb</i>) according to the <a href="/wiki/K%C3%B6ppen_climate_classification" title="Köppen climate classification">Köppen climate classification</a> system.<sup id="cite_ref-koppen_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-koppen-5">[5]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Peel_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Peel-6">[6]</a></sup> Summers are warm and sometimes humid, winters are relatively cold. The city's topography creates a microclimate caused through the basin position with sometimes <a href="/wiki/Inversion_(meteorology)" title="Inversion (meteorology)">inversion</a> in winter (quite cold nights under −20&#160;°C (−4&#160;°F)) and heat and inadequate air circulation in summer. Annual precipitation is only 591 millimeters (23.3&#160;in) with moderate rainfall throughout the year. Light snowfall mainly occurs from December through February, but snow cover does not usually remain for long.</p>

<p>Gera abuts the following municipalities:</p>
<ul>
<li>in the <a href="/wiki/Burgenlandkreis" title="Burgenlandkreis">Burgenlandkreis</a> district in the north: <a href="/wiki/Wetterzeube" title="Wetterzeube">Wetterzeube</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gutenborn" title="Gutenborn">Gutenborn</a> in the federal state of <a href="/wiki/Saxony-Anhalt" title="Saxony-Anhalt">Saxony-Anhalt</a></li>
<li>in the <a href="/wiki/Greiz_(district)" title="Greiz (district)">Greiz</a> district in the east: <a href="/wiki/P%C3%B6lzig" title="Pölzig">Pölzig</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hirschfeld,_Thuringia" title="Hirschfeld, Thuringia">Hirschfeld</a>, <a href="/wiki/Brahmenau" title="Brahmenau">Brahmenau</a>, <a href="/wiki/Schwaara" title="Schwaara">Schwaara</a>, <a href="/wiki/Korbu%C3%9Fen" title="Korbußen">Korbußen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ronneburg,_Thuringia" title="Ronneburg, Thuringia">Ronneburg</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kauern" title="Kauern">Kauern</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hilbersdorf" title="Hilbersdorf">Hilbersdorf</a> and <a href="/wiki/Linda_bei_Weida" title="Linda bei Weida">Linda</a>; in the south: <a href="/wiki/Endsch%C3%BCtz" title="Endschütz">Endschütz</a>, <a href="/wiki/W%C3%BCnschendorf/Elster" title="Wünschendorf/Elster">Wünschendorf</a>, <a href="/wiki/Zedlitz" title="Zedlitz">Zedlitz</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hundhaupten" title="Hundhaupten">Hundhaupten</a>; in the west: <a href="/wiki/Saara,_Greiz" title="Saara, Greiz">Saara</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kraftsdorf" title="Kraftsdorf">Kraftsdorf</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hartmannsdorf,_Greiz" title="Hartmannsdorf, Greiz">Hartmannsdorf</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bad_K%C3%B6stritz" title="Bad Köstritz">Bad Köstritz</a></li>
<li>in the <a href="/wiki/Saale-Holzland-Kreis" title="Saale-Holzland-Kreis">Saale-Holzland</a> district: <a href="/wiki/Silbitz" title="Silbitz">Silbitz</a> in the north-west.</li>
</ul>
<p>Gera is divided in different ways. Historically, there are more than 60 villages in the densely settled Elster valley area belonging to Gera today. The actual division knows on the one hand statistical districts and on the other hand political districts. The 12 statistical districts are covering the whole city area and consist of (sometimes) several political districts, urban quarters and/or rural villages. The 16 political districts are covering only few parts of Gera, but not the central urban areas, instead usually rural districts got incorporated during the second half of the 20th century. Each political district can (but doesn't have to) consist of more than one village. Each political district elects an own district council and a district mayor, whereas the statistical districts don't have any political function.</p>

<p>The main urban quarters are the city centre, Untermhaus (incorporated 1919) in the west, Langenberg (1950), Tinz (1919) and Bieblach (1905) in the north, Leumnitz (1919) in the east, Pforten (1919) and Zwötzen (1919) in the south-east as well as Debschwitz (1912) and Lusan (1919) in the south-west.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Demographics">Demographics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gera&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Demographics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:332px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Gera_Einwohnerentwicklung1.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9e/Gera_Einwohnerentwicklung1.jpg/330px-Gera_Einwohnerentwicklung1.jpg" width="330" height="201" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9e/Gera_Einwohnerentwicklung1.jpg/495px-Gera_Einwohnerentwicklung1.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9e/Gera_Einwohnerentwicklung1.jpg/660px-Gera_Einwohnerentwicklung1.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1328" data-file-height="807" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Gera_Einwohnerentwicklung1.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Demographic development until 2003</div>

<p>During the centuries, Gera has been a quite small town of 2,000 inhabitants. As the textile business saw a first peak, the population rose to 7,000 in 1800 and further to 17,000 after the early stage of industrialisation in 1870. Gera's heyday between 1870 and 1930 led to a demographic boom with a population of 83,000 at the end of this phase in 1930. In 1950, Gera had 98,000 inhabitants and the all-time peak was reached in 1988 with a population of 135,000. After the German reunification in 1990, the city saw a significant decline in population, despite the large incorporations of 1994. The population shrunk to 113,000 in 2000 and 95,000 in 2012.</p>
<p>The average decrease of population between 2009 and 2012 was approximately 0.55% p. a, whereas the population in bordering rural regions is shrinking with accelerating tendency. Suburbanization played only a small role in Gera. It occurred after the reunification for a short time in the 1990s, but most of the suburban areas were situated within the administrative city borders. During the 1990s and the 2000s, many inhabitants left Gera to search a better life in west Germany or other major east German cities like <a href="/wiki/Jena" title="Jena">Jena</a> or <a href="/wiki/Leipzig" title="Leipzig">Leipzig</a>. Since 2010, emigration is no big issue anymore. Now, the birth deficit, caused by the high average age of the population, is getting a bigger problem because the immigration isn't sufficient to compensate it yet. Despite urban planning activities to tear down unused flats, vacancy is still a problem with rates around 12% (according to 2011 EU census). A positive side effect for the inhabitants is that Gera has one of the lowest rent levels in Germany.</p>
<p>The birth deficit was 715 in 2012, this is -7.5 per 1,000 inhabitants (Thuringian average: -4.5; national average: -2.4). The net migration rate was +3.6 per 1,000 inhabitants in 2012 (Thuringian average: -0.8; national average: +4.6).<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> The most important regions of origin of Gera migrants are bordering rural areas of Thuringia, <a href="/wiki/Saxony-Anhalt" title="Saxony-Anhalt">Saxony-Anhalt</a> and <a href="/wiki/Saxony" title="Saxony">Saxony</a> as well as foreign countries like Poland, Russia, Ukraine, Hungary, Serbia, Romania and Bulgaria.</p>
<p>Like other eastern German cities, Gera has only a small amount of foreign population: circa 1.6% are non-Germans by citizenship and overall 5.0% are migrants (according to <a href="/wiki/2011_EU_census" class="mw-redirect" title="2011 EU census">2011 EU census</a>). Differing from the national average, the biggest groups of migrants in Gera are <a href="/wiki/Russians_in_Germany" title="Russians in Germany">Russians</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vietnamese_people_in_Germany" title="Vietnamese people in Germany">Vietnamese people</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ukrainians_in_Germany" title="Ukrainians in Germany">Ukrainians</a>. During recent years, the economic situation of the city improved a bit: the unemployment rate declined from 22% in 2005 to 11% in 2013, which is still the highest one out of all Thuringian districts. Due to the official atheism in former <a href="/wiki/East_Germany" title="East Germany">GDR</a>, most of the population is non-religious. 9.8% are members of the <a href="/wiki/Evangelical_Church_in_Central_Germany" title="Evangelical Church in Central Germany">Evangelical Church in Central Germany</a> and 2.6% are Catholics (according to 2011 EU census) making one of the world's lowest amounts of religious people.</p>

<h2><span id="Culture,_sights_and_cityscape"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Culture.2C_sights_and_cityscape">Culture, sights and cityscape</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gera&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Culture, sights and cityscape">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Museums">Museums</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gera&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Museums">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>There are some museums in Gera:</p>
<ul>
<li>The <i>Orangerie</i> at Orangerieplatz hosts an exhibition of art from the Middle Ages to the contemporary period.</li>
<li>The <i>Otto-Dix-Haus</i> at Mohrenplatz 4, birth place and childhood home of the famous artist <a href="/wiki/Otto_Dix" title="Otto Dix">Otto Dix</a>, has a collection of his drawings, prints, paintings and childhood sketch books, and an exhibition about his life.</li>
<li>The <i>Museum für Angewandte Kunst</i> at Greizer Straße shows 20th century contemporary art (inside "Ferber House"). It displays collections of <a href="/wiki/Bauhaus" title="Bauhaus">Bauhaus</a> ceramics by the artists <a href="/wiki/Otto_Lindig" title="Otto Lindig">Otto Lindig</a> und Theodor Bogler; architectural works of <a href="/w/index.php?title=Thilo_Schoder&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Thilo Schoder (page does not exist)">Thilo Schoder</a>; and photographs by <a href="/wiki/Aenne_Biermann" title="Aenne Biermann">Aenne Biermann</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></li>
<li>The <i>Museum für Naturkunde</i> at Nicolaiberg hosts a natural history exhibition (inside the "Schreiber House", the oldest building in the city), with its adjacent <a href="/wiki/Botanical_garden" title="Botanical garden">botanical garden</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Botanischer_Garten_Gera" title="Botanischer Garten Gera">Botanischer Garten Gera</a></li>
<li>The <i>Stadtmuseum</i> at Museumsplatz shows an exhibition of Gera's municipal history.</li>
<li>The <i>Haus Schulenburg</i> at Straße des Friedens is a factory owner's mansion, built in 1913/14 by the famous architect <a href="/wiki/Henry_van_de_Velde" title="Henry van de Velde">Henry van de Velde</a>. It hosts topical furniture and decoration and can be visited.</li>
</ul>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cityscape">Cityscape</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gera&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Cityscape">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Like its western neighbour-city <a href="/wiki/Jena" title="Jena">Jena</a>, Gera is a protracted city along a wide valley in south-northern direction. The historic city centre is quite small and located between Sorge in the north, Nicolaistraße in the east, Stadtgraben in the south and Reichsstraße in the west. It survived World War II, but during the 1960s and 1970s, the GDR government demolished larger inner-city areas to rebuild them with modern concrete architecture, which marks the view of Gera's centre, particularly in the north-west until today. Between 1870 and 1930, the city was largely extended in all directions. As distinct from other German cities, there is no citywide spatial separation between the worker's quarters and the upper-class mansion districts, instead, the mansions and the tenements are situated near to each other, spread over all the districts. The hilly areas and those next to the river and the parks are more upscale, whereas the areas next to the railway and the factories are more working-class styled. Nevertheless, the <a href="/wiki/Gr%C3%BCnderzeit" title="Gründerzeit">Gründerzeit</a> architecture is quite diverse and interesting in Gera. Most buildings were extensively refurbished after 1990. Especially Gera's mansion architecture from the 1900s and 1910s is unique. The 1920s brought some modern-style <a href="/wiki/Bauhaus" title="Bauhaus">Bauhaus</a> buildings to the then rich city. During the GDR period, urban growth was handled by establishing big <a href="/wiki/Plattenbau" title="Plattenbau">Plattenbau</a> settlements on the city's periphery, like the Lusan district in south and the Bieblach district in north.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sights_and_architectural_heritage">Sights and architectural heritage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gera&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Sights and architectural heritage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Churches">Churches</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gera&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Churches">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<ul>
<li>The <i>St. Saviour's Church</i> at Nicolaiberg is the evangelical main church of Gera, built between 1717 and 1720 by <a href="/w/index.php?title=David_Schatz&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="David Schatz (page does not exist)">David Schatz</a> in Baroque style.</li>
<li>The <i>Trinity Church</i> at Heinrichstraße is an evangelical parish church (since 1886), built between 1609 and 1611 as cemetery church.</li>
<li>The <i>St. John's Church</i> at Clara-Zetkin-Straße is an evangelical parish church, built between 1881 and 1885 in neo-Gothic style.</li>
<li>The <i>St. Mary's Church</i> at Untermhaus district is the evangelical parish church of the former village Untermhaus, built around 1450.</li>
<li>The <i>St. Elizabeth's Church</i> at Kleiststraße is the catholic parish church of Gera, built in 2000 in modern style as one of only few bigger new churches in Thuringia after 1990.</li>
<li>The <i>St. Martin's Church</i> at Zwötzen district is an evangelical parish church, built in 1895 in neo-Gothic style.</li>
<li>The <i>Fourteen Holy Helpers Church</i> at Langenberg district is an evangelical parish church, was rebuilt during the 1750s.</li>
</ul>

<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_sights">Other sights</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gera&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Other sights">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<ul>
<li>The <i>town hall</i> at Marktplatz is one of the biggest Renaissance town halls in Germany with a nearly 60 m high tower, built between 1573 and 1576. The Marktplatz itself forms a good ensemble of 18th century patricians houses in late-Baroque style.</li>
<li>The <i>Stadttheater</i> at Küchengartenallee is the former royal Reussian theatre, built in 1902 by <a href="/wiki/Heinrich_Seeling" title="Heinrich Seeling">Heinrich Seeling</a> in neo-Baroque style.</li>
<li>The <i>Stadtapotheke</i> (city pharmacy) is a Renaissance building at Marktplatz with a beautiful bay, established in 1592.</li>
<li>The <i>Küchengarten</i> with the <i>Orangerie</i> is a park ensemble west of the city centre, established in 1732 by the Reussians.</li>
<li>The <i>Höhler</i> is a cellar system under the city centre with a length of approx. 9&#160;km (6&#160;mi), established in 17th and 18th century for storing beer. A part can be visited via an entrance at Nicolaiberg 3.</li>
<li>The <i>Schloss Tinz</i> was the royal summer residence of the Reussians, built in 1748 in Baroque style with a surrounding park.</li>
<li>The <i>Schloss Osterstein</i> was the main royal residence of the Reussians, built during the 16th century in Renaissance style. It was hit by bombs in 1945 and partially demolished in 1962. Only the tower remained.</li>
<li>The <i>city wall</i> was demolished in the 19th century, only a small part at Stadtgraben remained.</li>
</ul>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Economy_and_infrastructure">Economy and infrastructure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gera&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Economy and infrastructure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span id="Agriculture,_industry_and_services"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Agriculture.2C_industry_and_services">Agriculture, industry and services</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gera&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Agriculture, industry and services">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>Agriculture has some importance in the rural districts of Gera, especially in the northern and eastern city parts. Approximately 57% of the municipal territory is in agricultural use: growing maize, rapeseed and crops as well as pasturing cattle.</p>
<p>The city's economy features industrial machinery (Dagro Gera GmbH), communications (DTKS GmbH, <a href="/wiki/Deutsche_Telekom" title="Deutsche Telekom">Deutsche Telekom</a>), security locks (Schloßsicherungen Gera GmbH), optics (POG Präzisionsoptik Gera GmbH), electrical equipment (Electronicon Kondensatoren GmbH), and margarine manufacturing (Othüna). Other companies include a compressor manufacturer (Kompressorenwerk Kaeser), a precision-tool maker (SMK-Präzisionsmechanik), and a subsidiary of the construction company Max Bögl. The e-commerce service-provider D+S Europe has a service centre in Gera with several hundred workers, and Rittal, a manufacturer of information-technology enclosures, recently<sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Gera&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup> moved from Bad Köstritz to Gera. Some industrial branches operating before 1990 no longer have major importance. Sectors either no longer existing or sharply reduced include those in toolmaking (<a href="/wiki/Volkseigener_Betrieb" title="Volkseigener Betrieb">VEB</a> Wema Union), textiles (VEB Modedruck), textile machinery (VEB Textima), electronic equipment (VEB Elektronik Gera). Other industries that had a presence included <a href="/wiki/Carl_Zeiss_AG" title="Carl Zeiss AG">VEB Carl Zeiss Jena</a> and a brewery. One important industrial branch had been uranium-ore mining in nearby Ronneburg (<a href="/wiki/Wismut_(mining_company)" title="Wismut (mining company)">Wismut</a>), whereby the region became the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet Union</a>'s leading uranium supplier. In 2012 Gera had 41 companies with more than 20 workers in the industrial sector, employing 3,400 people and generating an overall turnover of €452 million.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>Gera is a supra-centre according to the <a href="/wiki/Central_Place_Theory" class="mw-redirect" title="Central Place Theory">Central Place Theory</a> in German regional planning. This makes the city a regional centre for retailing, with three major shopping centres: <i>Gera-Arcaden</i>, <i>Amthor-Passage</i> and <i>Elster-Forum</i>. Health services are important, with one of the biggest hospitals in Thuringia, the SRH Waldklinikum. Nevertheless, Gera's economy is weak compared to equal-sized neighbouring cities like <a href="/wiki/Jena" title="Jena">Jena</a> or <a href="/wiki/Zwickau" title="Zwickau">Zwickau</a>. While Jena counts 51,000 and Zwickau 50,000 jobs liable to pay into the German social insurance, Gera has only 35,000 of that full-time jobs in 2012. The commuter balance was +14,000 in Jena and +16,000 in Zwickau, but only +2,000 in Gera, which is one of the lowest ratios among German supra-centres, highlighting the lack of ability of Gera to provide jobs for the region. The unemployment rate reached 11.2% in September 2013 - the highest among all Thuringian districts.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Transport">Transport</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gera&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Transport">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="By_rail">By rail</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gera&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: By rail">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Gera_Bahnhofshalle.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a2/Gera_Bahnhofshalle.JPG/220px-Gera_Bahnhofshalle.JPG" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a2/Gera_Bahnhofshalle.JPG/330px-Gera_Bahnhofshalle.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a2/Gera_Bahnhofshalle.JPG/440px-Gera_Bahnhofshalle.JPG 2x" data-file-width="1600" data-file-height="1200" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Gera_Bahnhofshalle.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Gera main station hall</div>

<p>Since the late 19th century, Gera has been a hub in the railway network. The first railway ran to the <a href="/wiki/Thuringian_Railway" class="mw-redirect" title="Thuringian Railway">Thuringian Railway</a> in <a href="/wiki/Wei%C3%9Fenfels" title="Weißenfels">Weißenfels</a> (with connection to <a href="/wiki/Halle_(Saale)" title="Halle (Saale)">Halle</a>) via <a href="/wiki/Zeitz" title="Zeitz">Zeitz</a> in 1859. Further main lines were opened to <a href="/wiki/G%C3%B6%C3%9Fnitz" title="Gößnitz">Gößnitz</a> (with connection to <a href="/wiki/Chemnitz" title="Chemnitz">Chemnitz</a>) in 1865, to <a href="/wiki/Saalfeld" title="Saalfeld">Saalfeld</a> in 1871, to <a href="/wiki/Leipzig" title="Leipzig">Leipzig</a> (via Zeitz) in 1873, to <a href="/wiki/Plauen" title="Plauen">Plauen</a> in 1875, to <a href="/wiki/Weimar" title="Weimar">Weimar</a> (with connection to <a href="/wiki/Erfurt" title="Erfurt">Erfurt</a>) via <a href="/wiki/Jena" title="Jena">Jena</a> in 1876 and to <a href="/wiki/Hof,_Bavaria" title="Hof, Bavaria">Hof</a> in 1883. The secondary railways to <a href="/wiki/Werdau" title="Werdau">Werdau</a> (opened in 1876) and <a href="/wiki/Meuselwitz" title="Meuselwitz">Meuselwitz</a> (opened via <a href="/wiki/P%C3%B6lzig" title="Pölzig">Pölzig</a> in 1901 and via <a href="/wiki/Lumpzig" title="Lumpzig">Lumpzig</a> in 1887) are abandoned. Nevertheless, none of these lines are electrified or in use for long-distance trains. Today, there are regional express trains to <a href="/wiki/G%C3%B6ttingen" title="Göttingen">Göttingen</a> via Jena and Erfurt, to Leipzig via Zeitz, to <a href="/wiki/Altenburg" title="Altenburg">Altenburg</a>, <a href="/wiki/Glauchau" title="Glauchau">Glauchau</a> and <a href="/wiki/Zwickau" title="Zwickau">Zwickau</a> via the Gößnitz line, to <a href="/wiki/Greiz" title="Greiz">Greiz</a>, to Hof and to Saalfeld. Local trains provide connections to Weimar via Jena, Leipzig, <a href="/wiki/Weischlitz" title="Weischlitz">Weischlitz</a> (near Plauen), Hof and Saalfeld. The most lines run every two hours, so that there is hourly service (express and local trains in alternation) to most directions. The electrification of the west-eastern mainline Weimar – Jena – Gera – Gößnitz is in discussion for closing that gap in the network and enable Gera's connection to long-distance trains, which can be reached today either via Jena or via Leipzig.</p>
<p>The most important stations in Gera are the <i><a href="/wiki/Gera_Hauptbahnhof" title="Gera Hauptbahnhof">Gera main station</a></i> (former Prussian station), where all the trains stop, and the <i>Gera southern station</i> (former Saxonian station), where all the trains, except the local ones to Weimar, stop. More stations in Gera are <i>Langenberg</i> at the Leipzig line, <i>Zwötzen</i> at the Saalfeld and Hof line as well as <i>Gera Ost</i> and <i>Liebschwitz</i> at the Plauen line. Freight transport by rail is immaterial in Gera since the 1990s.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="By_road">By road</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gera&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: By road">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The two <a href="/wiki/German_autobahns" class="mw-redirect" title="German autobahns">Autobahnen</a> crossing each other nearby at <i>Hermsdorf junction</i> are the <a href="/wiki/Bundesautobahn_4" title="Bundesautobahn 4">Bundesautobahn 4</a> (Frankfurt–Dresden) and the <a href="/wiki/Bundesautobahn_9" title="Bundesautobahn 9">Bundesautobahn 9</a> (Berlin–Munich), which were both built during the 1930s. Furthermore, there are three <a href="/wiki/Bundesstra%C3%9Fe" title="Bundesstraße">Bundesstraßen</a> connecting Gera: the <a href="/wiki/Bundesstra%C3%9Fe_2" title="Bundesstraße 2">Bundesstraße 2</a> to <a href="/wiki/Zeitz" title="Zeitz">Zeitz</a> in the north and <a href="/wiki/Hof,_Bavaria" title="Hof, Bavaria">Hof</a> in the south, the <a href="/wiki/Bundesstra%C3%9Fe_7" title="Bundesstraße 7">Bundesstraße 7</a> to <a href="/wiki/Jena" title="Jena">Jena</a> (via <a href="/wiki/Eisenberg,_Thuringia" title="Eisenberg, Thuringia">Eisenberg</a>) in the west and to <a href="/wiki/Altenburg" title="Altenburg">Altenburg</a> in the east and the <a href="/wiki/Bundesstra%C3%9Fe_92" title="Bundesstraße 92">Bundesstraße 92</a> to <a href="/wiki/Plauen" title="Plauen">Plauen</a> (via <a href="/wiki/Greiz" title="Greiz">Greiz</a>) in the south. Important secondary roads run to Altenburg (via <a href="/wiki/Lumpzig" title="Lumpzig">Lumpzig</a>), to <a href="/wiki/Werdau" title="Werdau">Werdau</a> (via <a href="/wiki/Linda_bei_Weida" title="Linda bei Weida">Linda</a>), to <a href="/wiki/W%C3%BCnschendorf/Elster" title="Wünschendorf/Elster">Wünschendorf</a>, to <a href="/wiki/Stadtroda" title="Stadtroda">Stadtroda</a> and to <a href="/wiki/Hermsdorf,_Thuringia" title="Hermsdorf, Thuringia">Hermsdorf</a>. As part of the <a href="/wiki/Bundesgartenschau" title="Bundesgartenschau">Bundesgartenschau</a> 2007, a new bypass road was built in the east to improve the connection of southern city parts to the A 4 and to relieve the city centre from transit traffic.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="By_aviation">By aviation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gera&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: By aviation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The closest regional airports are the <a href="/wiki/Leipzig/Halle_Airport" title="Leipzig/Halle Airport">Leipzig/Halle Airport</a>, about 70&#160;km (43&#160;mi) north and the <a href="/wiki/Erfurt-Weimar_Airport" class="mw-redirect" title="Erfurt-Weimar Airport">Erfurt-Weimar Airport</a>, about 90&#160;km (56&#160;mi) west of Gera. Both serve mainly holiday flights. The nearest major airports are the <a href="/wiki/Frankfurt_Airport" title="Frankfurt Airport">Frankfurt Airport</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Munich_Airport" title="Munich Airport">Munich Airport</a> and the prospective <a href="/wiki/Berlin_Brandenburg_Airport" title="Berlin Brandenburg Airport">Berlin Brandenburg Airport</a>. In the eastern part of Gera lies the airfield Gera-Leumnitz meant for private aviation.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="By_bike">By bike</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gera&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: By bike">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Cycling is becoming more popular since the construction of quality cycle tracks began in the 1990s. For tourists, there are the <i>Weiße Elster track</i> and the <i>Thuringian city string track (Radweg Thüringer Städtekette)</i>. Both connect points of tourist interest, the first along the <a href="/wiki/Wei%C3%9Fe_Elster" class="mw-redirect" title="Weiße Elster">Weiße Elster</a> valley from the <a href="/wiki/Elster_Mountains" title="Elster Mountains">Elster Mountains</a> at the Czech border to <a href="/wiki/Saale" title="Saale">Saale</a> river in <a href="/wiki/Halle_(Saale)" title="Halle (Saale)">Halle</a> and second from <a href="/wiki/Eisenach" title="Eisenach">Eisenach</a> via <a href="/wiki/Erfurt" title="Erfurt">Erfurt</a>, <a href="/wiki/Weimar" title="Weimar">Weimar</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jena" title="Jena">Jena</a> and Gera to <a href="/wiki/Altenburg" title="Altenburg">Altenburg</a>. For inner city every-day traffic, some cycle lanes exist along several main streets.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Trams_and_buses">Trams and buses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gera&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Trams and buses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>

<p>The <a href="/wiki/Trams_in_Gera" title="Trams in Gera">Gera tram network</a> was the second in Germany that launched electrical engines in 1892. Today, there are two long lines, one from Bieblach via city centre to Lusan (line 3) and another one – opened in 2006 – from Untermhaus via city centre to Zwötzen (line 1). The third short line is a connection between Lusan and the Zwötzen railway station (line 2). Another line is planned to connect Langenberg and the northern city parts. On line 3 is one course every 5 minutes, on line 1 every 10 minutes and on line 2 every 20 minutes.</p>
<p>The bus network connects districts without trams as well as neighbouring municipalities that do not have a rail connection.</p>

<p>Tertiary institutions are the private college <i>SRH Fachhochschule für Gesundheit Gera</i> (<a href="/wiki/Fachhochschule" title="Fachhochschule">university of applied sciences</a> for health) with 500 students and the <i>Berufsakademie Gera</i> (<a href="/wiki/Cooperative_education" title="Cooperative education">Cooperative education</a>) with 700 students.</p>
<p>Furthermore, there are four <a href="/wiki/Gymnasium_(Germany)" title="Gymnasium (Germany)">Gymnasiums</a>, all of them are state-owned. The Goethe-Gymnasium/Rutheneum focuses on music education as an elite boarding school, in addition to the common curriculum.</p>
