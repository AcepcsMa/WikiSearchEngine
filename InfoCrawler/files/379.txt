			<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Guild</h1>
									<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
									<div id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
								<div id="contentSub"></div>
												<div id="jump-to-nav" class="mw-jump">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>
				</div>
				<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">For other uses, see <a href="/wiki/Guild_(disambiguation)" class="mw-disambig" title="Guild (disambiguation)">Guild (disambiguation)</a>.</div>
<p>A <b>guild</b> <span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ɡ/: 'g' in 'guy'">ɡ</span><span title="/ɪ/: 'i' in 'kit'">ɪ</span><span title="'l' in 'lie'">l</span><span title="'d' in 'dye'">d</span></span>/</a></span></span> is an association of <a href="/wiki/Artisan" title="Artisan">artisans</a> or <a href="/wiki/Merchant" title="Merchant">merchants</a> who oversee the practice of their craft in a particular town. The earliest types of guild were formed as <a href="/wiki/Confraternity" title="Confraternity">confraternities</a> of tradesmen. They were organized in a manner something between a <a href="/wiki/Professional_association" title="Professional association">professional association</a>, <a href="/wiki/Trade_union" title="Trade union">trade union</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Cartel" title="Cartel">cartel</a>, and a <a href="/wiki/Secret_society" title="Secret society">secret society</a>. They often depended on grants of <a href="/wiki/Letters_patent" title="Letters patent">letters patent</a> by a <a href="/wiki/Monarch" title="Monarch">monarch</a> or other authority to enforce the flow of trade to their self-employed members, and to retain ownership of tools and the supply of materials. A lasting legacy of traditional guilds are the <a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/guildhall" class="extiw" title="wikt:guildhall">guildhalls</a> constructed and used as meeting places.</p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Windsorguildhall.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/33/Windsorguildhall.jpg/300px-Windsorguildhall.jpg" width="300" height="216" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/33/Windsorguildhall.jpg/450px-Windsorguildhall.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/33/Windsorguildhall.jpg/600px-Windsorguildhall.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1500" data-file-height="1081" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Windsorguildhall.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
One of the legacies of the guilds, the elevated <a href="/wiki/Windsor_Guildhall" title="Windsor Guildhall">Windsor Guildhall</a> was originally a meeting place for guilds, as well as magistrates' seat and <a href="/wiki/Town_hall" class="mw-redirect" title="Town hall">town hall</a>.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>An important result of the guild framework was the emergence of <a href="/wiki/University" title="University">universities</a> at <a href="/wiki/University_of_Bologna" title="University of Bologna">Bologna</a> (established in 1088), <a href="/wiki/University_of_Oxford" title="University of Oxford">Oxford</a> (at least since 1096) and <a href="/wiki/University_of_Paris" title="University of Paris">Paris</a> (c. 1150); they originated as guilds of students as at Bologna, or of masters as at Paris.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History_of_guilds">History of guilds</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Guild&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History of guilds">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_guild-like_associations">Early guild-like associations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Guild&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Early guild-like associations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A type of guild was known in Roman times. Known as <i>collegium</i>, <i>collegia</i> or <i>corpus</i>, these were organised groups of merchants who specialised in a particular craft and whose membership of the group was voluntary. One such example is the <i>corpus naviculariorum</i>, the college of long-distance shippers based at Rome's La Ostia port. The Roman guilds failed to survive the collapse of the Roman Empire. <sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Medieval" class="mw-redirect" title="Medieval">medieval</a> cities, craftsmen tended to form associations based on their trades, confraternities of textile workers, masons, carpenters, carvers, glass workers, each of whom controlled <a href="/wiki/Trade_secret" title="Trade secret">secrets</a> of traditionally imparted technology, the "arts" or "mysteries" of their crafts. Usually the founders were free independent <a href="/wiki/Master_craftsman" title="Master craftsman">master craftsmen</a> who hired apprentices.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Zz_Glaser_P1010007a_retouched.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fe/Zz_Glaser_P1010007a_retouched.jpg/220px-Zz_Glaser_P1010007a_retouched.jpg" width="220" height="230" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fe/Zz_Glaser_P1010007a_retouched.jpg/330px-Zz_Glaser_P1010007a_retouched.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fe/Zz_Glaser_P1010007a_retouched.jpg/440px-Zz_Glaser_P1010007a_retouched.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1488" data-file-height="1554" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Zz_Glaser_P1010007a_retouched.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Traditional <a href="/wiki/Wrought-iron" class="mw-redirect" title="Wrought-iron">wrought-iron</a> guild sign of a <a href="/wiki/Glazier" title="Glazier">glazier</a> — in <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a>. These signs can be found in many old European towns where guild members marked their places of business. Many survived through time or staged a comeback in industrial times. Today they are restored or even newly created, especially in old town areas.</div>
</div>
</div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Medieval_guild">Medieval guild</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Guild&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Medieval guild">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>There were several types of guilds, including the two main categories of merchant guilds and craft guilds<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> but also the <a href="/wiki/Frith#Culture" title="Frith">frith guild</a> and religious guild.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>The continental system of guilds and merchants arrived in England after the <a href="/wiki/Norman_Conquest" class="mw-redirect" title="Norman Conquest">Norman Conquest</a>, with incorporated societies of merchants in each town or city holding exclusive rights of doing business there. In many cases they became the governing body of a town. For example, <a href="/wiki/Guildhall,_London" title="Guildhall, London">London's Guildhall</a> became the seat of the <a href="/wiki/Court_of_Common_Council#The_Court_of_Common_Council" class="mw-redirect" title="Court of Common Council">Court of Common Council</a> of the City of London Corporation, the world’s oldest continuously elected local government<sup id="cite_ref-cofhistory_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cofhistory-6">[6]</a></sup> whose members to this day must be Freemen of the City.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Freedom_of_the_City" title="Freedom of the City">Freedom of the City</a>, effective from the Middle Ages until 1835, gave the right to trade, and was only bestowed upon members of a Guild or Livery.<sup id="cite_ref-coffreedom_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-coffreedom-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>Trade guilds arose in the 14th century as craftsmen united to protect their common interest.</p>
<p>Early <a href="/wiki/Egalitarian_community" title="Egalitarian community">egalitarian communities</a> called "guilds" (for the gold deposited in their common funds) were denounced by Catholic clergy for their "conjurations"—the binding oaths sworn among the members to support one another in adversity, kill specific enemies, and back one another in feuds or in business ventures. The occasion for these oaths were drunken banquets held on December 26, the pagan feast of <a href="/wiki/Yule" title="Yule">Jul</a> (Yule)—in 858, <a href="/wiki/West_Francia" title="West Francia">West Francian</a> Bishop <a href="/wiki/Hincmar" title="Hincmar">Hincmar</a> sought vainly to Christianise the guilds.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Early_Middle_Ages" title="Early Middle Ages">Early Middle Ages</a>, most of the <a href="/wiki/Associations_in_Ancient_Rome#Trade_associations" title="Associations in Ancient Rome">Roman craft organisations</a>, originally formed as <a href="/wiki/Confraternity" title="Confraternity">religious confraternities</a>, had disappeared, with the apparent exceptions of stonecutters and perhaps glassmakers, mostly the people that had local skills. <a href="/wiki/Gregory_of_Tours" title="Gregory of Tours">Gregory of Tours</a> tells a miraculous tale of a builder whose art and techniques suddenly left him, but were restored by an apparition of the Virgin Mary in a dream. Michel Rouche<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> remarks that the story speaks for the importance of practically transmitted journeymanship.</p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a>, guilds were called <i>corps de métiers</i>. According to Viktor Ivanovich Rutenburg, "Within the guild itself there was very little division of labour, which tended to operate rather between the guilds. Thus, according to <a href="/wiki/%C3%89tienne_Boileau" title="Étienne Boileau">Étienne Boileau</a>'s Book of Handicrafts, by the mid-13th century there were no less than 100 guilds in <a href="/wiki/Paris" title="Paris">Paris</a>, a figure which by the 14th century had risen to 350."<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> There were different guilds of metal-workers: the farriers, knife-makers, locksmiths, chain-forgers, nail-makers, often formed separate and distinct corporations; the armourers were divided into helmet-makers, escutcheon-makers, harness-makers, harness-polishers, etc.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> In Catalan towns, specially at <a href="/wiki/Barcelona" title="Barcelona">Barcelona</a>, guilds or <i>gremis</i> were a basic agent in the society: a shoemakers' guild is recorded in 1208.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>In England, specifically in the <a href="/wiki/City_of_London_Corporation" title="City of London Corporation">City of London Corporation</a>, more than 110 guilds,<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> referred to as <a href="/wiki/Livery_company" title="Livery company">livery companies</a>, survive today,<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> with the oldest more than a thousand years old.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Other groups, such as the <a href="/wiki/Worshipful_Company_of_Tax_Advisers" title="Worshipful Company of Tax Advisers">Worshipful Company of Tax Advisers</a>, have been formed far more recently. Membership in a livery company is expected for individuals participating in the governance of <i>The City</i>, as the <a href="/wiki/Lord_Mayor" class="mw-redirect" title="Lord Mayor">Lord Mayor</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Remembrancer" title="Remembrancer">Remembrancer</a>.</p>

<p>The guild system reached a mature state in <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a> circa 1300 and held on in German cities into the 19th century, with some special privileges for certain occupations remaining today. In the 15th century, Hamburg had 100 guilds, Cologne 80, and Lübeck 70.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> The latest guilds to develop in Western Europe were the <i><span lang="es" xml:lang="es">gremios</span></i> of Spain: e.g., Valencia (1332) or Toledo (1426).</p>
<p>Not all city economies were controlled by guilds; some cities were "free." Where guilds were in control, they shaped labor, production and trade; they had strong controls over <a href="/wiki/Instructional_capital" title="Instructional capital">instructional capital</a>, and the modern concepts of a lifetime progression of <a href="/wiki/Apprentice" class="mw-redirect" title="Apprentice">apprentice</a> to <a href="/wiki/Artisan" title="Artisan">craftsman</a>, and then from <a href="/wiki/Journeyman" title="Journeyman">journeyman</a> eventually to widely recognized <a href="/wiki/Master_craftsman" title="Master craftsman">master</a> and <a href="/wiki/Master_craftsman" title="Master craftsman">grandmaster</a> began to emerge. In order to become a Master, a Journeyman would have to go on a three-year voyage called <a href="/wiki/Journeyman_years" title="Journeyman years">Journeyman years</a>. The practice of the Journeyman years still exists in Germany and France.</p>
<p>As production became more specialized, trade guilds were divided and subdivided, eliciting the squabbles over jurisdiction that produced the paperwork by which economic historians trace their development: The metalworking guilds of Nuremberg were divided among dozens of independent trades in the boom economy of the 13th century, and there were 101 trades in Paris by 1260.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/Ghent" title="Ghent">Ghent</a>, as in <a href="/wiki/Florence" title="Florence">Florence</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Wool#History" title="Wool">woolen textile industry</a> developed as a congeries of specialized guilds. The appearance of the European guilds was tied to the emergent <a href="/wiki/Money" title="Money">money</a> economy, and to <a href="/wiki/Urbanization" title="Urbanization">urbanization</a>. <p>The guild was at the center of <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">European</a> handicraft organization into the 16th century. In France, a resurgence of the guilds in the second half of the 17th century is symptomatic of the monarchy's concerns to impose unity, control production and reap the benefits of transparent structure in the shape of more efficient taxation.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>The guilds were identified with organizations enjoying certain <a href="/wiki/Privilege_(legal_ethics)" title="Privilege (legal ethics)">privileges</a> (<a href="/wiki/Letters_patent" title="Letters patent">letters patent</a>), usually issued by the <a href="/wiki/Monarch" title="Monarch">king</a> or <a href="/wiki/State_(polity)" title="State (polity)">state</a> and overseen by local town business authorities (some kind of <a href="/wiki/Chamber_of_commerce" title="Chamber of commerce">chamber of commerce</a>). These were the predecessors of the modern <a href="/wiki/Patent" title="Patent">patent</a> and <a href="/wiki/Trademark" title="Trademark">trademark</a> system. The guilds also maintained funds in order to support infirm or elderly members, as well as widows and orphans of guild members, funeral benefits, and a 'tramping' allowance for those needing to travel to find work. As the guild system of the <a href="/wiki/City_of_London" title="City of London">City of London</a> declined during the 17th century, the <a href="/wiki/Livery_Companies" class="mw-redirect" title="Livery Companies">Livery Companies</a> transformed into mutual assistance fraternities along such lines.</p>
<p>European guilds imposed long standardized periods of <a href="/wiki/Apprenticeship" title="Apprenticeship">apprenticeship</a>, and made it difficult for those lacking the capital to set up for themselves or without the approval of their peers to gain access to materials or knowledge, or to sell into certain markets, an area that equally dominated the guilds' concerns. These are defining characteristics of <a href="/wiki/Mercantilism" title="Mercantilism">mercantilism</a> in economics, which dominated most European thinking about <a href="/wiki/Political_economy" title="Political economy">political economy</a> until the rise of <a href="/wiki/Classical_economics" title="Classical economics">classical economics</a>.</p>
<p>The guild system survived the emergence of early <a href="/wiki/Capitalism" title="Capitalism">capitalists</a>, which began to divide guild members into "haves" and dependent "have-nots". The civil struggles that characterize the 14th-century towns and cities were struggles in part between the greater guilds and the lesser artisanal guilds, which depended on <a href="/wiki/Piecework" class="mw-redirect" title="Piecework">piecework</a>. "In Florence, they were openly distinguished: the <i>Arti maggiori</i> and the <i>Arti minori</i>—already there was a <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Popolo_grasso&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Popolo grasso (page does not exist)">popolo grasso</a></i> and a <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Popolo_magro&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Popolo magro (page does not exist)">popolo magro</a></i>".<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> Fiercer struggles were those between essentially conservative guilds and the <a href="/wiki/Merchant" title="Merchant">merchant</a> class, which increasingly came to control the means of production and the capital that could be ventured in expansive schemes, often under the rules of guilds of their own. German social historians trace the <i>Zunftrevolution</i>, the urban revolution of guildmembers against a controlling urban patriciate, sometimes reading into them, however, perceived foretastes of the class struggles of the 19th century.</p>

<p>In the countryside, where guild rules did not operate, there was freedom for the entrepreneur with capital to organize <a href="/wiki/Cottage_industry" class="mw-redirect" title="Cottage industry">cottage industry</a>, a network of cottagers who spun and wove in their own premises on his account, provided with their raw materials, perhaps even their looms, by the capitalist who took a share of the profits. Such a dispersed system could not so easily be controlled where there was a vigorous local market for the raw materials: wool was easily available in sheep-rearing regions, whereas silk was not.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Organization">Organization</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Guild&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Organization">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In Florence, Italy, there were seven to 12 "greater guilds" and 14 "lesser guilds" the most important of the greater guilds was that for judges and notaries, who handled the legal business of all the other guilds and often served as an arbitrator of disputes. Other greater guilds include the wool, silk, and the money changers' guilds. They prided themselves on a reputation for very high quality work, which was rewarded with premium prices. The guilds fined members who deviated from standards. Other greater guilds included those of doctors, druggists, and furriers. Among the lesser guilds, were those for bakers, saddle makers, ironworkers and other artisans. They had a sizable membership, but lacked the political and social standing necessary to influence city affairs.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>The guild was made up by experienced and confirmed experts in their field of handicraft. They were called <a href="/wiki/Master_craftsman" title="Master craftsman">master craftsmen</a>. Before a new employee could rise to the level of mastery, he had to go through a schooling period during which he was first called an <a href="/wiki/Apprenticeship" title="Apprenticeship">apprentice</a>. After this period he could rise to the level of <a href="/wiki/Journeyman" title="Journeyman">journeyman</a>. Apprentices would typically not learn more than the most basic techniques until they were trusted by their peers to keep the guild's or company's secrets.</p>
<p>Like <i>journey</i>, the distance that could be travelled in a day, the title 'journeyman' derives from the French words for 'day' (<i>jour</i> and <i>journée</i>) from which came the middle English word <i>journei</i>. Journeymen were able to work for other masters, unlike apprentices, and generally paid by the day and were thus day labourers. After being employed by a master for several years, and after producing a qualifying piece of work, the apprentice was granted the rank of journeyman and was given documents (letters or certificates from his master and/or the guild itself) which certified him as a journeyman and entitled him to travel to other towns and countries to learn the art from other masters. These journeys could span large parts of Europe and were an unofficial way of communicating new methods and techniques, though by no means all journeymen made such travels — they were most common in Germany and Italy, and in other countries journeymen from small cities would often visit the capital.<sup id="cite_ref-Ogilvie11_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ogilvie11-20">[20]</a></sup></p>

<p>After this journey and several years of experience, a journeyman could be received as master craftsman, though in some guilds this step could be made straight from apprentice. This would typically require the approval of all masters of a guild, a donation of money and other goods (often omitted for sons of existing members), and the production of a so-called "<a href="/wiki/Masterpiece" title="Masterpiece">masterpiece</a>,' which would illustrate the abilities of the aspiring master craftsman; this was often retained by the guild.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>The medieval guild was established by charters or letters patent or similar authority by the city or the ruler and normally held a monopoly on trade in its craft within the city in which it operated: handicraft workers were forbidden by law to run any business if they were not members of a guild, and only masters were allowed to be members of a guild. Before these privileges were legislated, these groups of handicraft workers were simply called 'handicraft associations'.</p>
<p>The town authorities might be represented in the guild meetings and thus had a means of controlling the handicraft activities. This was important since towns very often depended on a good reputation for export of a narrow range of products, on which not only the guild's, but the town's, reputation depended. Controls on the association of physical locations to well-known exported products, e.g. wine from the <a href="/wiki/Champagne,_France" class="mw-redirect" title="Champagne, France">Champagne</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bordeaux" title="Bordeaux">Bordeaux</a> regions of <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a>, tin-glazed earthenwares from certain cities in <a href="/wiki/Holland" title="Holland">Holland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lace" title="Lace">lace</a> from <a href="/wiki/Chantilly,_Oise" title="Chantilly, Oise">Chantilly</a>, etc., helped to establish a town's place in global commerce — this led to modern <a href="/wiki/Trademark" title="Trademark">trademarks</a>.</p>
<p>In many German and Italian cities, the more powerful guilds often had considerable political influence, and sometimes attempted to control the city authorities. In the 14th century, this led to numerous bloody uprisings, during which the guilds dissolved town councils and detained <a href="/wiki/Patricianship" class="mw-redirect" title="Patricianship">patricians</a> in an attempt to increase their influence. In fourteenth-century north-east Germany, people of <a href="/wiki/Wends" title="Wends">Wendish</a>, i.e. <a href="/wiki/Slavic_peoples" class="mw-redirect" title="Slavic peoples">Slavic</a>, origin were not allowed to join some guilds.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> According to Wilhelm Raabe, <i>"down into the eighteenth century no German guild accepted a Wend."</i><sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>

<p>As <a href="/wiki/Sheilagh_Ogilvie" title="Sheilagh Ogilvie">Ogilvie</a> (2004) shows, the guilds negatively affected quality, skills, and innovation. Through what economists now call "<a href="/wiki/Rent-seeking" title="Rent-seeking">rent-seeking</a>" they imposed deadweight losses on the economy. Ogilvie says they generated no demonstrable positive externalities and notes that industry began to flourish only after the guilds faded away. Guilds persisted over the centuries because they redistributed resources to politically powerful merchants. On the other hand, Ogilvie agrees, guilds created "social capital" of shared norms, common information, mutual sanctions, and collective political action. This social capital benefited guild members, even as it hurt outsiders.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<p>The guild system became a target of much criticism towards the end of the 18th century and the beginning of the 19th century. They were believed to oppose <a href="/wiki/Free_trade" title="Free trade">free trade</a> and hinder <a href="/wiki/Technological_innovation" class="mw-redirect" title="Technological innovation">technological innovation</a>, <a href="/wiki/Technology_transfer" title="Technology transfer">technology transfer</a> and <a href="/wiki/Business_development" title="Business development">business development</a>. According to several accounts of this time, guilds became increasingly involved in simple territorial struggles against each other and against free practitioners of their arts.</p>
<p>Two of the most outspoken critics of the guild system were <a href="/wiki/Jean-Jacques_Rousseau" title="Jean-Jacques Rousseau">Jean-Jacques Rousseau</a> and <a href="/wiki/Adam_Smith" title="Adam Smith">Adam Smith</a>, and all over Europe a tendency to oppose government control over trades in favour of <a href="/wiki/Laissez-faire" title="Laissez-faire">laissez-faire</a> <a href="/wiki/Free_market" title="Free market">free market</a> systems was growing rapidly and making its way into the political and legal system. The French Revolution saw guilds as a last remnant of <a href="/wiki/Feudalism" title="Feudalism">feudalism</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Le_Chapelier_Law" class="mw-redirect" title="Le Chapelier Law">Le Chapelier Law</a> of 1791 abolished the guilds in France.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> Smith wrote in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Wealth_of_Nations" title="The Wealth of Nations">The Wealth of Nations</a></i> (Book I, Chapter X, paragraph 72):</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>It is to prevent this reduction of price, and consequently of wages and profit, by restraining that free competition which would most certainly occasion it, that all corporations, and the greater part of corporation laws, have been established. (...) and when any particular class of artificers or traders thought proper to act as a corporation without a charter, such adulterine guilds, as they were called, were not always disfranchised upon that account, but obliged to fine annually to the king for permission to exercise their usurped privileges.</p>
</blockquote>
<p><a href="/wiki/Karl_Marx" title="Karl Marx">Karl Marx</a> in his <i><a href="/wiki/The_Communist_Manifesto" title="The Communist Manifesto">Communist Manifesto</a></i> also criticized the guild system for its rigid gradation of social rank and the relation of oppressor/oppressed entailed by this system. From this time comes the low regard in which some people hold the guilds to this day. In part due to their own inability to control unruly <a href="/wiki/Corporation" title="Corporation">corporate</a> behavior, the tide turned against the guilds.</p>
<p>Because of industrialization and modernization of the trade and industry, and the rise of powerful nation-states that could directly issue <a href="/wiki/Patent" title="Patent">patent</a> and <a href="/wiki/Copyright" title="Copyright">copyright</a> protections — often revealing the <a href="/wiki/Trade_secret" title="Trade secret">trade secrets</a> — the guilds' power faded. After the <a href="/wiki/French_Revolution" title="French Revolution">French Revolution</a> they fell in most European nations through the 19th century, as the guild system was disbanded and replaced by free trade laws. By that time, many former handicraft workers had been forced to seek employment in the emerging manufacturing industries, using not closely guarded techniques but standardized methods controlled by <a href="/wiki/Corporation" title="Corporation">corporations</a>.</p>

<p>Guilds are sometimes said to be the precursors of modern <a href="/wiki/Trade_union" title="Trade union">trade unions</a>. Guilds, however, can also be seen as a set of self-employed skilled craftsmen with ownership and control over the materials and tools they needed to produce their goods. Guilds were more like <a href="/wiki/Cartels" class="mw-redirect" title="Cartels">cartels</a> than they were like trade unions (Olson 1982). However, the journeymen organizations, which were at the time illegal,<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> may have been influential.</p>
<p>The exclusive privilege of a guild to produce certain goods or provide certain services was similar in spirit and character with the original <a href="/wiki/Patent" title="Patent">patent</a> systems that surfaced in England in 1624. These systems played a role in ending the guilds' dominance, as <a href="/wiki/Trade_secret" title="Trade secret">trade secret</a> methods were superseded by modern firms directly revealing their techniques, and counting on the state to enforce their legal <a href="/wiki/Monopoly" title="Monopoly">monopoly</a>.</p>
<p>Some guild traditions still remain in a few handicrafts, in Europe especially among <a href="/wiki/Shoemaker" class="mw-redirect" title="Shoemaker">shoemakers</a> and <a href="/wiki/Barber" title="Barber">barbers</a>. Some <a href="/wiki/Ritual" title="Ritual">ritual</a> traditions of the guilds were conserved in <a href="/wiki/Order_(religious)" class="mw-redirect" title="Order (religious)">order</a> organisations such as the <a href="/wiki/Freemasonry" title="Freemasonry">Freemasons</a>, allegedly deriving from the Masons Guild, and the <a href="/wiki/Oddfellows" class="mw-redirect" title="Oddfellows">Oddfellows</a>, allegedly derived from various smaller guilds. These are, however, not very important economically except as reminders of the responsibilities of some trades toward the public.</p>
<p>Modern <a href="/wiki/Antitrust" class="mw-redirect" title="Antitrust">antitrust</a> law could be said to derive in some ways from the original statutes by which the guilds were abolished in Europe.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Economic_consequences">Economic consequences</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Guild&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Economic consequences">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The economic consequences of guilds have led to heated debates among economic historians. On the one side, scholars say that since merchant guilds persisted over long periods they must have been efficient institutions (since inefficient institutions die out). Others say they persisted not because they benefited the entire economy but because they benefited the owners, who used political power to protect them. Ogilvie (2011) says they regulated trade for their own benefit, were monopolies, distorted markets, fixed prices, and restricted entrance into the guild.<sup id="cite_ref-Ogilvie11_20-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ogilvie11-20">[20]</a></sup> Ogilvie (2008) argues that their long apprenticeships were unnecessary to acquire skills, and their conservatism reduced the rate of innovation and made the society poorer. She says their main goal was <a href="/wiki/Rent_seeking" class="mw-redirect" title="Rent seeking">rent seeking</a>, that is, to shift money to the membership at the expense of the entire economy.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p>Epstein and Prak's book (2008) rejects Ogilvie's conclusions.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> Specifically, Epstein argues that guilds were cost-sharing rather than rent-seeking institutions. They located and matched masters and likely apprentices through monitored learning. Whereas the acquisition of craft skills required experience-based learning, he argues that this process necessitated many years in apprenticeship.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<p>The extent to which guilds were able to monopolize markets is also debated.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Modern">Modern</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Guild&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Modern">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Scholars from the <a href="/wiki/History_of_ideas" title="History of ideas">history of ideas</a> have noticed that <a href="/wiki/Consultant" title="Consultant">consultants</a> play a part similar to that of the journeymen of the guild systems: they often travel a lot, work at many companies and spread new practices and knowledge between companies and corporations.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Profession" title="Profession">Professional</a> organizations replicate guild structure and operation.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> Professions such as architecture, engineering, geology, and land surveying require varying lengths of apprenticeships before one can gain a "professional" certification. These certifications hold great legal weight: most states make them a prerequisite to practicing there.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Thomas_W._Malone" title="Thomas W. Malone">Thomas W. Malone</a> champions a modern variant of the guild structure for modern "e-lancers", professionals who do mostly <a href="/wiki/Telework" class="mw-redirect" title="Telework">telework</a> for multiple employers. <a href="/wiki/Insurance" title="Insurance">Insurance</a> including any professional <a href="/wiki/Legal_liability" title="Legal liability">liability</a>, <a href="/wiki/Intellectual_capital" title="Intellectual capital">intellectual capital</a> protections, an <a href="/wiki/Ethical_code" title="Ethical code">ethical code</a> perhaps enforced by peer pressure and software, and other benefits of a strong association of producers of knowledge, benefit from <a href="/wiki/Economies_of_scale" title="Economies of scale">economies of scale</a>, and may prevent cut-throat competition that leads to inferior services undercutting prices.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> And, as with historical guilds, such a structure will resist foreign competition. The <a href="/wiki/Free_software_community" class="mw-redirect" title="Free software community">free software community</a> has from time to time explored a guild-like structure to unite against competition from <a href="/wiki/Microsoft" title="Microsoft">Microsoft</a>, e.g. <a href="/wiki/Advogato" title="Advogato">Advogato</a> assigns journeyer and master ranks to those committing to work only or mostly on free software.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Europe">Europe</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Guild&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Europe">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In many European countries guilds have experienced a revival as local organizations for craftsmen, primarily in traditional skills. They may function as forums for developing competence and are often the local units of a national employer's organisation.</p>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/City_of_London" title="City of London">City of London</a>, the ancient guilds survive as <a href="/wiki/Livery_Company" class="mw-redirect" title="Livery Company">Livery Companies</a>, all of which play a ceremonial role in the City's many customs. The City of London Livery Companies maintain strong links with their respective trade, craft or profession, some still retain regulatory, inspection or enforcement roles. The senior members of the City of London Livery Companies (known as Liverymen) elect the Sheriffs and approve the candidates for the office of Lord Mayor of London. Guilds also survive in many other towns and cities the UK including in <a href="/wiki/Preston,_Lancashire" title="Preston, Lancashire">Preston, Lancashire</a>, as the <a href="/wiki/Preston_Guild" class="mw-redirect" title="Preston Guild">Preston Guild</a> Merchant where among other celebrations descendants of Burgesses are still admitted into membership. With the City of London Livery Companies the UK have over 300 extant guilds and growing.</p>
<p>In 1878 the London Livery companies established the <a href="/wiki/City_and_Guilds_of_London_Institute" title="City and Guilds of London Institute">City and Guilds of London Institute</a> the forerunner of the engineering school (still called City and Guilds college) at <a href="/wiki/Imperial_College_London" title="Imperial College London">Imperial College London</a>. The aim of the City and Guilds of London Institute was the Advancement of Technical Education. As of 2013<sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Guild&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup> "City and Guilds" operates as an examining and accreditation body for vocational, managerial and engineering qualifications from entry-level craft and trade skills up to post-doctoral achievement.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> A separate organisation, the <a href="/wiki/City_and_Guilds_of_London_Art_School" title="City and Guilds of London Art School">City and Guilds of London Art School</a> has also close ties with the London livery companies, and is involved in the training of master craftworkers in stone and wood carving, as well as fine artists.</p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a> there are no longer any <i>Zünfte</i> (or <i>Gilden</i> - the terms used were rather different from town to town), nor any restriction of a craft to a privileged corporation. However, under a new name, <i>Innungen</i>, guilds continue to exist as private member clubs with membership limited to practitioners of particular trades or activities. These clubs are corporations under public law, albeit the membership is voluntary; the president normally comes from the ranks of master-craftsmen and is called <i>Obermeister</i> ("Master-in-chief"). Journeymen elect their own representative bodies, with their president having the traditional title of <i>Altgesell</i> (Senior Journeyman).</p>
<p>There are also "Craft Chambers" (<i>Handwerkskammern</i>), which have less resemblance to ancient guilds in that they are organized for all crafts in a certain region, not just one. In them membership is mandatory, and they serve to establish self-governance of the crafts.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="North_America">North America</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Guild&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: North America">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> guilds exist in several fields.</p>
<p>In the film and television industry, guild membership is generally a prerequisite for working on major productions in certain capacities. The <a href="/wiki/Screen_Actors_Guild" title="Screen Actors Guild">Screen Actors Guild</a>, <a href="/wiki/Directors_Guild_of_America" title="Directors Guild of America">Directors Guild of America</a>, <a href="/wiki/Writers_Guild_of_America,_East" title="Writers Guild of America, East">Writers Guild of America, East</a>, <a href="/wiki/Writers_Guild_of_America,_West" title="Writers Guild of America, West">Writers Guild of America, West</a> and other profession-specific guilds have the ability to exercise strong control in the <a href="/wiki/Cinema_of_the_United_States" title="Cinema of the United States">cinema of the United States</a> as a result of a rigid system of <a href="/wiki/Intellectual-property" class="mw-redirect" title="Intellectual-property">intellectual-property</a> rights and a history of power-brokers also holding guild membership (e.g., <a href="/wiki/DreamWorks" title="DreamWorks">DreamWorks</a> founder <a href="/wiki/Steven_Spielberg" title="Steven Spielberg">Steven Spielberg</a> was, and is, a DGA member). These guilds maintain their own contracts with production companies to ensure a certain number of their members are hired for roles in each film or television production, and that their members are paid a minimum of guild "scale," along with other labor protections. These guilds set high standards for membership, and exclude professional actors, writers, etc. who do not abide by the strict rules for competing within the film and television industry in America.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/The_Newspaper_Guild" class="mw-redirect" title="The Newspaper Guild">The Newspaper Guild</a> is a <a href="/wiki/Labor_union" class="mw-redirect" title="Labor union">labor union</a> for journalists and other newspaper workers, with over 30,000 members in North America.</p>
<p>Real-estate brokerage offers an example of a modern American guild system. Signs of guild behavior in real-estate brokerage include: standard pricing (6% of the home price), strong affiliation among all practitioners, self-regulation (see <a href="/wiki/National_Association_of_Realtors" title="National Association of Realtors">National Association of Realtors</a>), strong cultural identity (see <a href="/wiki/Realtor" class="mw-redirect" title="Realtor">realtor</a>), little price variation with quality differences, and traditional methods in use by all practitioners. In September 2005 the U.S. Department of Justice filed an antitrust lawsuit against the National Association of Realtors, challenging NAR practices that (the DOJ asserted) prevent competition from practitioners who use different methods. The DOJ and the Federal Trade Commission in 2005 advocated against state laws, supported by NAR, that disadvantage new kinds of brokers.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> <i>U.S. v. National Assoc. of Realtors</i>, Civil Action No. 05C-5140 (N.D. Ill. Sept. 7, 2005).</p>
<p>The practice of law in the United States also exemplifies modern guilds at work. Every state maintains its own <a href="/wiki/Bar_association" title="Bar association">bar association</a>, supervised by that state's highest court. The court decides the criteria for entering and staying in the legal profession. In most states, every attorney must become a member of that state's bar association in order to practice law. State laws forbid any person from engaging in the unauthorized practice of law and practicing attorneys are subject to rules of professional conduct that are enforced by the state's high court.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Medical associations comparable to guilds include the state Medical Boards, the <a href="/wiki/American_Medical_Association" title="American Medical Association">American Medical Association</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/American_Dental_Association" title="American Dental Association">American Dental Association</a>. Medical licensing in most states requires specific training, tests and years of low-paid apprenticeship (internship and residency) under harsh working conditions. Even qualified international or out-of-state doctors may not practice without acceptance by the local medical guild (Medical board). Similarly, nurses and physicians' practitioners have their own guilds. A doctor cannot work as a physician's assistant unless (s)he separately trains, tests and apprentices as one.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Australia">Australia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Guild&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Australia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Australia is home to <a href="/wiki/The_Pharmacy_Guild_of_Australia" title="The Pharmacy Guild of Australia">The Pharmacy Guild of Australia</a> (the peak association in the pharmacy industry) and the Guild of Commercial Filmmakers (an association of makers of commercial, short and feature films).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Australia's fine jewellery industry has members of the Gold and Silversmith's Guild of Australia (GSGA) who practice their manufacturing locally.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Virtual_world_guilds">Virtual world guilds</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Guild&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Virtual world guilds">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Clan_(video_gaming)" class="mw-redirect" title="Clan (video gaming)">Clan (video gaming)</a></div>
<p>Groups called guilds exist in online communities such as <a href="/wiki/Massively_multiplayer_online_games" class="mw-redirect" title="Massively multiplayer online games">massively multiplayer online games</a>.</p>
<p>These guilds usually represent a group of individuals that share the same interests and goals. While they may be organized around in-game economic production, they generally do not control production. Guilds in online games can range in size from a small group of a few players to massive guilds that have players from around the world.</p>
