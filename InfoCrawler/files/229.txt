<p><b>Lynn Rene Anderson</b> (September 26, 1947&#160;– July 30, 2015) was an <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">American</a> <a href="/wiki/Country_music" title="Country music">country music</a> singer known for a string of hits throughout the late <a href="/wiki/List_of_years_in_country_music" title="List of years in country music">1960s, 1970s and 1980s</a>, most notably her 1970 worldwide megahit "<a href="/wiki/Rose_Garden_(Lynn_Anderson_song)" title="Rose Garden (Lynn Anderson song)">(I Never Promised You A) Rose Garden</a>." Anderson's crossover appeal and regular exposure on national television helped her to become one of the most popular and successful country singers of the 1970s.<sup id="cite_ref-allmusic_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-allmusic-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Anderson charted 12 No. 1, 18 Top 10, and more than 50 Top 40 hit singles. In addition to being named "Top Female Vocalist" by the <a href="/wiki/Academy_of_Country_Music" title="Academy of Country Music">Academy of Country Music</a> (ACM) twice and "Female Vocalist of the Year" by the <a href="/wiki/Country_Music_Association" title="Country Music Association">Country Music Association</a> (CMA), Anderson won a <a href="/wiki/Grammy_Award" title="Grammy Award">Grammy Award</a> (earning seven nominations), <a href="/wiki/People%27s_Choice_Award" class="mw-redirect" title="People's Choice Award">People's Choice Award</a> and an <a href="/wiki/American_Music_Award" title="American Music Award">American Music Award</a> (AMA).<sup id="cite_ref-countrypolitan_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-countrypolitan-2">[2]</a></sup> Anderson was the first female country artist to win the American Music Award (1974), as well as the first to headline and sellout <a href="/wiki/Madison_Square_Garden" title="Madison Square Garden">Madison Square Garden</a> that same year.<sup id="cite_ref-countrypolitan_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-countrypolitan-2">[2]</a></sup> She was the #13 music artist of the 1970s according to <a href="/wiki/Joel_Whitburn" title="Joel Whitburn">Joel Whitburn</a> and the highest ranking artist of the list not yet in the <a href="/wiki/Country_Music_Hall_of_Fame_and_Museum" title="Country Music Hall of Fame and Museum">Country Music Hall of Fame and Museum</a>.</p>
<p>According to Kyle Young, CEO of the Country Music Hall of Fame and Museum, "Anderson’s television background and her ability to bring show-business dynamism to recordings and concert performances helped her achieve crossover success. With talent and tenacity, the country music star brought increased visibility to the genre."</p>
<p>Anderson debuted in 1966, at the age of 19, and had her first hit with <i><a href="/wiki/Ride,_Ride,_Ride" title="Ride, Ride, Ride">Ride, Ride, Ride</a></i>. After a series of Top 10 hit singles on the country charts during the late 1960s, Anderson signed with <a href="/wiki/Columbia_Records" title="Columbia Records">Columbia Records</a> in 1970. Under Columbia, she had her most successful string of hits. Her signature song, "<a href="/wiki/Rose_Garden_(Lynn_Anderson_song)" title="Rose Garden (Lynn Anderson song)">(I Never Promised You A) Rose Garden</a>," remains one of the biggest selling country crossover hits of all time. In addition to topping the U.S. country charts for five weeks, the song reached No.&#160;3 on the <a href="/wiki/Billboard_Hot_100" title="Billboard Hot 100"><i>Billboard</i> Pop Chart</a>. It also topped the charts in several countries around the globe, an unprecedented achievement at the time. <a href="/wiki/Country_Music_Television" class="mw-redirect" title="Country Music Television">CMT</a> ranks "Rose Garden" at No.&#160;83 on its list of the "100 Greatest Songs in Country Music History". Anderson continued to record and remained a popular concert attraction until her death, regularly headlining major casino showrooms, performing arts centers, and theaters.<sup id="cite_ref-cd_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cd-3">[3]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lynn_Anderson&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Although she was recorded as having been born in North Dakota, Anderson, according to family history, was actually born just north of the US border in <a href="/wiki/Winnipeg" title="Winnipeg">Winnipeg</a>, <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a>, and the birth was recorded in Grand Forks so that she would gain US citizenship. She was later raised in <a href="/wiki/Fair_Oaks,_California" title="Fair Oaks, California">Fair Oaks, California</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-cd_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cd-3">[3]</a></sup> She was the daughter of country music songwriters Casey and <a href="/wiki/Liz_Anderson" title="Liz Anderson">Liz Anderson</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-allmusic_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-allmusic-1">[1]</a></sup> Lynn Anderson's great-grandfather was born in <a href="/wiki/Aremark" title="Aremark">Aremark</a>, <a href="/wiki/Norway" title="Norway">Norway</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> In later life, Anderson met her Norwegian relatives through the Norwegian TV series <i><a href="/wiki/Tore_p%C3%A5_sporet" title="Tore på sporet">Tore på sporet</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-cd_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cd-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Anderson became interested in singing at age six. She had her first success in the horse show arena in and around California, where she would eventually win a total of 700 trophies,<sup id="cite_ref-oldies_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-oldies-5">[5]</a></sup> including the "California Horse Show Queen" title in 1966. In her teens, she performed regularly on the local television program <i>Country Caravan</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-cd_3-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cd-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1965, she was working as a secretary at Top 40 radio station <a href="/wiki/KROY_(defunct)" title="KROY (defunct)">KROY</a> in <a href="/wiki/Sacramento,_California" title="Sacramento, California">Sacramento, California</a>, when one of her mother's compositions, "<a href="/wiki/(My_Friends_Are_Gonna_Be)_Strangers" title="(My Friends Are Gonna Be) Strangers">(My Friends Are Gonna Be) Strangers</a>", was recorded by <a href="/wiki/Merle_Haggard" title="Merle Haggard">Merle Haggard</a> and became a No.&#160;10 country hit.<sup id="cite_ref-cd_3-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cd-3">[3]</a></sup> Her mother signed with <a href="/wiki/RCA_Victor" class="mw-redirect" title="RCA Victor">RCA Victor</a> as a country music recording artist that year.<sup id="cite_ref-allmusic_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-allmusic-1">[1]</a></sup> While accompanying her mother to <a href="/wiki/Nashville,_Tennessee" title="Nashville, Tennessee">Nashville</a>, Anderson participated in an informal sing-along in a hotel room with country stars <a href="/wiki/Merle_Haggard" title="Merle Haggard">Merle Haggard</a> and <a href="/wiki/Freddie_Hart_(musician)" class="mw-redirect" title="Freddie Hart (musician)">Freddie Hart</a>. One of the people present at the sing-along, Slim Williamson, owned <a href="/wiki/Chart_Records" title="Chart Records">Chart Records</a>, a local record label. Williamson recognized Lynn Anderson's talent and invited her to record for his label. She began recording for Chart in 1966.<sup id="cite_ref-cd_3-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cd-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Music_career">Music career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lynn_Anderson&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Music career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span id="1966–1969:_Country_music_success"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="1966.E2.80.931969:_Country_music_success">1966–1969: Country music success</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lynn_Anderson&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: 1966–1969: Country music success">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1966, Lynn Anderson released her debut single, "For Better or for Worse", a duet with Jerry Lane which did not chart. Her first charting single and her third release on the Chart Label, "Ride, Ride, Ride", hit the Country Top 40.<sup id="cite_ref-oldies_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-oldies-5">[5]</a></sup> She had her first major hit single, "<a href="/wiki/If_I_Kiss_You_(Will_You_Go_Away)" class="mw-redirect" title="If I Kiss You (Will You Go Away)">If I Kiss You (Will You Go Away)</a>", the following year. It peaked at No.&#160;5 on the Billboard country chart. It was followed by another Top 5 hit, "<a href="/wiki/Promises,_Promises_(Lynn_Anderson_song)" title="Promises, Promises (Lynn Anderson song)">Promises, Promises</a>",<sup id="cite_ref-allmusic_1-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-allmusic-1">[1]</a></sup> from an album of the same name, which would also spawn a second Top 10 hit, "No Another Time", in 1968. Then she released "Mother May I", a Top 25 duet that she recorded with her mother. (The elder Anderson also achieved success as a country artist around the same time, achieving two Top 10 hits—"Mama Spank" (1966) and a trio with <a href="/wiki/Bobby_Bare" title="Bobby Bare">Bobby Bare</a> and <a href="/wiki/Norma_Jean_(singer)" title="Norma Jean (singer)">Norma Jean</a>, "The Game of Triangles" (1967).)</p>
<p>In 1967, Lynn Anderson became a regular performer on <i><a href="/wiki/The_Lawrence_Welk_Show" title="The Lawrence Welk Show">The Lawrence Welk Show</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-cd_3-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cd-3">[3]</a></sup> and toured with the Welk Road Show. Her appearances on the show would later redound to her benefit. Because of the Welk show's widespread appeal, she was able to achieve success on the pop charts. In 1969, as her popularity grew, she left the Welk show in favor of sporadic guest appearances (<a href="/wiki/Clay_Hart" title="Clay Hart">Clay Hart</a> would ultimately take Anderson's place in the Welk "musical family").<sup id="cite_ref-cd_3-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cd-3">[3]</a></sup> In 1968, Anderson married songwriter and producer <a href="/wiki/Glenn_Sutton" title="Glenn Sutton">Glenn Sutton</a>, who would later produce and write many of her records during her tenure with Columbia. Their marriage lasted nine years.<sup id="cite_ref-oldies_5-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-oldies-5">[5]</a></sup> Anderson released her biggest hit single under the Chart label, "That's a No No",<sup id="cite_ref-allmusic_1-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-allmusic-1">[1]</a></sup> which peaked at No.&#160;2 on the Billboard Country Chart in 1969. Soon after, she left the label; signing with <a href="/wiki/Columbia_Records" title="Columbia Records">Columbia Records</a> in 1970.<sup id="cite_ref-oldies_5-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-oldies-5">[5]</a></sup> Chart Records would continue to release Lynn Anderson singles thru the end of 1971, including five Top 20 hits: "He'd Still Love Me", "<a href="/wiki/I%27ve_Been_Everywhere" title="I've Been Everywhere">I've Been Everywhere</a>", "<a href="/wiki/Rocky_Top" title="Rocky Top">Rocky Top</a>", "<a href="/wiki/It_Wasn%27t_God_Who_Made_Honky_Tonk_Angels" title="It Wasn't God Who Made Honky Tonk Angels">It Wasn't God Who Made Honky Tonk Angels</a>" and "I'm Alright".<sup id="cite_ref-lynnpage_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-lynnpage-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span id="1970–1980:_Pop_crossover"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="1970.E2.80.931980:_Pop_crossover">1970–1980: Pop crossover</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lynn_Anderson&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: 1970–1980: Pop crossover">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>After signing with Columbia in 1970, Anderson released the <a href="/wiki/Joe_South" title="Joe South">Joe South</a> song, "<a href="/wiki/Rose_Garden_(Lynn_Anderson_song)" title="Rose Garden (Lynn Anderson song)">(I Never Promised You A) Rose Garden</a>", which became a major crossover pop hit in 1970 and early 1971.<sup id="cite_ref-allmusic_1-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-allmusic-1">[1]</a></sup> The song was produced by her then husband Glenn Sutton. Anderson actually had to do some arm-twisting to get her producer-husband to allow her to record the song. Sutton was concerned that "Rose Garden" was a song to be sung by a man, with the line "I could promise you things like big diamond rings". It was Columbia executive <a href="/wiki/Clive_Davis" title="Clive Davis">Clive Davis</a> who determined the song would be Anderson's next single released.<sup id="cite_ref-book_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-book-7">[7]</a></sup> The single peaked at No.&#160;1 on the Billboard country chart and No.&#160;3 on the <a href="/wiki/Billboard_Hot_100" title="Billboard Hot 100">Billboard pop chart</a>, becoming an international success.<sup id="cite_ref-allmusic_1-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-allmusic-1">[1]</a></sup> In the United Kingdom, the single reached No.&#160;3<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> and in Germany it peaked at No.&#160;1 and stayed there for four weeks.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> The album <i><a href="/wiki/Rose_Garden_(Lynn_Anderson_album)" class="mw-redirect" title="Rose Garden (Lynn Anderson album)">Rose Garden</a></i> was released in 1971 and was also hugely successful, receiving <a href="/wiki/Platinum_certification" class="mw-redirect" title="Platinum certification">platinum certification</a> by the <a href="/wiki/RIAA" class="mw-redirect" title="RIAA">RIAA</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Anderson won the Academy of Country Music's "Top Female Vocalist" Award and the Country Music Association's "Female Vocalist of the Year" Award in 1970 and 1971, respectively. In addition, she won a <a href="/wiki/Grammy_Award" title="Grammy Award">Grammy Award</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-cmt_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cmt-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>Lynn Anderson had two No.&#160;1 hit singles on the Billboard country chart in 1971 with "<a href="/wiki/You%27re_My_Man" title="You're My Man">You're My Man</a>" and "<a href="/wiki/How_Can_I_Unlove_You" title="How Can I Unlove You">How Can I Unlove You</a>",<sup id="cite_ref-allmusic_1-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-allmusic-1">[1]</a></sup> both peaking at No.&#160;63 on the Billboard pop chart. In 1972, Anderson had three Top 5 hits on the country charts, beginning with a cover version of the 50s pop hit, "<a href="/wiki/Cry_(Churchill_Kohlman_song)" title="Cry (Churchill Kohlman song)">Cry</a>", followed by "<a href="/wiki/Listen_to_a_Country_Song_(song)" title="Listen to a Country Song (song)">Listen to a Country Song</a>" and "<a href="/wiki/Fool_Me" title="Fool Me">Fool Me</a>".<sup id="cite_ref-oldies_5-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-oldies-5">[5]</a></sup> These songs were included on the <i>Listen to a Country Song</i> album. "Cry" peaked at No.&#160;3 on the <i><a href="/wiki/Billboard_magazine" class="mw-redirect" title="Billboard magazine">Billboard magazine</a></i> country chart and at No.&#160;16 on the Adult Contemporary chart. In 1973, she had a fourth No.&#160;1 country hit with "<a href="/wiki/Keep_Me_in_Mind_(Lynn_Anderson_song)" title="Keep Me in Mind (Lynn Anderson song)">Keep Me in Mind</a>", and an album of the same name was released. Then followed a second 1973 album, <i><a href="/wiki/Top_of_the_World_(Lynn_Anderson_album)" title="Top of the World (Lynn Anderson album)">Top of the World</a></i>, whose title track was a No.&#160;2 country hit. It was also a No.&#160;1 pop hit for <a href="/wiki/The_Carpenters" title="The Carpenters">The Carpenters</a> that same year. However, Anderson's version was the first to be released as a single and become a hit. The second single released from the <i>Top of the World</i> album, "Sing About Love", also peaked at No.&#160;3. In 1974, "<a href="/wiki/What_a_Man_My_Man_Is" title="What a Man My Man Is">What a Man My Man Is</a>" was Anderson's fifth No.&#160;1 country hit. That same year, she also won the <a href="/wiki/American_Music_Awards" class="mw-redirect" title="American Music Awards">American Music Awards</a>' "Favorite Female Country Artist" Award.</p>
<p>Throughout the 1970s, Anderson made frequent guest appearances on many television specials, <a href="/wiki/Talk_show" title="Talk show">talk shows</a> and variety shows. Because of her crossover appeal, she often appeared on shows where country artists were not regularly seen. At the height of the show's popularity, she had a starring role in an episode of <i><a href="/wiki/Starsky_%26_Hutch" title="Starsky &amp; Hutch">Starsky &amp; Hutch</a></i>. She made several appearances on <i><a href="/wiki/The_Tonight_Show" title="The Tonight Show">The Tonight Show</a></i>, and appeared on three <a href="/wiki/Bob_Hope" title="Bob Hope">Bob Hope</a> television specials. Anderson frequently guest-starred on various <a href="/wiki/Dean_Martin" title="Dean Martin">Dean Martin</a> television specials. She also hosted her own television special in 1977, with guest star <a href="/wiki/Tina_Turner" title="Tina Turner">Tina Turner</a>. In 1979, Anderson performed live at the <a href="/wiki/Ford_Theatre" title="Ford Theatre">Ford Theatre</a> in <a href="/wiki/Washington,_D.C." title="Washington, D.C.">Washington, D.C.</a>, included in the audience was President <a href="/wiki/Jimmy_Carter" title="Jimmy Carter">Jimmy Carter</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-lynnpage_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-lynnpage-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>Anderson's success slowed down toward the end of the 1970s.<sup id="cite_ref-cmt_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cmt-11">[11]</a></sup> She continued making appearances on the country charts every year for the rest of the decade.<sup id="cite_ref-allmusic_1-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-allmusic-1">[1]</a></sup> Anderson hit the Top 20 with two from her <i><a href="/wiki/I%27ve_Never_Loved_Anyone_More" title="I've Never Loved Anyone More">I've Never Loved Anyone More</a></i> album in 1975: "He Turns it into Love Again" and the title track. She had a Top 20 hit with "All the King's Horses" in 1976 from an album of the same name. In 1977, <i><a href="/wiki/Wrap_Your_Love_All_Around_Your_Man" title="Wrap Your Love All Around Your Man">Wrap Your Love All Around Your Man</a></i>, partly due to its promotion on the television series <i><a href="/wiki/Starsky_%26_Hutch" title="Starsky &amp; Hutch">Starsky &amp; Hutch</a></i>, became a major hit. In 1979, she had her first Top 10 hit since 1974 with "Isn't It Always Love" <sup id="cite_ref-allmusic_1-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-allmusic-1">[1]</a></sup> from her album <i><a href="/wiki/Outlaw_is_Just_a_State_of_Mind" class="mw-redirect" title="Outlaw is Just a State of Mind">Outlaw is Just a State of Mind</a></i>. The album also produced the Top 20 hit, "I Love How You Love Me" and the Top 40 hit "Sea of Heartbreak". In 1980, she recorded her final album for Columbia, <i><a href="/wiki/Even_Cowgirls_Get_the_Blues_(Lynn_Anderson_album)" title="Even Cowgirls Get the Blues (Lynn Anderson album)">Even Cowgirls Get the Blues</a></i>, which featured two Top 30 hits. Following her separation from Columbia she did not record for nearly three years.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span id="1983–1989:_Commercial_resurgence"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="1983.E2.80.931989:_Commercial_resurgence">1983–1989: Commercial resurgence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lynn_Anderson&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: 1983–1989: Commercial resurgence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>After three years away from recording, Anderson signed with the Permian Records label in 1983, and had a Top 10 country hit with "You're Welcome to Tonight", a duet with <a href="/wiki/Gary_Morris" title="Gary Morris">Gary Morris</a> <sup id="cite_ref-oldies_5-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-oldies-5">[5]</a></sup> At Permian, she recorded <i><a href="/wiki/Back_(album)" title="Back (album)">Back</a></i>, her first studio album since 1980. The album's first single, "You Can't Lose What You Never Had", peaked outside the Top 40, but the second single, "What I've Learned from Loving You", was a Top 20 hit. She left Permian in 1984. In 1986, she recorded "Fools for Each Other", a duet with <a href="/wiki/Ed_Bruce" title="Ed Bruce">Ed Bruce</a>, which was included on his <i>Night Things</i> album. The single peaked just at number 49.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>That same year, Anderson recorded a single for <a href="/wiki/MCA_Records" title="MCA Records">MCA Records</a>. In 1986, she signed with <a href="/wiki/Mercury_Records" title="Mercury Records">Mercury Records</a>, which produced one album, <i><a href="/wiki/What_She_Does_Best" title="What She Does Best">What She Does Best</a></i>, and five singles that were minor hits on the Billboard country chart in the late 1980s.<sup id="cite_ref-oldies_5-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-oldies-5">[5]</a></sup> She had two Top 40 hit singles with MCA—"Read Between the Lines" and a cover version of <a href="/wiki/The_Drifters" title="The Drifters">The Drifters</a>' "<a href="/wiki/Under_the_Boardwalk" title="Under the Boardwalk">Under the Boardwalk</a>", which hit Top 25 country in 1988. In 1989, Anderson released her last charting single to date with "How Many Hearts", which peaked at No.&#160;69.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span id="1990–2015:_Later_music_career"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="1990.E2.80.932015:_Later_music_career">1990–2015: Later music career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lynn_Anderson&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: 1990–2015: Later music career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1990, Anderson starred as singer Betsy Hall in the <a href="/wiki/BBC" title="BBC">BBC</a> <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a> TV drama <i>The Wreck on the Highway.</i> She performed the song "Dream On" in the film, which consequently became a minor hit in a BBC collection of country standards. In 1992, she recorded a new studio album titled <i><a href="/wiki/Cowboy%27s_Sweetheart" title="Cowboy's Sweetheart">Cowboy's Sweetheart</a>,</i> released by Laselight Records.<sup id="cite_ref-cmt_11-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cmt-11">[11]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Emmylou_Harris" title="Emmylou Harris">Emmylou Harris</a> and <a href="/wiki/Marty_Stuart" title="Marty Stuart">Marty Stuart</a> appeared as guest performers on the album.<sup id="cite_ref-lynnpage_6-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-lynnpage-6">[6]</a></sup> During the same time, the American Rose Society created a hybrid tea rose and named it the "Lynn Anderson"<sup id="cite_ref-countrypolitan_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-countrypolitan-2">[2]</a></sup> Anderson did not record any studio albums for the rest of the decade and became more focused on touring and performing, as well as non-musical projects. In 1999, she was inducted into the North American Country Music Association's International Hall of Fame.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2000, <a href="/wiki/Tennessee" title="Tennessee">Tennessee</a> governor <a href="/wiki/Don_Sundquist" title="Don Sundquist">Don Sundquist</a> made June 15 "Lynn Anderson Day" throughout the state. Anderson produced a <a href="/wiki/The_National_Network" class="mw-redirect" title="The National Network">TNN</a> special, "American Country Cowboys," which helped <a href="/wiki/Handicapped" class="mw-redirect" title="Handicapped">handicapped</a> groups also during this time.<sup id="cite_ref-countrypolitan_2-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-countrypolitan-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2002, Anderson was ranked at No.&#160;29 on CMT's television special of the <i>40 Greatest Women of Country Music</i>. In 2000, she recorded a live album titled, <i><a href="/wiki/Live_at_Billy_Bob%27s_Texas_(Lynn_Anderson_album)" title="Live at Billy Bob's Texas (Lynn Anderson album)">Live at Billy Bob's Texas</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-allmusic_1-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-allmusic-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2004, she recorded her first studio album in 12 years, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Bluegrass_Sessions_(Lynn_Anderson_album)" title="The Bluegrass Sessions (Lynn Anderson album)">The Bluegrass Sessions</a>,</i> a <a href="/wiki/Bluegrass_music" title="Bluegrass music">Bluegrass</a> album that consisted of Anderson's major hits from the 1960s and 1970s re-recorded in a Bluegrass format.<sup id="cite_ref-oldies_5-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-oldies-5">[5]</a></sup> The album was nominated for a <a href="/wiki/Grammy_Award_for_Best_Bluegrass_Album" title="Grammy Award for Best Bluegrass Album">Grammy Award for Best Bluegrass Album</a> in 2005, along with <a href="/wiki/Ricky_Skaggs" title="Ricky Skaggs">Ricky Skaggs</a>' album <i>Brand New Strings,</i> Ralph Stanley II's <i>Carrying on,</i> as well as a multi-artist album.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2005, she performed on the <a href="/wiki/Grand_Ole_Opry" title="Grand Ole Opry">Grand Ole Opry</a> with country singer, <a href="/wiki/Martina_McBride" title="Martina McBride">Martina McBride</a>, performing a duet version of "(I Never Promised You A) Rose Garden."<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>In June 2007, she performed as part of the CMA's 2007 Music Festival in Nashville. She performed an outdoor concert at the Riverfront Park area, which also included concerts by <a href="/wiki/Aaron_Tippin" title="Aaron Tippin">Aaron Tippin</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jason_Michael_Carroll" title="Jason Michael Carroll">Jason Michael Carroll</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>In April 2009, Anderson was part of the concert line-up at the annual Stagecoach Festival in Palm Springs, which also included concerts by <a href="/wiki/Charlie_Daniels" title="Charlie Daniels">Charlie Daniels</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kevin_Costner" title="Kevin Costner">Kevin Costner</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Reba_McEntire" title="Reba McEntire">Reba McEntire</a>. Throughout 2010 and 2011, she performed a series of concerts backed by the Metropole Symphony Orchestra. Until the end of her life, Anderson remained a popular concert attraction; regularly headlining casino showrooms, performing arts centers &amp; theatres throughout the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> and <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a>.</p>
<p>In 2015, Anderson signed with Center Sound Records to release a new country gospel album, <i>Bridges</i>. The album featured a gospel version of the <a href="/wiki/Mentor_Williams" title="Mentor Williams">Mentor Williams</a>' penned hit "<a href="/wiki/Drift_Away" title="Drift Away">Drift Away</a>", with new lyrics by the writer. It also contained vocal collaborations of Anderson with <a href="/wiki/The_Martins" title="The Martins">The Martins</a> and <a href="/wiki/Country_Music_Hall_of_Fame" class="mw-redirect" title="Country Music Hall of Fame">Country Music Hall of Fame</a> members <a href="/wiki/The_Oak_Ridge_Boys" title="The Oak Ridge Boys">The Oak Ridge Boys</a>. The album was released on June 9, 2015, as both digital download and vinyl 45.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Equestrian_career">Equestrian career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lynn_Anderson&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Equestrian career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Outside of her music career, Anderson also maintained an equestrian (<a href="/wiki/Cutting_(sport)" title="Cutting (sport)">cutting</a> and <a href="/wiki/Horse_show" title="Horse show">show horse</a>) career from the 1960s until her death. As a horsewoman, she won 16 national, eight world, and several celebrity championships.<sup id="cite_ref-countrypolitan_2-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-countrypolitan-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Her championships included the National Chevy Truck Cutting Horse Champion in 1999, the American U.S. Open Invitational Champion in 2000, and the <a href="/wiki/National_Cutting_Horse_Association" title="National Cutting Horse Association">National Cutting Horse Association Champion</a> in 1999.<sup id="cite_ref-countrypolitan_2-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-countrypolitan-2">[2]</a></sup> Anderson raised horses at her ranch in <a href="/wiki/New_Mexico" title="New Mexico">New Mexico</a> and worked with the "Special Riders of Animaland", which is a horseback-riding therapy program for children.<sup id="cite_ref-countrypolitan_2-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-countrypolitan-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Her sorrel <a href="/wiki/American_Quarter_Horse" title="American Quarter Horse">quarter horses</a> "Lady Phase" and "Skipster’s Chief" were produced as plastic models by <a href="/wiki/Breyer_Animal_Creations" title="Breyer Animal Creations">Breyer Animal Creations</a>. "Skipster’s Chief" was also the poster horse for the <a href="/wiki/North_American_Riding_for_the_Handicapped_Association" class="mw-redirect" title="North American Riding for the Handicapped Association">North American Riding for the Handicapped Association</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life">Personal life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lynn_Anderson&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Personal life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Anderson was married to Grammy Award-winning songwriter <a href="/wiki/Glenn_Sutton" title="Glenn Sutton">Glenn Sutton</a> from 1968 to 1977. They had one child together, Lisa, born in 1970. In 1978, she married Louisiana oilman Harold "Spook" Stream III, with whom she had two children. Stream and Anderson divorced in 1982.<sup id="cite_ref-lynnpage_6-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-lynnpage-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>Anderson lived in Nashville<sup id="cite_ref-lynnpage_6-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-lynnpage-6">[6]</a></sup> and at the time of her death she had been in a relationship for 26 years with songwriter and producer <a href="/wiki/Mentor_Williams" title="Mentor Williams">Mentor Williams</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legal_issues">Legal issues</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lynn_Anderson&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Legal issues">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Anderson was sentenced to two days in jail for contempt of court for cursing her two children during a court-approved visit. "This kind of conduct on her part has got to cease," Circuit Judge Muriel Robinson Rice said when she sentenced Anderson. Rice stayed the sentence pending appeal. Rice eventually awarded custody of the children to Anderson's ex-husband, Harold Stream. The hearing concerned whether Anderson cursed her 12-year-old son and 10-year-old daughter in private during a court-approved visit with the children. Anderson, 43, and Stream, 42, were divorced in 1982 after four years of marriage. Anderson originally had custody of the children, but Stream won temporary custody while authorities investigated his allegations that his former wife had physically abused her children.</p>
<p>Anderson had a self-professed issue with alcohol and had a list of run-ins with the law which included an arrest on December 2, 2004, in which she was charged with <a href="/wiki/Driving_under_the_influence" title="Driving under the influence">driving while intoxicated</a> in <a href="/wiki/Denton,_Texas" title="Denton, Texas">Denton, Texas</a>. A driver following Anderson called the police after noticing her car weaving in and out of lanes. After failing a field sobriety test, Anderson was arrested and released on bond.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> On January 24, 2005, a short time after her last arrest, Anderson was accused of shoplifting a <a href="/wiki/Harry_Potter" title="Harry Potter">Harry Potter</a> DVD from a local supermarket in her hometown of <a href="/wiki/Taos,_New_Mexico" title="Taos, New Mexico">Taos, New Mexico</a>. Upon her arrest she punched the arresting officer. She was charged with shoplifting, resisting arrest and assault on a police officer. The assault charges were later dropped.<sup id="cite_ref-Anderson_Arrested_for_Shoplifting_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Anderson_Arrested_for_Shoplifting-25">[25]</a></sup> Her next arrest was on May 3, 2006, when she was arrested on a second <a href="/wiki/Driving_under_the_influence" title="Driving under the influence">driving under the influence of alcohol</a> charge following a minor traffic accident near <a href="/wiki/Espanola,_New_Mexico" class="mw-redirect" title="Espanola, New Mexico">Espanola, New Mexico</a>. According to police, Anderson failed a sobriety test and refused to take a breathalyzer test after her car hit the back of another car. No one was injured in the collision and she was again charged and released on bond.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> Her last arrest occurred on September 11, 2014, after being involved in a minor traffic accident in <a href="/wiki/Nashville,_Tennessee" title="Nashville, Tennessee">Nashville, Tennessee</a>, on West End Avenue.<sup id="cite_ref-Anderson_Arrested_for_DUI_and_Civil_Consent_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Anderson_Arrested_for_DUI_and_Civil_Consent-27">[27]</a></sup> Anderson was arrested after she admitted to drinking alcohol and taking prescription medication. She was booked on DUI and released on a $5,000 bond. Anderson later went through rehabilitation at the <a href="/wiki/Betty_Ford_Center" title="Betty Ford Center">Betty Ford Center</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Lynn_Anderson.27s_Arrest_Record_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lynn_Anderson.27s_Arrest_Record-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Death">Death</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lynn_Anderson&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Death">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Anderson died on July 30, 2015 at the <a href="/wiki/Vanderbilt_University_Medical_Center" title="Vanderbilt University Medical Center">Vanderbilt University Medical Center</a> in <a href="/wiki/Nashville,_Tennessee" title="Nashville, Tennessee">Nashville, Tennessee</a> from a <a href="/wiki/Heart_attack" class="mw-redirect" title="Heart attack">heart attack</a> at the age of 67. She had been briefly hospitalized due to <a href="/wiki/Pneumonia" title="Pneumonia">pneumonia</a> after vacationing in <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-death_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-death-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
