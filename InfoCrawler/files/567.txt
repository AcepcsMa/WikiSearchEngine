<p><b>Bratwurst</b> (<small>German:</small> <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Standard_German" title="Help:IPA/Standard German">[ˈbʁaːtvʊɐ̯st]</a></span><small class="nowrap metadata">&#160;(<span class="unicode haudio"><span class="fn"><span style="white-space:nowrap"><a href="/wiki/File:De-Bratwurst.ogg" title="About this sound"><img alt="About this sound" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/11px-Loudspeaker.svg.png" width="11" height="11" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/17px-Loudspeaker.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/22px-Loudspeaker.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="20" data-file-height="20" /></a>&#160;</span><a href="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/7/77/De-Bratwurst.ogg" class="internal" title="De-Bratwurst.ogg">listen</a></span></span>)</small>) is a type of German <a href="/wiki/Sausage" title="Sausage">sausage</a> made from <a href="/wiki/Veal" title="Veal">veal</a>, <a href="/wiki/Beef" title="Beef">beef</a>, or most commonly <a href="/wiki/Pork" title="Pork">pork</a>. The name is derived from the <a href="/wiki/Old_High_German" title="Old High German">Old High German</a> <i>Brätwurst</i>, from <i>brät-</i>, finely chopped meat, and <i>Wurst</i>, sausage, although in modern German it is often associated with the verb <i>braten</i>, to pan fry or roast.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bratwurst&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>While sausage recipes can be found as early as 228 AD, the first documented evidence of the <i>Bratwurst</i> in Germany dates back to 1313, and can be found in the Franconian city of <a href="/wiki/Nuremberg" title="Nuremberg">Nuremberg</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> which is still an internationally renowned center for the production of grill sausages.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Types_and_traditions">Types and traditions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bratwurst&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Types and traditions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Germany">Germany</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bratwurst&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Germany">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Recipes for the sausage vary by region and even locality; some sources list over 40 different varieties of <a href="/wiki/German_cuisine" title="German cuisine">German</a> bratwurst,<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-5">[5]</a></sup> many of the best known originating in <a href="/wiki/Franconia" title="Franconia">Franconia</a> (today for the most part situated in northern <a href="/wiki/Bavaria" title="Bavaria">Bavaria</a>, but still culturally quite distinct), its northern neighbour <a href="/wiki/Thuringia" title="Thuringia">Thuringia</a> and adjacent areas. How the sausages are served is also locally different, but most commonly they are regarded as a snack served with or in a white <a href="/wiki/Bread_roll" title="Bread roll">bread roll</a> made from <a href="/wiki/Wheat" title="Wheat">wheat</a> flour and eaten with <a href="/wiki/Mustard_(condiment)" title="Mustard (condiment)">mustard</a>. As a pub dish, it is often accompanied by <a href="/wiki/Sauerkraut" title="Sauerkraut">sauerkraut</a> or <a href="/wiki/Potato_salad" title="Potato salad">potato salad</a> and sometimes served with dark, crusty country bread made predominantly from <a href="/wiki/Rye" title="Rye">rye</a> flour, less commonly with a <i>Brezel</i> (<a href="/wiki/Pretzel" title="Pretzel">pretzel</a>). It is a very popular form of <a href="/wiki/Fast_food" title="Fast food">fast food</a> in German-speaking countries, often cooked and sold by street vendors from small stands.</p>

<p>The Franconian sausage is a relatively long (10–20&#160;cm&#160;[3.9–7.9&#160;in]), thick, coarse sausage, common to the whole Franconian region with slight variations. It dates back to 1313.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> With <a href="/wiki/Marjoram" title="Marjoram">marjoram</a> as a characteristic ingredient, it is close in taste to the <i>Nürnberger Bratwurst</i> but juicier, due to its size and coarseness. The <i>Fränkische Bratwurst</i> is traditionally served with sauerkraut or potato salad, but with no mustard—although many customers<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Accuracy_dispute#Disputed_statement" title="Wikipedia:Accuracy dispute"><span title="The material near this tag is possibly inaccurate or nonfactual. (April 2016)">dubious</span></a> <span class="metadata">– <a href="/wiki/Talk:Bratwurst#Dubious" title="Talk:Bratwurst">discuss</a></span></i>]</sup> disregard this tradition and demand plenty of it.</p>
<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="Coburger_Bratwurst"><i>Coburger Bratwurst</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bratwurst&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Coburger Bratwurst">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>
<p><i>Bratwurst</i> originating in the city of <a href="/wiki/Coburg" title="Coburg">Coburg</a> in Franconia was first documented there in 1498.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> It is made from a minimum of 15% veal or beef, and its seasonings include only salt, pepper, nutmeg, and lemon zest. It is coarse in texture and measures about 25&#160;cm (9.8&#160;in) in length. Traditionally, it is grilled over pinecones and served in a bread roll (<i>Brötchen</i>).</p>
<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="Kulmbacher_Bratwurst"><i>Kulmbacher Bratwurst</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bratwurst&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Kulmbacher Bratwurst">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>
<p>The <i>Kulmbacher Bratwurst</i> is a fine-grained Rohwurst from the city of <a href="/wiki/Kulmbach" title="Kulmbach">Kulmbach</a> in <a href="/wiki/Upper_Franconia" title="Upper Franconia">Upper Franconia</a>. Long and thin, it is made mostly from very finely ground veal, with very little pork. This sausage may be seasoned with salt, white pepper, nutmeg, lemon peel, marjoram, caraway, and garlic. The exact mixture is a closely guarded trade secret of each butcher. Kulmbacher bratwursts are usually pan fried or grilled over a wood fire. They are sold and served freshly grilled from vendors' stands in the Marktplatz, in pairs, with or without mustard, on crusty rolls sprinkled with anise.</p>
<h5><span id="Nürnberger_Rostbratwurst"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="N.C3.BCrnberger_Rostbratwurst"><i>Nürnberger Rostbratwurst</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bratwurst&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Nürnberger Rostbratwurst">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>

<p>The small, thin bratwurst from Franconia's largest city, Nuremberg, was first documented in 1567; it is surprisingly small, being only 7 to 9&#160;cm (2.8 to 3.5&#160;in) in length and weighing between 20 and 25 g. The denominations <i>Nürnberger Bratwurst</i> and <i>Nürnberger Rostbratwurst</i> (<i>Rost</i> refers to the cooking grate above the flames) are <a href="/wiki/Protected_Geographical_Status" class="mw-redirect" title="Protected Geographical Status">Protected Geographical Indications (PGI)</a> under EU law since 2003,<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> and may therefore only be produced in the city of Nürnberg, where an "Association for the Protection of <i>Nürnberger Bratwürste</i>" was established in 1997.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Pork" title="Pork">Pork</a>-based and typically seasoned with fresh marjoram which gives them their distinctive flavour, these sausages are traditionally grilled over a beechwood fire. As a main dish, they are served in sets of six, eight, 10 or 12 on a <a href="/wiki/Pewter" title="Pewter">pewter</a> plate (round but also frequently heart- or bell-shaped) with either sauerkraut or potato salad, and accompanied by a dollop of horseradish or mustard. They are also sold as a snack by street vendors as <i>Drei im Weckla</i> (three in a bun; the spelling <i>Drei im Weggla</i> is also common, <i>Weggla</i>/<i>Weckla</i> being the word for "bread roll" in the Nuremberg dialect), with mustard being offered to spice them up to personal taste.</p>
<p>A particular way of preparing Nuremberg sausages without grilling them is to cook them in a spiced vinegar and onion stock; this variety is called <a href="/wiki/Saure_Zipfel" title="Saure Zipfel"><i>Blaue Zipfel</i></a> (blue lobes).</p>

<h5><span id="Würzburger_Bratwurst"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="W.C3.BCrzburger_Bratwurst"><i>Würzburger Bratwurst</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bratwurst&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Würzburger Bratwurst">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>
<p>The <i>Würzburger Bratwurst</i>, also known as the <i>Winzerbratwurst</i>, comes from the city of <a href="/wiki/W%C3%BCrzburg" title="Würzburg">Würzburg</a> in Franconia. Its size is similar to the <i>Thüringer Rostbratwurst</i>, but its ingredients include white <a href="/wiki/Franconia_(wine_region)" title="Franconia (wine region)">Franken-Wine</a>.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_varieties">Other varieties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bratwurst&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Other varieties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<h5><span id="Thüringer_Rostbratwurst"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Th.C3.BCringer_Rostbratwurst"><i>Thüringer Rostbratwurst</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bratwurst&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Thüringer Rostbratwurst">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>

<p>The <i>Thüringer Rostbratwurst</i> is a spicy sausage from <a href="/wiki/Thuringia" title="Thuringia">Thuringia</a>. It is 15–20&#160;cm (5.9–7.9&#160;in) long and thin in shape, traditionally grilled over a charcoal fire and eaten with mustard and bread. The name <i>Thüringer Rostbratwurst</i> is also recognised as a PGI under EU law.</p>
<p>Triggered by the discovery in 2000 of an account entry of 1404 first mentioning the <i>Bratwurst</i> in Thuringia in the town of <a href="/wiki/Arnstadt" title="Arnstadt">Arnstadt</a>, the association "Friends of the <i>Thuringian Bratwurst</i>" was founded in 2006. In the same year, the association established the <i>Erstes Deutsches Bratwurstmuseum</i> (First German Bratwurst Museum) in the village of <a href="/wiki/Wachsenburggemeinde" title="Wachsenburggemeinde">Holzhausen</a>. A two-metre-high wooden monument of a <i>Bratwurst</i> in a bun on a local traffic roundabout advertises the museum.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>

<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="Nordhessische_Bratwurst"><i>Nordhessische Bratwurst</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bratwurst&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Nordhessische Bratwurst">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>
<p>The <i>Nordhessische Bratwurst</i> (from northern <a href="/wiki/Hessen" class="mw-redirect" title="Hessen">Hessen</a>) is similar to the <i>Thüringer Rostbratwurst</i> in taste. It is made from coarsely ground pork and is heavily seasoned. It measures around 20&#160;cm (7.9&#160;in) in length. Traditionally, it is grilled over a wood fire and served on a cut-open roll with mustard.</p>
<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="Rote_Wurst"><i>Rote Wurst</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bratwurst&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Rote Wurst">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>

<p>The <i>Rote Wurst</i> (red sausage) is a favorite <i>Bratwurst</i> of the <a href="/wiki/Swabia" title="Swabia">Swabian</a> region. It is similar to the <i><a href="/wiki/Bockwurst" title="Bockwurst">Bockwurst</a></i>, and is made from finely ground pork and bacon. Its taste is spicy. To prevent splitting during grilling or pan frying, an X is cut into the ends of the sausage. The ends open during cooking, but the rest of the sausage remains intact, giving it its traditional shape.</p>
<p>Another variant is making a row of x-shaped cuts along its length.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="United_States">United States</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bratwurst&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: United States">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Bratwurst, often shortened to "brat" in <a href="/wiki/American_English" title="American English">American English</a>, is a common type of sausage in the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>, especially in the state of <a href="/wiki/Wisconsin" title="Wisconsin">Wisconsin</a>, where the largest ancestry group is German.<sup id="cite_ref-census.gov_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-census.gov-10">[10]</a></sup> Originally brought to North America by German immigrants, it is a common sight at summer cookouts, alongside the more famous hot dog. Wisconsin is also the origin of the "beer brat", a regional favorite where the bratwurst are simmered in beer (generally a mixture of a pilsner style beer with butter and onions) prior to grilling over charcoal.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>Bratwurst was popularized in <a href="/wiki/Sheboygan_County,_Wisconsin" title="Sheboygan County, Wisconsin">Sheboygan County, Wisconsin</a> in the 1920s. In general, each local butcher shop would take orders and hand make bratwurst fresh to be picked up on a particular day. The fat content of the sausages was substantial, making daily pick up necessary to avoid spoilage. Some of the fat is removed as a result of the cooking over charcoal.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>Bratwurst has also become popular as a mainstay of sports stadiums, especially baseball parks, after Bill Sperling introduced bratwurst to <a href="/wiki/Major_League_Baseball" title="Major League Baseball">Major League Baseball</a> in <a href="/wiki/Milwaukee_County_Stadium" title="Milwaukee County Stadium">Milwaukee County Stadium</a> in 1954. The brats, which sold for 35 cents then, were grilled and placed into a container of a special tomato sauce before being served.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> The bratwurst were such a hit, Sperling said, that <a href="/wiki/Duke_Snider" title="Duke Snider">Duke Snider</a> of the <a href="/wiki/History_of_the_Brooklyn_Dodgers" title="History of the Brooklyn Dodgers">Brooklyn Dodgers</a> took a case back to New York.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Miller_Park_(Milwaukee)" title="Miller Park (Milwaukee)">Miller Park</a> in <a href="/wiki/Milwaukee" title="Milwaukee">Milwaukee</a> sells more bratwurst than hot dogs.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>Every year, during the <a href="/wiki/Memorial_Day" title="Memorial Day">Memorial Day</a> weekend, the city of <a href="/wiki/Madison,_Wisconsin" title="Madison, Wisconsin">Madison, Wisconsin</a> hosts the <a href="/wiki/Brat_Fest" title="Brat Fest">Brat Fest</a>, which is billed as the "world's largest bratwurst festival."</p>
<p>The town of <a href="/wiki/Bucyrus,_Ohio" title="Bucyrus, Ohio">Bucyrus, Ohio</a> (which calls itself the "Bratwurst Capital of America") has held the three-day Bucyrus Bratwurst Festival annually since 1967.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
