<p><b><a href="/wiki/Science" title="Science">Science</a></b> is the methodical study of nature including testable <a href="/wiki/Scientific_theory" title="Scientific theory">explanations</a> and <a href="/wiki/Predictability" title="Predictability">predictions</a>. From <a href="/wiki/Classical_antiquity" title="Classical antiquity">classical antiquity</a> through the 19th century, science as a type of knowledge was more closely linked to <a href="/wiki/Philosophy" title="Philosophy">philosophy</a> than it is now and, in fact, in the <a href="/wiki/Western_world" title="Western world">Western world</a>, the term "<a href="/wiki/Natural_philosophy" title="Natural philosophy">natural philosophy</a>" encompassed fields of study that are today associated with science, such as <a href="/wiki/Astronomy" title="Astronomy">astronomy</a>, medicine, and <a href="/wiki/Physics" title="Physics">physics</a>. However, during the <a href="/wiki/Islamic_Golden_Age" title="Islamic Golden Age">Islamic Golden Age</a> foundations for the <a href="/wiki/Scientific_method" title="Scientific method">scientific method</a> were laid by <a href="/wiki/Ibn_al-Haytham" title="Ibn al-Haytham">Ibn al-Haytham</a> in his <i><a href="/wiki/Book_of_Optics" title="Book of Optics">Book of Optics</a></i>. While the classification of the material world by the ancient <a href="/wiki/Mah%C4%81bh%C5%ABta" title="Mahābhūta">Indians</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greece#Science_and_technology" title="Ancient Greece">Greeks</a> into <a href="/wiki/Classical_elements" class="mw-redirect" title="Classical elements">air, earth, fire and water</a> was more philosophical, medieval <a href="/wiki/Science_in_the_medieval_Islamic_world#Notable_scientists" title="Science in the medieval Islamic world">Middle Easterns</a> used <a href="/wiki/Islamic_Golden_Age#Scientific_method" title="Islamic Golden Age">practical, experimental observation</a> to classify materials.</p>
<p>Today, the ever-evolving term "science" refers to the pursuit of knowledge, not the knowledge itself. It is often synonymous with "natural and physical science" and often restricted to those branches of study relating to the phenomena of the material universe and their laws. Although the term implies exclusion of pure mathematics, many university faculties include Mathematics Departments within their Faculty of Science. The dominant sense in ordinary use has a narrower use for the term "science." It developed as a part of science becoming a distinct enterprise of defining the "<a href="/wiki/Laws_of_nature" class="mw-redirect mw-disambig" title="Laws of nature">laws of nature</a>"; early examples include <a href="/wiki/Kepler" class="mw-redirect" title="Kepler">Kepler</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Kepler%27s_laws_of_planetary_motion" title="Kepler's laws of planetary motion">laws</a>, <a href="/wiki/Galileo" class="mw-redirect" title="Galileo">Galileo</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Law_of_falling_bodies" class="mw-redirect" title="Law of falling bodies">laws</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Isaac_Newton" title="Isaac Newton">Newton</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Newton%27s_laws_of_motion" title="Newton's laws of motion">laws of motion</a>. In this period it became more common to refer to natural philosophy as "natural science." Over the course of the 19th century, the word "science" became increasingly associated with the <a href="/wiki/Scientific_method" title="Scientific method">disciplined study</a> of the natural world, including <a href="/wiki/Physics" title="Physics">physics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chemistry" title="Chemistry">chemistry</a>, <a href="/wiki/Geology" title="Geology">geology</a> and <a href="/wiki/Biology" title="Biology">biology</a>. This sometimes left the study of human thought and society in a linguistic limbo, which was resolved by classifying these areas of academic study as <a href="/wiki/Social_science" title="Social science">social science</a>. For example, psychology evolved from philosophy, and has grown into an area of study.</p>
<p>Currently, there are both "hard" (e.g. biological psychology) and "soft" science (e.g. social psychology) fields within the discipline. As a result, and as is consistent with the unfolding of the study of knowledge and development of methods to establish facts, each area of psychology employs a scientific method. Reflecting the evolution of the development of knowledge and established facts and the use of the scientific method, Psychology Departments in universities are found within: Faculty of Arts and Science, Faculty of Arts, and a Faculty of Science. Similarly, several other major areas of disciplined study and knowledge exist today under the general rubric of "science", such as <a href="/wiki/Formal_science" title="Formal science">formal science</a> and <a href="/wiki/Applied_science" title="Applied science">applied science</a>.</p>

<div style="border: 1px solid #069; border-top-width: 1px; vertical-align: top;background: white;opacity: 1; color: black; text-align: left; margin: 0 0 10px; padding: 1em;padding-top: .3em;">
<div style="float:left;margin-right:1em"><a href="/wiki/File:Plates_tect2_en.svg" class="image" title="The tectonic plates of the world were mapped in the second half of the 20th century."><img alt="The tectonic plates of the world were mapped in the second half of the 20th century." src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Plates_tect2_en.svg/250px-Plates_tect2_en.svg.png" width="250" height="171" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Plates_tect2_en.svg/375px-Plates_tect2_en.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Plates_tect2_en.svg/500px-Plates_tect2_en.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="4167" data-file-height="2844" /></a></div>
<b><a href="/wiki/Plate_tectonics" title="Plate tectonics">Plate tectonics</a></b> (from <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a> τέκτων, <i>tektōn</i> "builder" or "mason") is a <a href="/wiki/Theory" title="Theory">theory</a> of <a href="/wiki/Geology" title="Geology">geology</a> which was developed to explain the observed evidence for large scale motions within the Earth's <a href="/wiki/Crust_(geology)" title="Crust (geology)">crust</a>. The theory encompassed and superseded the older theory of <a href="/wiki/Continental_drift" title="Continental drift">continental drift</a> from the first half of the 20th century and the concept of <a href="/wiki/Sea_floor_spreading" class="mw-redirect" title="Sea floor spreading">sea floor spreading</a> developed during the 1960s.
<p>The outermost part of the <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a>'s interior is made up of two layers: above is the <a href="/wiki/Lithosphere" title="Lithosphere">lithosphere</a>, comprising the <a href="/wiki/Crust_(geology)" title="Crust (geology)">crust</a> and the rigid uppermost part of the <a href="/wiki/Earth%27s_mantle" class="mw-redirect" title="Earth's mantle">mantle</a>. Below the lithosphere lies the <a href="/wiki/Asthenosphere" title="Asthenosphere">asthenosphere</a>, which is a more viscous zone of the mantle. Although solid, the asthenosphere has very low shear strength and can flow like a liquid on geological time scales. The deeper mantle below the asthenosphere is more rigid again.</p>

<p><b><a href="/wiki/Rust" title="Rust">Rust</a></b> is the chemical substance formed when <a href="/wiki/Iron" title="Iron">iron</a> compounds <a href="/wiki/Corrode" class="mw-redirect" title="Corrode">corrode</a> in the presence of <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a> and <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">water</a>. It is a mixture of iron <a href="/wiki/Oxides" class="mw-redirect" title="Oxides">oxides</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hydroxides" class="mw-redirect" title="Hydroxides">hydroxides</a>. Rusting is a common term for corrosion, and usually corrosion of steel. Iron is found naturally in the ore <a href="/wiki/Haematite" class="mw-redirect" title="Haematite">haematite</a> as iron oxide, and metallic iron tends to return to a similar state when exposed to air, (<a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen" title="Nitrogen">nitrogen</a>, etc.) and <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">water</a>. This corrosion is due to the oxidation reaction when iron metal returns to an energetically favourable state. Energy is given off when rust forms. The process of rusting can be summarized as three basic stages: The formation of iron(II) ions from the metal; the formation of hydroxide ions; and their reaction together, with the addition of oxygen, to create rust.</p>

<h2 style="font-family:sans-serif; font-size:100%; font-weight:bold; border: none; margin: 0; padding:0; padding-bottom:.1em; color:#069;"><span class="mw-headline" id="Selected_biography"><i>Selected biography</i></span></h2>
</div>
<div style="border: 1px solid #069; border-top-width: 1px; vertical-align: top;background: white;opacity: 1; color: black; text-align: left; margin: 0 0 10px; padding: 1em;padding-top: .3em;">
<div style="float:right;margin-left:0.9em"><a href="/wiki/File:Leonardo_da_Vinci_-_presumed_self-portrait_-_WGA12798.jpg" class="image" title="Leonardo da Vinci"><img alt="Leonardo da Vinci" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/38/Leonardo_da_Vinci_-_presumed_self-portrait_-_WGA12798.jpg/200px-Leonardo_da_Vinci_-_presumed_self-portrait_-_WGA12798.jpg" width="200" height="312" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/38/Leonardo_da_Vinci_-_presumed_self-portrait_-_WGA12798.jpg/300px-Leonardo_da_Vinci_-_presumed_self-portrait_-_WGA12798.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/38/Leonardo_da_Vinci_-_presumed_self-portrait_-_WGA12798.jpg/400px-Leonardo_da_Vinci_-_presumed_self-portrait_-_WGA12798.jpg 2x" data-file-width="740" data-file-height="1153" /></a></div>
<b><a href="/wiki/Leonardo_di_ser_Piero_da_Vinci" class="mw-redirect" title="Leonardo di ser Piero da Vinci">Leonardo di ser Piero da Vinci</a></b> (<span class="unicode haudio"><span class="fn"><span style="white-space:nowrap"><a href="/wiki/File:It-Leonardo_di_ser_Piero_da_Vinci.ogg" title="About this sound"><img alt="About this sound" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/11px-Loudspeaker.svg.png" width="11" height="11" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/17px-Loudspeaker.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/22px-Loudspeaker.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="20" data-file-height="20" /></a>&#160;</span><a href="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/8/89/It-Leonardo_di_ser_Piero_da_Vinci.ogg" class="internal" title="It-Leonardo di ser Piero da Vinci.ogg">pronunciation</a></span>&#160;<small class="metadata audiolinkinfo" style="cursor:help;">(<a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Media_help" title="Wikipedia:Media help"><span style="cursor:help;">help</span></a>·<a href="/wiki/File:It-Leonardo_di_ser_Piero_da_Vinci.ogg" title="File:It-Leonardo di ser Piero da Vinci.ogg"><span style="cursor:help;">info</span></a>)</small></span>), April 15, 1452 – May 2, 1519) was an <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italian</a> <a href="/wiki/Polymath" title="Polymath">polymath</a>, having been a scientist, <a href="/wiki/Mathematician" title="Mathematician">mathematician</a>, <a href="/wiki/Engineer" title="Engineer">engineer</a>, <a href="/wiki/Inventor" title="Inventor">inventor</a>, <a href="/wiki/Anatomist" class="mw-redirect" title="Anatomist">anatomist</a>, <a href="/wiki/Painting" title="Painting">painter</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sculptor" class="mw-redirect" title="Sculptor">sculptor</a>, <a href="/wiki/Architect" title="Architect">architect</a>, <a href="/wiki/Botanist" class="mw-redirect" title="Botanist">botanist</a>, <a href="/wiki/Musician" title="Musician">musician</a> and <a href="/wiki/Writer" title="Writer">writer</a>. Born as the illegitimate son of a notary, Piero da Vinci, and a peasant girl, Caterina, at <a href="/wiki/Vinci,_Italy" class="mw-redirect" title="Vinci, Italy">Vinci</a> in the region of <a href="/wiki/Florence" title="Florence">Florence</a>, Leonardo was educated in the studio of the renowned Florentine painter, <a href="/wiki/Verrocchio" class="mw-redirect" title="Verrocchio">Verrocchio</a>. Much of his earlier working life was spent in the service of <a href="/wiki/Ludovico_il_Moro" class="mw-redirect" title="Ludovico il Moro">Ludovico il Moro</a> in <a href="/wiki/Milan" title="Milan">Milan</a>. He later worked in <a href="/wiki/Rome" title="Rome">Rome</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bologna" title="Bologna">Bologna</a> and <a href="/wiki/Venice" title="Venice">Venice</a>, spending his final years in France at the home given to him by King <a href="/wiki/Fran%C3%A7ois_I_of_France" class="mw-redirect" title="François I of France">François I</a>.
<p>Leonardo has often been described as the <a href="/wiki/Archetype" title="Archetype">archetype</a> of the "<a href="/wiki/Polymath" title="Polymath">Renaissance man</a>", a man whose seemingly infinite curiosity was equalled only by his powers of invention.<sup id="cite_ref-HG_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HG-1">[1]</a></sup> He is widely considered to be one of the greatest <a href="/wiki/Painting" title="Painting">painters</a> of all time and perhaps the most diversely talented person ever to have lived.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>It is primarily as a painter that Leonardo was and is renowned. Two of his works, the <i><a href="/wiki/Mona_Lisa" title="Mona Lisa">Mona Lisa</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/The_Last_Supper_(Leonardo)" class="mw-redirect" title="The Last Supper (Leonardo)">The Last Supper</a></i> occupy unique positions as the most famous, most reproduced and most parodied portrait and religious painting of all time, their fame approached only by <a href="/wiki/Michelangelo" title="Michelangelo">Michelangelo</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Creation_of_Adam" class="mw-redirect" title="Creation of Adam">Creation of Adam</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-HG_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HG-1">[1]</a></sup> Leonardo's drawing of the <i><a href="/wiki/Vitruvian_Man" title="Vitruvian Man">Vitruvian Man</a></i> is also iconic. Perhaps fifteen of his paintings survive, the small number due to his constant, and frequently disastrous, experimentation with new techniques, and his chronic procrastination.<sup class="reference" id="ref_bnone"><a href="#endnote_bnone">[b]</a></sup> Nevertheless, these few works together with his notebooks, which contain drawings, scientific diagrams, and his thoughts on the nature of painting, comprise a contribution to later generations of artists only rivalled by that of his contemporary, <a href="/wiki/Michelangelo" title="Michelangelo">Michelangelo</a>.</p>
