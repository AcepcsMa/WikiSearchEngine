<p><b>Kavala</b> (<a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a>: <span lang="el" xml:lang="el">Καβάλα</span>, <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Greek" title="Help:IPA/Greek">[kaˈvala]</a></span>) is a city in northern <a href="/wiki/Greece" title="Greece">Greece</a>, the principal <a href="/wiki/Seaport" class="mw-redirect" title="Seaport">seaport</a> of eastern <a href="/wiki/Macedonia_(Greece)" title="Macedonia (Greece)">Macedonia</a> and the capital of <a href="/wiki/Kavala_(regional_unit)" title="Kavala (regional unit)">Kavala regional unit</a>.</p>
<p>It is situated on the Bay of Kavala, across from the island of <a href="/wiki/Thasos" title="Thasos">Thasos</a> and on the Egnatia motorway, a one-and-a-half-hour drive to <a href="/wiki/Thessaloniki" title="Thessaloniki">Thessaloniki</a> (160 kilometres (99 miles) west) and a forty-minute drive to <a href="/wiki/Drama,_Greece" title="Drama, Greece">Drama</a> (37&#160;km (23 miles) north) and <a href="/wiki/Xanthi" title="Xanthi">Xanthi</a> (56&#160;km (35 miles) east).</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Names">Names</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kavala&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Names">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In Antiquity the name of the city was <b>Neapolis</b> ('new city', like many Greek colonies). During the Middle Ages, it was renamed <b>Christoupolis</b> ('city of Christ').</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kavala&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The etymology of the modern name of the city is disputed. Some mention an ancient Greek village <i>Skavala</i> near the town. Other proposals include either from the Italian <i>cavallo</i> (= horse), or from the Hebrew <i><a href="/wiki/Kabbalah" title="Kabbalah">Kabbalah</a></i> due to the city's large Jewish population in the past. Its nickname is <i>The cyan city</i> (Η γαλάζια πόλη).</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kavala&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Antiquity">Antiquity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kavala&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Antiquity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Ancient_Philippi_-_panoramio.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ac/Ancient_Philippi_-_panoramio.jpg/220px-Ancient_Philippi_-_panoramio.jpg" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ac/Ancient_Philippi_-_panoramio.jpg/330px-Ancient_Philippi_-_panoramio.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ac/Ancient_Philippi_-_panoramio.jpg/440px-Ancient_Philippi_-_panoramio.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3072" data-file-height="2304" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Ancient_Philippi_-_panoramio.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
View of the archaeological site of <a href="/wiki/Philippi" title="Philippi">Philippi</a>.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The city was founded in the late 7th century BC by settlers from <a href="/wiki/Thassos" class="mw-redirect" title="Thassos">Thassos</a>. It was one of several Thassian colonies along the coastline, all founded in order to take advantage of rich gold and silver mines, especially those located in the nearby Pangaion mountain (which were eventually exploited by <a href="/wiki/Phillip_II_of_Macedonia" class="mw-redirect" title="Phillip II of Macedonia">Phillip II of Macedonia</a>).</p>
<p>Worship of Parthenos / the Virgin, a female deity of Greek–Ionian origin associated with Athena, is archaeologically attested in the Archaic period. At the end of the 6th century BC Neapolis claimed independence from Thassos and began issuing its own silver coins with the <a href="/wiki/Head_of_Gorgo" class="mw-redirect" title="Head of Gorgo">head of Gorgo</a> (γοργὀνειο) on one side. A few decades later a large Ionic temple made from Thassian marble replaced the Archaic one. Parts of it can now be seen in the town's archaeological museum.</p>
<p>In 411 BC, during the <a href="/wiki/Peloponnesian_War" title="Peloponnesian War">Peloponnesian War</a>, Neapolis was besieged by the allied armies of the Spartans and the Thassians but remained faithful to Athens. Two Athenian honorary decrees in 410 and 407 BC rewarded Neapolis for its loyalty.</p>
<p>Neapolis was a town of <a href="/wiki/Macedonia_(region)" title="Macedonia (region)">Macedonia</a>, located 14&#160;km (9&#160;mi) from the harbour of <a href="/wiki/Philippi" title="Philippi">Philippi</a>. It was a member of the <a href="/wiki/Second_Athenian_Empire" title="Second Athenian Empire">Athenian League</a>; a pillar found in Athens mentions the contribution of Neapolis to the alliance.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Roman_Era">Roman Era</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kavala&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Roman Era">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Kavala_egnatia_2.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b5/Kavala_egnatia_2.JPG/220px-Kavala_egnatia_2.JPG" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b5/Kavala_egnatia_2.JPG/330px-Kavala_egnatia_2.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b5/Kavala_egnatia_2.JPG/440px-Kavala_egnatia_2.JPG 2x" data-file-width="3888" data-file-height="2592" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">

<a href="/wiki/Via_Egnatia" title="Via Egnatia">Via Egnatia</a> in Kavala.</div>

<p>The military Roman road <a href="/wiki/Via_Egnatia" title="Via Egnatia">Via Egnatia</a> passed through the city and helped commerce to flourish. It became a <a href="/wiki/Roman_Republic" title="Roman Republic">Roman</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Civitas" title="Civitas">civitas</a></i> in 168 BC, and was a base for <a href="/wiki/Marcus_Junius_Brutus" class="mw-redirect" title="Marcus Junius Brutus">Brutus</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gaius_Cassius_Longinus" title="Gaius Cassius Longinus">Cassius</a> in 42 BC, before their defeat in the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Philippi" title="Battle of Philippi">Battle of Philippi</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Apostle <a href="/wiki/Paul_of_Tarsus" class="mw-redirect" title="Paul of Tarsus">Paul</a> landed at Kavala on his first voyage to Europe.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span id="Byzantine,_Bulgarian_and_Crusader_Era"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Byzantine.2C_Bulgarian_and_Crusader_Era">Byzantine, Bulgarian and Crusader Era</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kavala&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Byzantine, Bulgarian and Crusader Era">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Byzantine_Greece" title="Byzantine Greece">Byzantine Greece</a> and <a href="/wiki/Frankokratia" title="Frankokratia">Frankokratia</a></div>

<p>In the 6th century, <a href="/wiki/Byzantine_emperor" class="mw-redirect" title="Byzantine emperor">Byzantine emperor</a> <a href="/wiki/Justinian_I" title="Justinian I">Justinian I</a> fortified the city in an effort to protect it from barbarian raids. In later <a href="/wiki/Byzantine_Empire" title="Byzantine Empire">Byzantine</a> times the city was called Christoupolis (Χριστούπολις, "city of Christ") and belonged to the <a href="/wiki/Theme_(Byzantine_district)" title="Theme (Byzantine district)">theme</a> of <a href="/wiki/Macedonia_(theme)" title="Macedonia (theme)">Macedonia</a>. The first surviving mention of the new name is in a <i>taktikon</i> of the early 9th century. The city is also mentioned in the "Life of St. <a href="/wiki/Gregory_of_Dekapolis" title="Gregory of Dekapolis">Gregory of Dekapolis</a>". In the 8th and 9th centuries, <a href="/wiki/Bulgarians" title="Bulgarians">Bulgarian</a> attacks forced the Byzantines to reorganise the defence of the area, giving great care to Christoupolis with fortifications and a notable garrison. Bulgarians also ruled it briefly. In 926 the Byzantine general (<i><a href="/wiki/Strategos" title="Strategos">strategos</a></i>) Basil Klaudon reconstructed the town's fallen walls according to an inscription now in the archaeological museum. Thanks to its location, the city experienced an economic resurgence, securing contact between <a href="/wiki/Constantinople" title="Constantinople">Constantinople</a> and <a href="/wiki/Thessaloniki" title="Thessaloniki">Thessaloniki</a>.</p>
<p>During a <a href="/wiki/Normans" title="Normans">Norman</a> raid of Macedonia in 1185, the city was captured and burned. In 1302, the <a href="/wiki/Catalan_Company" title="Catalan Company">Catalans</a> failed to capture the city. In order to prevent them from coming back, the Byzantine emperor <a href="/wiki/Andronikos_III_Palaiologos" title="Andronikos III Palaiologos">Andronikos III Palaiologos</a> built a new long defensive wall. In 1357 two Byzantine officers and brothers, Alexios and John, controlled the city and its territory. Excavations have revealed the ruins of an early Byzantine basilica under an Ottoman mosque in the Old Town. It was used until the late Byzantine era.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ottoman_Era">Ottoman Era</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kavala&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Ottoman Era">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Ottoman_Greece" title="Ottoman Greece">Ottoman Greece</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sanjak_of_Kavala" title="Sanjak of Kavala">Sanjak of Kavala</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Kavala,_Mohammed_Ali_House.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3d/Kavala%2C_Mohammed_Ali_House.JPG/220px-Kavala%2C_Mohammed_Ali_House.JPG" width="220" height="124" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3d/Kavala%2C_Mohammed_Ali_House.JPG/330px-Kavala%2C_Mohammed_Ali_House.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3d/Kavala%2C_Mohammed_Ali_House.JPG/440px-Kavala%2C_Mohammed_Ali_House.JPG 2x" data-file-width="4224" data-file-height="2376" /></a>

<p>The Ottoman Turks first captured the city in 1387 and completely destroyed it in 1391, as a Mount Athos chronicle testifies. Kavala was part of the <a href="/wiki/Ottoman_Empire" title="Ottoman Empire">Ottoman Empire</a> from 1387 to 1912. In the middle of the 16th century, <a href="/wiki/Pargal%C4%B1_%C4%B0brahim_Pasha" class="mw-redirect" title="Pargalı İbrahim Pasha">Ibrahim Pasha</a>, <a href="/wiki/Grand_Vizier" class="mw-redirect" title="Grand Vizier">Grand Vizier</a> of <a href="/wiki/Suleiman_the_Magnificent" title="Suleiman the Magnificent">Suleiman the Magnificent</a>, contributed to the town's prosperity and growth by the construction of <a href="/wiki/Kavala_aqueduct" title="Kavala aqueduct">an aqueduct</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> The Ottomans also extended the Byzantine fortress on the hill of <i>Panagia</i>. Both landmarks are among the most recognizable symbols of the city today.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Muhammad_Ali_of_Egypt" title="Muhammad Ali of Egypt">Mehmet Ali</a>, the founder of a <a href="/wiki/Muhammad_Ali_Dynasty" class="mw-redirect" title="Muhammad Ali Dynasty">dynasty</a> that ruled <a href="/wiki/Egypt" title="Egypt">Egypt</a>, was born in Kavala in 1769. His house has been preserved as a museum.</p>

<p>Kavala was briefly occupied by the Bulgarians during the first Balkan War in 1912, but was finally captured by Greece in 1913 during a successful <a href="/wiki/Landing_operation" title="Landing operation">landing operation</a> by the <a href="/wiki/Greek_Navy" class="mw-redirect" title="Greek Navy">Greek Navy</a> that was commanded by the famous admiral <a href="/wiki/Pavlos_Kountouriotis" title="Pavlos Kountouriotis">Pavlos Kountouriotis</a>.</p>
<p>In August 1916 rests of the <a href="/wiki/IV_Army_Corps_(Greece)" title="IV Army Corps (Greece)">IV Army Corps</a>, stationed at Kavala under <a href="/wiki/Ioannis_Hatzopoulos" title="Ioannis Hatzopoulos">Ioannis Hatzopoulos</a> surrendered to the advancing <a href="/wiki/Bulgarian_Army" class="mw-redirect" title="Bulgarian Army">Bulgarian Army</a>. These events provoked a military revolt in <a href="/wiki/Thessaloniki" title="Thessaloniki">Thessaloniki</a>, which led to the establishment of the <a href="/wiki/Provisional_Government_of_National_Defence" title="Provisional Government of National Defence">Provisional Government of National Defence</a>, and eventually Greece's formal entry into the <a href="/wiki/First_World_War" class="mw-redirect" title="First World War">First World War</a>.</p>
<p>After the <a href="/wiki/Greco-Turkish_War_(1919%E2%80%931922)" title="Greco-Turkish War (1919–1922)">Greco-Turkish War</a> of 1919–1922, the city entered a new era of prosperity because of the labour offered by the thousands of refugees that moved to the area from Asia Minor. The development was both industrial and agricultural. Kavala became greatly involved in the processing and trading of tobacco. Many buildings related to the storage and processing of tobacco from that era are preserved in the city.</p>
<p>During <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a> and after the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Greece" title="Battle of Greece">fall of Athens</a>, the Nazis awarded Kavala to their Bulgarian allies in 1941, causing the city to suffer once again, but it finally was liberated in 1944.</p>
<p>In the late 1950s Kavala expanded towards the sea by <a href="/wiki/Land_reclamation" title="Land reclamation">reclaiming land</a> from the area west of the port.</p>
<p>In 1967, King <a href="/wiki/Constantine_II_of_Greece" title="Constantine II of Greece">Constantine II</a> left Athens for Kavala in an unsuccessful attempt to launch a counter-coup against the <a href="/wiki/Greek_military_junta_of_1967%E2%80%931974" title="Greek military junta of 1967–1974">military junta</a>.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Province">Province</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kavala&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Province">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The province of Kavala (<a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a>: <span lang="el" xml:lang="el">Επαρχία Καβάλας</span>) was one of the <a href="/wiki/Provinces_of_Greece" title="Provinces of Greece">provinces</a> of the Kavala Prefecture. Its territory corresponded with that of the current municipality Kavala, and part of the municipal unit <a href="/wiki/Eleftheroupoli" title="Eleftheroupoli">Eleftheroupoli</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-census91_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-census91-7">[7]</a></sup> It was abolished in 2006.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Economy">Economy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kavala&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Economy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Kavala-oil.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3b/Kavala-oil.jpg/220px-Kavala-oil.jpg" width="220" height="132" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3b/Kavala-oil.jpg/330px-Kavala-oil.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3b/Kavala-oil.jpg/440px-Kavala-oil.jpg 2x" data-file-width="920" data-file-height="551" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Kavala-oil.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Prinos_oil_field" title="Prinos oil field">Prinos oil field</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Traditionally the primary occupation of the population of Kavala was the fishing. The fishermen of the town were well known all over northern Greece.</p>
<p>After the industrialization of the country, Kavala became also a center in northern Greece of the <a href="/wiki/Tobacco_industry" title="Tobacco industry">tobacco industry</a>. Today exists also the building of the "Municipal Tobacco Warehouse".</p>
<p>In the middle of the 20th century <a href="/wiki/Oil_field" title="Oil field">oil deposits</a> were found outside the city and today exists an <a href="/wiki/Oil_platform" title="Oil platform">oil rig</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Climate">Climate</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kavala&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Climate">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Kavala has a <a href="/wiki/Humid_subtropical_climate" title="Humid subtropical climate">humid subtropical climate</a> (<a href="/wiki/K%C3%B6ppen_climate_classification" title="Köppen climate classification">Köppen climate classification</a> <i>Cfa</i>) that borders on a <a href="/wiki/Semi-arid_climate" title="Semi-arid climate">semi-arid climate</a> (Köppen climate classification "BSk" or "BSh" depending on the system used) with annual average precipitation of 460&#160;mm (18.1&#160;in). Snowfalls are sporadic, but happen more or less every year. The humidity is always very high</p>
<p>The absolute maximum temperature ever recorded was 38.0&#160;°C (100&#160;°F), while the absolute minimum ever recorded was −5.8&#160;°C (22&#160;°F).<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Culture">Culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kavala&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Festivals_and_events">Festivals and events</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kavala&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Festivals and events">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Kavala hosts a wide array of cultural events, which mostly take place during the summer months. The top festival is the <b>Festival of <a href="/wiki/Philippi" title="Philippi">Philippi</a></b>,<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> which lasts from July to September and includes theatrical performances and music concerts. Since 1957, it has been the city's most important cultural event and one of the most important of Greece.</p>
<p><b>Cosmopolis</b> is an International Festival held in the Old Town of Kavala that offers an acquaintance with cultures around the world through dancing and musical groups, traditional national cuisines, cinema, and exhibits at the kiosks of the participant countries.</p>
<p><b>Giannis Papaioannou's</b> Festival includes concerts and music seminars.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p><b>Ilios ke Petra (Sun and Stone)</b>(July): a Festival held in "Akontisma" of Nea Karvali. The event is of folkloric character, with the participation of traditional dancing groups from all over the world.</p>
<p><b>Wood Water Wild Festival</b>:<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> Wood Water Wild is an outdoor activities festival, inspired by nature. It includes live bands &amp; DJ sets, body&amp;mind activities, a book fair, outdoor theatre, ecology, camping, and debates.</p>
<p><b>Kavala AirSea Show</b>:<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> An annual air show, which takes place during the last days of June</p>
<p>Besides, various cultural events are held in all municipalities of Kavala during the summer months.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cuisine">Cuisine</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kavala&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Cuisine">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Macedonian_cuisine_(Greek)" title="Macedonian cuisine (Greek)">Macedonian cuisine (Greek)</a></div>
<p>Fish and sea food, as well as the products of the local livestock breeding and agricultural sectors are the prevailing elements of Kavala courses. In Kavala, the traditional local recipes have been influenced by the cuisine of the refugees from Pontos, Asia Minor and Kappadokia. Fresh fish and sea food, salted food, mackerel "gouna" (sun dried mackerel on the grill), sardine pantremeni, mussels with rice, herring saganaki, anchovies wrapped in grape leaves, Stuffed eggplant: these are some very renowned recipes in Kavala and the coastal settlements of the region. The grapes, wine and tsipouro produced in the area, as well as the kourabiedes (sugar-coated almond biscuits) from Nea Karvali are particularly famous.</p>

<div class="thumbinner" style="width:142px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Cavalla_rare_blue_cancellation.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3d/Cavalla_rare_blue_cancellation.jpg/140px-Cavalla_rare_blue_cancellation.jpg" width="140" height="167" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3d/Cavalla_rare_blue_cancellation.jpg/210px-Cavalla_rare_blue_cancellation.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3d/Cavalla_rare_blue_cancellation.jpg/280px-Cavalla_rare_blue_cancellation.jpg 2x" data-file-width="362" data-file-height="433" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Cavalla_rare_blue_cancellation.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Austrian Levant stamp with blue Cavalla cancellation<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></div>

</div>
<p>Austria opened a post office in Kavala before 1864.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> Between 1893 and 1903, the French post office in the city issued its own postage stamps; at first stamps of France <a href="/wiki/Overprint" title="Overprint">overprinted</a> with "Cavalle" and a value in <a href="/wiki/Piaster" class="mw-redirect" title="Piaster">piasters</a>, then in 1902 the French designs inscribed "CAVALLE".</p>

<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Muhammad_Ali_of_Egypt" title="Muhammad Ali of Egypt">Muhammad Ali Pasha of Kavala</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Albanians" title="Albanians">Albanian</a> <a href="/wiki/Wali" title="Wali">Wali</a> (governor) of <a href="/wiki/Egypt" title="Egypt">Egypt</a> between 1805 and 1848 and founder of the modern state of Egypt</li>

<li><a href="/wiki/Nikos_Karageorgiou" title="Nikos Karageorgiou">Nikos Karageorgiou</a>, (born 9 December 1962) Manager of Greek football team <a href="/wiki/Ergotelis" class="mw-redirect" title="Ergotelis">Ergotelis</a>, based in Heraklion, <a href="/wiki/Crete" title="Crete">Crete</a></li>

<li><a href="/wiki/Leontios_Petmezas" title="Leontios Petmezas">Leontios Petmezas</a>, theorist, art historian, book critic, author and journalist.</li>
<li><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mitsos_Partsalidis&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Mitsos Partsalidis (page does not exist)">Mitsos Partsalidis</a>, first elected communist mayor in modern Greek history, back in 1 April 1934</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Antigone_Valakou" class="mw-redirect" title="Antigone Valakou">Antigone Valakou</a>, actress</li>

<li><a href="/w/index.php?title=Thanasis_Euthimiadis&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Thanasis Euthimiadis (page does not exist)">Thanasis Euthimiadis</a>, Greek actor</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Anna_Verouli" title="Anna Verouli">Anna Verouli</a>, 1982 Gold Medalist, European Championship, javelin thrower</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Zisis_Vryzas" title="Zisis Vryzas">Zisis Vryzas</a> (born 9 November 1973), former footballer</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Theodoros_Zagorakis" title="Theodoros Zagorakis">Theodoros Zagorakis</a> (born 27 October 1971), former footballer, captain of national team of Greece-European champion 2004</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Anna_Gerasimou" title="Anna Gerasimou">Anna Gerasimou</a>, Greek tennis player</li>

<li><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kleon_Krantonellis&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Kleon Krantonellis (page does not exist)">Kleon Krantonellis</a>, architect</li>
