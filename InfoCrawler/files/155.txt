<p><b>Literary Hall</b> is a mid-19th-century brick <a href="/wiki/Library" title="Library">library</a>, building and museum located in <a href="/wiki/Romney,_West_Virginia" title="Romney, West Virginia">Romney</a>, a city in the U.S. state of <a href="/wiki/West_Virginia" title="West Virginia">West Virginia</a>. It is located at the intersection of North High Street (<a href="/wiki/West_Virginia_Route_28" title="West Virginia Route 28">West Virginia Route 28</a>) and West Main Street (<a href="/wiki/U.S._Route_50" title="U.S. Route 50">U.S. Route 50</a>). Literary Hall was constructed between 1869 and 1870 by the <a href="/wiki/Romney_Literary_Society" title="Romney Literary Society">Romney Literary Society</a>.</p>
<p>Founded in 1819, the Romney Literary Society was the first <a href="/wiki/Literary_society" title="Literary society">literary organization</a> of its kind in the present-day state of <a href="/wiki/West_Virginia" title="West Virginia">West Virginia</a>, and one of the first in the United States. In 1846, the society constructed a building which housed the <a href="/wiki/Romney_Classical_Institute" title="Romney Classical Institute">Romney Classical Institute</a> and its library. The Romney Literary Society and the Romney Classical Institute both flourished and continued to grow in importance and influence until the onset of the <a href="/wiki/American_Civil_War" title="American Civil War">American Civil War</a> in 1861.</p>
<p>During the war, the contents of the society's library were plundered by <a href="/wiki/Union_Army" title="Union Army">Union Army</a> forces, and many of its 3,000 volumes were either scattered or destroyed. After a reorganization in 1869, the society commenced construction of the present Literary Hall in downtown Romney. It transferred ownership of its Romney Classical Institute campus to the <a href="/wiki/West_Virginia_Schools_for_the_Deaf_and_Blind" title="West Virginia Schools for the Deaf and Blind">West Virginia Schools for the Deaf and Blind</a> in 1870 and in that year completed Literary Hall, where the society reconstituted its library collection and revived its literary activities.</p>
<p>The Romney Literary Society's last meeting was held at Literary Hall in 1886. From that point to 1973 the building was used as a meeting space by the Clinton Lodge of <a href="/wiki/Masonic_lodge" title="Masonic lodge">Ancient Free and Accepted Masons</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Order_of_the_Eastern_Star" title="Order of the Eastern Star">Order of the Eastern Star</a>. In 1973, the building was purchased by prominent Romney lawyer Ralph Haines, who used it as a law office and museum. From 1937 to the early 1940s the building also housed a community library. Literary Hall was listed on the <a href="/wiki/National_Register_of_Historic_Places" title="National Register of Historic Places">National Register of Historic Places</a> on May 29, 1979.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geography_and_setting">Geography and setting</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Literary_Hall&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Geography and setting">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Literary Hall is located in the center of downtown <a href="/wiki/Romney,_West_Virginia" title="Romney, West Virginia">Romney, West Virginia</a>, at the intersection of North High Street (<a href="/wiki/West_Virginia_Route_28" title="West Virginia Route 28">West Virginia Route 28</a>) and West Main Street (<a href="/wiki/U.S._Route_50" title="U.S. Route 50">U.S. Route 50</a>) on a city lot less than one acre (0.40&#160;ha) in size.<sup id="cite_ref-Pauley1_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pauley1-4">[4]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Pauley5_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pauley5-5">[5]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-USGS1_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-USGS1-6">[6]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Hampshire_County_Courthouse_(West_Virginia)" title="Hampshire County Courthouse (West Virginia)">Hampshire County Courthouse</a> is immediately to its east across North High Street, and the Romney First United Methodist Church is situated immediately to the building's north.<sup id="cite_ref-USGS1_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-USGS1-6">[6]</a></sup> The Old National Building is located to the immediate south of Literary Hall across West Main Street.<sup id="cite_ref-USGS1_6-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-USGS1-6">[6]</a></sup> Literary Hall stands at an elevation of 820.3 feet (250.0&#160;m) above sea level.<sup id="cite_ref-USGS1_6-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-USGS1-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Literary_Hall&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Location">Location</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Literary_Hall&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Location">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The land upon which Literary Hall was established was originally part of the <a href="/wiki/Northern_Neck_Proprietary" title="Northern Neck Proprietary">Northern Neck Proprietary</a>, a 5,000,000-acre (20,000&#160;km<sup>2</sup>) land grant that <a href="/wiki/Charles_II_of_England" title="Charles II of England">Charles II of England</a> awarded to seven of his supporters in the <a href="/wiki/Colony_of_Virginia" title="Colony of Virginia">Colony of Virginia</a> in 1649 and renewed by an official patent in 1688.<sup id="cite_ref-Munske5_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Munske5-7">[7]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Coleman1_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Coleman1-8">[8]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Rose_1_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rose_1-9">[9]</a></sup> One of these seven supporters, <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Colepeper,_2nd_Baron_Colepeper" title="Thomas Colepeper, 2nd Baron Colepeper">Thomas Colepeper, 2nd Baron Colepeper</a>, acquired the entire area in 1681; his grandson, <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Fairfax,_6th_Lord_Fairfax_of_Cameron" title="Thomas Fairfax, 6th Lord Fairfax of Cameron">Thomas Fairfax, 6th Lord Fairfax of Cameron</a>, inherited it in 1719.<sup id="cite_ref-Munske5_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Munske5-7">[7]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-W.26MQ1_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-W.26MQ1-10">[10]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Rice1_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rice1-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>Literary Hall was built upon a land lot designated "Lot 56" to the immediate west of the public square of Romney.<sup id="cite_ref-FWP3_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FWP3-12">[12]</a></sup> In 1790, the <a href="/wiki/Trustee" title="Trustee">trustees</a> of the Town of Romney commissioned John Mitchel to draft a <a href="/wiki/Cadastral_surveying" title="Cadastral surveying">cadastral survey map</a> of Romney.<sup id="cite_ref-FWP1_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FWP1-13">[13]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FWP2_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FWP2-14">[14]</a></sup> Prior to this survey, Lord Fairfax had commissioned a similar cadastral survey of Romney sometime before the town's <a href="/wiki/Municipal_corporation" title="Municipal corporation">incorporation</a> on December 23, 1762.<sup id="cite_ref-FWP1_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FWP1-13">[13]</a></sup> On June 30, 1790, Mitchel submitted to the trustees a "Plan of the Town of Romney", which divided the town into 100 land lots of equal size, with four lots adjacent to the <a href="/wiki/Courthouse" title="Courthouse">courthouse</a> comprising the public square.<sup id="cite_ref-FWP2_14-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FWP2-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Romney_Literary_Society">Romney Literary Society</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Literary_Hall&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Romney Literary Society">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Romney_Literary_Society" title="Romney Literary Society">Romney Literary Society</a>, which built Literary Hall between 1869 and 1870, was organized by nine prominent men in Romney on January 30, 1819.<sup id="cite_ref-Maxwell1_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Maxwell1-15">[15]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Brannon8_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Brannon8-16">[16]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FWP7_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FWP7-17">[17]</a></sup> With its establishment in 1819, the Romney Literary Society became the first literary organization of its kind in the present-day state of <a href="/wiki/West_Virginia" title="West Virginia">West Virginia</a>, and one of the first in the United States.<sup id="cite_ref-Maxwell1_15-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Maxwell1-15">[15]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FWP8_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FWP8-18">[18]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Munske1_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Munske1-19">[19]</a></sup> On February 4, 1819, the constitution of the society was adopted, which provided that the organization should be known as the "<a href="/wiki/Polemic" title="Polemic">Polemic</a> Society of Romney".<sup id="cite_ref-Maxwell1_15-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Maxwell1-15">[15]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Brannon8_16-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Brannon8-16">[16]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Pauley3_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pauley3-20">[20]</a></sup> The society founded its library in 1819 with the acquisition of two books, and by 1861 the society's humble library had grown to contain approximately 3,000 volumes, consisting of books on literature, science, history and art.<sup id="cite_ref-Maxwell2_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Maxwell2-21">[21]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FWP9_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FWP9-22">[22]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Brannon4_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Brannon4-23">[23]</a></sup></p>

<p>The Romney Literary Society commenced a movement to establish an institution for "the higher education of the youth of the community".<sup id="cite_ref-Pauley3_20-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pauley3-20">[20]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Brannon4_23-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Brannon4-23">[23]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FWP10_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FWP10-24">[24]</a></sup> As a result of this initiative, the teaching of the classics was introduced into the curriculum of <a href="/wiki/Romney_Academy" title="Romney Academy">Romney Academy</a> in 1820, thus making the institution the first school of higher education in the <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Panhandle_of_West_Virginia" title="Eastern Panhandle of West Virginia">Eastern Panhandle</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Pauley3_20-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pauley3-20">[20]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Brannon4_23-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Brannon4-23">[23]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FWP10_24-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FWP10-24">[24]</a></sup> In 1846, the society constructed a new building to house the <a href="/wiki/Romney_Classical_Institute" title="Romney Classical Institute">Romney Classical Institute</a> and its library, both of which fell under the society's supervision.<sup id="cite_ref-FWP11_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FWP11-25">[25]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Maxwell3_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Maxwell3-26">[26]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Brannon5_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Brannon5-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Romney Literary Society and the Romney Classical Institute both flourished and continued to grow in importance and influence until the onset of the <a href="/wiki/American_Civil_War" title="American Civil War">Civil War</a> in 1861.<sup id="cite_ref-Pauley3_20-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pauley3-20">[20]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FWP9_22-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FWP9-22">[22]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Maxwell4_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Maxwell4-28">[28]</a></sup> During the war, many members fought for the <a href="/wiki/Confederate_States_Army" title="Confederate States Army">Confederate States Army</a> forces and were killed during the conflict.<sup id="cite_ref-Pauley3_20-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pauley3-20">[20]</a></sup> The contents of the society's library were plundered by <a href="/wiki/Union_Army" title="Union Army">Union Army</a> forces, and many of its 3,000 volumes were either scattered or destroyed.<sup id="cite_ref-Pauley3_20-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pauley3-20">[20]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Maxwell4_28-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Maxwell4-28">[28]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FWP12_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FWP12-29">[29]</a></sup> Following the war's end, only 400 of those volumes could be recovered, with just 200 remaining on the library's shelves.<sup id="cite_ref-Maxwell4_28-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Maxwell4-28">[28]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FWP12_29-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FWP12-29">[29]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Brannon1_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Brannon1-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Romney Literary Society reorganized on May 15, 1869.<sup id="cite_ref-Maxwell4_28-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Maxwell4-28">[28]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FWP12_29-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FWP12-29">[29]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Munske2_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Munske2-31">[31]</a></sup> Following the reorganization, the society built Literary Hall between 1869 and 1870 while also undertaking an initiative to bring the <a href="/wiki/West_Virginia_Schools_for_the_Deaf_and_Blind" title="West Virginia Schools for the Deaf and Blind">West Virginia Schools for the Deaf and Blind</a> to its old Romney Classical Institute campus. The construction of Literary Hall was part of the society's effort to revive the organization and reassemble its original library.<sup id="cite_ref-Brannon1_30-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Brannon1-30">[30]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Pauley4_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pauley4-32">[32]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FWP4_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FWP4-33">[33]</a></sup> The society set about recovering original volumes and purchasing new ones, and the library reopened with 700 volumes. For a period of ten years between 1870 and 1880, much of Romney's intellectual life centered on Literary Hall. As older members died, interest in the society began to dwindle.<sup id="cite_ref-Brannon1_30-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Brannon1-30">[30]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Pauley4_32-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pauley4-32">[32]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FWP5_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FWP5-34">[34]</a></sup> The society's meetings were held less frequently, and its last recorded meeting was held on February 15, 1886.<sup id="cite_ref-Pauley4_32-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pauley4-32">[32]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FWP5_34-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FWP5-34">[34]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Brannon2_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Brannon2-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Clinton_Lodge">Clinton Lodge</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Literary_Hall&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Clinton Lodge">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Clinton Lodge of Ancient Free and Accepted Masons was the second organization to utilize Literary Hall as a meeting place.<sup id="cite_ref-Pauley4_32-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pauley4-32">[32]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Brannon2_35-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Brannon2-35">[35]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FWP6_36-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FWP6-36">[36]</a></sup> The Clinton Lodge was the first Masonic lodge to be chartered in <a href="/wiki/Hampshire_County,_West_Virginia" title="Hampshire County, West Virginia">Hampshire County</a> and is among the earliest Masonic lodges to be established in present-day West Virginia.<sup id="cite_ref-Brannon9_37-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Brannon9-37">[37]</a></sup> It was chartered by the <a href="/wiki/Grand_Lodge_of_Virginia" title="Grand Lodge of Virginia">Grand Lodge of Virginia</a> on December 13, 1825.<sup id="cite_ref-Brannon9_37-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Brannon9-37">[37]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-CET1_38-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CET1-38">[38]</a></sup> The lodge underwent four periods of hiatus: first, between 1838 and December 15, 1846; a second time between 1855 and its rechartering on July 3, 1857; a third time between 1861 and December 11, 1867, during the Civil War and <a href="/wiki/Reconstruction_Era" title="Reconstruction Era">Reconstruction</a>; and a fourth time between November 29, 1881 and November 12, 1890, when it was again reactivated.<sup id="cite_ref-CET1_38-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CET1-38">[38]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Brannon10_39-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Brannon10-39">[39]</a></sup></p>
<p>Following the completion of Literary Hall in 1870, the Clinton Lodge Masons regularly utilized the first floor of the building as a meeting space, while the Romney Literary Society maintained its use of the second floor as a library.<sup id="cite_ref-CET1_38-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CET1-38">[38]</a></sup> Between its various periods of dormancy, the Clinton Lodge and the associated <a href="/wiki/Order_of_the_Eastern_Star" title="Order of the Eastern Star">Order of the Eastern Star</a>, which was organized in July 1919, utilized Literary Hall as a meeting space, and following the Romney Literary Society's disestablishment, the Masons and Eastern Star continued their use of the building.<sup id="cite_ref-Pauley4_32-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pauley4-32">[32]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FWP6_36-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FWP6-36">[36]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Brannon11_40-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Brannon11-40">[40]</a></sup> In 1973, prominent Romney lawyer Ralph Haines purchased Literary Hall from the Clinton Lodge Masons and provided the lodge with a land lot for the construction of a new Masonic Temple at the corner of Washington and Center Streets in Romney.<sup id="cite_ref-CET1_38-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CET1-38">[38]</a></sup> Following his purchase of Literary Hall, Haines used the historic building as a law office building and private museum.<sup id="cite_ref-Pauley4_32-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pauley4-32">[32]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Brannon2_35-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Brannon2-35">[35]</a></sup> Literary Hall was listed on the <a href="/wiki/National_Register_of_Historic_Places" title="National Register of Historic Places">National Register of Historic Places</a> on May 29, 1979.<sup id="cite_ref-WVSHPO1_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WVSHPO1-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Hampshire_County_Public_Library">Hampshire County Public Library</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Literary_Hall&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Hampshire County Public Library">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The building again served as a library in March 1937, when Romney's community library was relocated to the first floor of Literary Hall from its previous facility in the basement of the Hampshire County Courthouse. The library had been established on April 11, 1935 as a project by the <a href="/wiki/American_Legion_Auxiliary" title="American Legion Auxiliary">American Legion Auxiliary</a>, and was housed in a room of the courthouse basement where it was staffed by volunteers and open from 2pm to 4pm on Saturday afternoons. When the library's collection of both purchased and donated books outgrew its space in the courthouse basement, the Clinton Lodge Masons granted permission to the library to house its collection at Literary Hall in 1937.<sup id="cite_ref-Brannon6_41-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Brannon6-41">[41]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Munske3_42-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Munske3-42">[42]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-HCPL1_43-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HCPL1-43">[43]</a></sup> Following its move to Literary Hall, the library expanded its hours,<sup id="cite_ref-Munske3_42-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Munske3-42">[42]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Brannon7_44-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Brannon7-44">[44]</a></sup> and expanded them again, to five afternoons a week, through the assistance of the <a href="/wiki/National_Youth_Administration" title="National Youth Administration">National Youth Administration</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Works_Progress_Administration" title="Works Progress Administration">Works Progress Administration</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Munske3_42-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Munske3-42">[42]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Brannon7_44-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Brannon7-44">[44]</a></sup></p>
<p>In January 1942, a meeting was held for the purpose of establishing a more extensive library service. Later that month, on January 29, a charter was issued by <a href="/wiki/Secretary_of_State_of_West_Virginia" title="Secretary of State of West Virginia">West Virginia Secretary of State</a> <a href="/wiki/William_Smith_O%27Brien_(Congressman)" title="William Smith O'Brien (Congressman)">William Smith O'Brien</a> for the creation of the non-profit Hampshire County Library Association.<sup id="cite_ref-Munske3_42-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Munske3-42">[42]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-HCPL1_43-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HCPL1-43">[43]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Brannon7_44-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Brannon7-44">[44]</a></sup> Following its incorporation, the library was relocated from Literary Hall to two rear adjoining rooms on the second story of the Hampshire County Courthouse.<sup id="cite_ref-Munske3_42-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Munske3-42">[42]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Brannon7_44-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Brannon7-44">[44]</a></sup> The association continued to house its library collection in the courthouse until 1967, when the new Hampshire County Public Library building at 153&#160;West Main Street was formally dedicated on September 28, 1967.<sup id="cite_ref-Brannon8_16-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Brannon8-16">[16]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Munske3_42-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Munske3-42">[42]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-HCPL1_43-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HCPL1-43">[43]</a></sup> The library had been built on land donated by sisters Kate and Mary Davis, who had been engaged in the restoration of Literary Hall and whose father, Charles Maurice Davis, had been a member of both the Romney Literary Society and the Clinton Lodge Masons, which had both held meetings in the building.<sup id="cite_ref-Munske4_45-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Munske4-45">[45]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Architecture">Architecture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Literary_Hall&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Architecture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>The interior and exterior of Literary Hall remain largely intact. At two stories, the building is tall in its proportion and incorporates elements from both early American and <a href="/wiki/Victorian_architecture" title="Victorian architecture">Victorian</a> styles, which were common in academic buildings built during this period.<sup id="cite_ref-Pauley2_46-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pauley2-46">[46]</a></sup></p>
<p>Architectural historian S.&#160;Allen Chambers described Literary Hall as an anomaly because the basic design and <a href="/wiki/Fenestration" class="mw-disambig" title="Fenestration">fenestration</a> patterns, which invoke early <a href="/wiki/Federal_architecture" title="Federal architecture">Federal</a> and <a href="/wiki/Greek_Revival_architecture" title="Greek Revival architecture">Greek Revival</a> design elements, are adorned with details more characteristic of the Victorian era.<sup id="cite_ref-Chambers1_47-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Chambers1-47">[47]</a></sup></p>
<p>According to architectural historian Michael J. Pauley of the West Virginia Department of Culture and History's Historic Preservation Unit, Literary Hall's unique structural features make the building "one of Romney's and Hampshire County's most notable landmarks, and one in which this community is justifiably proud".<sup id="cite_ref-Pauley2_46-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pauley2-46">[46]</a></sup> Pauley further averred that the building is "highly representative of the development of education and literature in the early United States".<sup id="cite_ref-Pauley3_20-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pauley3-20">[20]</a></sup> In describing its impact on Romney's streetscape, Chambers described Literary Hall as "adding distinction to Romney's major street intersection".<sup id="cite_ref-Chambers1_47-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Chambers1-47">[47]</a></sup> Chambers also noted the building's resemblance and "strong architectural kinship" to the <a href="/wiki/Romney_Presbyterian_Church" title="Romney Presbyterian Church">Romney Presbyterian Church</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Chambers3_48-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Chambers3-48">[48]</a></sup></p>
<p>Literary Hall is a two-story red <a href="/wiki/Brick" title="Brick">brick</a> structure, rectangular in plan, and topped with a <a href="/wiki/Gable" title="Gable">gable</a> roof.<sup id="cite_ref-Pauley2_46-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pauley2-46">[46]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Chambers1_47-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Chambers1-47">[47]</a></sup> The first floor of Literary Hall consists of four rooms, and the second story is a single large <a href="/wiki/Ballroom" title="Ballroom">ballroom</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Pauley2_46-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pauley2-46">[46]</a></sup> Fused with symmetrical elements evoking Federal and Greek Revival architectural styles are exterior <a href="/wiki/Molding_(decorative)" title="Molding (decorative)">decorative moldings</a> and brick <a href="/wiki/Corbel" title="Corbel">corbeling</a> in the Victorian style.<sup id="cite_ref-Pauley2_46-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pauley2-46">[46]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Chambers1_47-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Chambers1-47">[47]</a></sup></p>

<p>The main façade (south elevation) of Literary Hall is divided into three <a href="/wiki/Bay_(architecture)" title="Bay (architecture)">bays</a>, and its west and east elevations are divided into five bays.<sup id="cite_ref-Pauley2_46-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pauley2-46">[46]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Chambers1_47-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Chambers1-47">[47]</a></sup> Each of the building's bays consists of a <a href="/wiki/Sash_window" title="Sash window">double-hung sash wooden window</a>, with nine panes of glass within each sash.<sup id="cite_ref-Pauley2_46-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pauley2-46">[46]</a></sup> The windows are symmetrically placed within recessed brick panels that are defined at the first and second stories of the building with elementary brick ribs, or pseudo-pilasters, that form the outer surface of the building's exterior walls and provide separation of the three bays of the main façade and the five bays of the west and east elevations.<sup id="cite_ref-Pauley2_46-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pauley2-46">[46]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Chambers1_47-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Chambers1-47">[47]</a></sup> The windows are framed by white wooden <a href="/wiki/Louver" title="Louver">louvered</a> <a href="/wiki/Window_shutter" title="Window shutter">window shutters</a>. The building's brick is laid in an <a href="/wiki/Brickwork#American.2C_or_common_bond" title="Brickwork">American bond</a> pattern, with five courses of stretchers between every course of headers.<sup id="cite_ref-Pauley2_46-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pauley2-46">[46]</a></sup></p>
<p>The main façade is topped by a simple wooden <a href="/wiki/Cornice#Rake" title="Cornice">raking cornice</a> that surmounts an ornamental corbel brick pendant. A semicircular <a href="/wiki/Fanlight" title="Fanlight">fanlight</a>, or lunette, is located in the center of the gable of the building's main façade, providing lighting to the building's attic.<sup id="cite_ref-Pauley2_46-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pauley2-46">[46]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Chambers1_47-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Chambers1-47">[47]</a></sup> Chambers described this window as the most antiquated of the building's architectural elements and may have been based on the fanlight of the county's earlier courthouse built in 1833, which was located to the building's east.<sup id="cite_ref-Chambers2_49-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Chambers2-49">[49]</a></sup></p>
<p>Each of the main façade's first- and second-story windows and the entrance are adorned with white wooden <a href="/wiki/Hood_mould" title="Hood mould">label moldings</a>. The main entrance is composed of a tall double wooden entrance door, with its original handle and locks. Each door consists of four vertical wooden panels. A rectangular <a href="/wiki/Transom_(architectural)" title="Transom (architectural)">transom</a> light of four vertical glass panels tops the main entryway's double wooden doors. Outside the entryway is a spacious brick <a href="/wiki/Stoop_(architecture)" title="Stoop (architecture)">stoop</a> that is accessed by a cement walkway from West Main Street. Its brick steps leading up to the stoop were last restored in the late 1970s.<sup id="cite_ref-Pauley2_46-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pauley2-46">[46]</a></sup></p>
<p>Literary Hall is flanked by two interior side <a href="/wiki/Chimney" title="Chimney">chimneys</a>, between the second and third bays of the west and east elevations of the building. The building rests on a sandstone, <a href="/wiki/Ashlar" title="Ashlar">ashlar</a> block foundation. Its gable roof is sheathed with <a href="/wiki/Metal_roof" title="Metal roof">standing seam tin sheeting</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Pauley2_46-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pauley2-46">[46]</a></sup></p>
