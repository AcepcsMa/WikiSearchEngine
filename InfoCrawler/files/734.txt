<p><b>Alexander Schreiner</b> (July 31, 1901 – September 15, 1987) was one of the most noted organists of the <a href="/wiki/Salt_Lake_Tabernacle_organ" title="Salt Lake Tabernacle organ">Salt Lake Tabernacle</a>. He also wrote the music to several <a href="/wiki/The_Church_of_Jesus_Christ_of_Latter-day_Saints_hymns" title="The Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints hymns">LDS hymns</a>, several of which are in the <a href="/wiki/Hymns_of_The_Church_of_Jesus_Christ_of_Latter-day_Saints_(1985_book)" title="Hymns of The Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints (1985 book)">current edition of the hymn book</a> of <a href="/wiki/The_Church_of_Jesus_Christ_of_Latter-day_Saints" title="The Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints">The Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints</a> (LDS Church).</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alexander_Schreiner&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Alexander Ferdinand Schreiner <sup id="cite_ref-FamilySearch.org_-_Search_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FamilySearch.org_-_Search-1">[1]</a></sup> was born on July 31, 1901 in <a href="/wiki/Nuremberg" title="Nuremberg">Nuremberg</a>, <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> His parents were Johann Christian Schreiner and Margarethe Schwemmer. Johann and Margarethe joined the LDS Church in 1903, and the local congregation held meetings in the family's home. Schreiner performed in public for the first time at age five, and after he was <a href="/wiki/Baptism" title="Baptism">baptized</a> at age eight was almost immediately appointed as a <a href="/wiki/Sunday_School_(LDS_Church)" title="Sunday School (LDS Church)">Sunday School</a> organist. In 1912 Schreiner moved with his family to Salt Lake City.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Among his early instructors on the organ was <a href="/wiki/John_J._McClellan" title="John J. McClellan">John J. McClellan</a>.</p>
<p>Schreiner first performed on an organ professionally during the fall of 1917 at the American Theater of Salt Lake City.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> In 1920, just after graduating from high school, he took a job as a theater organist at the Rialto Theatre in <a href="/wiki/Butte,_Montana" title="Butte, Montana">Butte, Montana</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Schreiner first performed in the Salt Lake Tabernacle at age 20. That same year he left on a <a href="/wiki/Missionary_(LDS_Church)" title="Missionary (LDS Church)">mission</a> to <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a>. He served as a missionary under <a href="/wiki/Joseph_W._McMurrin" title="Joseph W. McMurrin">Joseph W. McMurrin</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> In early 1924, he was presiding over the Los Angeles <a href="/wiki/District_(LDS_Church)" title="District (LDS Church)">Conference</a> which had 35 missionaries.</p>
<p>In 1924, just after returning from his mission, Schreiner was appointed an assistant organist of the Salt Lake Mormon Tabernacle.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Six months later Schreiner took a leave of absence from this appointment to go to Paris to further his musical studies<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> with Henri Libert, <a href="/wiki/Charles_Marie_Widor" class="mw-redirect" title="Charles Marie Widor">Charles Marie Widor</a> and <a href="/wiki/Louis_Vierne" title="Louis Vierne">Louis Vierne</a>. In Paris, Schreiner would associate with other Utahns at the home of <a href="/wiki/James_L._Barker" title="James L. Barker">James L. Barker</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>Schreiner married Margaret Lyman, the daughter of <a href="/wiki/Richard_R._Lyman" title="Richard R. Lyman">Richard R. Lyman</a> and <a href="/wiki/Amy_Brown_Lyman" class="mw-redirect" title="Amy Brown Lyman">Amy Brown Lyman</a>, in 1927.<sup id="cite_ref-FamilySearch.org_-_Search_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FamilySearch.org_-_Search-1">[1]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> They had gone to high school together, but did not really start dating until they were both studying in Paris.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="California_career">California career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alexander_Schreiner&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: California career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>After his studies in France, Schreiner returned to his position as organist in <a href="/wiki/Salt_Lake_City" title="Salt Lake City">Salt Lake City</a> from July 1926 to January 1927. He then went to southern California to earn enough money to pay off his debts and be in a position to marry Margaret. He served as organist at <a href="/wiki/Grauman%27s_Metropolitan_Theatre" class="mw-redirect" title="Grauman's Metropolitan Theatre">Grauman's Metropolitan Theatre</a>. The following June he returned to Salt Lake City and he and Margaret married, after which they returned to southern California. During this time period Schreiner also worked as an organist at the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Barker_Brother%27s_Department_Store&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Barker Brother's Department Store (page does not exist)">Barker Brother's Department Store</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>In August 1928, Schreiner once again returned to Salt Lake City, where he resumed his position as Tabernacle Organist and also served as the chief organist at the Capitol Theatre.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1929 Schreiner returned to southern California in an attempt to overcome his influenza.<sup id="cite_ref-Berghout._p._53_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Berghout._p._53-14">[14]</a></sup> He was appointed chief organist of the First Methodist Episcopal Church in Los Angeles.<sup id="cite_ref-Berghout._p._53_14-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Berghout._p._53-14">[14]</a></sup> Schreiner returned to Salt Lake and the Tabernacle in the summer of 1930, but in September 1930 he began a term as the organist of the <a href="/wiki/University_of_California_at_Los_Angeles" class="mw-redirect" title="University of California at Los Angeles">University of California at Los Angeles</a> (UCLA).<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> Through 1939 Schreiner retained this position at UCLA and would return to the Tabernacle for summers. During this time, he also was a member of the LDS Church's Music Committee.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> Schreiner was also director of music for the Jewish Wilshire Boulevard Temple during this time. By 1930 Schreiner's reputation as an organist was great enough he was recruited on occasion to inaugurate new organs.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>Despite his major commitments to music Schreiner also served in the LDS Church in other ways. He was a member of the <a href="/wiki/High_council_(Latter_Day_Saints)" title="High council (Latter Day Saints)">high council</a> of the church's <a href="/wiki/Hollywood,_California" class="mw-redirect" title="Hollywood, California">Hollywood</a> <a href="/wiki/Stake_(Latter_Day_Saints)" title="Stake (Latter Day Saints)">Stake</a>. Some of his children followed him into music, one beginning to serve as a Sunday School organist at age seven.</p>
<p>In September 1936, Schreiner was made the stake music director of the Hollywood Stake.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Return_to_Salt_Lake">Return to Salt Lake</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alexander_Schreiner&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Return to Salt Lake">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In the summer of 1937, Schreiner took up the position of organist at the LDS chapel in <a href="/wiki/Washington_DC" class="mw-redirect" title="Washington DC">Washington DC</a> which had been vacant since the death the previous March of his one time fellow Salt Lake Tabernacle Organist, <a href="/wiki/Edward_P._Kimball" title="Edward P. Kimball">Edward P. Kimball</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> In the 1930s Schreiner also served as a member of the LDS Sunday School General Board.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the summer of 1938 Schreiner met with LDS Church <a href="/wiki/President_of_the_Church_(LDS_Church)" title="President of the Church (LDS Church)">president</a> <a href="/wiki/Heber_J._Grant" title="Heber J. Grant">Heber J. Grant</a> and they decided he should return to Salt Lake City on a permanent basis as soon as possible. Schreiner had already signed another one-year contract with UCLA, but that would be his last year with that institution.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>From that time until 1977, Schreiner was closely involved with the Tabernacle Choir both at home and abroad. In the late 1940s, Schreiner was involved in a major rebuild of the Tabernacle Organ.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Concert_tours">Concert tours</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alexander_Schreiner&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Concert tours">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Beginning in 1943 Schreiner performed several concert tours. Initially his touring was managed by <a href="/w/index.php?title=Bernard_LaBerge&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Bernard LaBerge (page does not exist)">Bernard LaBerge</a>. He eventually performed in at least 44 states in the United States.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Works">Works</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alexander_Schreiner&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Works">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Besides his many hymns, Schreiner wrote a book entitled <i>Organ Voluntaries</i>.</p>
<p>Schreiner wrote the music to the following hymns in the <a href="/wiki/Hymns_of_The_Church_of_Jesus_Christ_of_Latter-day_Saints_(1985_book)" title="Hymns of The Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints (1985 book)">1985 English edition</a> of the Latter-day Saint hymnal:</p>
<ul>
<li>Truth Eternal (#4)</li>
<li>Lead Me Into Life Eternal (#45)</li>
<li>Thy Spirit, Lord, Has Stirred Our Souls (#157)</li>
<li>While of These Emblems We Partake (Aeolian) (#174)</li>
<li>God Loved Us, So He Sent His Son (#187)</li>
<li>In Memory of the Crucified (#190)</li>
<li>Lord, Accept into thy Kingdom (#236)</li>
<li>Behold Thy Sons and Daughters Lord (#238)</li>
<li>Holy Temples on Mount Zion (#289)</li>
</ul>
<p>Some of his writings refer specifically to his association with the Tabernacle organ such as the following:</p>
<ul>
<li>Schreiner, Alexander. Alexander Schreiner Reminisces (Salt Lake City, 1984).</li>
<li>Schreiner, Alexander. "100 Years of Organs in the Mormon Tabernacle." The Diapason (November 1967)</li>
<li>Schreiner, Alexander. "The Tabernacle Organ in Salt Lake City." Organ Institute Quarterly, Vol. 7, No. 1 (1957)<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
