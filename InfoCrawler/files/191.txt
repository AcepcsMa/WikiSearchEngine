<p><i><b>Veep</b></i> is an American <a href="/wiki/Political_satire" title="Political satire">political satire</a> comedy television series, starring <a href="/wiki/Julia_Louis-Dreyfus" title="Julia Louis-Dreyfus">Julia Louis-Dreyfus</a>, that premiered on <a href="/wiki/HBO" title="HBO">HBO</a> on April 22, 2012.<sup id="cite_ref-PremiereDateAnnounced_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PremiereDateAnnounced-1">[1]</a></sup> The series was created by <a href="/wiki/Armando_Iannucci" title="Armando Iannucci">Armando Iannucci</a> as an adaptation of the British sitcom <i><a href="/wiki/The_Thick_of_It" title="The Thick of It">The Thick of It</a></i>. <i>Veep</i> is set in the office of <a href="/wiki/Selina_Meyer" title="Selina Meyer">Selina Meyer</a>, a fictional <a href="/wiki/Vice_President_of_the_United_States" title="Vice President of the United States">Vice President</a> (and, later, <a href="/wiki/President_of_the_United_States" title="President of the United States">President</a>) of the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>. The series follows Meyer and her team as they attempt to make their mark and leave a legacy without getting tripped up in the day-to-day political games that define the <a href="/wiki/American_government" class="mw-redirect" title="American government">American government</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p><i>Veep</i> has received critical acclaim, and has won several major awards. It has been nominated six years in a row for the <a href="/wiki/Primetime_Emmy_Award_for_Outstanding_Comedy_Series" title="Primetime Emmy Award for Outstanding Comedy Series">Primetime Emmy Award for Outstanding Comedy Series</a>, winning the award for its fourth, fifth, and sixth seasons. Its second and fourth seasons won the <a href="/wiki/Writers_Guild_of_America_Award_for_Television:_Comedy_Series" title="Writers Guild of America Award for Television: Comedy Series">Writers Guild of America Award for Television: Comedy Series</a>, and the third season won the <a href="/wiki/TCA_Award_for_Outstanding_Achievement_in_Comedy" title="TCA Award for Outstanding Achievement in Comedy">Television Critics Association Award for Outstanding Achievement in Comedy</a>. Louis-Dreyfus' performance as Selina Meyer has won her six consecutive <a href="/wiki/Primetime_Emmy_Award_for_Outstanding_Lead_Actress_in_a_Comedy_Series" title="Primetime Emmy Award for Outstanding Lead Actress in a Comedy Series">Primetime Emmy Awards</a>, two <a href="/wiki/Screen_Actors_Guild_Awards" class="mw-redirect" title="Screen Actors Guild Awards">Screen Actors Guild Awards</a>, two <a href="/wiki/Critics%27_Choice_Television_Awards" class="mw-redirect" title="Critics' Choice Television Awards">Critics' Choice Television Awards</a>, a <a href="/wiki/TCA_Awards" title="TCA Awards">Television Critics Association Award</a>, and five consecutive <a href="/wiki/Golden_Globe" class="mw-redirect" title="Golden Globe">Golden Globe</a> nominations. For his portrayal of Selina's personal aide, <a href="/wiki/Tony_Hale" title="Tony Hale">Tony Hale</a> has received five consecutive <a href="/wiki/Emmy_Award" title="Emmy Award">Emmy</a> nominations for <a href="/wiki/Primetime_Emmy_Award_for_Outstanding_Supporting_Actor_in_a_Comedy_Series" title="Primetime Emmy Award for Outstanding Supporting Actor in a Comedy Series">Outstanding Supporting Actor in a Comedy Series</a>, winning in 2013 and 2015. Supporting cast members have also received Emmy nominations, among them <a href="/wiki/Anna_Chlumsky" title="Anna Chlumsky">Anna Chlumsky</a> (five nominations), <a href="/wiki/Gary_Cole" title="Gary Cole">Gary Cole</a> (one nomination), and <a href="/wiki/Matt_Walsh_(comedian)" title="Matt Walsh (comedian)">Matt Walsh</a> (two nominations).</p>
<p>The sixth season premiered on April 16, 2017.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Its seventh and final season will premiere in 2018.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>

<li><a href="/wiki/Julia_Louis-Dreyfus" title="Julia Louis-Dreyfus">Julia Louis-Dreyfus</a> as <a href="/wiki/Selina_Meyer" title="Selina Meyer">Selina Meyer</a> (<a href="/wiki/N%C3%A9e" class="mw-redirect" title="Née">née</a> Eaton):<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> a former Maryland Senator who, in the start of the series, is the titular <a href="/wiki/Vice_President" class="mw-redirect" title="Vice President">Vice President</a>, or "Veep", who has a strained relationship with the <a href="/wiki/President_of_the_United_States" title="President of the United States">President</a>. After the President declines to run for a second term, she begins campaigning for the presidency in Season 3. At the end of Season 3, she becomes President when he resigns for personal issues. Due to a complex manipulation of constitutional law, she loses the presidential race in Season 5. She is divorced with one daughter, but remains romantically entangled with her ex-husband during the first two seasons and the sixth. Louis-Dreyfus has received widespread critical acclaim for her performance, winning a record-breaking six <a href="/wiki/Primetime_Emmy_Awards" class="mw-redirect" title="Primetime Emmy Awards">Primetime Emmy Awards</a><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> and two <a href="/wiki/Screen_Actors_Guild_Awards" class="mw-redirect" title="Screen Actors Guild Awards">Screen Actors Guild Awards</a>, and receiving five consecutive <a href="/wiki/Golden_Globe" class="mw-redirect" title="Golden Globe">Golden Globe</a> nominations.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Anna_Chlumsky" title="Anna Chlumsky">Anna Chlumsky</a> as Amy Brookheimer:<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> the <a href="/wiki/Chief_of_Staff_to_the_Vice_President_of_the_United_States" title="Chief of Staff to the Vice President of the United States">Vice President's Chief of Staff</a>. She credits herself as the Vice President's "trouble-shooter, problem-solver, issue-mediator, doubt-remover, conscience-examiner, thought-thinker and all-round everything-doer". Amy is constantly sacrificing her own reputation to save Selina's political credibility. She is known to be uptight and overly dedicated to her career, unwilling to settle down and have children, much to the dismay of her family. She has romantic history with Dan, and may still have feelings for him. Amy becomes Selina's campaign manager during her presidential run, but resigns as a result of the brief appointment of an equivocating, yet omnipresent, old friend of Selina's to the campaign team. She rejoins the Meyer team when a tie in the general election leads to a statewide recount in Nevada. Chlumsky previously portrayed a similar character, Liza Weld, in Iannucci's 2009 film, <i><a href="/wiki/In_the_Loop_(film)" class="mw-redirect" title="In the Loop (film)">In the Loop</a></i>. She has received five consecutive <a href="/wiki/Primetime_Emmy_Award" title="Primetime Emmy Award">Primetime Emmy Award</a> nominations for her performance.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Tony_Hale" title="Tony Hale">Tony Hale</a> as Gary Walsh:<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Selina's personal aide and <a href="/wiki/Body_man" title="Body man">body man</a>. A long-term associate and confidant of Selina, Gary is portrayed as incredibly loyal and devoted. Despite his menial job, Gary is actually a graduate of <a href="/wiki/Cornell_University" title="Cornell University">Cornell University</a>, having majored in <a href="/wiki/Hotel_management" class="mw-redirect" title="Hotel management">hotel management</a>. In the fourth and fifth seasons, Gary is portrayed as having issues adapting to Selina's presidency, since he can no longer be as close to her as previously, due to lack of security clearance. When Selina fails to win reelection, he remains on as her personal aide. Hale describes Gary's loyalty to Selina stemming from the idea that the character "is one of those guys who never really had an identity. He attached himself to people to find who he was."<sup id="cite_ref-halevariety_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-halevariety-10">[10]</a></sup> Hale received two <a href="/wiki/Primetime_Emmy_Awards" class="mw-redirect" title="Primetime Emmy Awards">Primetime Emmy Awards</a> for his performance on the series, with three further nominations.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Reid_Scott_(actor)" title="Reid Scott (actor)">Reid Scott</a> as Dan Egan:<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> the Deputy Director of Communications in the Vice President's Office, Dan is a highly ambitious up-and-comer in D.C. who takes pride in his contacts and networking skills. He has dated the daughters of influential politicians to get ahead in his career. He often butts heads with Amy, whom he previously dated (and it is suggested he may still have feelings for her). He is fired from the presidential staff after a mental breakdown following several crises. After briefly working unsuccessfully as a lobbyist and as a <a href="/wiki/CNN" title="CNN">CNN</a> analyst, he returns to the campaign staff, as a senior campaign official. When Selina fails to win reelection, Dan goes to work at CBS.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Timothy_Simons" title="Timothy Simons">Timothy Simons</a> as Jonah Ryan:<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> the White House liaison to Vice President Meyer's office, he constantly clashes with most members of the Veep's office, particularly Amy. It is shown that he is disliked by everyone he encounters, even foreign politicians. In the third season, he is temporarily fired from the White House for running a blog disclosing insider information, leading him to create his own news website, <i>Ryantology</i>. In season four, he works again as a liaison, this time between President Meyer and Vice President Doyle. He later works for the Meyer general election campaign, until a New Hampshire congressman dies. He is then drafted to run for that seat in order to secure Meyer's vote in the electoral college. He is elected and becomes a congressman, appointing Richard as his Chief of Staff. According to <a href="/wiki/Matt_Walsh_(comedian)" title="Matt Walsh (comedian)">Matt Walsh</a>, Jonah Ryan was originally envisioned by the show's writers as "just a fat, short, heavy smoker", but was changed to his current characterization after Simons rehearsed for the role.<sup id="cite_ref-walshdeadline_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-walshdeadline-13">[13]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Matt_Walsh_(comedian)" title="Matt Walsh (comedian)">Matt Walsh</a> as Mike McLintock:<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> the Vice President's Director of Communications. Mike has served as her Communications Director since her tenure as Senator from Maryland. His career dedication is often questionable, to the extent where he pretends to have a pet dog so he can escape from work commitments. The other characters in the show often mock his lack of ambition, suggesting that he's reached the peak of his career. In the third season, he marries a reporter named Wendy Keegan. In Season 4, Mike becomes the <a href="/wiki/White_House_Press_Secretary" title="White House Press Secretary">White House Press Secretary</a>. In Season 5, Mike and Wendy attempt to adopt a baby. They ultimately adopt a Chinese toddler, and also have twins via a surrogate. Walsh has received two <a href="/wiki/Primetime_Emmy_Award" title="Primetime Emmy Award">Primetime Emmy Award</a> nominations for his performance.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Sufe_Bradshaw" title="Sufe Bradshaw">Sufe Bradshaw</a> as Sue Wilson:<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> the Vice President's personal secretary. A direct and no-nonsense personality, Sue boasts she is the third most important person in the world, as she is the one who arranges for people to see Selina, the second most important person in the world. In a deposition, the judge states that Sue "could organize the D-Day landings and still have time for Iwo Jima". Sue becomes the Chief of Scheduling for the White House in Season 4. She remains on in that capacity when President Montez is inaugurated. Bradshaw based her character on that of a <a href="/wiki/Department_of_Motor_Vehicles" title="Department of Motor Vehicles">DMV</a> employee, elaborating that, "DMV workers are strait-laced and go by the book, and they don't have much time because there's so much to do in a day."<sup id="cite_ref-bradshawdigitaljournal_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bradshawdigitaljournal-16">[16]</a></sup> (Seasons 1–5)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Kevin_Dunn" title="Kevin Dunn">Kevin Dunn</a> as Ben Cafferty:<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> the <a href="/wiki/White_House_Chief_of_Staff" title="White House Chief of Staff">White House Chief of Staff</a>, under both the unseen former President and President Meyer. Although he is depressed and hapless, he is often very insightful and is treated with respect and even fear throughout Washington. Ben shows little regard for his co-workers or his job, and appears to love his nine-cup coffee thermos more than anything else. Selina refers to him as a "burned-out loser", but he apparently considers her a close friend and resolves to help her become President. Though he was planning on leaving the White House imminently, he agrees to remain with the administration indefinitely. When Selina fails to win reelection, he joins Congressman Ryan's staff with Kent. (Season 3–present; recurring Season 2)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Gary_Cole" title="Gary Cole">Gary Cole</a> as Kent Davison:<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> the Senior Strategist to the President, under both the unseen former President and President Meyer. He is a number-cruncher, and is often referred to as being cold and robotic. His obsession with polling statistics is shown negatively to influence the President's decision-making during several episodes in the second season. Kent is also focused on the public images of Selina and Catherine. It is implied that he and Sue are in some form of ersatz relationship. Although Selina initially dislikes him, she comes to appreciate his useful polling and statistical data, and he becomes a key part of her Presidential administration. When Selina fails to win reelection, he joins Congressman Ryan's staff with Ben. Cole has received a <a href="/wiki/Primetime_Emmy_Award" title="Primetime Emmy Award">Primetime Emmy Award</a> nomination for his performance. (Season 4–present; recurring Seasons 2–3)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Sam_Richardson_(actor)" title="Sam Richardson (actor)">Sam Richardson</a> as Richard Splett: an incompetent campaign aide who fills in for Gary during Selina's book tour, later becoming Amy's assistant on Selina's presidential campaign, then Jonah's personal assistant. In Season 5, Selina promotes Richard after discovering he has a doctorate in electoral law. As part of Richard's promotion, Jonah becomes his assistant. When Jonah is elected to congress, Richard becomes his Chief of Staff. Richard makes many allusions to his blog, splettnet.net. After losing the presidency, Selina hires him to be her Chief of Staff for the Meyer Fund. (Season 4–present; recurring Season 3)<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Recurring">Recurring</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Veep&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Recurring">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_characters">Personal characters</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Veep&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Personal characters">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Sarah_Sutherland" title="Sarah Sutherland">Sarah Sutherland</a> as Catherine Meyer: Selina's reserved, put-upon daughter. Catherine is often caught in the middle of Selina's issues, especially with her father. She tends to have highly liberal views concerning social justice. Although she tries to highlight the differences between herself and her mother, it is consistently shown that Catherine suppresses her own streak of foul-mouthed ambition. She attends film school, and attracts attention for dating a Middle Eastern man. Later, she causes issues for the Meyer campaign when she becomes engaged to a lobbyist. She ultimately breaks off her engagement to protect her mother's administration. She is seen in the fifth season to be filming a White House-based documentary, and becomes romantically involved with her mother's bodyguard and lookalike, Marjorie, alarming her mother. The two subsequently marry. She is currently pregnant with Richard's child through artificial insemination. (Season 1–present)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Andy_Buckley" title="Andy Buckley">Andy Buckley</a> as Ted Cullen: Selina's former lover. (Season 1)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Zach_Woods" title="Zach Woods">Zach Woods</a> as Ed Webster: Amy's boyfriend who is often neglected in favor of her job. Woods also appeared in <i>In the Loop</i> as a State Department aide who was a rival to Chlumsky's character. (Seasons 2–3)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Jessica_St._Clair" title="Jessica St. Clair">Jessica St. Clair</a> as Dana: Gary's over-possessive girlfriend. She wants to move abroad and begin a cheese business with Gary, who declines at Selina's request. She has not been seen or mentioned since season 3. (Seasons 2–3)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/David_Pasquesi" title="David Pasquesi">David Pasquesi</a> as Andrew Meyer: Selina's ex-husband and occasional lover. He is disliked by Selina's staff, primarily for being one of her weaknesses. He is shown to have frequently cheated on Selina over the course of their on-off relationship. (Seasons 2–3, 5–present)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Kathy_Najimy" title="Kathy Najimy">Kathy Najimy</a> as Wendy Keegan: a reporter and Mike's wife. In the fifth season, she attempts to adopt a baby with Mike. (Season 3–present)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/John_Slattery" title="John Slattery">John Slattery</a> as Charlie Baird Jr.: A Wall Street executive with whom Selina becomes romantically entangled. After Leon West breaks the story that they have slept together, the two engage in a somewhat-forced courtship. He develops a close bond with Gary. He is described as being extremely affluent, and is a major benefactor to many museums. After he had slept with Selina but prior to their dating, her general election opponent Senator O'Brien had offered him the position of Secretary of the Treasury should he win. He later accepts the same position from President Montez upon her surprise win. (Season 5)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Clea_DuVall" title="Clea DuVall">Clea DuVall</a> as Marjorie Palmiotti: Selina's bodyguard and lookalike. She later resigns when she begins a relationship with Catherine. In the Season 6 premiere, it is revealed that she has married Catherine in the year since the House vote. She now serves as Director of the Meyer Fund. (Season 5–present)</li>
</ul>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Politicians">Politicians</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Veep&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Politicians">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/List_of_politicians_on_Veep" title="List of politicians on Veep">List of politicians on Veep</a></div>
<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="House_of_Representatives">House of Representatives</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Veep&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: House of Representatives">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Dan_Bakkedahl" title="Dan Bakkedahl">Dan Bakkedahl</a> as Congressman Roger Furlong: an ambitious Ohio Congressman and ranking member of a congressional oversight committee. Ill-mannered and foul-mouthed, he constantly hounds the Vice President's office and threatens investigations, even after he loses his campaign to be <a href="/wiki/Governor_of_Ohio" class="mw-redirect" title="Governor of Ohio">Governor of Ohio</a>. Despite this, however, Furlong supports Selina's presidential campaign by helping her prepare for a primary debate and doing post-debate "spin" on her behalf. In Season 6, he becomes the House Minority Leader. (Season 1–present)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Nelson_Franklin" title="Nelson Franklin">Nelson Franklin</a> as Will: Congressman Furlong's aide. He is often subjected to vulgar verbal abuse from Furlong. The Congressman often makes him say demeaning things for his own amusement. In Season 5, it is revealed that he is married, and that Furlong brings the couple to dinner for their anniversary. (Season 1–present)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/David_Rasche" title="David Rasche">David Rasche</a> as Speaker Jim Marwood: Marwood serves as the <a href="/wiki/Speaker_of_the_United_States_House_of_Representatives" title="Speaker of the United States House of Representatives">Speaker of the House of Representatives</a>, who ends up in league with Laura Montez and delivers her the presidency by refusing to hold another house vote. (Seasons 2; 4–5). Rasche also appeared in <i>In the Loop</i> as an American official.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Paul_Fitzgerald_(actor)" title="Paul Fitzgerald (actor)">Paul Fitzgerald</a> as Congressman Owen Pierce: a socially awkward <a href="/wiki/Member_of_Congress" title="Member of Congress">congressman</a> and previously one of Selina's rivals for the presidential nomination. He is described as completely incompetent. He holds great respect for President Meyer, and helps her swing a vote against his own wishes to protect her. (Season 3–present)</li>
</ul>
<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="Senate">Senate</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Veep&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Senate">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Phil_Reeves" title="Phil Reeves">Phil Reeves</a> as Senator Andrew Doyle: Vice President under President Meyer, originally a senator and chairman of the <a href="/wiki/Senate_Intelligence_Committee" class="mw-redirect" title="Senate Intelligence Committee">Senate Intelligence Committee</a>. While serving as her Vice President, he finds himself sidelined in a similar way as she had been by the former President. When Selina becomes her party's nominee, he declines to run for a full term as Vice President, and steps off the ticket. He's later reinstated as Vice President after Selina has difficulties with Tom and continues to be belittled and ignored. He later gets revenge when he casts a deciding vote in the senate hearing to determine the presidency. He serves as Secretary of State under President Montez. (Season 1–present)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Patton_Oswalt" title="Patton Oswalt">Patton Oswalt</a> as Teddy Sykes: the <a href="/wiki/Chief_of_Staff_to_the_Vice_President_of_the_United_States" title="Chief of Staff to the Vice President of the United States">Chief of Staff</a> to Vice President Doyle. (Season 4–present)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Brad_Leland" title="Brad Leland">Brad Leland</a> as Senator Bill O'Brien: an Arizona senator who is Selina's opponent in her presidential bid. (Seasons 1–2, 4–present)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Hugh_Laurie" title="Hugh Laurie">Hugh Laurie</a> as Senator Tom James: a charming <a href="/wiki/United_States_Senate" title="United States Senate">senator</a> and Selina's new running mate after Doyle leaves the ticket. He is extremely popular with his colleagues and with voters. Selina was reluctant to pick him as a running mate out of fear that he would remember a time they nearly slept together. He frustrates Meyer at his desire to be involved with the economy, going so far as to request to serve simultaneously as Vice President and Secretary of the Treasury. In the general election, loophole rules in the Constitution dictate that in certain circumstances, James could become the Acting President (and subsequently President), further angering Selina. He offers that, should this occur, he would like her to be his Vice President. She later appoints him as her economy czar. Before the fourth season, <a href="/wiki/Armando_Iannucci" title="Armando Iannucci">Armando Iannucci</a> met with Laurie in the UK after learning that he was a fan of <i>Veep</i>. The two began developing the character Tom James alongside the show's writers (also based in the UK). Iannucci describes Tom James as a "normal, but ambitious operator ... this is someone who seems to outdo [Selina Meyers] in his ability."<sup id="cite_ref-iannucciinterview_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-iannucciinterview-20">[20]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-iannucciew_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-iannucciew-21">[21]</a></sup> (Seasons 4–6)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Andrea_Savage" title="Andrea Savage">Andrea Savage</a> as President Laura Montez: Originally a Senator from New Mexico, she becomes Senator Bill O'Brien's running mate in the Presidential election. When there is a tie in the electoral college, a deadlock in the House of Representatives, and another tie in the Senate, Doyle ultimately votes for her, and she becomes President. President Montez is inaugurated in the season 5 finale, "Inauguration". (Season 5–present)</li>
</ul>
<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_politicians">Other politicians</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Veep&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Other politicians">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Randall_Park" title="Randall Park">Randall Park</a> as <a href="/wiki/Governor_of_Minnesota" title="Governor of Minnesota">Minnesota Governor</a> Danny Chung: a young veteran who is not shy about exploiting his military record for political gain. A member of the President's party, he covets the presidency himself and is seen as Selina's chief rival for the nomination after the President leaves office. A running gag is that Chung never appears on television without mentioning his military record. Selina offers him the position of running mate, but he declines. He is subsequently revealed to participate regularly in a Gilbert and Sullivan group along with Richard. (Season 1–present)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Isiah_Whitlock,_Jr." class="mw-redirect" title="Isiah Whitlock, Jr.">Isiah Whitlock, Jr.</a> as General George Maddox: the former <a href="/wiki/United_States_Secretary_of_Defense" title="United States Secretary of Defense">Secretary of Defense</a> and one of Selina's rivals for the presidential nomination. He appears to bear an unusually high degree of personal animosity towards Selina. He is forced to suspend his campaign after being unable to compete on the debate stage. When she secures her party nomination, she offers him the position of running mate, but rescinds the offer. (Season 2–present)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Diedrich_Bader" title="Diedrich Bader">Diedrich Bader</a> as Bill Ericsson: a high-profile campaign official, formerly the campaign manager for Joe Thornhill. He later abandons Thornhill, and Selina appoints him her new Director of Communications. When a scandal breaks that the Meyer campaign used stolen confidential medical data to send flyers appealing to bereaved parents of deceased children, the core characters band together to scapegoat him in their place, resulting in his imprisonment. His sentence is later overturned on appeal and he turns against Selina, claiming he is "consumed" by desire to destroy her. (Season 3–present)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Glenn_Wrage" title="Glenn Wrage">Glenn Wrage</a> as Joe Thornhill: a former <a href="/wiki/Major_League_Baseball" title="Major League Baseball">Major League Baseball</a> coach and one of Selina's rivals for the presidential nomination. He constantly uses sports analogies to describe politics, something which greatly annoys Selina and her staff. Despite a good early start in the primaries, he later loses momentum in the race. (Season 3)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Martin_Mull" title="Martin Mull">Martin Mull</a> as Bob Bradley: Selina's folksy out-of-touch political advisor with a long history in politics. His nickname is "The Eagle". (Season 5)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Peter_MacNicol" title="Peter MacNicol">Peter MacNicol</a> as Jeff Kane: An influential political power broker in New Hampshire, and Jonah Ryan's uncle. (Season 5-6)</li>
</ul>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_characters">Other characters</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Veep&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Other characters">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Peter_Grosz" title="Peter Grosz">Peter Grosz</a> as Sidney Purcell: an oil lobbyist. Purcell attempts to gain power through the Meyer Vice Presidency via her environmental regulation committee. When Dan is fired, he works as a lobbyist for Purcell, who in turn ends up firing him as well. (Seasons 1–2; 4–present)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Brian_Huskey" title="Brian Huskey">Brian Huskey</a> as Leon West: a veteran political reporter who frequently antagonizes Mike at briefings. He is later taken hostage in <a href="/wiki/Iran" title="Iran">Iran</a>, and Selina must retrieve him on a goodwill mission. (Season 1–present)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Sally_Phillips" title="Sally Phillips">Sally Phillips</a> as Minna Häkkinen: the former <a href="/wiki/Prime_Minister_of_Finland" title="Prime Minister of Finland">Prime Minister of Finland</a>. She first meets Selina on a state visit in the wake of an anti-Europe gaffe on Selina's part. She later holds a position with the <a href="/wiki/International_Monetary_Fund" title="International Monetary Fund">International Monetary Fund</a>, where she impresses enough world leaders that she now seems to work as a back-channel diplomat. She is known for being blunt about personal issues and clueless about the vitriol of American politics. (Season 2–present)</li>
<li>William L. Thomas as Martin Collins: a Secret Service agent reassigned for laughing in Selina's presence. (Seasons 1–3)</li>
<li>Jessie Ennis as Lee Patterson: a competent and straightforward staffer for Selina, whose name is constantly misremembered by her and the rest of the White House staff. Lee reveals the Meyer campaign used stolen confidential medical data to appeal to bereaved parents, and testifies against Selina, Gary, Bill, Amy, Dan, Sue, Kent, and Ben. (Season 4)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Margaret_Colin" title="Margaret Colin">Margaret Colin</a> as Jane McCabe: anchor of <i><a href="/wiki/CBS_This_Morning" title="CBS This Morning">CBS This Morning</a></i>. (Season 6)</li>
</ul>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Thick_of_It"><i>The Thick of It</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Veep&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: The Thick of It">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="BBC_series">BBC series</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Veep&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: BBC series">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Before creating <i>Veep</i>, British satirist <a href="/wiki/Armando_Iannucci" title="Armando Iannucci">Armando Iannucci</a> created a <a href="/wiki/BBC" title="BBC">BBC</a> sitcom, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Thick_of_It" title="The Thick of It">The Thick of It</a></i>, set in a fictional department of the <a href="/wiki/Government_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Government of the United Kingdom">British government</a>. <i>The Thick of It</i> was first broadcast in 2005 and won a number of awards. Iannucci directed a <a href="/wiki/Spin-off_(media)" title="Spin-off (media)">spin-off</a> film, <i><a href="/wiki/In_the_Loop_(film)" class="mw-redirect" title="In the Loop (film)">In the Loop</a></i>, which was released in 2009 and nominated for the <a href="/wiki/Academy_Award_for_Best_Adapted_Screenplay" title="Academy Award for Best Adapted Screenplay">Academy Award for Best Adapted Screenplay</a>.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="ABC_pilot">ABC pilot</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Veep&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: ABC pilot">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>A pilot for an American version of <i>The Thick of It</i> was produced as a candidate for the 2007–08 season on <a href="/wiki/American_Broadcasting_Company" title="American Broadcasting Company">ABC</a>. The ABC pilot, also titled <i>The Thick of It</i>, was developed for American audiences by writers <a href="/wiki/Mitch_Hurwitz" class="mw-redirect" title="Mitch Hurwitz">Mitch Hurwitz</a> and <a href="/wiki/Richard_Day_(writer)" title="Richard Day (writer)">Richard Day</a> and followed a low-level member of the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Congress" title="United States Congress">United States Congress</a> and his staff. Iannucci had a production credit on the show, but he was not otherwise involved. The pilot was produced by <a href="/wiki/Sony_Pictures_Television" title="Sony Pictures Television">Sony Pictures Television</a> and <a href="/wiki/BBC_Worldwide" title="BBC Worldwide">BBC Worldwide</a> and directed by <a href="/wiki/Christopher_Guest" title="Christopher Guest">Christopher Guest</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Zap2it0226_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Zap2it0226-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the pilot, <a href="/wiki/John_Michael_Higgins" title="John Michael Higgins">John Michael Higgins</a> played Albert Alger, a newly elected Congressman, and <a href="/wiki/Oliver_Platt" title="Oliver Platt">Oliver Platt</a> played committee chairman Malcolm Tucker.<sup id="cite_ref-Zap2it0309_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Zap2it0309-23">[23]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Rhea_Seehorn" title="Rhea Seehorn">Rhea Seehorn</a> portrayed Ollie Tadzio, a young and ambitious speech writer, and <a href="/wiki/Michael_McKean" title="Michael McKean">Michael McKean</a> played Glen Glahm, "a former campaign operative who's now the Chief of Staff" for the congressman.<sup id="cite_ref-Zap2it0315_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Zap2it0315-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<p>ABC did not pick up the show for its fall 2007 schedule.<sup id="cite_ref-SFChron070521_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SFChron070521-25">[25]</a></sup> Iannucci distanced himself from the pilot, stating, "It was terrible...they took the idea and chucked out all the style. It was all conventionally shot and there was no improvisation or swearing. It didn't get picked up, thank God."<sup id="cite_ref-HBO_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HBO-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="HBO_development_of_Veep">HBO development of <i>Veep</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Veep&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: HBO development of Veep">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>After <i>The Thick of It</i> was dropped by ABC, several networks including <a href="/wiki/HBO" title="HBO">HBO</a>, <a href="/wiki/Showtime_(TV_network)" title="Showtime (TV network)">Showtime</a> and <a href="/wiki/NBC" title="NBC">NBC</a> expressed interest in adapting the show.<sup id="cite_ref-Yahoo070604_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Yahoo070604-27">[27]</a></sup> Iannucci re-entered talks with HBO (his initial preference) about adapting the series, with the result that a new pilot episode for a series situated in the office of the <a href="/wiki/Vice_President_of_the_United_States" title="Vice President of the United States">Vice President of the United States</a> called <i>Veep</i> (a nickname derived from the position's initials "VP") was commissioned in late 2009.<sup id="cite_ref-HBO_26-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HBO-26">[26]</a></sup> Iannucci was given much more creative control over the production,<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> and co-wrote the pilot with British comedy writer <a href="/wiki/Simon_Blackwell" title="Simon Blackwell">Simon Blackwell</a>, who also contributed to the British series <i><a href="/wiki/The_Thick_of_It" title="The Thick of It">The Thick of It</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-OrderedToSeries_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-OrderedToSeries-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<p>In April 2011, HBO announced that it had ordered <i>Veep</i> as a series,<sup id="cite_ref-OrderedToSeries_29-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-OrderedToSeries-29">[29]</a></sup> and later announced in January 2012 that the series would premiere on April 22, 2012.<sup id="cite_ref-PremiereDateAnnounced_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PremiereDateAnnounced-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Louis-Dreyfus described <i>Veep'</i>s intent not to have the President on-screen, or to reveal the political party of the characters.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> Meyer's party affiliation is somewhat implied in "Election Night" to be Democratic, since CNN represents states that she wins as blue.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Production">Production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Veep&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Directors for Season 1 included <a href="/wiki/Armando_Iannucci" title="Armando Iannucci">Armando Iannucci</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tristram_Shapeero" title="Tristram Shapeero">Tristram Shapeero</a> and <a href="/wiki/Chris_Morris_(satirist)" title="Chris Morris (satirist)">Chris Morris</a>. <i>Veep</i> is executive produced by Iannucci, Christopher Godsick and <a href="/wiki/Frank_Rich" title="Frank Rich">Frank Rich</a>. Co-executive producers are <a href="/wiki/Simon_Blackwell" title="Simon Blackwell">Simon Blackwell</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tony_Roche_(writer)" title="Tony Roche (writer)">Tony Roche</a>, with <a href="/wiki/Julia_Louis-Dreyfus" title="Julia Louis-Dreyfus">Julia Louis-Dreyfus</a> and Stephanie Laing as producers. The series' first four seasons featured an entirely British writing staff, consisting of Iannucci, Blackwell, Roche, <a href="/wiki/Sean_Gray" title="Sean Gray">Sean Gray</a>, <a href="/wiki/Will_Smith_(comedian)" title="Will Smith (comedian)">Will Smith</a>, <a href="/wiki/Roger_Drew" title="Roger Drew">Roger Drew</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ian_Martin_(writer)" title="Ian Martin (writer)">Ian Martin</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Jesse_Armstrong" title="Jesse Armstrong">Jesse Armstrong</a>, among others,<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> all of whom had previously worked with Iannucci on <i>The Thick of It</i>.</p>
<p>Series creator Armando Iannucci departed as <a href="/wiki/Showrunner" title="Showrunner">showrunner</a> following the fourth season's end of production. Iannucci stated that his continuing busy schedule, as well as the challenge of maintaining his family life while switching between <a href="/wiki/Baltimore" title="Baltimore">Baltimore</a> and London, would not allow him to "[dedicate] one hundred percent" as head of the show, and he had chosen to "fire" himself as a result. <a href="/wiki/David_Mandel" title="David Mandel">David Mandel</a> took over as showrunner for future episodes, becoming <i>Veep'</i>s first American writer. Mandel retained a small number of Ianucci's writing staff, as well as <a href="/wiki/Chris_Addison" title="Chris Addison">Chris Addison</a> as director and supervising producer, whilst also bringing in his own staff, and American writers.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-ibtveep_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ibtveep-35">[35]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-etiannucci_36-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-etiannucci-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Filming">Filming</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Veep&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Filming">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Calvert_Street_In_Charles_Village_Baltimore.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2f/Calvert_Street_In_Charles_Village_Baltimore.jpg/220px-Calvert_Street_In_Charles_Village_Baltimore.jpg" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2f/Calvert_Street_In_Charles_Village_Baltimore.jpg/330px-Calvert_Street_In_Charles_Village_Baltimore.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2f/Calvert_Street_In_Charles_Village_Baltimore.jpg/440px-Calvert_Street_In_Charles_Village_Baltimore.jpg 2x" data-file-width="700" data-file-height="525" /></a>

<a href="/wiki/Charles_Village,_Baltimore" title="Charles Village, Baltimore">Charles Village, Baltimore</a>, one of the areas where <i>Veep</i> filmed for its first season production<sup id="cite_ref-patchcharles_37-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-patchcharles-37">[37]</a></sup></div>

<p>The pilot episode was filmed in February 2011 in <a href="/wiki/Maryland" title="Maryland">Maryland</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> and filming for the series began in October 2011 in <a href="/wiki/Baltimore" title="Baltimore">Baltimore</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> after several months of rehearsal designed to get the actors comfortable improvising with one another.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> For its first season, <i>Veep</i> reportedly hired 978 local Maryland residents and generated $40 million for the state, according to the Maryland Film Office.<sup id="cite_ref-marylandfilmoffice_41-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-marylandfilmoffice-41">[41]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-baltimoremarylandfilmoffice_42-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-baltimoremarylandfilmoffice-42">[42]</a></sup> Season 2 production began shooting in November 2012, continuing to film in <a href="/wiki/Baltimore" title="Baltimore">Baltimore</a> and other areas of <a href="/wiki/Maryland" title="Maryland">Maryland</a>. <i>Veep</i> primarily filmed on a <a href="/wiki/Sound_stage" title="Sound stage">sound stage</a> constructed from a <a href="/wiki/Columbia,_Maryland" title="Columbia, Maryland">Columbia, Maryland</a> industrial warehouse, where replicas of places such as the <a href="/wiki/Eisenhower_Executive_Office_Building" title="Eisenhower Executive Office Building">Eisenhower Executive Office Building</a> and <a href="/wiki/West_Wing" title="West Wing">West Wing</a> are also built.<sup id="cite_ref-louisdreyfusvulture_43-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-louisdreyfusvulture-43">[43]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-usatodayveep_44-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-usatodayveep-44">[44]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-wapostveep2013_45-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wapostveep2013-45">[45]</a></sup> The show continued filming in Maryland for its third and fourth seasons, as a bill was approved by <a href="/wiki/Maryland_House_of_Delegates" title="Maryland House of Delegates">state lawmakers</a> in April 2013 that increased tax credits for film and TV productions in the state.<sup id="cite_ref-baltimoremarylandfilmoffice_42-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-baltimoremarylandfilmoffice-42">[42]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-marylandfilmbill2013_46-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-marylandfilmbill2013-46">[46]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-hborenewseasonfour_47-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hborenewseasonfour-47">[47]</a></sup> Later filming locations included <a href="/wiki/Annapolis" class="mw-redirect" title="Annapolis">Annapolis</a> and the Physical Sciences Complex in the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Maryland" class="mw-redirect" title="University of Maryland">University of Maryland</a>, <a href="/wiki/College_Park,_Maryland" title="College Park, Maryland">College Park</a> campus.<sup id="cite_ref-wmarcastcall_48-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wmarcastcall-48">[48]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-washingtonianveep_49-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-washingtonianveep-49">[49]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-diamondbackveep_50-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-diamondbackveep-50">[50]</a></sup></p>
<p>Principal photography moved from Baltimore to <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles" title="Los Angeles">Los Angeles</a> in the show's fifth season after being one of a few series to be awarded <a href="/wiki/Movie_production_incentives_in_the_United_States#State-by-state" title="Movie production incentives in the United States">tax incentives</a> from the <a href="/wiki/California_Film_Commission" title="California Film Commission">California Film Commission</a>, as part of an expanded $330 million California Film Tax Credit program signed by <a href="/wiki/Governor_of_California" title="Governor of California">Governor</a> <a href="/wiki/Jerry_Brown" title="Jerry Brown">Jerry Brown</a> in 2014.<sup id="cite_ref-veeplawapost_51-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-veeplawapost-51">[51]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-veepcalivariety_52-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-veepcalivariety-52">[52]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-deadlinecalitax_53-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-deadlinecalitax-53">[53]</a></sup> Filming took place for part of the show's fifth season in <a href="/wiki/Washington,_D.C." title="Washington, D.C.">Washington, D.C.</a>, from February 25, 2016, to March 3, 2016. As a result of <a href="/wiki/HBO" title="HBO">HBO</a>'s Community Impact program, a select number of local D.C. residents also worked on the production during the eight-day film shoot in the area.<sup id="cite_ref-filmingdc_54-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-filmingdc-54">[54]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-hbocommunityprogram_55-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hbocommunityprogram-55">[55]</a></sup> Areas in D.C. where production was reportedly found filming include the <a href="/wiki/Superior_Court_of_the_District_of_Columbia" title="Superior Court of the District of Columbia">Superior Court</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Spring_Valley,_Washington,_D.C." title="Spring Valley, Washington, D.C.">Spring Valley neighborhood</a> (where Julia Louis-Dreyfus once lived), and <a href="/wiki/Dupont_Circle" title="Dupont Circle">Dupont Circle</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Kramerbooks_%26_Afterwords" title="Kramerbooks &amp; Afterwords">Kramerbooks</a> <a href="/wiki/Independent_bookstore" title="Independent bookstore">independent bookstore</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-veepfilmingdc_56-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-veepfilmingdc-56">[56]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-veepkramerbooks_57-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-veepkramerbooks-57">[57]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Season_1">Season 1</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Veep&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Season 1">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The first season received generally positive reviews from television critics. Review aggregator site <a href="/wiki/Metacritic" title="Metacritic">Metacritic</a> gave the season a score of 72 out of 100 based on reviews from 30 critics.<sup id="cite_ref-MetacriticS1_58-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MetacriticS1-58">[58]</a></sup> The review aggregator website <a href="/wiki/Rotten_Tomatoes" title="Rotten Tomatoes">Rotten Tomatoes</a> reported a 71% approval rating with an average rating of 6.94 out of 10 based on 31 reviews. The site's consensus reads, "The jokes are funny and Julia Louis-Dreyfus is great in the lead, but <i>Veep</i> is still working to find its voice."<sup id="cite_ref-64" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup> Hank Stuever of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Washington_Post" title="The Washington Post">The Washington Post</a></i> praised the series, writing, "Thanks to Louis-Dreyfus, and the show's remarkable knack for dialogue and timing, Veep is instantly engaging and outrageously fun."<sup id="cite_ref-65" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup> Rob Brunner of <i><a href="/wiki/Entertainment_Weekly" title="Entertainment Weekly">Entertainment Weekly</a></i> gave the season a positive review: "Charmingly goofy as ever, Louis-Dreyfus isn't quite believable as a Vice President&#160;– even a sitcom VP whose lack of gravitas is the show's central joke. But she's still a joy to watch, especially when she shows off that famous gift for physical comedy."<sup id="cite_ref-66" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup> Maureen Ryan of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Huffington_Post" class="mw-redirect" title="The Huffington Post">The Huffington Post</a></i> gave the show a lukewarm review, writing, "Despite the clear talents of the assembled cast, Veep merely reinforces what most people already think and revisits territory many other politically-oriented movies and TV shows have thoroughly covered."<sup id="cite_ref-67" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup> Brian Lowry of <i><a href="/wiki/Variety_(magazine)" title="Variety (magazine)">Variety</a></i> gave the show a negative review and said a "show about an always-second office becomes second-tier TV."<sup id="cite_ref-68" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Season_2">Season 2</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Veep&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Season 2">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The second season received acclaim from critics. It averaged a Metacritic score of 75 out of 100 based on reviews from 10 critics.<sup id="cite_ref-MetacriticS2_59-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MetacriticS2-59">[59]</a></sup> On Rotten Tomatoes, it received an 88% approval rating with an average score of 8.38 out of 10 based on 16 reviews. The site's consensus reads, "In <i>Veep</i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span>s second season, the satire is sharper, the insights are deeper, the tone is more consistent, and the result is a comedy of unexpected heft."<sup id="cite_ref-69" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-69">[69]</a></sup> David Hiltbrand of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Philadelphia_Inquirer" title="The Philadelphia Inquirer">The Philadelphia Inquirer</a></i> praised the series saying, "HBO's Veep is the sharpest Beltway satire the medium has ever seen, mostly because it focuses not on the power wielded by politicians, but on their desperate venality".<sup id="cite_ref-70" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-70">[70]</a></sup> Bruce Miller of <i><a href="/wiki/Sioux_City_Journal" title="Sioux City Journal">Sioux City Journal</a></i> also praised the show, writing: "The show is smart—smarter than most on network television—and it has life."<sup id="cite_ref-71" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-71">[71]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Season_3">Season 3</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Veep&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Season 3">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The third season received acclaim from critics. It received a Metacritic score of 86 out of 100 based on 10 reviews.<sup id="cite_ref-MetacriticS3_60-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MetacriticS3-60">[60]</a></sup> It scored a 100% approval rating on Rotten Tomatoes, with an average rating of 8.86 out of 10 based on 21 reviews. The site's consensus reads, "<i>Veep</i> continues its winning streak with a mix of smart comedy, bright performances and a refreshing approach to D.C. politics."<sup id="cite_ref-72" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-72">[72]</a></sup> Matt Roush of <i><a href="/wiki/TV_Guide" title="TV Guide">TV Guide</a></i> praised the show, and in a joint review of <i>Veep</i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Silicon_Valley_(TV_series)" title="Silicon Valley (TV series)">Silicon Valley</a></i> wrote: "[<i>Silicon Valley</i> is] paired with the third season of the savagely hilarious <i>Veep</i>; this combo promises to be HBO's most robust and certainly most entertaining comedy hour in years."<sup id="cite_ref-73" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-73">[73]</a></sup> Brandon Nowalk of <i><a href="/wiki/The_A.V._Club" title="The A.V. Club">The A.V. Club</a></i> wrote the show "has become the clearest heir to <i><a href="/wiki/30_Rock" title="30 Rock">30 Rock</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Arrested_Development_(TV_series)" title="Arrested Development (TV series)">Arrested Development</a></i>, and specific bits throughout the season recall both series."<sup id="cite_ref-74" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-74">[74]</a></sup> Tim Molloy of <i><a href="/wiki/TheWrap" title="TheWrap">TheWrap</a></i> praised the cast saying, "The show works because all of its actors seem so human, so likable, despite the words coming from their mouths."<sup id="cite_ref-75" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-75">[75]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Season_4">Season 4</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Veep&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Season 4">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The fourth season received acclaim from critics. It received a Metacritic score of 90 out of 100 based on 11 reviews.<sup id="cite_ref-MetacriticS4_61-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MetacriticS4-61">[61]</a></sup> As with the previous season, <i>Veep</i> scored a 100% rating on Rotten Tomatoes, based on 18 reviews, with an average rating of 8.94 out of 10. The site's consensus reads, "Veep shows no signs of slowing down in its fourth season, thanks to sharp, funny, rapid-fire dialogue between POTUS and her hilariously incompetent staff."<sup id="cite_ref-76" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-76">[76]</a></sup> Tim Goodman of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Hollywood_Reporter" title="The Hollywood Reporter">The Hollywood Reporter</a></i> wrote, "<i>Veep</i> enters its fourth season, firmly established as one of television's best comedies, and then immediately does what seems impossible—it delivers its most thoroughly assured, hilarious and brilliantly written and acted episodes."<sup id="cite_ref-77" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-77">[77]</a></sup> Ben Travers of <i><a href="/wiki/Indiewire" class="mw-redirect" title="Indiewire">Indiewire</a></i> wrote, "<i>Veep</i> is incomparable in comedy" and that "the HBO comedy has crafted a style so unique the series itself is entirely its own beast."<sup id="cite_ref-78" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-78">[78]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Season_5">Season 5</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Veep&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Season 5">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The fifth season received acclaim from critics. It received a Metacritic score of 88 out of 100 based on 18 reviews.<sup id="cite_ref-MetacriticS5_62-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MetacriticS5-62">[62]</a></sup> The season scored a 93% rating on Rotten Tomatoes, based on 27 reviews, with an average rating of 8.66 out of 10. The site's consensus reads, "Thanks to the spot-on comedic prowess of Julia Louis-Dreyfus and company <i>Veep</i> is back with as many laughs and expletive-filled absurdities as ever."<sup id="cite_ref-79" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-79">[79]</a></sup> Tim Goodman of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Hollywood_Reporter" title="The Hollywood Reporter">The Hollywood Reporter</a></i> wrote that "<i>Veep</i> doesn't just feel like it's firing on all cylinders, it feels invigorated and out to prove something",<sup id="cite_ref-80" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-80">[80]</a></sup> while Kevin Sullivan of <i><a href="/wiki/Entertainment_Weekly" title="Entertainment Weekly">Entertainment Weekly</a></i> wrote that "in the switch to new showrunner David Mandel, the state of <i>Veep</i> is strong".<sup id="cite_ref-81" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-81">[81]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Season_6">Season 6</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Veep&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Season 6">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The sixth season has received critical acclaim. On Metacritic, it has a score of 88 out of 100 based on 15 reviews, indicating "universal acclaim".<sup id="cite_ref-MetacriticS6_63-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MetacriticS6-63">[63]</a></sup> It has a 96% rating on Rotten Tomatoes based on 27 reviews with an average score of 8.35 out of 10. The site's critical consensus reads, "A move from the White House hasn't dulled <i>Veep</i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span>s razor-sharp satirical edge, thanks to Julia Louis-Dreyfus and her castmates' deft comic chemistry."<sup id="cite_ref-82" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-82">[82]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Awards_and_honors">Awards and honors</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Veep&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Awards and honors">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/List_of_awards_and_nominations_received_by_Veep" title="List of awards and nominations received by Veep">List of awards and nominations received by Veep</a></div>
<p>Through its first six seasons, <i>Veep</i> has received critical acclaim and won several major awards, including twelve <a href="/wiki/Primetime_Emmy_Awards" class="mw-redirect" title="Primetime Emmy Awards">Primetime Emmy Awards</a>, two <a href="/wiki/Critics%27_Choice_Television_Awards" class="mw-redirect" title="Critics' Choice Television Awards">Critics' Choice Television Awards</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Peabody_Award" title="Peabody Award">Peabody Award</a>, two <a href="/wiki/Screen_Actors_Guild_Awards" class="mw-redirect" title="Screen Actors Guild Awards">Screen Actors Guild Awards</a>, two <a href="/wiki/Television_Critics_Association_Awards" class="mw-redirect" title="Television Critics Association Awards">Television Critics Association Awards</a> and two <a href="/wiki/Writers_Guild_of_America_Awards" class="mw-redirect" title="Writers Guild of America Awards">Writers Guild of America Awards</a>.</p>
