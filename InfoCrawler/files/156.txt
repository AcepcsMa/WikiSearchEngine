<dt><b>September 1</b></dt>

<div id="mp-tfa-img" style="float: left; margin: 0.5em 0.9em 0.4em 0em;">
<div class="thumbinner mp-thumb" style="background: transparent; border: none; padding: 0; max-width: 152px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Sioux_Narrows_ON.JPG" class="image" title="King's Highway 71 at Sioux  Narrows"><img alt="King's Highway 71 at Sioux  Narrows" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/87/Sioux_Narrows_ON.JPG/150px-Sioux_Narrows_ON.JPG" width="150" height="100" class="thumbborder" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/87/Sioux_Narrows_ON.JPG/225px-Sioux_Narrows_ON.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/87/Sioux_Narrows_ON.JPG/300px-Sioux_Narrows_ON.JPG 2x" data-file-width="2000" data-file-height="1333" /></a></div>
</div>
<p><b><a href="/wiki/Ontario_Highway_71" title="Ontario Highway 71">King's Highway 71</a></b> is a <a href="/wiki/Highways_in_Ontario" title="Highways in Ontario">provincially maintained highway</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canadian</a> province of <a href="/wiki/Ontario" title="Ontario">Ontario</a>. Part of the <a href="/wiki/Trans-Canada_Highway" title="Trans-Canada Highway">Trans-Canada Highway</a>, the 194-kilometre (121&#160;mi) route travels west from the <a href="/wiki/Fort_Frances-International_Falls_International_Bridge" class="mw-redirect" title="Fort Frances-International Falls International Bridge">Fort Frances-International Falls International Bridge</a> in <a href="/wiki/Fort_Frances" title="Fort Frances">Fort Frances</a>, <a href="/wiki/Concurrency_(road)" title="Concurrency (road)">concurrently</a> with <a href="/wiki/Ontario_Highway_11" title="Ontario Highway 11">Highway&#160;11</a>, for 40 kilometres (25&#160;mi). At <a href="/wiki/Chapple,_Ontario" title="Chapple, Ontario">Chapple</a>, Highway&#160;71 branches from Highway&#160;11, travelling 154 kilometres (96&#160;mi) north to a junction with <a href="/wiki/Ontario_Highway_17" title="Ontario Highway 17">Highway&#160;17</a> just east of <a href="/wiki/Kenora" title="Kenora">Kenora</a>. The concurrent portion of the highway follows the Cloverleaf Trail, which was completed by the end of the 1880s and improved over the next several decades. The portion between Highway&#160;11 and Highway&#160;17 follows the Heenan Highway, which connects the Rainy River region with Kenora and the remainder of Ontario's road network; before its opening the area was accessible only via the United States. Both highways were incorporated into the provincial highway system in 1937 following the merger of the <a href="/wiki/Department_of_Highways" class="mw-redirect" title="Department of Highways">Department of Highways</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Department_of_Northern_Development" class="mw-redirect" title="Department of Northern Development">Department of Northern Development</a>. <dt><b>September 2</b></dt>

<div id="mp-tfa-img" style="float: left; margin: 0.5em 0.9em 0.4em 0em;">
<div class="thumbinner mp-thumb" style="background: transparent; border: none; padding: 0; max-width: 164px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Em_-_Hydrodamalis_gigas_model_(mirrored).jpg" class="image" title="Model of Steller's sea cow"><img alt="Model of Steller's sea cow" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/55/Em_-_Hydrodamalis_gigas_model_%28mirrored%29.jpg/162px-Em_-_Hydrodamalis_gigas_model_%28mirrored%29.jpg" width="162" height="100" class="thumbborder" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/55/Em_-_Hydrodamalis_gigas_model_%28mirrored%29.jpg/243px-Em_-_Hydrodamalis_gigas_model_%28mirrored%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/55/Em_-_Hydrodamalis_gigas_model_%28mirrored%29.jpg/324px-Em_-_Hydrodamalis_gigas_model_%28mirrored%29.jpg 2x" data-file-width="752" data-file-height="464" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption" style="padding: 0.25em 0; word-wrap: break-word;">Model of Steller's sea cow</div>

<p><b><a href="/wiki/Steller%27s_sea_cow" title="Steller's sea cow">Steller's sea cow</a></b> (<i>Hydrodamalis gigas</i>) was a <a href="/wiki/Sirenia" title="Sirenia">sirenian</a> that lived until 1768 around the <a href="/wiki/Commander_Islands" title="Commander Islands">Commander Islands</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Bering_Sea" title="Bering Sea">Bering Sea</a>, named for the naturalist <a href="/wiki/Georg_Wilhelm_Steller" title="Georg Wilhelm Steller">Georg Wilhelm Steller</a>. He discovered the <a href="/wiki/Species" title="Species">species</a> in 1741 on <a href="/wiki/Vitus_Bering" title="Vitus Bering">Vitus Bering</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Great_Northern_Expedition" title="Great Northern Expedition">Great Northern Expedition</a>, when the crew was shipwrecked on <a href="/wiki/Bering_Island" title="Bering Island">Bering Island</a>, and described it in <i>The Beasts of the Sea</i>, published after his death. The sea cow reached weights of 8–10 metric tons (8.8–11.0 short tons) and lengths of up to 9 metres (30&#160;ft). Its closest living relative, the 3-metre-long (9.8&#160;ft) <a href="/wiki/Dugong" title="Dugong">dugong</a>, is the sole surviving member of its family, <a href="/wiki/Dugongidae" title="Dugongidae">Dugongidae</a>. It had a thicker layer of <a href="/wiki/Blubber" title="Blubber">blubber</a> than other sirenians, and its tail was forked like those of whales and other <a href="/wiki/Cetacea" title="Cetacea">cetaceans</a>. Instead of teeth, the sea cow had an array of white bristles on its upper lip and two <a href="/wiki/Keratin" title="Keratin">keratinous</a> mouth plates for chewing. It fed mainly on <a href="/wiki/Kelp" title="Kelp">kelp</a>, and communicated via sighs and snorting sounds. There is evidence that it was a <a href="/wiki/Monogamous" class="mw-redirect" title="Monogamous">monogamous</a> and <a href="/wiki/Social_animal" title="Social animal">social animal</a>, living in small family groups and <a href="/wiki/Parental_investment" title="Parental investment">raising its young</a>. Twenty-seven years after its discovery, the slow-moving species had been hunted to <a href="/wiki/Extinction" title="Extinction">extinction</a> for its meat, fat, and hide. (<a href="/wiki/Steller%27s_sea_cow" title="Steller's sea cow"><b>Full&#160;article...</b></a>)</p>

<dt><b>September 3</b></dt>

<div id="mp-tfa-img" style="float: left; margin: 0.5em 0.9em 0.4em 0em;"><a href="/wiki/File:APF_Chapman.jpg" class="image"><img alt="APF Chapman.jpg" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b2/APF_Chapman.jpg/100px-APF_Chapman.jpg" width="100" height="200" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b2/APF_Chapman.jpg/150px-APF_Chapman.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b2/APF_Chapman.jpg/200px-APF_Chapman.jpg 2x" data-file-width="300" data-file-height="600" /></a></div>
<p><b><a href="/wiki/Percy_Chapman" title="Percy Chapman">Percy Chapman</a></b> (3 September 1900 – 16 September 1961) captained the <a href="/wiki/England_cricket_team" title="England cricket team">England cricket team</a> between 1926 and 1931. Representing the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Cambridge" title="University of Cambridge">Cambridge</a> cricket team in 1920, he once scored <a href="/wiki/Century_(cricket)" title="Century (cricket)">centuries</a> against <a href="/wiki/Oxford_University_Cricket_Club" title="Oxford University Cricket Club">Oxford</a> and in the <a href="/wiki/Gentlemen_v_Players" title="Gentlemen v Players">Gentlemen v Players</a> match within the space of a week. Chapman made his Test debut in 1924, although he had yet to play <a href="/wiki/County_Championship" title="County Championship">County Cricket</a>. A left-handed batsman, he went on to play 26 <a href="/wiki/Test_cricket" title="Test cricket">Test matches</a> for England. After he took over from <a href="/wiki/Arthur_Carr_(cricketer)" title="Arthur Carr (cricketer)">Arthur Carr</a> as captain in 1926, England defeated Australia for the first time since 1912. He achieved victory in his first nine matches in charge but lost two and drew six of his remaining games. An <a href="/wiki/Amateur_status_in_first-class_cricket" title="Amateur status in first-class cricket">amateur cricketer</a>, Chapman played <a href="/wiki/First-class_cricket" title="First-class cricket">first-class cricket</a> for <a href="/wiki/Kent_County_Cricket_Club" title="Kent County Cricket Club">Kent</a>, eventually as captain. He had a respectable batting record, could score runs quickly, and was popular with spectators. Contemporaries rated him highly as a <a href="/wiki/Fielding_(cricket)" title="Fielding (cricket)">fielder</a>. Although opinions were divided on his tactical ability as a captain, he was seen as an inspirational leader. Chapman's success gave him access to <a href="/wiki/High_society_(group)" class="mw-redirect" title="High society (group)">fashionable society</a> for a time, but in the 1930s, his health and cricketing form declined. For the rest of his life, he suffered from alcoholism. (<a href="/wiki/Percy_Chapman" title="Percy Chapman"><b>Full&#160;article...</b></a>)</p>

<p><b><a href="/wiki/Northern_England" title="Northern England">Northern England</a></b> roughly coincides with the <a href="/wiki/Regions_of_England" title="Regions of England">statistical regions</a> of <a href="/wiki/North_East_England" title="North East England">North East England</a>, <a href="/wiki/North_West_England" title="North West England">North West England</a> and <a href="/wiki/Yorkshire_and_the_Humber" title="Yorkshire and the Humber">Yorkshire and the Humber</a>, which have a combined population of 14.9 million as of the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_Census_2011" title="United Kingdom Census 2011">2011 Census</a>. It contains much of <a href="/wiki/National_parks_of_England_and_Wales" title="National parks of England and Wales">England's national parkland</a> as well as the <a href="/wiki/Conurbation" title="Conurbation">conurbations</a> of <a href="/wiki/Greater_Manchester" title="Greater Manchester">Greater Manchester</a>, <a href="/wiki/Merseyside" title="Merseyside">Merseyside</a>, <a href="/wiki/Teesside" title="Teesside">Teesside</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tyneside" title="Tyneside">Tyneside</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wearside" title="Wearside">Wearside</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Sheffield_Urban_Area" class="mw-redirect" title="Sheffield Urban Area">South</a> and <a href="/wiki/West_Yorkshire_Urban_Area" title="West Yorkshire Urban Area">West Yorkshire</a>. Until the <a href="/wiki/History_of_the_formation_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="History of the formation of the United Kingdom">unification of Britain</a> under the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Stuart" title="House of Stuart">Stuarts</a>, the area experienced <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Scottish_border" title="Anglo-Scottish border">Anglo-Scottish border</a> fighting. Many of the innovations of the <a href="/wiki/Industrial_Revolution" title="Industrial Revolution">Industrial Revolution</a> began in Northern England, and its cities were the crucibles of many of the political changes that accompanied this social upheaval, from <a href="/wiki/Trade_unionism" class="mw-redirect" title="Trade unionism">trade unionism</a> to <a href="/wiki/Manchester_Liberalism" title="Manchester Liberalism">Manchester Capitalism</a>. In the late 19th and early 20th centuries, the economy of the North was dominated by <a href="/wiki/Heavy_industry" title="Heavy industry">heavy industry</a> such as weaving, shipbuilding, steelmaking and mining, but <a href="/wiki/Deindustrialisation" class="mw-redirect" title="Deindustrialisation">deindustrialisation</a> in the late 20th century hit hard. Urban renewal projects and the transition to a <a href="/wiki/Service_economy" title="Service economy">service economy</a> have resulted in strong economic growth in some areas, but a <a href="/wiki/North%E2%80%93South_divide_(England)" title="North–South divide (England)">North–South divide</a> remains in both the <a href="/wiki/Economy_of_England" title="Economy of England">economy</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Culture_of_England" title="Culture of England">culture of England</a>. <div id="mp-tfa-img" style="float: left; margin: 0.5em 0.9em 0.4em 0em;">
<div class="thumbinner mp-thumb" style="background: transparent; border: none; padding: 0; max-width: 100px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Liquid_oxygen_in_a_beaker_(cropped_and_retouched).jpg" class="image" title="Liquid oxygen"><img alt="Liquid oxygen" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a0/Liquid_oxygen_in_a_beaker_%28cropped_and_retouched%29.jpg/100px-Liquid_oxygen_in_a_beaker_%28cropped_and_retouched%29.jpg" width="100" height="125" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a0/Liquid_oxygen_in_a_beaker_%28cropped_and_retouched%29.jpg/150px-Liquid_oxygen_in_a_beaker_%28cropped_and_retouched%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a0/Liquid_oxygen_in_a_beaker_%28cropped_and_retouched%29.jpg/200px-Liquid_oxygen_in_a_beaker_%28cropped_and_retouched%29.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1024" data-file-height="1280" /></a>

<p><b><a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">Oxygen</a></b> is a <a href="/wiki/Chemical_element" title="Chemical element">chemical element</a> with symbol&#160;O and <a href="/wiki/Atomic_number" title="Atomic number">atomic number</a> 8. A member of the <a href="/wiki/Chalcogen" title="Chalcogen">chalcogen</a> group on the <a href="/wiki/Periodic_table" title="Periodic table">periodic table</a>, it is a highly reactive <a href="/wiki/Nonmetal" title="Nonmetal">nonmetal</a> and <a href="/wiki/Oxidizing_agent" title="Oxidizing agent">oxidizing agent</a> that forms <a href="/wiki/Oxide" title="Oxide">oxides</a> with most elements as well as other <a href="/wiki/Chemical_compound" title="Chemical compound">compounds</a>. By mass, oxygen is the most <a href="/wiki/Abundance_of_the_chemical_elements" title="Abundance of the chemical elements">abundant element</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Earth%27s_crust" class="mw-redirect" title="Earth's crust">Earth's crust</a> and the third-most abundant in the universe, after <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a> and <a href="/wiki/Helium" title="Helium">helium</a>. Two atoms of the element can <a href="/wiki/Chemical_bond" title="Chemical bond">bind</a> to form <a href="/wiki/Allotropes_of_oxygen#Dioxygen" title="Allotropes of oxygen">dioxygen</a>, a colorless and odorless <a href="/wiki/Gas" title="Gas">gas</a> that constitutes 20.8% of the <a href="/wiki/Earth%27s_atmosphere" class="mw-redirect" title="Earth's atmosphere">Earth's atmosphere</a>, produced by <a href="/wiki/Photosynthesis" title="Photosynthesis">photosynthesis</a> from water and carbon dioxide. Oxygen is present in water, <a href="/wiki/Protein" title="Protein">proteins</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nucleic_acid" title="Nucleic acid">nucleic acids</a>, <a href="/wiki/Carbohydrate" title="Carbohydrate">carbohydrates</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Fat" title="Fat">fats</a>. Its name was coined in 1777 by <a href="/wiki/Antoine_Lavoisier" title="Antoine Lavoisier">Antoine Lavoisier</a>, whose experiments helped to discredit the then-popular <a href="/wiki/Phlogiston_theory" title="Phlogiston theory">phlogiston theory</a> of <a href="/wiki/Combustion" title="Combustion">combustion</a> and <a href="/wiki/Corrosion" title="Corrosion">corrosion</a>. Common uses of oxygen include residential <a href="/wiki/HVAC" title="HVAC">heating</a>, <a href="/wiki/Internal_combustion_engine" title="Internal combustion engine">internal combustion engines</a>, production of <a href="/wiki/Steel" title="Steel">steel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Plastics" class="mw-redirect" title="Plastics">plastics</a> and <a href="/wiki/Textiles" class="mw-redirect" title="Textiles">textiles</a>, <a href="/wiki/Oxy-fuel_welding_and_cutting" title="Oxy-fuel welding and cutting">brazing, welding and cutting</a> of steels and other <a href="/wiki/Metals" class="mw-redirect" title="Metals">metals</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rocket_propellant" title="Rocket propellant">rocket propellant</a>, <a href="/wiki/Oxygen_therapy" title="Oxygen therapy">oxygen therapy</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Life_support_system" title="Life support system">life support systems</a> in <a href="/wiki/Aircraft" title="Aircraft">aircraft</a>, <a href="/wiki/Submarines" class="mw-redirect" title="Submarines">submarines</a>, <a href="/wiki/Human_spaceflight" title="Human spaceflight">spaceflight</a> and <a href="/wiki/Underwater_diving" title="Underwater diving">diving</a>. (<a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen"><b>Full&#160;article...</b></a>)</p>

<dt><b>September 6</b></dt>

<div id="mp-tfa-img" style="float: left; margin: 0.5em 0.9em 0.4em 0em;">

<p>The <b><a href="/wiki/2014_Lamar_Hunt_U.S._Open_Cup_Final" title="2014 Lamar Hunt U.S. Open Cup Final">2014 Lamar Hunt U.S. Open Cup Final</a></b> was the 101st edition of <a href="/wiki/Lamar_Hunt_U.S._Open_Cup" title="Lamar Hunt U.S. Open Cup">the oldest competition</a> in American soccer, played on September 6, 2014, at <a href="/wiki/PPL_Park" class="mw-redirect" title="PPL Park">PPL Park</a> <i>(pictured)</i> in <a href="/wiki/Chester,_Pennsylvania" title="Chester, Pennsylvania">Chester, Pennsylvania</a>. <a href="/wiki/Seattle_Sounders_FC" title="Seattle Sounders FC">Seattle Sounders FC</a> won the match before a crowd of 15,256, defeating the <a href="/wiki/Philadelphia_Union" title="Philadelphia Union">Philadelphia Union</a>. Philadelphia and Seattle both play in the <a href="/wiki/United_States_soccer_league_system" title="United States soccer league system">top tier</a> of American soccer, <a href="/wiki/Major_League_Soccer" title="Major League Soccer">Major League Soccer</a>, and bypassed the initial stages of the tournament with entries into the third round of play. The Sounders were in the midst of a <a href="/wiki/Supporters%27_Shield" title="Supporters' Shield">Supporters' Shield</a>-winning regular season, while the Union's start was so poor that their coach was replaced a week prior to their first game in the competition. The Union's <a href="/wiki/Maurice_Edu" title="Maurice Edu">Maurice Edu</a> gave his team the lead with a goal in the first half, but the Sounders equalized with a second half strike by <a href="/wiki/Chad_Barrett" title="Chad Barrett">Chad Barrett</a>, and the match went into <a href="/wiki/Extra_time#Association_football" class="mw-redirect" title="Extra time">extra time</a>. <a href="/wiki/Clint_Dempsey" title="Clint Dempsey">Clint Dempsey</a> took the lead for Seattle, and <a href="/wiki/Obafemi_Martins" title="Obafemi Martins">Obafemi Martins</a> sealed the victory with a late goal. Seattle earned a $250,000 cash prize, as well as a berth in the <a href="/wiki/2015%E2%80%9316_CONCACAF_Champions_League" title="2015–16 CONCACAF Champions League">2015–16 CONCACAF Champions League</a>. <dt><b>September 7</b></dt>
</dl>
<div id="mp-tfa-img" style="float: left; margin: 0.5em 0.9em 0.4em 0em;">
<div class="thumbinner mp-thumb" style="background: transparent; border: none; padding: 0; max-width: 120px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Antennae_Galaxies_reloaded.jpg" class="image" title="Galaxies in Corvus"><img alt="Galaxies in Corvus" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/30/Antennae_Galaxies_reloaded.jpg/120px-Antennae_Galaxies_reloaded.jpg" width="120" height="119" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/30/Antennae_Galaxies_reloaded.jpg/180px-Antennae_Galaxies_reloaded.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/30/Antennae_Galaxies_reloaded.jpg/240px-Antennae_Galaxies_reloaded.jpg 2x" data-file-width="4240" data-file-height="4211" /></a>

<p><b><a href="/wiki/Corvus_(constellation)" title="Corvus (constellation)">Corvus</a></b> is a small <a href="/wiki/Constellation" title="Constellation">constellation</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Southern_Celestial_Hemisphere" title="Southern Celestial Hemisphere">Southern Celestial Hemisphere</a>. Its name means "<a href="/wiki/Raven" title="Raven">raven</a>" in <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a>. In the <a href="/wiki/Babylonian_star_catalogues" title="Babylonian star catalogues">Babylonian star catalogues</a> dating from at least 1100 <a href="/wiki/BCE" class="mw-redirect" title="BCE">BCE</a>, it was called the Babylonian Raven. One of the 48 constellations listed by the 2nd-century astronomer <a href="/wiki/Ptolemy" title="Ptolemy">Ptolemy</a>, it depicts a raven, a bird associated with stories about the god <a href="/wiki/Apollo_(god)" class="mw-redirect" title="Apollo (god)">Apollo</a>, perched on the back of <a href="/wiki/Hydra_(constellation)" title="Hydra (constellation)">Hydra</a> the water snake. It is also bordered by the constellations <a href="/wiki/Virgo_(constellation)" title="Virgo (constellation)">Virgo</a> and <a href="/wiki/Crater_(constellation)" title="Crater (constellation)">Crater</a>. Its four brightest stars, <a href="/wiki/Gamma_Corvi" title="Gamma Corvi">Gamma</a>, <a href="/wiki/Delta_Corvi" title="Delta Corvi">Delta</a>, <a href="/wiki/Epsilon_Corvi" title="Epsilon Corvi">Epsilon</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Beta_Corvi" title="Beta Corvi">Beta Corvi</a>, form a distinctive quadrilateral in the night sky. With an <a href="/wiki/Apparent_magnitude" title="Apparent magnitude">apparent magnitude</a> of 2.59, Gamma Corvi—also known as Gienah—is the brightest star in the constellation. It is an aging <a href="/wiki/Blue_giant" title="Blue giant">blue giant</a> around four times as massive as the Sun. The young star <a href="/wiki/Eta_Corvi" title="Eta Corvi">Eta Corvi</a> has been found to have two <a href="/wiki/Debris_disk" title="Debris disk">debris disks</a>. Three star systems have exoplanets, and a fourth planetary system is unconfirmed. <a href="/wiki/TV_Corvi" title="TV Corvi">TV Corvi</a> is a <a href="/wiki/Dwarf_nova" title="Dwarf nova">dwarf nova</a>—a <a href="/wiki/White_dwarf" title="White dwarf">white dwarf</a> and <a href="/wiki/Brown_dwarf" title="Brown dwarf">brown dwarf</a> in very close orbit. <dt><b>September 8</b></dt>
</dl>
<div id="mp-tfa-img" style="float: left; margin: 0.5em 0.9em 0.4em 0em;">
<div class="thumbinner mp-thumb" style="background: transparent; border: none; padding: 0; max-width: 100px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Pietro_Vermigli_by_Hans_Asper.jpg" title="Painting of Peter Martyr Vermigli"><img alt="Painting of Peter Martyr Vermigli" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fa/Pietro_Vermigli_by_Hans_Asper_%28cropped_and_retouched%29.jpg/100px-Pietro_Vermigli_by_Hans_Asper_%28cropped_and_retouched%29.jpg" width="100" height="133" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fa/Pietro_Vermigli_by_Hans_Asper_%28cropped_and_retouched%29.jpg/150px-Pietro_Vermigli_by_Hans_Asper_%28cropped_and_retouched%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fa/Pietro_Vermigli_by_Hans_Asper_%28cropped_and_retouched%29.jpg/200px-Pietro_Vermigli_by_Hans_Asper_%28cropped_and_retouched%29.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1200" data-file-height="1600" /></a></div>

<p><b><a href="/wiki/Peter_Martyr_Vermigli" title="Peter Martyr Vermigli">Peter Martyr Vermigli</a></b> (8 September 1499&#160;– 12 November 1562) was an Italian-born <a href="/wiki/Reformed_Christianity" class="mw-redirect" title="Reformed Christianity">Reformed</a> theologian. His early work as a reformer in <a href="/wiki/Catholic_Church_in_Italy" title="Catholic Church in Italy">Catholic Italy</a> and decision to flee for <a href="/wiki/Protestantism" title="Protestantism">Protestant</a> northern Europe influenced many other Italians to convert and flee as well. English reformer <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Cranmer" title="Thomas Cranmer">Thomas Cranmer</a> invited him to leave a teaching position at <a href="/wiki/Strasbourg" title="Strasbourg">Strasbourg</a> in <a href="/wiki/Alsace" title="Alsace">Alsace</a> to take <a href="/wiki/Regius_Professor_of_Divinity" title="Regius Professor of Divinity">an influential post</a> at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Oxford" title="University of Oxford">University of Oxford</a>, where he defended his <a href="/wiki/Eucharist" title="Eucharist">Eucharistic</a> beliefs against <a href="/wiki/Catholic_Church" title="Catholic Church">Catholic</a> proponents of <a href="/wiki/Transubstantiation" title="Transubstantiation">transubstantiation</a> in a public disputation. He influenced the <a href="/wiki/Edward_VI_of_England" title="Edward VI of England">Edwardian</a> Reformation, including the Eucharistic service of the 1552 <i><a href="/wiki/Book_of_Common_Prayer" title="Book of Common Prayer">Book of Common Prayer</a></i>. Forced to leave England on the accession of the Catholic <a href="/wiki/Mary_I_of_England" title="Mary I of England">Queen Mary I</a>, he eventually settled in Reformed <a href="/wiki/Z%C3%BCrich" title="Zürich">Zürich</a>, where he taught until his death. He was considered an authority on the <a href="/wiki/Lord%27s_Supper_in_Reformed_theology" title="Lord's Supper in Reformed theology">Eucharist among the Reformed churches</a>, and engaged in controversies on the subject by writing treatises. His <i>Loci Communes</i>, a compilation of excerpts from his <a href="/wiki/Biblical_commentaries" class="mw-redirect" title="Biblical commentaries">biblical commentaries</a> organized by the topics of <a href="/wiki/Systematic_theology" title="Systematic theology">systematic theology</a>, became a standard Reformed theological textbook. (<a href="/wiki/Peter_Martyr_Vermigli" title="Peter Martyr Vermigli"><b>Full&#160;article...</b></a>)</p>

<dt><b>September 9</b></dt>
</dl>
<div id="mp-tfa-img" style="float: left; margin: 0.5em 0.9em 0.4em 0em;">
<div class="thumbinner mp-thumb" style="background: transparent; border: none; padding: 0; max-width: 120px;"><a href="/wiki/File:California_half_dollar_obverse.jpg" title="Obverse of a 1925 California Diamond Jubilee half dollar"><img alt="Obverse of a 1925 California Diamond Jubilee half dollar" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/a/ac/California_half_dollar_obverse_%28transparent%29_-_TFA.png/120px-California_half_dollar_obverse_%28transparent%29_-_TFA.png" width="120" height="120" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/a/ac/California_half_dollar_obverse_%28transparent%29_-_TFA.png/180px-California_half_dollar_obverse_%28transparent%29_-_TFA.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/a/ac/California_half_dollar_obverse_%28transparent%29_-_TFA.png 2x" data-file-width="240" data-file-height="240" /></a></div>

<p>The <b><a href="/wiki/California_Diamond_Jubilee_half_dollar" title="California Diamond Jubilee half dollar">California Diamond Jubilee half dollar</a></b> was a US commemorative fifty cent piece, struck at the <a href="/wiki/San_Francisco_Mint" title="San Francisco Mint">San Francisco Mint</a> in 1925. The San Francisco Citizens' Committee wanted to use coin sales to fund a celebration of the 75th anniversary of <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a> statehood. A California congressman attached the authorization to another coinage bill, which was approved in early 1925. Designs by sculptor <a href="/wiki/Jo_Mora" title="Jo Mora">Jo Mora</a> met a hostile reception at the <a href="/wiki/Commission_of_Fine_Arts" class="mw-redirect" title="Commission of Fine Arts">Commission of Fine Arts</a>, but the Citizens' Committee would not change them, and they were approved. The coin has been widely praised for its beauty; the <a href="/wiki/Obverse_and_reverse" title="Obverse and reverse">obverse</a> depicts a Gold Rush-era prospector, and the reverse is an adaptation of the <a href="/wiki/Flag_of_California" title="Flag of California">Flag of California</a>, showing a <a href="/wiki/Grizzly_bear" title="Grizzly bear">grizzly bear</a>. Some 150,000 of the authorized mintage of 300,000 coins were struck in August 1925 in San Francisco. They were offered for sale the following month, but nearly half went unsold, and were later melted. The coin is catalogued at between $200 and $1,300, though exceptional specimens have sold for more. <dt><b>September 10</b></dt>
</dl>
<div id="mp-tfa-img" style="float: left; margin: 0.5em 0.9em 0.4em 0em;">
<div class="thumbinner mp-thumb" style="background: transparent; border: none; padding: 0; max-width: 135px;"><a href="/wiki/File:NER_War_Memorial_full_view_1_-_2017-02-18.jpg" class="image" title="North Eastern Railway War Memorial"><img alt="North Eastern Railway War Memorial" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a5/NER_War_Memorial_full_view_1_-_2017-02-18.jpg/133px-NER_War_Memorial_full_view_1_-_2017-02-18.jpg" width="133" height="100" class="thumbborder" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a5/NER_War_Memorial_full_view_1_-_2017-02-18.jpg/200px-NER_War_Memorial_full_view_1_-_2017-02-18.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a5/NER_War_Memorial_full_view_1_-_2017-02-18.jpg/266px-NER_War_Memorial_full_view_1_-_2017-02-18.jpg 2x" data-file-width="5184" data-file-height="3888" /></a></div>
</div>
<p>The <b><a href="/wiki/North_Eastern_Railway_War_Memorial" title="North Eastern Railway War Memorial">North Eastern Railway War Memorial</a></b> is a <a href="/wiki/First_World_War_memorial" class="mw-redirect" title="First World War memorial">First World War memorial</a> in <a href="/wiki/York" title="York">York</a> in northern England designed by <a href="/wiki/Sir_Edwin_Lutyens" class="mw-redirect" title="Sir Edwin Lutyens">Sir Edwin Lutyens</a>. It commemorates employees of the <a href="/wiki/North_Eastern_Railway_(United_Kingdom)" title="North Eastern Railway (United Kingdom)">North Eastern Railway</a> (NER) who were killed while serving in the <a href="/wiki/First_World_War" class="mw-redirect" title="First World War">First World War</a>. The NER, one of the largest employers in the north of England, released over 18,000 of its employees to serve in the armed forces. By the end of the war, 2,236 men from the company had died on military service overseas; others were killed at home by bombardments of east coast ports, including a <a href="/wiki/Raid_on_Scarborough,_Hartlepool_and_Whitby" title="Raid on Scarborough, Hartlepool and Whitby">raid on Scarborough, Hartlepool and Whitby</a>, and three <a href="/wiki/Zeppelin" title="Zeppelin">Zeppelin</a> raids on <a href="/wiki/York" title="York">York</a>. After the war, thousands of memorials were built across Britain. Among the most prominent designers of memorials was Lutyens, described by <a href="/wiki/Historic_England" title="Historic England">Historic England</a> as "the leading English architect of his generation". The NER memorial, unveiled in 1924, consists of a 54-foot-high (16-metre) obelisk rising from the rear portion of a three-sided screen wall. The wall forms a recess in which stands Lutyens' characteristic <a href="/wiki/Stone_of_Remembrance" title="Stone of Remembrance">Stone of Remembrance</a>. The memorial is a grade II* <a href="/wiki/Listed_building" title="Listed building">listed building</a>. <dt><b>September 11</b></dt>
</dl>
<div id="mp-tfa-img" style="float: left; margin: 0.5em 0.9em 0.4em 0em;">
<div class="thumbinner mp-thumb" style="background: transparent; border: none; padding: 0; max-width: 100px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Benedict_Arnold_1color.jpg" title="Benedict Arnold"><img alt="Benedict Arnold" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d9/Benedict_Arnold_1color_%28crop%29.jpg/100px-Benedict_Arnold_1color_%28crop%29.jpg" width="100" height="133" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d9/Benedict_Arnold_1color_%28crop%29.jpg/150px-Benedict_Arnold_1color_%28crop%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d9/Benedict_Arnold_1color_%28crop%29.jpg/200px-Benedict_Arnold_1color_%28crop%29.jpg 2x" data-file-width="600" data-file-height="800" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption" style="padding: 0.25em 0; word-wrap: break-word;">Benedict Arnold</div>
</div>
</div>
<p><b><a href="/wiki/Benedict_Arnold%27s_expedition_to_Quebec" title="Benedict Arnold's expedition to Quebec">Benedict Arnold led an expedition</a></b> early in the <a href="/wiki/American_Revolutionary_War" title="American Revolutionary War">American Revolutionary War</a> from <a href="/wiki/Cambridge,_Massachusetts" title="Cambridge, Massachusetts">Cambridge, Massachusetts</a>, through the wilderness of what is now <a href="/wiki/Maine" title="Maine">Maine</a> to the gates of <a href="/wiki/Quebec_City" title="Quebec City">Quebec City</a>, setting out on September 11, 1775. Colonel <a href="/wiki/Benedict_Arnold" title="Benedict Arnold">Arnold</a>'s force of 1,100 <a href="/wiki/Continental_Army" title="Continental Army">Continental Army</a> troops was part of a two-pronged <a href="/wiki/Invasion_of_Canada_(1775)" class="mw-redirect" title="Invasion of Canada (1775)">invasion</a> of the British <a href="/wiki/Province_of_Quebec_(1763-1791)" class="mw-redirect" title="Province of Quebec (1763-1791)">Province of Quebec</a>, along with <a href="/wiki/Richard_Montgomery" title="Richard Montgomery">Richard Montgomery</a>'s expedition pushing north from <a href="/wiki/Lake_Champlain" title="Lake Champlain">Lake Champlain</a>. By the time Arnold reached the French settlements above the <a href="/wiki/Saint_Lawrence_River" title="Saint Lawrence River">Saint Lawrence River</a> in November, his force was reduced to 600 starving men. They had traveled about 350 miles (560&#160;km) through poorly charted wilderness, twice the distance they had expected to cover. Assisted by the local French-speaking <a href="/wiki/Canadien" class="mw-redirect" title="Canadien">Canadiens</a>, Arnold's troops crossed the Saint Lawrence on November 13 and 14 and attempted to put Quebec City under siege. Failing in this, they withdrew until Montgomery arrived to lead an <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Quebec_(1775)" title="Battle of Quebec (1775)">unsuccessful attack</a> on the city. Arnold received a promotion to <a href="/wiki/Brigadier_general_(United_States)" title="Brigadier general (United States)">brigadier general</a>. His route through northern Maine has been listed on the <a href="/wiki/National_Register_of_Historic_Places" title="National Register of Historic Places">National Register of Historic Places</a> as the Arnold Trail to Quebec. (<a href="/wiki/Benedict_Arnold%27s_expedition_to_Quebec" title="Benedict Arnold's expedition to Quebec"><b>Full&#160;article...</b></a>)</p>

<div id="mp-tfa-img" style="float: left; margin: 0.5em 0.9em 0.4em 0em;">
<div class="thumbinner mp-thumb" style="background: transparent; border: none; padding: 0; max-width: px;"><a href="/wiki/File:SteveBiko_DaanWildschut.jpg" class="image" title="Stained glass window depicting Steve Biko"><img alt="Stained glass window depicting Steve Biko" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e6/SteveBiko_DaanWildschut.jpg/120px-SteveBiko_DaanWildschut.jpg" width="120" height="133" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e6/SteveBiko_DaanWildschut.jpg/180px-SteveBiko_DaanWildschut.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e6/SteveBiko_DaanWildschut.jpg/239px-SteveBiko_DaanWildschut.jpg 2x" data-file-width="450" data-file-height="500" /></a>

<p><b><a href="/wiki/Steve_Biko" title="Steve Biko">Steve Biko</a></b> (18 December 1946&#160;– 12 September 1977) was a South African <a href="/wiki/Xhosa_people" title="Xhosa people">Xhosa</a> <a href="/wiki/Internal_resistance_to_apartheid" title="Internal resistance to apartheid">anti-apartheid</a> activist. Fighting <a href="/wiki/Racial_segregation" title="Racial segregation">racial segregation</a> and <a href="/wiki/White_South_African" title="White South African">white-minority</a> rule in South Africa, Biko was at the forefront of the grassroots <a href="/wiki/Black_Consciousness_Movement" title="Black Consciousness Movement">Black Consciousness Movement</a> during the late 1960s and 1970s. Frustrated by the domination of white <a href="/wiki/Liberalism" title="Liberalism">liberals</a> in the anti-apartheid movement, he became a leading figure in the creation of the <a href="/wiki/South_African_Students%27_Organisation" title="South African Students' Organisation">South African Students' Organisation</a> in 1968. An <a href="/wiki/African_nationalism" title="African nationalism">African nationalist</a> and <a href="/wiki/African_socialism" title="African socialism">African socialist</a>, he was influenced by <a href="/wiki/Frantz_Fanon" title="Frantz Fanon">Frantz Fanon</a> and the African-American <a href="/wiki/Black_Power_movement" title="Black Power movement">Black Power movement</a>. He promoted its slogan "<a href="/wiki/Black_is_beautiful" title="Black is beautiful">black is beautiful</a>", believing that black people needed to rid themselves of any sense of racial inferiority. In 1972, he helped found the <a href="/wiki/Black_People%27s_Convention" title="Black People's Convention">Black People's Convention</a> to spread these ideas among the wider population. Though the government <a href="/wiki/Banning_order" class="mw-redirect" title="Banning order">banned</a> Biko in 1973, he remained politically active. He was arrested in August 1977 and severely beaten by State security officers, resulting in his death. One of the earliest icons of the movement against apartheid, Biko is regarded as a political <a href="/wiki/Martyr_(politics)" title="Martyr (politics)">martyr</a>. (<a href="/wiki/Steve_Biko" title="Steve Biko"><b>Full&#160;article...</b></a>)</p>

<div id="mp-tfa-img" style="float: left; margin: 0.5em 0.9em 0.4em 0em;">
<div class="thumbinner mp-thumb" style="background: transparent; border: none; padding: 0; max-width: 100px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Bachtiar_Effendi_p104.JPG" class="image" title="Bachtiar Effendi, the film's star"><img alt="Bachtiar Effendi, the film's star" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3f/Bachtiar_Effendi_p104.JPG/100px-Bachtiar_Effendi_p104.JPG" width="100" height="133" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3f/Bachtiar_Effendi_p104.JPG/150px-Bachtiar_Effendi_p104.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3f/Bachtiar_Effendi_p104.JPG/200px-Bachtiar_Effendi_p104.JPG 2x" data-file-width="756" data-file-height="1008" /></a>

<p><i><b><a href="/wiki/Si_Ronda" title="Si Ronda">Si Ronda</a></b></i> is a 1930 <a href="/wiki/Silent_film" title="Silent film">silent film</a> from the <a href="/wiki/Dutch_East_Indies" title="Dutch East Indies">Dutch East Indies</a>, directed by <a href="/wiki/Lie_Tek_Swie" title="Lie Tek Swie">Lie Tek Swie</a> and starring <a href="/wiki/Bachtiar_Effendi" title="Bachtiar Effendi">Bachtiar Effendi</a> <i>(pictured)</i>. It was adapted from a <i><a href="/wiki/Lenong" title="Lenong">lenong</a></i> (a <a href="/wiki/Betawi_people" title="Betawi people">Betawi</a> oral tradition similar to a stage play) that was popular with <a href="/wiki/Chinese_Indonesians" title="Chinese Indonesians">ethnic Chinese</a> and <a href="/wiki/Native_Indonesians" title="Native Indonesians">native</a> audiences of the time. Adaptations of the genre, manifested as bandit films, appeared in domestic cinema following the release of <i><a href="/wiki/Si_Tjonat" title="Si Tjonat">Si Tjonat</a></i> by Batavia Motion Picture in 1929. The Ronda stories follow the Betawi bandit of the same name, who is skilled at <i><a href="/wiki/Silat" title="Silat">silat</a></i> (traditional martial arts) and reputed to take from the rich to give to the poor. The Indonesian film scholar <a href="/wiki/Misbach_Yusa_Biran" title="Misbach Yusa Biran">Misbach Yusa Biran</a> suggests that Ronda was selected for adaptation because of its action sequences. In the domestic cinema, such sequences had generally been inspired by American works and been well received by audiences. The production, now thought <a href="/wiki/Lost_film" title="Lost film">lost</a>, was one of a series of martial arts films released between 1929 and 1931. <i>Si Ronda</i> received little coverage in the media upon its release. A second adaptation of the tale, <i>Si Ronda Macan Betawi</i>, was made in 1978. (<a href="/wiki/Si_Ronda" title="Si Ronda"><b>Full&#160;article...</b></a>)</p>

<div id="mp-tfa-img" style="float: left; margin: 0.5em 0.9em 0.4em 0em;">

<p><b><a href="/wiki/Lost_operas_by_Claudio_Monteverdi" title="Lost operas by Claudio Monteverdi">Seven of Claudio Monteverdi's operas are lost</a></b>, apart from a few fragments, out of ten the <a href="/wiki/Claudio_Monteverdi" title="Claudio Monteverdi">Italian composer</a> wrote in whole or in part between 1607 and 1643. A few <a href="/wiki/Libretto" title="Libretto">librettos</a> from these <a href="/wiki/Baroque_music" title="Baroque music">early baroque</a> works have survived. Opera as a genre emerged during Monteverdi's creative lifetime, and he became a principal exponent of this new form, first at the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Gonzaga" title="House of Gonzaga">Mantuan court</a> and later as director of music at <a href="/wiki/St_Mark%27s_Basilica" title="St Mark's Basilica">St Mark's Basilica</a> in <a href="/wiki/Venice" title="Venice">Venice</a>. The loss of these works, written during a critical period of early opera history, has been much regretted by historians and musicologists, but reflects the habit of the times, when stage music was thought to have little relevance beyond its initial performance and often vanished quickly. Contemporary documents, including many letters written by Monteverdi, have provided most of the available information on the lost works, and have established that four of them were completed and performed in the composer's lifetime. Of the little music that has survived, the <a href="/wiki/Lamento_d%27arianna#.22Lamento_d.27Arianna.22" class="mw-redirect" title="Lamento d'arianna">lamento</a> from <i><a href="/wiki/L%27Arianna" title="L'Arianna">L'Arianna</a></i> (1608) is frequently performed. (<a href="/wiki/Lost_operas_by_Claudio_Monteverdi" title="Lost operas by Claudio Monteverdi"><b>Full&#160;article...</b></a>)</p>

<p><i><b><a href="/wiki/Eve_(2003_TV_series)" title="Eve (2003 TV series)">Eve</a></b></i> is an American television <a href="/wiki/Sitcom" title="Sitcom">sitcom</a>, created by Meg DeLoatch, that originally aired on <a href="/wiki/United_Paramount_Network" class="mw-redirect" title="United Paramount Network">United Paramount Network</a> from September 15, 2003, to May 11, 2006. It features an <a href="/wiki/Ensemble_cast" title="Ensemble cast">ensemble cast</a> with <a href="/wiki/Eve_(rapper)" title="Eve (rapper)">Eve</a> <i>(pictured)</i>, <a href="/wiki/Jason_George" class="mw-redirect" title="Jason George">Jason George</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ali_Landry" title="Ali Landry">Ali Landry</a>, <a href="/wiki/Natalie_Desselle-Reid" title="Natalie Desselle-Reid">Natalie Desselle-Reid</a>, <a href="/wiki/Brian_Hooks" title="Brian Hooks">Brian Hooks</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Sean_Maguire" title="Sean Maguire">Sean Maguire</a> as six friends attempting to navigate relationships with the opposite sex. Produced by <a href="/wiki/The_Greenblatt/Janollari_Studio" class="mw-redirect" title="The Greenblatt/Janollari Studio">The Greenblatt-Janollari Studio</a>, Mega Diva Inc., and <a href="/wiki/Warner_Bros._Television" title="Warner Bros. Television">Warner Bros. Television</a>, the series was developed as a <a href="/wiki/Star_vehicle" title="Star vehicle">vehicle</a> for Eve under the working title <i>The Opposite Sex,</i> and was targeted to younger audiences. After being picked up, the show was renamed <i>Eve</i> to attract the rapper's fans. The series was set in Miami, but filmed at <a href="/wiki/Sunset_Gower_Studios" title="Sunset Gower Studios">Sunset Gower Studios</a> in <a href="/wiki/Hollywood" title="Hollywood">Hollywood</a>, Los Angeles. <i>Eve</i> suffered from low viewership in spite of high ratings among young Hispanic women. The cancellations of this series and other <a href="/wiki/Black_sitcom" title="Black sitcom">black sitcoms</a> were criticized by media outlets for reducing representation of African American characters and the number of roles for African American actors on television. Critical response to <i>Eve</i> was mixed, but the show and Eve received several award nominations. <div id="mp-tfa-img" style="float: left; margin: 0.5em 0.9em 0.4em 0em;">
<div class="thumbinner mp-thumb" style="background: transparent; border: none; padding: 0; max-width: 133px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Shrimp_gumbo.jpg" class="image" title="Gumbo"><img alt="Gumbo" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5f/Shrimp_gumbo.jpg/133px-Shrimp_gumbo.jpg" width="133" height="100" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5f/Shrimp_gumbo.jpg/200px-Shrimp_gumbo.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5f/Shrimp_gumbo.jpg/266px-Shrimp_gumbo.jpg 2x" data-file-width="800" data-file-height="600" /></a></div>

<p><b><a href="/wiki/Gumbo" title="Gumbo">Gumbo</a></b> is a <a href="/wiki/Stew" title="Stew">stew</a> or <a href="/wiki/Soup" title="Soup">soup</a> associated with southern <a href="/wiki/Louisiana" title="Louisiana">Louisiana</a> since the 18th century. It consists primarily of meat or shellfish, a strongly flavored <a href="/wiki/Stock_(food)" title="Stock (food)">stock</a>, a thickener, celery, <a href="/wiki/Bell_pepper" title="Bell pepper">bell peppers</a>, and onions. The dish likely derived its name from either the Bantu word for okra (<i>ki ngombo</i>) or the Choctaw word for <a href="/wiki/Fil%C3%A9_powder" title="Filé powder">filé</a> (<i>kombo</i>). Several varieties exist; <a href="/wiki/Louisiana_Creole_people" title="Louisiana Creole people">Creole</a> gumbo generally contains shellfish and tomatoes, while <a href="/wiki/Cajun" class="mw-redirect" title="Cajun">Cajun</a> gumbo is generally based on a dark <a href="/wiki/Roux" title="Roux">roux</a> and is spicier, with either shellfish or fowl. Sausage or ham are often added to gumbos. After the base is prepared, the vegetables are cooked down, and meat is added. The dish simmers for a minimum of three hours, with shellfish and some spices added near the end. It is traditionally served over rice. The dish combines ingredients and culinary practices of several cultures, including French, Spanish, German, West African, and Choctaw. It was first described in 1802, and was listed in various cookbooks in the latter half of the 19th century. Chef <a href="/wiki/Paul_Prudhomme" title="Paul Prudhomme">Paul Prudhomme</a>'s popularity in the 1980s spurred further interest in gumbo. The dish is the official cuisine of Louisiana. (<a href="/wiki/Gumbo" title="Gumbo"><b>Full&#160;article...</b></a>)</p>

<p>More than a thousand people <a href="/wiki/Development_of_Grand_Theft_Auto_V" title="Development of Grand Theft Auto V"><b>developed <i>Grand Theft Auto V</i></b></a>, an <a href="/wiki/Action-adventure_game" title="Action-adventure game">action-adventure</a> video game. <a href="/wiki/Rockstar_Games" title="Rockstar Games">Rockstar Games</a> released <a href="/wiki/Grand_Theft_Auto_V" title="Grand Theft Auto V">the game</a> on 17 September 2013 for <a href="/wiki/PlayStation_3" title="PlayStation 3">PlayStation 3</a> and <a href="/wiki/Xbox_360" title="Xbox 360">Xbox 360</a>, on 18 November 2014 for <a href="/wiki/PlayStation_4" title="PlayStation 4">PlayStation 4</a> and <a href="/wiki/Xbox_One" title="Xbox One">Xbox One</a>, and on 14 April 2015 for <a href="/wiki/Microsoft_Windows" title="Microsoft Windows">Microsoft Windows</a>, as the fifteenth entry in the <a href="/wiki/Grand_Theft_Auto_(series)" class="mw-redirect" title="Grand Theft Auto (series)"><i>Grand Theft Auto</i> series</a>. Its re-release added a <a href="/wiki/First-person_(video_games)" title="First-person (video games)">first-person view</a> option along with the traditional <a href="/wiki/Virtual_camera_system#Third-person_view" title="Virtual camera system">third-person view</a>. <div id="mp-tfa-img" style="float: left; margin: 0.5em 0.9em 0.4em 0em;">
<div class="thumbinner mp-thumb" style="background: transparent; border: none; padding: 0; max-width: 133px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Morchella_rufobrunnea_154543.jpg" class="image" title="Blushing morel"><img alt="Blushing morel" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/60/Morchella_rufobrunnea_154543.jpg/133px-Morchella_rufobrunnea_154543.jpg" width="133" height="100" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/60/Morchella_rufobrunnea_154543.jpg/200px-Morchella_rufobrunnea_154543.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/60/Morchella_rufobrunnea_154543.jpg/266px-Morchella_rufobrunnea_154543.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2592" data-file-height="1944" /></a></div>
</div>
<p><i><b><a href="/wiki/Morchella_rufobrunnea" title="Morchella rufobrunnea">Morchella rufobrunnea</a></b></i>, the blushing morel, is a <a href="/wiki/Ascomycete" class="mw-redirect" title="Ascomycete">sac fungus</a> in the family <a href="/wiki/Morchellaceae" title="Morchellaceae">Morchellaceae</a>. A prized <a href="/wiki/Edible_mushroom" title="Edible mushroom">edible</a> species, the fungus was first <a href="/wiki/Species_description" title="Species description">described</a> in 1998 by mycologists <a href="/wiki/Gast%C3%B3n_Guzm%C3%A1n" title="Gastón Guzmán">Gastón Guzmán</a> and Fidel Tapia from collections made in <a href="/wiki/Veracruz" title="Veracruz">Veracruz</a>, Mexico. Several <a href="/wiki/Molecular_phylogenetics" title="Molecular phylogenetics">DNA studies</a> have suggested that it is common in the <a href="/wiki/West_Coast_of_the_United_States" title="West Coast of the United States">West Coast of the United States</a>, <a href="/wiki/Israel" title="Israel">Israel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Cyprus" title="Cyprus">Cyprus</a>. It grows in disturbed soil or in <a href="/wiki/Woodchips" title="Woodchips">woodchips</a> used in landscaping, and occasionally under olive trees (<i><a href="/wiki/Olea_europaea" class="mw-redirect" title="Olea europaea">Olea europaea</a></i>) in the <a href="/wiki/Mediterranean_Basin" title="Mediterranean Basin">Mediterranean Basin</a>. Young <a href="/wiki/Ascocarp" title="Ascocarp">fruit bodies</a> have conical, grayish <a href="/wiki/Pileus_(mycology)" title="Pileus (mycology)">caps</a> covered with pale ridges and dark pits; mature specimens are yellowish to ochraceous-buff, and grow to a height of 9.0–15.5&#160;cm (3.5–6.1&#160;in). <i>M.&#160;rufobrunnea</i> differs from other <i><a href="/wiki/Morchella" title="Morchella">Morchella</a></i> species in its urban or suburban habitat preferences, the color and form of the fruit body, the lack of a <a href="/wiki/Sinus_(botany)" title="Sinus (botany)">sinus</a> at the attachment of the cap with the <a href="/wiki/Stipe_(mycology)" title="Stipe (mycology)">stalk</a>, the length of the pits on the surface, and a brownish-orange or pinkish bruising reaction. A process to <a href="/wiki/Fungiculture" title="Fungiculture">cultivate</a> morels now known to be <i>M.&#160;rufobrunnea</i> was described and patented in the 1980s. (<a href="/wiki/Morchella_rufobrunnea" title="Morchella rufobrunnea"><b>Full&#160;article...</b></a>)</p>

<div id="mp-tfa-img" style="float: left; margin: 0.5em 0.9em 0.4em 0em;">

<div class="thumbcaption" style="padding: 0.25em 0; word-wrap: break-word;">The Temple of <a href="/wiki/Isis" title="Isis">Isis</a> at <a href="/wiki/Philae" title="Philae">Philae</a></div>

<p><b><a href="/wiki/Egyptian_temple" title="Egyptian temple">Egyptian temples</a></b> were built to commemorate the <a href="/wiki/Pharaoh" title="Pharaoh">pharaohs</a> and to support the central functions of <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Egyptian_religion" title="Ancient Egyptian religion">their religion</a>: giving <a href="/wiki/Sacrifice" title="Sacrifice">offerings</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Egyptian_pantheon" class="mw-redirect" title="Egyptian pantheon">gods</a>, reenacting their mythological interactions through festivals, and warding off the forces of chaos. <a href="/wiki/Ritual" title="Ritual">Rituals</a>, it was believed, invoked the <a href="/wiki/Divine_presence" title="Divine presence">divine presence</a>, sustained the god, and enabled it to continue to uphold the <a href="/wiki/Maat" title="Maat">divine order</a> of the universe. Temples were important religious sites for all classes of Egyptians even though most people were forbidden from entering their most sacred areas. They are among the largest and most enduring examples of <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Egyptian_architecture" title="Ancient Egyptian architecture">Egyptian architecture</a>, with their elements arranged and decorated according to complex patterns of religious symbolism. A large temple could own sizable tracts of land and employ thousands of laymen to supply its needs. Some temples, such as <a href="/wiki/Abu_Simbel" class="mw-redirect" title="Abu Simbel">Abu Simbel</a>, have become <a href="/wiki/Tourism_in_Egypt" title="Tourism in Egypt">tourist</a> attractions that contribute significantly to the modern <a href="/wiki/Economy_of_Egypt" title="Economy of Egypt">Egyptian economy</a>. <a href="/wiki/Egyptology" title="Egyptology">Egyptologists</a> continue to study the surviving temples for their invaluable sources of information about <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Egypt" title="Ancient Egypt">ancient Egyptian</a> society. (<a href="/wiki/Egyptian_temple" title="Egyptian temple"><b>Full&#160;article...</b></a>)</p>

<div id="mp-tfa-img" style="float: left; margin: 0.5em 0.9em 0.4em 0em;"><a href="/wiki/File:Planet_Stories_March_1951_cover.jpg" class="image" title="March 1951 cover"><img alt="March 1951 cover" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9b/Planet_Stories_March_1951_cover.jpg/100px-Planet_Stories_March_1951_cover.jpg" width="100" height="144" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9b/Planet_Stories_March_1951_cover.jpg/150px-Planet_Stories_March_1951_cover.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9b/Planet_Stories_March_1951_cover.jpg/200px-Planet_Stories_March_1951_cover.jpg 2x" data-file-width="350" data-file-height="504" /></a></div>
<p><i><b><a href="/wiki/Planet_Stories" title="Planet Stories">Planet Stories</a></b></i> was an American <a href="/wiki/Pulp_magazine" title="Pulp magazine">pulp</a> <a href="/wiki/Science_fiction_magazine" title="Science fiction magazine">science fiction magazine</a>, published by <a href="/wiki/Fiction_House" title="Fiction House">Fiction House</a> between 1939 and 1955. It featured adventures in space and on other planets, and was initially focused on a young readership. Malcolm Reiss was editor or editor-in-chief for all of its 71 issues. It was launched at the same time as Fiction House's more successful <i><a href="/wiki/Planet_Comics" title="Planet Comics">Planet Comics</a></i>. Almost every issue's cover emphasized scantily clad <a href="/wiki/Damsels_in_distress" class="mw-redirect" title="Damsels in distress">damsels in distress</a> or alien princesses. <i>Planet Stories</i> did not pay well enough to regularly attract the leading science fiction writers of the day, but did on occasion manage to obtain work from well-known names including <a href="/wiki/Isaac_Asimov" title="Isaac Asimov">Isaac Asimov</a>, <a href="/wiki/Clifford_D._Simak" title="Clifford D. Simak">Clifford Simak</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Philip_K._Dick" title="Philip K. Dick">Philip K. Dick</a>. The two writers most identified with the magazine are <a href="/wiki/Leigh_Brackett" title="Leigh Brackett">Leigh Brackett</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ray_Bradbury" title="Ray Bradbury">Ray Bradbury</a>, both of whom set many of their stories on a romanticized version of Mars that owed much to the depiction of <a href="/wiki/Barsoom" title="Barsoom">Barsoom</a> in the works of <a href="/wiki/Edgar_Rice_Burroughs" title="Edgar Rice Burroughs">Edgar Rice Burroughs</a>. Bradbury contributed an early story in his <i><a href="/wiki/The_Martian_Chronicles" title="The Martian Chronicles">Martian Chronicles</a></i> sequence, and Brackett authored a series of adventures featuring Eric John Stark. (<a href="/wiki/Planet_Stories" title="Planet Stories"><b>Full&#160;article...</b></a>)</p>

<p><i><b><a href="/wiki/The_Blue_Flame_(play)" title="The Blue Flame (play)">The Blue Flame</a></b></i> is a four-act <a href="/wiki/Play_(theatre)" title="Play (theatre)">play</a> written by George V. Hobart and <a href="/wiki/John_Willard_(playwright)" title="John Willard (playwright)">John Willard</a>, who revised an earlier version by Leta Vance Nicholson. In 1920, producer <a href="/wiki/Albert_H._Woods" title="Albert H. Woods">Albert H. Woods</a> staged the play on <a href="/wiki/Broadway_theatre" title="Broadway theatre">Broadway</a> and on tour across the United States. Ruth Gordon, the main character, is a religious young woman who dies and is revived by her scientist fiancé as a soulless <a href="/wiki/Femme_fatale" title="Femme fatale">femme fatale</a>. She seduces several men and involves them in crimes, including drug use and murder. In the final act, her death and resurrection are revealed to be a <a href="/wiki/Dream_sequence" title="Dream sequence">dream</a>. The production starred <a href="/wiki/Theda_Bara" title="Theda Bara">Theda Bara</a> <i>(pictured)</i>, a popular <a href="/wiki/Silent_film" title="Silent film">silent film</a> actress who was known for playing similar roles in movies. Critics panned the play, ridiculing the plot, the dialog, and Bara's acting. Theater historian <a href="/wiki/Ward_Morehouse" title="Ward Morehouse">Ward Morehouse</a> called it "one of the worst plays ever written". Bara's movie fame drew large crowds to theaters, and the play was a commercial success, breaking attendance records at some venues. Ruth Gordon was Bara's only Broadway role, and <i>The Blue Flame</i> was one of her last professional acting projects. (<a href="/wiki/The_Blue_Flame_(play)" title="The Blue Flame (play)"><b>Full&#160;article...</b></a>)</p>

<p><a href="/wiki/INS_Vikrant_(R11)" title="INS Vikrant (R11)"><b>INS <i>Vikrant</i></b></a> (from <a href="/wiki/Sanskrit" title="Sanskrit">Sanskrit</a> for "courageous") was a <a href="/wiki/Majestic-class_aircraft_carrier" class="mw-redirect" title="Majestic-class aircraft carrier"><i>Majestic</i>-class&#160;aircraft carrier</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Indian_Navy" title="Indian Navy">Indian Navy</a>. The ship was <a href="/wiki/Keel_laying" title="Keel laying">laid down</a> as HMS <i>Hercules</i> for the British <a href="/wiki/Royal_Navy" title="Royal Navy">Royal Navy</a> during <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a> and launched on 22 September 1945, but construction was put on hold when the war ended. India purchased the incomplete carrier in 1957, and construction was completed in 1961. <i>Vikrant</i> was <a href="/wiki/Ship_commissioning" title="Ship commissioning">commissioned</a> as the first <a href="/wiki/Aircraft_carrier" title="Aircraft carrier">aircraft carrier</a> of the Indian Navy and played a key role in enforcing the <a href="/wiki/Naval_blockade" class="mw-redirect" title="Naval blockade">naval blockade</a> of <a href="/wiki/East_Pakistan" title="East Pakistan">East Pakistan</a> during the <a href="/wiki/Indo-Pakistan_War_of_1971" class="mw-redirect" title="Indo-Pakistan War of 1971">Indo-Pakistan War of 1971</a>. In its later years, the ship underwent major refits to embark modern aircraft, before being <a href="/wiki/Ship_decommissioning" class="mw-redirect" title="Ship decommissioning">decommissioned</a> in January 1997. <i>Vikrant</i> was preserved as a <a href="/wiki/Museum_ship" title="Museum ship">museum ship</a> in <a href="/wiki/Cuffe_Parade" title="Cuffe Parade">Cuffe Parade</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mumbai" title="Mumbai">Mumbai</a>, until 2012. The ship was sold through an online auction in January 2014 and <a href="/wiki/Ship_breaking" title="Ship breaking">scrapped</a> in November 2014 after final clearance from the <a href="/wiki/Supreme_Court_of_India" title="Supreme Court of India">Supreme Court</a>. The Indian Navy is currently constructing its first home-built carrier, also named <a href="/wiki/INS_Vikrant_(2013)" title="INS Vikrant (2013)">INS <i>Vikrant</i></a>, scheduled to be commissioned by the end of 2018. (<a href="/wiki/INS_Vikrant_(R11)" title="INS Vikrant (R11)"><b>Full&#160;article...</b></a>)</p>

<dt><b>September 23</b></dt>

<div id="mp-tfa-img" style="float: left; margin: 0.5em 0.9em 0.4em 0em;"><a href="/wiki/File:Literary_Hall_Romney_WV_2013_07_14_03.jpg" class="image"><img alt="Literary Hall Romney WV 2013 07 14 03.jpg" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/03/Literary_Hall_Romney_WV_2013_07_14_03.jpg/100px-Literary_Hall_Romney_WV_2013_07_14_03.jpg" width="100" height="133" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/03/Literary_Hall_Romney_WV_2013_07_14_03.jpg/150px-Literary_Hall_Romney_WV_2013_07_14_03.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/03/Literary_Hall_Romney_WV_2013_07_14_03.jpg/200px-Literary_Hall_Romney_WV_2013_07_14_03.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2640" data-file-height="3520" /></a></div>
<p><b><a href="/wiki/Literary_Hall" title="Literary Hall">Literary Hall</a></b> is a brick library building and museum in <a href="/wiki/Romney,_West_Virginia" title="Romney, West Virginia">Romney, West Virginia</a>, built in 1869 and 1870 by the <a href="/wiki/Romney_Literary_Society" title="Romney Literary Society">Romney Literary Society</a>. Founded in 1819, the society was the first literary organization of its kind in the present-day state of <a href="/wiki/West_Virginia" title="West Virginia">West Virginia</a>, and one of the first in the United States. In 1846, the society constructed a building which housed the <a href="/wiki/Romney_Classical_Institute" title="Romney Classical Institute">Romney Classical Institute</a> and its library. During the <a href="/wiki/American_Civil_War" title="American Civil War">Civil War</a> the library's contents were plundered by <a href="/wiki/Union_Army" title="Union Army">Union Army</a> forces, and many of its 3,000 volumes were scattered or destroyed. The society transferred ownership of its Romney Classical Institute campus to the <a href="/wiki/West_Virginia_Schools_for_the_Deaf_and_Blind" title="West Virginia Schools for the Deaf and Blind">West Virginia Schools for the Deaf and Blind</a> in 1870 and in that year completed Literary Hall, where the society reconstituted its library collection and revived its literary activities. The Romney Literary Society's last meeting was held there in 1886. In 1979 the hall was placed on the <a href="/wiki/National_Register_of_Historic_Places" title="National Register of Historic Places">National Register of Historic Places</a>. Its basic design incorporates <a href="/wiki/Federal_architecture" title="Federal architecture">Federal</a> and <a href="/wiki/Greek_Revival_architecture" title="Greek Revival architecture">Greek Revival</a> styles along with <a href="/wiki/Victorian_architecture" title="Victorian architecture">Victorian</a> details. (<a href="/wiki/Literary_Hall" title="Literary Hall"><b>Full&#160;article...</b></a>)</p>

<div id="mp-tfa-img" style="float: left; margin: 0.5em 0.9em 0.4em 0em;">
<div class="thumbinner mp-thumb" style="background: transparent; border: none; padding: 0; max-width: 133px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Westlake_Station_DSTT_portal.jpg" class="image" title="Westlake Station"><img alt="Westlake Station" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0b/Westlake_Station_DSTT_portal.jpg/133px-Westlake_Station_DSTT_portal.jpg" width="133" height="100" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0b/Westlake_Station_DSTT_portal.jpg/200px-Westlake_Station_DSTT_portal.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0b/Westlake_Station_DSTT_portal.jpg/266px-Westlake_Station_DSTT_portal.jpg 2x" data-file-width="4000" data-file-height="3000" /></a>

<p>The <b><a href="/wiki/Downtown_Seattle_Transit_Tunnel" title="Downtown Seattle Transit Tunnel">Downtown Seattle Transit Tunnel</a></b> is a <a href="/wiki/Public_transit" class="mw-redirect" title="Public transit">public transit</a> tunnel for <a href="/wiki/Transit_bus" title="Transit bus">buses</a> and <a href="/wiki/Light_rail" title="Light rail">light rail</a> trains in <a href="/wiki/Seattle" title="Seattle">Seattle</a>, Washington, in the United States. It runs north–south through <a href="/wiki/Downtown_Seattle" title="Downtown Seattle">Downtown Seattle</a>, connecting five stations on 3rd Avenue and Pine Street. It is the busiest section of <a href="/wiki/Sound_Transit" title="Sound Transit">Sound Transit</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Link_light_rail" title="Link light rail">Link light rail</a> network, with an average of over 10,000 weekday train <a href="/wiki/Boarding_(transport)" title="Boarding (transport)">boardings</a> at the four stations served by light rail. The $469 million tunnel was planned in the late 1970s and built between 1987 and 1990, using <a href="/wiki/Tunnel_boring_machine" title="Tunnel boring machine">tunnel boring machines</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cut-and-cover" class="mw-redirect" title="Cut-and-cover">cut-and-cover</a> excavation. Between 1990 and 2004, the tunnel was exclusively used by <a href="/wiki/Dual-mode_bus" title="Dual-mode bus">dual-mode buses</a> that ran on <a href="/wiki/Overhead_wire" class="mw-redirect" title="Overhead wire">overhead wires</a>; they were later replaced with <a href="/wiki/Hybrid_electric_bus" title="Hybrid electric bus">hybrid electric buses</a> using batteries within the tunnel. After a two-year renovation, the tunnel reopened on September 24, 2007, and light rail service began in July 2009, sharing the platforms with existing buses. Planned expansion of the light rail system, along with the closure of one station, will necessitate the removal of buses from the tunnel by 2019. (<a href="/wiki/Downtown_Seattle_Transit_Tunnel" title="Downtown Seattle Transit Tunnel"><b>Full&#160;article...</b></a>)</p>

<p><b><a href="/wiki/Catherine_Zeta-Jones" title="Catherine Zeta-Jones">Catherine Zeta-Jones</a></b> (born 25 September 1969) is a film and stage actress. Raised in <a href="/wiki/Swansea" title="Swansea">Swansea</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wales" title="Wales">Wales</a>, she studied musical theatre at the <a href="/wiki/Arts_Educational_Schools,_London" title="Arts Educational Schools, London">Arts Educational Schools, London</a>, and made her adult stage breakthrough with a leading role in 1987 in <i><a href="/wiki/42nd_Street_(musical)" title="42nd Street (musical)">42nd Street</a></i>. She found great success as a regular in the British television series <i><a href="/wiki/The_Darling_Buds_of_May_(TV_series)" title="The Darling Buds of May (TV series)">The Darling Buds of May</a></i> (1991–93). Dismayed at being <a href="/wiki/Typecasting_(acting)" title="Typecasting (acting)">typecast</a> as the token pretty girl in British films, Zeta-Jones relocated to Los Angeles. Critics praised her portrayal of a vengeful pregnant woman in <i><a href="/wiki/Traffic_(2000_film)" title="Traffic (2000 film)">Traffic</a></i> (2000) and a murderous singer in the musical film <i><a href="/wiki/Chicago_(2002_film)" title="Chicago (2002 film)">Chicago</a></i> (2002), winning her the <a href="/wiki/Academy_Award_for_Best_Supporting_Actress" title="Academy Award for Best Supporting Actress">Academy Award for Best Supporting Actress</a>. She continued to star in high-profile films for much of the 2000s, including the black comedy <i><a href="/wiki/Intolerable_Cruelty" title="Intolerable Cruelty">Intolerable Cruelty</a></i> (2003), the heist film <i><a href="/wiki/Ocean%27s_Twelve" title="Ocean's Twelve">Ocean's Twelve</a></i> (2004), the comedy <i><a href="/wiki/The_Terminal" title="The Terminal">The Terminal</a></i> (2004), and the romantic comedy <i><a href="/wiki/No_Reservations_(film)" title="No Reservations (film)">No Reservations</a></i> (2007). During a decrease in workload, she returned to the stage and portrayed an ageing actress in <i><a href="/wiki/A_Little_Night_Music" title="A Little Night Music">A Little Night Music</a></i> (2009), winning the <a href="/wiki/Tony_Award_for_Best_Actress_in_a_Musical" title="Tony Award for Best Actress in a Musical">Tony Award for Best Actress</a>. <div id="mp-tfa-img" style="float: left; margin: 0.5em 0.9em 0.4em 0em;"><a href="/wiki/File:St_Botolph,_Quarrington_-_geograph.org.uk_-_105619.jpg" class="image"><img alt="St Botolph, Quarrington - geograph.org.uk - 105619.jpg" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/37/St_Botolph%2C_Quarrington_-_geograph.org.uk_-_105619.jpg/100px-St_Botolph%2C_Quarrington_-_geograph.org.uk_-_105619.jpg" width="100" height="133" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/37/St_Botolph%2C_Quarrington_-_geograph.org.uk_-_105619.jpg/150px-St_Botolph%2C_Quarrington_-_geograph.org.uk_-_105619.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/37/St_Botolph%2C_Quarrington_-_geograph.org.uk_-_105619.jpg/200px-St_Botolph%2C_Quarrington_-_geograph.org.uk_-_105619.jpg 2x" data-file-width="480" data-file-height="640" /></a></div>
<p><b><a href="/wiki/St_Botolph%27s_Church,_Quarrington" title="St Botolph's Church, Quarrington">St Botolph's Church</a></b> is an <a href="/wiki/Church_of_England" title="Church of England">Anglican</a> place of worship in the village of <a href="/wiki/Quarrington,_Lincolnshire" title="Quarrington, Lincolnshire">Quarrington</a>, part of the <a href="/wiki/Civil_parish" title="Civil parish">civil parish</a> of <a href="/wiki/Sleaford" title="Sleaford">Sleaford</a> in <a href="/wiki/Lincolnshire" title="Lincolnshire">Lincolnshire</a>, England. By the time <i><a href="/wiki/Domesday_Book" title="Domesday Book">Domesday Book</a></i> was compiled in 1086, a church in Quarrington was part of <a href="/wiki/Ramsey_Abbey" title="Ramsey Abbey">Ramsey Abbey</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Fief" title="Fief">fee</a>, and around 1165 it was granted to <a href="/wiki/Haverholme_Priory" title="Haverholme Priory">Haverholme Priory</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Advowson" title="Advowson">right to present the rector</a> was claimed by the Abbey in the 13th century, by the <a href="/wiki/Bishop_of_Lincoln" title="Bishop of Lincoln">Bishop of Lincoln</a> in the early 16th century, and by Robert Carre and his descendants after Carre acquired a manor at Quarrington. The oldest parts of the current building date to the 13th century, although substantial rebuilding took place over the following century. Renovations followed and the local architect Charles Kirk the Younger carried out <a href="/wiki/Victorian_restoration" title="Victorian restoration">restoration</a> work in 1862 and 1863, when he added a <a href="/wiki/Chancel" title="Chancel">chancel</a> in his parents' memory. The church consists of a tower and spire with a <a href="/wiki/Nave" title="Nave">nave</a> and north aisle spanning eastwards to the chancel. With capacity for 124 people, the church serves the <a href="/wiki/Ecclesiastic_parish" class="mw-redirect" title="Ecclesiastic parish">ecclesiastic parish</a> of Quarrington with Old Sleaford. Recognised for its age and <a href="/wiki/Tracery" title="Tracery">tracery</a>, the church has been designated a grade II* <a href="/wiki/Listed_building" title="Listed building">listed building</a>. (<a href="/wiki/St_Botolph%27s_Church,_Quarrington" title="St Botolph's Church, Quarrington"><b>Full&#160;article...</b></a>)</p>

<div id="mp-tfa-img" style="float: left; margin: 0.5em 0.9em 0.4em 0em;"><a href="/wiki/File:Caloenas_maculata.jpg" class="image"><img alt="Caloenas maculata.jpg" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/10/Caloenas_maculata.jpg/100px-Caloenas_maculata.jpg" width="100" height="76" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/10/Caloenas_maculata.jpg/150px-Caloenas_maculata.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/10/Caloenas_maculata.jpg/200px-Caloenas_maculata.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1434" data-file-height="1093" /></a></div>
<p>The <b><a href="/wiki/Spotted_green_pigeon" title="Spotted green pigeon">spotted green pigeon</a></b> is a species of <a href="/wiki/Pigeon" class="mw-redirect" title="Pigeon">pigeon</a> which is most likely <a href="/wiki/Extinct" class="mw-redirect" title="Extinct">extinct</a>. It was first mentioned and <a href="/wiki/Scientific_description" class="mw-redirect" title="Scientific description">described</a> in 1783 by <a href="/wiki/John_Latham_(ornithologist)" title="John Latham (ornithologist)">John Latham</a>, who claimed to have seen two specimens and a drawing depicting the bird. Today, the species is only known from a specimen kept in <a href="/wiki/World_Museum,_Liverpool" class="mw-redirect" title="World Museum, Liverpool">World Museum, Liverpool</a>. Overlooked for much of the 20th century, it was only recognised as a valid extinct species by the <a href="/wiki/IUCN_Red_List" title="IUCN Red List">IUCN Red List</a> in 2008. In 2014 a genetic study confirmed it as a distinct species related to the <a href="/wiki/Nicobar_pigeon" title="Nicobar pigeon">Nicobar pigeon</a>, and showed that the two were the closest relatives of the extinct <a href="/wiki/Dodo" title="Dodo">dodo</a> and <a href="/wiki/Rodrigues_solitaire" title="Rodrigues solitaire">Rodrigues solitaire</a>. The remaining specimen is 32<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>cm (12.5<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>in) long, and has very dark, brownish <a href="/wiki/Plumage" title="Plumage">plumage</a> with a green gloss. The neck-feathers are elongated, and most of the feathers on the upperparts and wings have a yellowish spot on their tips. It has a black bill with a yellow tip, and the end of the tail has a pale band. It has relatively short legs and long wings. It may have been native to an island somewhere in the <a href="/wiki/South_Pacific_Ocean" class="mw-redirect" title="South Pacific Ocean">South Pacific Ocean</a> or the <a href="/wiki/Indian_Ocean" title="Indian Ocean">Indian Ocean</a>, and it has been suggested that a bird referred to in 1928 as <i>titi</i> by <a href="/wiki/Tahitian_islanders" class="mw-redirect" title="Tahitian islanders">Tahitian islanders</a> was this bird. (<a href="/wiki/Spotted_green_pigeon" title="Spotted green pigeon"><b>Full&#160;article...</b></a>)</p>

<p><i><b><a href="/wiki/Gods%27_Man" title="Gods' Man">Gods' Man</a></b></i> is a <a href="/wiki/Wordless_novel" title="Wordless novel">wordless novel</a> by American artist <a href="/wiki/Lynd_Ward" title="Lynd Ward">Lynd Ward</a> (1905–1985) published in 1929. In 139 captionless woodblock prints it tells the <a href="/wiki/Faust" title="Faust">Faustian</a> story of an artist who signs away his soul for a magic paintbrush. It was the first American wordless novel, and is seen as a precursor of, and influence on, the <a href="/wiki/Graphic_novel" title="Graphic novel">graphic novel</a>. Ward first encountered the wordless novel with <a href="/wiki/Frans_Masereel" title="Frans Masereel">Frans Masereel</a>'s <i>The Sun</i> (1919) while studying art in Germany in 1926. He returned to the United States in 1927 and established a career for himself as an illustrator. He found <a href="/wiki/Otto_N%C3%BCckel" title="Otto Nückel">Otto Nückel</a>'s wordless novel <i><a href="/wiki/Destiny_(wordless_novel)" title="Destiny (wordless novel)">Destiny</a></i> (1926) in New York City, and it inspired him to create a similar work. <i>Gods' Man</i> appeared a week before the <a href="/wiki/Wall_Street_Crash_of_1929" title="Wall Street Crash of 1929">Wall Street Crash of 1929</a>; it nevertheless enjoyed strong sales and remains the best-selling American wordless novel. Its success inspired other Americans to experiment with the medium, including cartoonist <a href="/wiki/Milt_Gross" title="Milt Gross">Milt Gross</a>, who parodied it in <i><a href="/wiki/He_Done_Her_Wrong" title="He Done Her Wrong">He Done Her Wrong</a></i> (1930). In the 1970s Ward's example inspired cartoonists <a href="/wiki/Art_Spiegelman" title="Art Spiegelman">Art Spiegelman</a> and <a href="/wiki/Will_Eisner" title="Will Eisner">Will Eisner</a> to create their first graphic novels. (<a href="/wiki/God%27s_Man" class="mw-redirect" title="God's Man"><b>Full&#160;article...</b></a>)</p>

<div id="mp-tfa-img" style="float: left; margin: 0.5em 0.9em 0.4em 0em;"><a href="/wiki/File:Michael_Egan.jpg" class="image"><img alt="Michael Egan.jpg" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/90/Michael_Egan.jpg/100px-Michael_Egan.jpg" width="100" height="130" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/90/Michael_Egan.jpg/150px-Michael_Egan.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/90/Michael_Egan.jpg/200px-Michael_Egan.jpg 2x" data-file-width="426" data-file-height="554" /></a></div>
<p><b><a href="/wiki/Michael_Francis_Egan" title="Michael Francis Egan">Michael Francis Egan</a></b> (September 29, 1761 – July 22, 1814) was a <a href="/wiki/Prelate" title="Prelate">prelate</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic_Church" class="mw-redirect" title="Roman Catholic Church">Roman Catholic Church</a>. Born in Ireland, he joined the <a href="/wiki/Franciscans" title="Franciscans">Franciscan Order</a> at a young age. He was <a href="/wiki/Holy_orders" title="Holy orders">ordained</a> a <a href="/wiki/Priesthood_(Catholic_Church)" class="mw-redirect" title="Priesthood (Catholic Church)">priest</a>, probably in <a href="/wiki/Prague" title="Prague">Prague</a>, in 1785 or 1786. He advanced rapidly to positions of responsibility in the Franciscan order, becoming <i><a href="/wiki/Custos_(Franciscans)" title="Custos (Franciscans)">custos</a></i> (guardian) in the province of <a href="/wiki/Munster" title="Munster">Munster</a> in Ireland, then at the <a href="/wiki/Pontifical_Irish_College" title="Pontifical Irish College">Pontifical College</a> at the home of Irish Franciscans in <a href="/wiki/Rome" title="Rome">Rome</a>, and later at <a href="/wiki/Ennis" title="Ennis">Ennis</a> in Ireland. Egan arrived in the United States in January 1802 to serve as an assistant <a href="/wiki/Pastor" title="Pastor">pastor</a> near <a href="/wiki/Lancaster,_Pennsylvania" title="Lancaster, Pennsylvania">Lancaster, Pennsylvania</a>. His reputation as a gifted preacher secured him a position in 1803 as a pastor at <a href="/wiki/St._Mary%27s_Roman_Catholic_Church_(Philadelphia)" title="St. Mary's Roman Catholic Church (Philadelphia)">St. Mary's Church</a> in Philadelphia. In 1808, he was appointed the first <a href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic_Archdiocese_of_Philadelphia" title="Roman Catholic Archdiocese of Philadelphia">Bishop of Philadelphia</a>, holding that position until his death in 1814. His tenure as bishop saw the construction of new churches and the expansion of the Catholic Church membership in his diocese, but much of his time was consumed by disputes with the lay trustees of St. Mary's Church. (<a href="/wiki/Michael_Francis_Egan" title="Michael Francis Egan"><b>Full&#160;article...</b></a>)</p>

<div id="mp-tfa-img" style="float: left; margin: 0.5em 0.9em 0.4em 0em;"><a href="/wiki/File:Seal_of_the_United_States_Supreme_Court.svg" class="image" title="Seal of the United States Supreme Court"><img alt="Seal of the United States Supreme Court" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f3/Seal_of_the_United_States_Supreme_Court.svg/100px-Seal_of_the_United_States_Supreme_Court.svg.png" width="100" height="100" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f3/Seal_of_the_United_States_Supreme_Court.svg/150px-Seal_of_the_United_States_Supreme_Court.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f3/Seal_of_the_United_States_Supreme_Court.svg/200px-Seal_of_the_United_States_Supreme_Court.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="720" data-file-height="720" /></a></div>
<p><i><b><a href="/wiki/Washington_v._Texas" title="Washington v. Texas">Washington v. Texas</a></b></i> (1967) is a <a href="/wiki/Supreme_Court_of_the_United_States" title="Supreme Court of the United States">US Supreme Court</a> case about the right of criminal defendants to have witnesses testify on their behalf. The Court <a href="/wiki/Holding_(law)" title="Holding (law)">decided</a> that the <a href="/wiki/Compulsory_Process_Clause" title="Compulsory Process Clause">Compulsory Process Clause</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Sixth_Amendment_to_the_United_States_Constitution" title="Sixth Amendment to the United States Constitution">Sixth Amendment to the Constitution</a> applied in state courts as well as federal courts. At his trial Jackie Washington had attempted to call his co-defendant as a witness but was blocked because state law prevented co-defendants from testifying for each other, under the theory that they might lie for each other on the stand. The Supreme Court reasoned that the <a href="/wiki/Due_Process_Clause" title="Due Process Clause">Due Process Clause</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Fourteenth_Amendment_to_the_United_States_Constitution" title="Fourteenth Amendment to the United States Constitution">Fourteenth Amendment</a> gives defendants the right to fair proceedings, including the right to compel defense witnesses to testify. In previous cases, the Supreme Court had ruled that the Fourteenth Amendment, which was adopted after the <a href="/wiki/American_Civil_War" title="American Civil War">Civil War</a>, makes many <a href="/wiki/Incorporation_of_the_Bill_of_Rights" title="Incorporation of the Bill of Rights">federal guarantees in the Bill of Rights</a> applicable to the states. The impact of <i>Washington</i> was narrowed by a later case, <i><a href="/wiki/Taylor_v._Illinois" title="Taylor v. Illinois">Taylor v. Illinois</a></i> (1988), in which the Court said that "countervailing public interests" could be balanced against a defendant's right to present witnesses.
