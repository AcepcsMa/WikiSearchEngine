				<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p><b>Information retrieval</b> (<b>IR</b>) is the activity of obtaining <a href="/wiki/Information" title="Information">information</a> resources relevant to an information need from a collection of information resources. Searches can be based on <a href="/wiki/Full-text_search" title="Full-text search">full-text</a> or other content-based indexing. Information retrieval is the science of searching for information in a document, searching for documents themselves, and also searching for <a href="/wiki/Metadata" title="Metadata">metadata</a> that describe data, and for databases of texts, images or sounds.</p>
<p>Automated information retrieval systems are used to reduce what has been called <a href="/wiki/Information_overload" title="Information overload">information overload</a>. Many <a href="/wiki/University" title="University">universities</a> and <a href="/wiki/Public_library" title="Public library">public libraries</a> use IR systems to provide access to books, journals and other documents. <a href="/wiki/Web_search_engine" title="Web search engine">Web search engines</a> are the most visible <a href="/wiki/Information_retrieval_applications" title="Information retrieval applications">IR applications</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Overview">Overview</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Information_retrieval&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Overview">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>An information retrieval process begins when a user enters a query into the system. Queries are formal statements of information needs, for example search strings in web search engines. In information retrieval a query does not uniquely identify a single object in the collection. Instead, several objects may match the query, perhaps with different degrees of <a href="/wiki/Relevance_(information_retrieval)" title="Relevance (information retrieval)">relevancy</a>.</p>
<p>An object is an entity that is represented by information in a content collection or <a href="/wiki/Database" title="Database">database</a>. User queries are matched against the database information. However, as opposed to classical SQL queries of a database, in information retrieval the results returned may or may not match the query, so results are typically ranked. This ranking of results is a key difference of information retrieval searching compared to database searching.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Depending on the <a href="/wiki/Information_retrieval_applications" title="Information retrieval applications">application</a> the data objects may be, for example, text documents, images,<sup id="cite_ref-goodron2000_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-goodron2000-2">[2]</a></sup> audio,<sup id="cite_ref-Foote99_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Foote99-3">[3]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Mind_maps" class="mw-redirect" title="Mind maps">mind maps</a><sup id="cite_ref-Beel2009_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Beel2009-4">[4]</a></sup> or videos. Often the documents themselves are not kept or stored directly in the IR system, but are instead represented in the system by document surrogates or <a href="/wiki/Metadata" title="Metadata">metadata</a>.</p>
<p>Most IR systems compute a numeric score on how well each object in the database matches the query, and rank the objects according to this value. The top ranking objects are then shown to the user. The process may then be iterated if the user wishes to refine the query.<sup id="cite_ref-Frakes1992_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Frakes1992-5">[5]</a></sup></p>

<p>The idea of using computers to search for relevant pieces of information was popularized in the article <i><a href="/wiki/As_We_May_Think" title="As We May Think">As We May Think</a></i> by <a href="/wiki/Vannevar_Bush" title="Vannevar Bush">Vannevar Bush</a> in 1945.<sup id="cite_ref-Singhal2001_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Singhal2001-6">[6]</a></sup> It would appear that Bush was inspired by patents for a 'statistical machine' - filed by <a href="/wiki/Emanuel_Goldberg" title="Emanuel Goldberg">Emanuel Goldberg</a> in the 1920s and '30s - that searched for documents stored on film.<sup id="cite_ref-Sanderson2012_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sanderson2012-7">[7]</a></sup> The first description of a computer searching for information was described by Holmstrom in 1948,<sup id="cite_ref-Holmstrom1948_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Holmstrom1948-8">[8]</a></sup> detailing an early mention of the <a href="/wiki/Univac" class="mw-redirect" title="Univac">Univac</a> computer. Automated information retrieval systems were introduced in the 1950s: one even featured in the 1957 romantic comedy, <a href="/wiki/Desk_Set" title="Desk Set">Desk Set</a>. In the 1960s, the first large information retrieval research group was formed by <a href="/wiki/Gerard_Salton" title="Gerard Salton">Gerard Salton</a> at Cornell. By the 1970s several different retrieval techniques had been shown to perform well on small <a href="/wiki/Text_corpora" class="mw-redirect" title="Text corpora">text corpora</a> such as the Cranfield collection (several thousand documents).<sup id="cite_ref-Singhal2001_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Singhal2001-6">[6]</a></sup> Large-scale retrieval systems, such as the Lockheed Dialog system, came into use early in the 1970s.</p>
<p>In 1992, the US Department of Defense along with the <a href="/wiki/National_Institute_of_Standards_and_Technology" title="National Institute of Standards and Technology">National Institute of Standards and Technology</a> (NIST), cosponsored the <a href="/wiki/Text_Retrieval_Conference" title="Text Retrieval Conference">Text Retrieval Conference</a> (TREC) as part of the TIPSTER text program. The aim of this was to look into the information retrieval community by supplying the infrastructure that was needed for evaluation of text retrieval methodologies on a very large text collection. This catalyzed research on methods that <a href="/wiki/Scalability" title="Scalability">scale</a> to huge corpora. The introduction of <a href="/wiki/Web_search_engine" title="Web search engine">web search engines</a> has boosted the need for very large scale retrieval systems even further.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Model_types">Model types</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Information_retrieval&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Model types">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>For effectively retrieving relevant documents by IR strategies, the documents are typically transformed into a suitable representation. Each retrieval strategy incorporates a specific model for its document representation purposes. The picture on the right illustrates the relationship of some common models. In the picture, the models are categorized according to two dimensions: the mathematical basis and the properties of the model.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="First_dimension:_mathematical_basis">First dimension: mathematical basis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Information_retrieval&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: First dimension: mathematical basis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul>
<li><i>Set-theoretic</i> models represent documents as sets of words or phrases. Similarities are usually derived from set-theoretic operations on those sets. Common models are:
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Standard_Boolean_model" title="Standard Boolean model">Standard Boolean model</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Extended_Boolean_model" title="Extended Boolean model">Extended Boolean model</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Fuzzy_retrieval" title="Fuzzy retrieval">Fuzzy retrieval</a></li>
</ul>
</li>
<li><i>Algebraic models</i> represent documents and queries usually as vectors, matrices, or tuples. The similarity of the query vector and document vector is represented as a scalar value.
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Vector_space_model" title="Vector space model">Vector space model</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Generalized_vector_space_model" title="Generalized vector space model">Generalized vector space model</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Topic-based_vector_space_model" title="Topic-based vector space model">(Enhanced) Topic-based Vector Space Model</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Extended_Boolean_model" title="Extended Boolean model">Extended Boolean model</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Latent_semantic_indexing" class="mw-redirect" title="Latent semantic indexing">Latent semantic indexing</a> a.k.a. <a href="/wiki/Latent_semantic_analysis" title="Latent semantic analysis">latent semantic analysis</a></li>
</ul>
</li>
<li><i>Probabilistic models</i> treat the process of document retrieval as a probabilistic inference. Similarities are computed as probabilities that a document is relevant for a given query. Probabilistic theorems like the <a href="/wiki/Bayes%27_theorem" title="Bayes' theorem">Bayes' theorem</a> are often used in these models.
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Binary_Independence_Model" title="Binary Independence Model">Binary Independence Model</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Probabilistic_relevance_model" title="Probabilistic relevance model">Probabilistic relevance model</a> on which is based the <a href="/wiki/Probabilistic_relevance_model_(BM25)" class="mw-redirect" title="Probabilistic relevance model (BM25)">okapi (BM25)</a> relevance function</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Uncertain_inference" title="Uncertain inference">Uncertain inference</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Language_model" title="Language model">Language models</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Divergence-from-randomness_model" title="Divergence-from-randomness model">Divergence-from-randomness model</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Latent_Dirichlet_allocation" title="Latent Dirichlet allocation">Latent Dirichlet allocation</a></li>
</ul>
</li>
<li><i>Feature-based retrieval models</i> view documents as vectors of values of <i>feature functions</i> (or just <i>features</i>) and seek the best way to combine these features into a single relevance score, typically by <a href="/wiki/Learning_to_rank" title="Learning to rank">learning to rank</a> methods. Feature functions are arbitrary functions of document and query, and as such can easily incorporate almost any other retrieval model as just another feature.</li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Second_dimension:_properties_of_the_model">Second dimension: properties of the model</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Information_retrieval&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Second dimension: properties of the model">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul>
<li><i>Models without term-interdependencies</i> treat different terms/words as independent. This fact is usually represented in vector space models by the <a href="/wiki/Orthogonality" title="Orthogonality">orthogonality</a> assumption of term vectors or in probabilistic models by an <a href="/wiki/Independence_(mathematical_logic)" title="Independence (mathematical logic)">independency</a> assumption for term variables.</li>
<li><i>Models with immanent term interdependencies</i> allow a representation of interdependencies between terms. However the degree of the interdependency between two terms is defined by the model itself. It is usually directly or indirectly derived (e.g. by <a href="/wiki/Dimension_reduction" class="mw-redirect" title="Dimension reduction">dimensional reduction</a>) from the <a href="/wiki/Co-occurrence" title="Co-occurrence">co-occurrence</a> of those terms in the whole set of documents.</li>
<li><i>Models with transcendent term interdependencies</i> allow a representation of interdependencies between terms, but they do not allege how the interdependency between two terms is defined. They rely an external source for the degree of interdependency between two terms. (For example, a human or sophisticated algorithms.)</li>
</ul>

<p><br />
The <b>evaluation of an information retrieval system</b> is the process of assessing how well a system meets the information needs of its users. Traditional evaluation metrics, designed for <a href="/wiki/Standard_Boolean_model" title="Standard Boolean model">Boolean retrieval</a> or top-k retrieval, include <a href="/wiki/Precision_and_recall" title="Precision and recall">precision and recall</a>. Many more measures for evaluating the performance of information retrieval systems have also been proposed. In general, measurement considers a collection of documents to be searched and a search query. All common measures described here assume a <a href="/wiki/Ground_truth" title="Ground truth">ground truth</a> notion of relevancy: every document is known to be either relevant or non-relevant to a particular query. In practice, queries may be <a href="/wiki/Ill-posed" class="mw-redirect" title="Ill-posed">ill-posed</a> and there may be different shades of relevancy.</p>
<p>Virtually all modern evaluation metrics (e.g., <a href="/wiki/Information_retrieval#Mean_average_precision" title="Information retrieval">mean average precision</a>, <a href="/wiki/Information_retrieval#Discounted_cumulative_gain" title="Information retrieval">discounted cumulative gain</a>) are designed for <b>ranked retrieval</b> without any explicit rank cutoff, taking into account the relative order of the documents retrieved by the search engines and giving more weight to documents returned at higher ranks.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>

<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Precision_and_recall" title="Precision and recall">Precision and recall</a></div>

<p>Note that the meaning and usage of "precision" in the field of information retrieval differs from the definition of <a href="/wiki/Accuracy_and_precision" title="Accuracy and precision">accuracy and precision</a> within other branches of science and <a href="/wiki/Statistics" title="Statistics">statistics</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Recall">Recall</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Information_retrieval&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Recall">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Precision_and_recall" title="Precision and recall">Precision and recall</a></div>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Precision_at_K">Precision at K</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Information_retrieval&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Precision at K">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>For modern (Web-scale) information retrieval, recall is no longer a meaningful metric, as many queries have thousands of relevant documents, and few users will be interested in reading all of them. <a href="/wiki/Precision_and_recall#Precision" title="Precision and recall">Precision</a> at k documents (P@k) is still a useful metric (e.g., P@10 or "Precision at 10" corresponds to the number of relevant results on the first search results page), but fails to take into account the positions of the relevant documents among the top k.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Another shortcoming is that on a query with fewer relevant results than k, even a perfect system will have a score less than 1.<sup id="cite_ref-stanford_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-stanford-14">[14]</a></sup> It is easier to score manually since only the top k results need to be examined to determine if they are relevant or not.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Timeline">Timeline</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Information_retrieval&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Timeline">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul>
<li>Before the <b>1900s</b>
<dl>
<dd><b>1801</b>: <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Marie_Jacquard" title="Joseph Marie Jacquard">Joseph Marie Jacquard</a> invents the <a href="/wiki/Jacquard_loom" title="Jacquard loom">Jacquard loom</a>, the first machine to use punched cards to control a sequence of operations.</dd>
<dd><b>1880s</b>: <a href="/wiki/Herman_Hollerith" title="Herman Hollerith">Herman Hollerith</a> invents an electro-mechanical data tabulator using punch cards as a machine readable medium.</dd>
<dd><b>1890</b> Hollerith <a href="/wiki/Punched_cards" class="mw-redirect" title="Punched cards">cards</a>, <a href="/wiki/Keypunch" title="Keypunch">keypunches</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tabulating_machine" title="Tabulating machine">tabulators</a> used to process the <a href="/wiki/1890_US_Census" class="mw-redirect" title="1890 US Census">1890 US Census</a> data.</dd>
</dl>
</li>
<li><b>1920s-1930s</b>
<dl>
<dd><a href="/wiki/Emanuel_Goldberg" title="Emanuel Goldberg">Emanuel Goldberg</a> submits patents for his "Statistical Machine” a document search engine that used photoelectric cells and pattern recognition to search the metadata on rolls of microfilmed documents.</dd>
</dl>
</li>
<li><b>1940s–1950s</b>
<dl>
<dd><b>late 1940s</b>: The US military confronted problems of indexing and retrieval of wartime scientific research documents captured from Germans.
<dl>
<dd><b>1945</b>: <a href="/wiki/Vannevar_Bush" title="Vannevar Bush">Vannevar Bush</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/As_We_May_Think" title="As We May Think">As We May Think</a></i> appeared in <i><a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Monthly" class="mw-redirect" title="Atlantic Monthly">Atlantic Monthly</a></i>.</dd>
<dd><b>1947</b>: <a href="/wiki/Hans_Peter_Luhn" title="Hans Peter Luhn">Hans Peter Luhn</a> (research engineer at IBM since 1941) began work on a mechanized punch card-based system for searching chemical compounds.</dd>
</dl>
</dd>
<dd><b>1950s</b>: Growing concern in the US for a "science gap" with the USSR motivated, encouraged funding and provided a backdrop for mechanized literature searching systems (<a href="/wiki/Allen_Kent" title="Allen Kent">Allen Kent</a> <i>et al.</i>) and the invention of citation indexing (<a href="/wiki/Eugene_Garfield" title="Eugene Garfield">Eugene Garfield</a>).</dd>
<dd><b>1950</b>: The term "information retrieval" was coined by <a href="/wiki/Calvin_Mooers" title="Calvin Mooers">Calvin Mooers</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></dd>
<dd><b>1951</b>: Philip Bagley conducted the earliest experiment in computerized document retrieval in a master thesis at <a href="/wiki/MIT" class="mw-redirect" title="MIT">MIT</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Doyle1975_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Doyle1975-20">[20]</a></sup></dd>
<dd><b>1955</b>: Allen Kent joined <a href="/wiki/Case_Western_Reserve_University" title="Case Western Reserve University">Case Western Reserve University</a>, and eventually became associate director of the Center for Documentation and Communications Research. That same year, Kent and colleagues published a paper in American Documentation describing the precision and recall measures as well as detailing a proposed "framework" for evaluating an IR system which included statistical sampling methods for determining the number of relevant documents not retrieved.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></dd>
<dd><b>1958</b>: International Conference on Scientific Information Washington DC included consideration of IR systems as a solution to problems identified. See: <i>Proceedings of the International Conference on Scientific Information, 1958</i> (National Academy of Sciences, Washington, DC, 1959)</dd>
<dd><b>1959</b>: <a href="/wiki/Hans_Peter_Luhn" title="Hans Peter Luhn">Hans Peter Luhn</a> published "Auto-encoding of documents for information retrieval."</dd>
</dl>
</li>
<li><b>1960s</b>:
<dl>
<dd><b>early 1960s</b>: <a href="/wiki/Gerard_Salton" title="Gerard Salton">Gerard Salton</a> began work on IR at Harvard, later moved to Cornell.</dd>
<dd><b>1960</b>: <a href="/wiki/Melvin_Earl_Maron" title="Melvin Earl Maron">Melvin Earl Maron</a> and John Lary Kuhns<sup id="cite_ref-Maron2008_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Maron2008-22">[22]</a></sup> published "On relevance, probabilistic indexing, and information retrieval" in the Journal of the ACM 7(3):216–244, July 1960.</dd>
<dd><b>1962</b>:
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Cyril_W._Cleverdon" class="mw-redirect" title="Cyril W. Cleverdon">Cyril W. Cleverdon</a> published early findings of the Cranfield studies, developing a model for IR system evaluation. See: Cyril W. Cleverdon, "Report on the Testing and Analysis of an Investigation into the Comparative Efficiency of Indexing Systems". Cranfield Collection of Aeronautics, Cranfield, England, 1962.</li>
<li>Kent published <i>Information Analysis and Retrieval</i>.</li>
</ul>
</dd>
<dd><b>1963</b>:
<ul>
<li>Weinberg report "Science, Government and Information" gave a full articulation of the idea of a "crisis of scientific information." The report was named after Dr. <a href="/wiki/Alvin_Weinberg" class="mw-redirect" title="Alvin Weinberg">Alvin Weinberg</a>.</li>
<li>Joseph Becker and <a href="/wiki/Robert_M._Hayes" title="Robert M. Hayes">Robert M. Hayes</a> published text on information retrieval. Becker, Joseph; Hayes, Robert Mayo. <i>Information storage and retrieval: tools, elements, theories</i>. New York, Wiley (1963).</li>
</ul>
</dd>
<dd><b>1964</b>:
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Karen_Sp%C3%A4rck_Jones" title="Karen Spärck Jones">Karen Spärck Jones</a> finished her thesis at Cambridge, <i>Synonymy and Semantic Classification</i>, and continued work on <a href="/wiki/Computational_linguistics" title="Computational linguistics">computational linguistics</a> as it applies to IR.</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/National_Bureau_of_Standards" class="mw-redirect" title="National Bureau of Standards">National Bureau of Standards</a> sponsored a symposium titled "Statistical Association Methods for Mechanized Documentation." Several highly significant papers, including G. Salton's first published reference (we believe) to the <a href="/wiki/SMART_Information_Retrieval_System" title="SMART Information Retrieval System">SMART</a> system.</li>
</ul>
</dd>
<dd><b>mid-1960s</b>:
<dl>
<dd>
<ul>
<li>National Library of Medicine developed <a href="/wiki/MEDLARS" class="mw-redirect" title="MEDLARS">MEDLARS</a> Medical Literature Analysis and Retrieval System, the first major machine-readable database and batch-retrieval system.</li>
<li>Project Intrex at MIT.</li>
</ul>
</dd>
<dd><b>1965</b>: <a href="/wiki/J._C._R._Licklider" title="J. C. R. Licklider">J. C. R. Licklider</a> published <i>Libraries of the Future</i>.</dd>
<dd><b>1966</b>: <a href="/wiki/Don_Swanson" class="mw-redirect" title="Don Swanson">Don Swanson</a> was involved in studies at University of Chicago on Requirements for Future Catalogs.</dd>
</dl>
</dd>
<dd><b>late 1960s</b>: <a href="/wiki/F._Wilfrid_Lancaster" class="mw-redirect" title="F. Wilfrid Lancaster">F. Wilfrid Lancaster</a> completed evaluation studies of the MEDLARS system and published the first edition of his text on information retrieval.
<dl>
<dd><b>1968</b>:</dd>
</dl>
<ul>
<li>Gerard Salton published <i>Automatic Information Organization and Retrieval</i>.</li>
<li>John W. Sammon, Jr.'s RADC Tech report "Some Mathematics of Information Storage and Retrieval..." outlined the vector model.</li>
</ul>
<dl>
<dd><b>1969</b>: Sammon's "A nonlinear mapping for data structure analysis" (IEEE Transactions on Computers) was the first proposal for visualization interface to an IR system.</dd>
</dl>
</dd>
</dl>
</li>
<li><b>1970s</b>
<dl>
<dd><b>early 1970s</b>:
<dl>
<dd>
<ul>
<li>First online systems—NLM's AIM-TWX, MEDLINE; Lockheed's Dialog; SDC's ORBIT.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Theodor_Nelson" class="mw-redirect" title="Theodor Nelson">Theodor Nelson</a> promoting concept of <a href="/wiki/Hypertext" title="Hypertext">hypertext</a>, published <i>Computer Lib/Dream Machines</i>.</li>
</ul>
</dd>
</dl>
</dd>
<dd><b>1971</b>: <a href="/wiki/Nicholas_Jardine" class="mw-redirect" title="Nicholas Jardine">Nicholas Jardine</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cornelis_J._van_Rijsbergen" class="mw-redirect" title="Cornelis J. van Rijsbergen">Cornelis J. van Rijsbergen</a> published "The use of hierarchic clustering in information retrieval", which articulated the "cluster hypothesis."<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></dd>
<dd><b>1975</b>: Three highly influential publications by Salton fully articulated his vector processing framework and term discrimination model:
<dl>
<dd>
<ul>
<li><i>A Theory of Indexing</i> (Society for Industrial and Applied Mathematics)</li>
<li><i>A Theory of Term Importance in Automatic Text Analysis</i> (<a href="/wiki/JASIS" class="mw-redirect" title="JASIS">JASIS</a> v. 26)</li>
<li><i>A Vector Space Model for Automatic Indexing</i> (<a href="/wiki/Communications_of_the_ACM" title="Communications of the ACM">CACM</a> 18:11)</li>
</ul>
</dd>
</dl>
</dd>
<dd><b>1978</b>: The First <a href="/wiki/Association_for_Computing_Machinery" title="Association for Computing Machinery">ACM</a> <a href="/wiki/Special_Interest_Group_on_Information_Retrieval" title="Special Interest Group on Information Retrieval">SIGIR</a> conference.</dd>
<dd><b>1979</b>: C. J. van Rijsbergen published <i>Information Retrieval</i> (Butterworths). Heavy emphasis on probabilistic models.</dd>
<dd><b>1979</b>: Tamas Doszkocs implemented the CITE natural language user interface for MEDLINE at the National Library of Medicine. The CITE system supported free form query input, ranked output and relevance feedback.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></dd>
</dl>
</li>
<li><b>1980s</b>
<dl>
<dd><b>1980</b>: First international ACM SIGIR conference, joint with British Computer Society IR group in Cambridge.</dd>
<dd><b>1982</b>: <a href="/wiki/Nicholas_J._Belkin" title="Nicholas J. Belkin">Nicholas J. Belkin</a>, Robert N. Oddy, and Helen M. Brooks proposed the ASK (Anomalous State of Knowledge) viewpoint for information retrieval. This was an important concept, though their automated analysis tool proved ultimately disappointing.</dd>
<dd><b>1983</b>: Salton (and Michael J. McGill) published <i>Introduction to Modern Information Retrieval</i> (McGraw-Hill), with heavy emphasis on vector models.</dd>
<dd><b>1985</b>: David Blair and <a href="/wiki/Bill_Maron" class="mw-redirect" title="Bill Maron">Bill Maron</a> publish: An Evaluation of Retrieval Effectiveness for a Full-Text Document-Retrieval System</dd>
<dd><b>mid-1980s</b>: Efforts to develop end-user versions of commercial IR systems.
<dl>
<dd><b>1985–1993</b>: Key papers on and experimental systems for visualization interfaces.</dd>
<dd>Work by <a href="/w/index.php?title=Donald_B._Crouch&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Donald B. Crouch (page does not exist)">Donald B. Crouch</a>, <a href="/wiki/Robert_R._Korfhage" title="Robert R. Korfhage">Robert R. Korfhage</a>, Matthew Chalmers, Anselm Spoerri and others.</dd>
</dl>
</dd>
<dd><b>1989</b>: First <a href="/wiki/World_Wide_Web" title="World Wide Web">World Wide Web</a> proposals by <a href="/wiki/Tim_Berners-Lee" title="Tim Berners-Lee">Tim Berners-Lee</a> at <a href="/wiki/CERN" title="CERN">CERN</a>.</dd>
</dl>
</li>
<li><b>1990s</b>
<dl>
<dd><b>1992</b>: First <a href="/wiki/Text_Retrieval_Conference" title="Text Retrieval Conference">TREC</a> conference.</dd>
<dd><b>1997</b>: Publication of <a href="/wiki/Robert_R._Korfhage" title="Robert R. Korfhage">Korfhage</a>'s <i>Information Storage and Retrieval</i><sup id="cite_ref-Korfhage1997_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Korfhage1997-25">[25]</a></sup> with emphasis on visualization and multi-reference point systems.</dd>
<dd><b>late 1990s</b>: <a href="/wiki/Web_search_engine" title="Web search engine">Web search engines</a> implementation of many features formerly found only in experimental IR systems. Search engines become the most common and maybe best instantiation of IR models.</dd>
</dl>
</li>
</ul>
