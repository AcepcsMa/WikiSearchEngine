<p>The <b>Nuremberg Rally</b> (officially <i><span class="unicode haudio"><span class="fn"><span style="white-space:nowrap"><a href="/wiki/File:De-Reichsparteitag.ogg" title="About this sound"><img alt="About this sound" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/11px-Loudspeaker.svg.png" width="11" height="11" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/17px-Loudspeaker.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/22px-Loudspeaker.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="20" data-file-height="20" /></a>&#160;</span><a href="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/0/0e/De-Reichsparteitag.ogg" class="internal" title="De-Reichsparteitag.ogg">Reichsparteitag</a></span>&#160;<small class="metadata audiolinkinfo" style="cursor:help;">(<a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Media_help" title="Wikipedia:Media help"><span style="cursor:help;">help</span></a>·<a href="/wiki/File:De-Reichsparteitag.ogg" title="File:De-Reichsparteitag.ogg"><span style="cursor:help;">info</span></a>)</small></span></i>, meaning <i><a href="/wiki/Nazi_Germany" title="Nazi Germany">Imperial</a> Party Convention</i>) was the annual rally of the <a href="/wiki/Nazi_Party" title="Nazi Party">Nazi Party</a> in <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a>, held from 1923 to 1938.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> They were large <a href="/wiki/Nazi_propaganda" class="mw-redirect" title="Nazi propaganda">Nazi propaganda</a> events, especially after <a href="/wiki/Adolf_Hitler%27s_rise_to_power" title="Adolf Hitler's rise to power">Adolf Hitler's rise to power</a> in 1933. These events were held at the <a href="/wiki/Nazi_party_rally_grounds" title="Nazi party rally grounds">Nazi party rally grounds</a> in <a href="/wiki/Nuremberg" title="Nuremberg">Nuremberg</a> from 1933 to 1938 and are usually referred to in English as the "Nuremberg Rallies". Many films were made to commemorate them, the most famous of which is <a href="/wiki/Leni_Riefenstahl" title="Leni Riefenstahl">Leni Riefenstahl</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Triumph_of_the_Will" title="Triumph of the Will">Triumph of the Will</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/The_Victory_of_Faith" title="The Victory of Faith">Der Sieg des Glaubens</a></i>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History_and_purpose">History and purpose</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nuremberg_Rally&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History and purpose">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The first Nazi Party rallies took place in 1923 in <a href="/wiki/Munich" title="Munich">Munich</a> and in 1926 in <a href="/wiki/Weimar" title="Weimar">Weimar</a>. From 1927 on, they took place exclusively in <a href="/wiki/Nuremberg" title="Nuremberg">Nuremberg</a>. The Party selected Nuremberg for pragmatic reasons: it was in the center of the <a href="/wiki/German_Reich" title="German Reich">German Reich</a> and the local <i><a href="/wiki/Nazi_party_rally_grounds#Luitpoldarena" title="Nazi party rally grounds">Luitpoldhain</a></i> (converted parkland) was well suited as a venue. In addition, the Nazis could rely on the well-organized local branch of the party in <a href="/wiki/Franconia" title="Franconia">Franconia</a>, then led by <i><a href="/wiki/Gauleiter" title="Gauleiter">Gauleiter</a></i> <a href="/wiki/Julius_Streicher" title="Julius Streicher">Julius Streicher</a>. The Nuremberg police were sympathetic to the event.</p>
<p>Later, the location was justified by the Nazi Party by putting it into the tradition of the <a href="/wiki/Imperial_Diet_(Holy_Roman_Empire)" title="Imperial Diet (Holy Roman Empire)">Imperial Diet</a> (German <i>Reichstag</i>) of the <a href="/wiki/Holy_Roman_Empire" title="Holy Roman Empire">Holy Roman Empire</a>, considered the First <i>Reich</i>. After 1933, the rallies took place near the time of the <a href="/wiki/Autumnal_equinox" class="mw-redirect" title="Autumnal equinox">Autumnal equinox</a>, under the title of "The German people's National Party days" (<i>Reichsparteitage des deutschen Volkes</i>), which was intended to symbolize the solidarity between the German people and the Nazi Party. This point was further emphasized by the yearly growing number of participants, which finally reached over half a million from all sections of the party, the army, and the state.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Rallies">Rallies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nuremberg_Rally&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Rallies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Each rally was given a programmatic title, which related to recent national events:</p>
<ul>
<li>1923 – The First Party Congress took place in Munich on January 27, 1923.</li>
<li>1923 – The "German day rally" was held in Nuremberg, September 1-2, 1923.</li>
<li>1926 – The 2nd Party Congress ("Refounding Congress") was held in Weimar, July 3-4, 1926.</li>
<li>1927 – The 3rd Party Congress ("Day of Awakening") was held in Nuremberg, August 19-21, 1927. The propaganda film <i><a href="/wiki/Eine_Symphonie_des_Kampfwillens" title="Eine Symphonie des Kampfwillens">Eine Symphonie des Kampfwillens</a></i> was made at this rally.</li>
<li>1929 – The 4th Party Congress, known as the "Day of Composure", was held in Nuremberg, August 1-4, 1929. The propaganda film <i><a href="/wiki/Der_N%C3%BCrnberger_Parteitag_der_NSDAP" title="Der Nürnberger Parteitag der NSDAP">Der Nürnberger Parteitag der NSDAP</a></i> was made at this rally.</li>
<li>1933 – The 5th Party Congress was held in Nuremberg, August 30 – September 3, 1933. It was called the "Rally of Victory" (<i>Reichsparteitag des Sieges</i>). The term "victory" relates to the <a href="/wiki/Machtergreifung" class="mw-redirect" title="Machtergreifung">Nazi seizure of power</a> and the victory over the <a href="/wiki/Weimar_Republic" title="Weimar Republic">Weimar Republic</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Leni_Riefenstahl" title="Leni Riefenstahl">Leni Riefenstahl</a> film <i><a href="/wiki/Der_Sieg_des_Glaubens" class="mw-redirect" title="Der Sieg des Glaubens">Der Sieg des Glaubens</a></i> was made at this rally. Hitler announced that from now on all Rallies would take place in Nuremberg.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></li>
<li>1934 – The 6th Party Congress was held in Nuremberg, September 5–10, 1934, which was attended by about 700,000 Nazi Party supporters. Initially it did not have a theme. Later it was labeled the "Rally of Unity and Strength" (<i>Reichsparteitag der Einheit und Stärke</i>), "Rally of Power" (<i>Reichsparteitag der Macht</i>), or "Rally of Will" (<i>Reichsparteitag des Willens</i>). The Leni Riefenstahl film <i><a href="/wiki/Triumph_of_the_Will" title="Triumph of the Will">Triumph des Willens</a></i> was made at this rally.<sup id="cite_ref-IMDB_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IMDB-3">[3]</a></sup> This rally was particularly notable due to <a href="/wiki/Albert_Speer" title="Albert Speer">Albert Speer</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Cathedral_of_light" title="Cathedral of light">Cathedral of light</a>: 152 searchlights that cast vertical beams into the sky around the <a href="/wiki/Nazi_party_rally_grounds#Zeppelin_Field" title="Nazi party rally grounds">Zeppelin Field</a> to symbolise the walls of a building<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></li>
<li>1935 – The 7th Party Congress was held in Nuremberg, September 10–16, 1935. It was called the "Rally of Freedom" (<i>Reichsparteitag der Freiheit</i>). "Freedom" referred to the reintroduction of compulsory military service and thus the German "liberation" from the <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Versailles" title="Treaty of Versailles">Treaty of Versailles</a>. Leni Riefenstahl made the film <i><a href="/wiki/Tag_der_Freiheit:_Unsere_Wehrmacht" title="Tag der Freiheit: Unsere Wehrmacht">Tag der Freiheit: Unsere Wehrmacht</a></i> at this rally, and the Nazis introduced the <a href="/wiki/Nuremberg_Laws" title="Nuremberg Laws">Nuremberg Laws</a>.</li>
<li>1936 – The 8th Party Congress was known as the "Rally of Honour" (<i>Reichsparteitag der Ehre</i>, September 8–14). The <a href="/wiki/Remilitarization_of_the_Rhineland" title="Remilitarization of the Rhineland">remilitarization</a> of the demilitarized <a href="/wiki/Rhineland" title="Rhineland">Rhineland</a> in March 1936 constituted the restoration of German honour in the eyes of many Germans. The film <i><a href="/wiki/Festliches_N%C3%BCrnberg" title="Festliches Nürnberg">Festliches Nürnberg</a></i> incorporated footage shot at this rally, as well as the rally of 1937.</li>
<li>1937 – The 9th Party Congress was called the "Rally of Labour" (<i>Reichsparteitag der Arbeit</i>, September 6–13). It celebrated the reduction of unemployment in Germany since the Nazi rise to power.</li>
<li>1938 – The 10th Party Congress was named the "Rally of Greater Germany" (<i>Reichsparteitag Großdeutschland</i>, September 5–12).<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> This was due to the <a href="/wiki/Anschluss" title="Anschluss">annexation</a> of <a href="/wiki/Austria" title="Austria">Austria</a> to Germany that had taken place earlier in the year.</li>
<li>1939 – The 11th Party Congress, scheduled for September 2-11, was given the name "Rally of Peace" (<i>Reichsparteitag des Friedens</i>). It was meant to reiterate the German desire for peace, both to the German population and to other countries. It was cancelled at short notice, as one day before the planned date, on September 1, Germany began its <a href="/wiki/German_invasion_of_Poland" class="mw-redirect" title="German invasion of Poland">offensive against Poland</a> (which ignited <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>).</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Propaganda_films">Propaganda films</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nuremberg_Rally&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Propaganda films">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Official films for the rallies began in 1927, with the establishment of the <a href="/wiki/NSDAP" class="mw-redirect" title="NSDAP">NSDAP</a> (Nazi Party) film office. The most famous films were made by <a href="/wiki/Leni_Riefenstahl" title="Leni Riefenstahl">Leni Riefenstahl</a> for the rallies between 1933 and 1935. Relating to the theme of the rally, she called her first movie <i>Victory of Faith</i> (<i><a href="/wiki/Der_Sieg_des_Glaubens" class="mw-redirect" title="Der Sieg des Glaubens">Der Sieg des Glaubens</a></i>). This movie was taken out of circulation after the <a href="/wiki/Night_of_the_Long_Knives" title="Night of the Long Knives">Night of the Long Knives</a>, although a copy survived in Britain and has recently been made available on the <a href="/wiki/Internet_Archive" title="Internet Archive">Internet Archive</a> for public viewing. The rally of 1934 became the setting for the award-winning <i><a href="/wiki/Triumph_of_the_Will" title="Triumph of the Will">Triumph of the Will</a></i> (<i>Triumph des Willens</i>). Several generals in the <i><a href="/wiki/Wehrmacht" title="Wehrmacht">Wehrmacht</a></i> protested over the minimal army presence in the film: Hitler apparently proposed modifying the film to placate the generals, but Riefenstahl refused his suggestion. She did agree to return to the 1935 rally and make a film exclusively about the Wehrmacht, which became <i><a href="/wiki/Tag_der_Freiheit:_Unsere_Wehrmacht" title="Tag der Freiheit: Unsere Wehrmacht">Tag der Freiheit: Unsere Wehrmacht</a></i>.</p>
<p>The rallies for 1936 and 1937 were covered in <i><a href="/wiki/Festliches_N%C3%BCrnberg" title="Festliches Nürnberg">Festliches Nürnberg</a></i>, which was shorter than the others, only 21 minutes.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Books">Books</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nuremberg_Rally&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Books">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>There were two sets of official or semi-official books covering the rallies. The "red books" were officially published by the NSDAP and contained the proceedings of the "congress" as well as full texts of every speech given in chronological order.</p>
<p>The "blue books" were published initially by <a href="/wiki/Julius_Streicher" title="Julius Streicher">Julius Streicher</a>, the <i><a href="/wiki/Gauleiter" title="Gauleiter">Gauleiter</a></i> of <a href="/wiki/Nuremberg" title="Nuremberg">Nuremberg</a>, later by <a href="/wiki/Hanns_Kerrl" title="Hanns Kerrl">Hanns Kerrl</a>, not by the party press. These were larger scale books that included the text of speeches and proceedings, as well as larger photographs.</p>
<p>In addition to these, collections of Heinrich Hoffman's photographs were published to commemorate each Party congress, as well as pamphlets of Hitler's speeches. Both series of books are much sought after collectors items.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
