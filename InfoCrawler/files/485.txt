			<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Deutscher Wetterdienst</h1>

<p>The <b><span lang="de" xml:lang="de">Deutscher Wetterdienst</span></b>, <small>German pronunciation:</small> <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Standard_German" title="Help:IPA/Standard German">[ˌdɔʏ̯ʧɐ ˈvɛtɐdiːnst]</a></span>, or <b>DWD</b> for short, is the <b>German Meteorological Office</b>, based in <a href="/wiki/Offenbach_am_Main" title="Offenbach am Main">Offenbach am Main</a>, Germany, which monitors <a href="/wiki/Weather" title="Weather">weather</a> and <a href="/wiki/Meteorology" title="Meteorology">meteorological</a> conditions over <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a> and provides weather services for the general public and for nautical, aviational or agricultural purposes. It is attached to the <a href="/wiki/Federal_Ministry_of_Transport_and_Digital_Infrastructure" title="Federal Ministry of Transport and Digital Infrastructure">Federal Ministry of Transport and Digital Infrastructure</a>. The DWDs principal tasks include warning against weather-related dangers and monitoring and rating climate changes affecting Germany. The organization runs <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_models" title="Atmospheric models">atmospheric models</a> on their <a href="/wiki/Supercomputer" title="Supercomputer">supercomputer</a> for precise <a href="/wiki/Weather_forecasting" title="Weather forecasting">weather forecasting</a>. The DWD also manages the national climate archive<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> and one of the largest specialized libraries on weather and climate worldwide.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Deutscher_Wetterdienst&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The DWD was formed in 1952 when the weather services of the western occupation zones were merged. In 1954, the Federal Republic of Germany joined the <a href="/wiki/World_Meteorological_Organization" title="World Meteorological Organization">World Meteorological Organization</a> (WMO). In 1975 the <a href="/wiki/European_Centre_for_Medium-Range_Weather_Forecasts" title="European Centre for Medium-Range Weather Forecasts">European Centre for Medium-Range Weather Forecasts</a> (ECMWF) was formed for <a href="/wiki/Numerical_weather_prediction" title="Numerical weather prediction">numerical weather prediction</a> up to ten days in advance. In 1990, following the <a href="/wiki/German_reunification" title="German reunification">reunification</a>, the weather services of the <a href="/wiki/East_Germany" title="East Germany">German Democratic Republic</a> were incorporated in the DWD.</p>
<p>Since the 1990s, the DWD has continuously reduced the number of manned weather stations, which entailed substantial staff cutbacks. The DWD does not expect a reduction in forecast quality, given techniques like weather radar or satellites, which have significantly improved weather data collection.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Numerical_weather_prediction">Numerical weather prediction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Deutscher_Wetterdienst&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Numerical weather prediction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/History_of_numerical_weather_prediction" title="History of numerical weather prediction">History of numerical weather prediction</a> and <a href="/wiki/Numerical_weather_prediction" title="Numerical weather prediction">Numerical weather prediction</a></div>
<p>The German Meteorological Office runs a global hydrostatic model of its own, <a href="/wiki/GME_of_Deutscher_Wetterdienst" title="GME of Deutscher Wetterdienst">the GME</a>, using a <a href="/wiki/Hexagon" title="Hexagon">hexagonal</a> <a href="/wiki/Icosahedron" title="Icosahedron">icosohedral</a> grid since 2002.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> They developed the High Resolution Regional Model (HRM) in 1999, which is widely run within the operational and research meteorological communities and run with hydrostatic assumptions.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> The German non-hydrostatic Lokal-Modell for Europe (LME) has been run since 2002, and an increase in areal domain became operational on September 28, 2005.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Since March 2009, the DWD operates a <a href="/wiki/NEC_SX-9" title="NEC SX-9">NEC SX-9</a> with a peak performance of 109 <a href="/wiki/FLOPS" title="FLOPS">teraFLOPS</a> to help in the weather forecasting process.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Public_Services">Public Services</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Deutscher_Wetterdienst&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Public Services">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Since 2005, the DWD has been publishing regional warnings against heat with the aim to reduce heat related fatalities. This decision was made because of the hot summer in 2003, when estimated 7000 people died from direct or indirect effects of the heat. Additionally it sends out sea weather reports as <a href="/wiki/Radioteletype" title="Radioteletype">radioteletype</a> and faxes. Since 2006, the pollen warnings can be subscribed to for free on the DWD web site. Within its duty of primary meteorological information, the DWD offers a free daily weather report for Germany which can be subscribed to by email on their official website. DWD offers free access to its climate data.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Structure">Structure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Deutscher_Wetterdienst&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Structure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <span lang="de" xml:lang="de">Deutsche Wetterdienst</span> is attached to the <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.bmvi.de">Federal Ministry of Transport and Digital Infrastructure</a> and thus closely linked to the German federal, regional and local <a href="/wiki/Governments" class="mw-redirect" title="Governments">governments</a>, to the business community, and to the industrial world in terms of cooperation and consulting. Its work is based on the German Meteorological Office Act (<i><span lang="de" xml:lang="de">Gesetz über den Deutschen Wetterdienst</span></i>). Currently, the DWD consists of roughly 2600 occupants. Besides the DWD central in Offenbach, there are regional centers in <a href="/wiki/Hamburg" title="Hamburg">Hamburg</a>, <a href="/wiki/Potsdam" title="Potsdam">Potsdam</a>, <a href="/wiki/Leipzig" title="Leipzig">Leipzig</a>, <a href="/wiki/Essen" title="Essen">Essen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Stuttgart" title="Stuttgart">Stuttgart</a> and <a href="/wiki/Munich" title="Munich">Munich</a>. Additionally, it runs Germany's densest network of meteorological measurement points with 183 full-time meteorological stations (60 of them manned), as well as about 1784 extraordinal <a href="/wiki/Weather_station" title="Weather station">weather stations</a> run by volunteering amateurs (2014).</p>
