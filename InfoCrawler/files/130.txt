<p><b><a href="/wiki/Science" title="Science">Science</a></b> is the methodical study of nature including testable <a href="/wiki/Scientific_theory" title="Scientific theory">explanations</a> and <a href="/wiki/Predictability" title="Predictability">predictions</a>. From <a href="/wiki/Classical_antiquity" title="Classical antiquity">classical antiquity</a> through the 19th century, science as a type of knowledge was more closely linked to <a href="/wiki/Philosophy" title="Philosophy">philosophy</a> than it is now and, in fact, in the <a href="/wiki/Western_world" title="Western world">Western world</a>, the term "<a href="/wiki/Natural_philosophy" title="Natural philosophy">natural philosophy</a>" encompassed fields of study that are today associated with science, such as <a href="/wiki/Astronomy" title="Astronomy">astronomy</a>, medicine, and <a href="/wiki/Physics" title="Physics">physics</a>. However, during the <a href="/wiki/Islamic_Golden_Age" title="Islamic Golden Age">Islamic Golden Age</a> foundations for the <a href="/wiki/Scientific_method" title="Scientific method">scientific method</a> were laid by <a href="/wiki/Ibn_al-Haytham" title="Ibn al-Haytham">Ibn al-Haytham</a> in his <i><a href="/wiki/Book_of_Optics" title="Book of Optics">Book of Optics</a></i>. While the classification of the material world by the ancient <a href="/wiki/Mah%C4%81bh%C5%ABta" title="Mahābhūta">Indians</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greece#Science_and_technology" title="Ancient Greece">Greeks</a> into <a href="/wiki/Classical_elements" class="mw-redirect" title="Classical elements">air, earth, fire and water</a> was more philosophical, medieval <a href="/wiki/Science_in_the_medieval_Islamic_world#Notable_scientists" title="Science in the medieval Islamic world">Middle Easterns</a> used <a href="/wiki/Islamic_Golden_Age#Scientific_method" title="Islamic Golden Age">practical, experimental observation</a> to classify materials.</p>
<p>Today, the ever-evolving term "science" refers to the pursuit of knowledge, not the knowledge itself. It is often synonymous with "natural and physical science" and often restricted to those branches of study relating to the phenomena of the material universe and their laws. Although the term implies exclusion of pure mathematics, many university faculties include Mathematics Departments within their Faculty of Science. The dominant sense in ordinary use has a narrower use for the term "science." It developed as a part of science becoming a distinct enterprise of defining the "<a href="/wiki/Laws_of_nature" class="mw-redirect mw-disambig" title="Laws of nature">laws of nature</a>"; early examples include <a href="/wiki/Kepler" class="mw-redirect" title="Kepler">Kepler</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Kepler%27s_laws_of_planetary_motion" title="Kepler's laws of planetary motion">laws</a>, <a href="/wiki/Galileo" class="mw-redirect" title="Galileo">Galileo</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Law_of_falling_bodies" class="mw-redirect" title="Law of falling bodies">laws</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Isaac_Newton" title="Isaac Newton">Newton</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Newton%27s_laws_of_motion" title="Newton's laws of motion">laws of motion</a>. In this period it became more common to refer to natural philosophy as "natural science." Over the course of the 19th century, the word "science" became increasingly associated with the <a href="/wiki/Scientific_method" title="Scientific method">disciplined study</a> of the natural world, including <a href="/wiki/Physics" title="Physics">physics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chemistry" title="Chemistry">chemistry</a>, <a href="/wiki/Geology" title="Geology">geology</a> and <a href="/wiki/Biology" title="Biology">biology</a>. This sometimes left the study of human thought and society in a linguistic limbo, which was resolved by classifying these areas of academic study as <a href="/wiki/Social_science" title="Social science">social science</a>. For example, psychology evolved from philosophy, and has grown into an area of study.</p>
<p>Currently, there are both "hard" (e.g. biological psychology) and "soft" science (e.g. social psychology) fields within the discipline. As a result, and as is consistent with the unfolding of the study of knowledge and development of methods to establish facts, each area of psychology employs a scientific method. Reflecting the evolution of the development of knowledge and established facts and the use of the scientific method, Psychology Departments in universities are found within: Faculty of Arts and Science, Faculty of Arts, and a Faculty of Science. Similarly, several other major areas of disciplined study and knowledge exist today under the general rubric of "science", such as <a href="/wiki/Formal_science" title="Formal science">formal science</a> and <a href="/wiki/Applied_science" title="Applied science">applied science</a>.</p>

<h2 style="font-family:sans-serif; font-size:100%; font-weight:bold; border: none; margin: 0; padding:0; padding-bottom:.1em; color:#069;"><span class="mw-headline" id="Selected_article"><i>Selected article</i></span></h2>
</div>
<div style="border: 1px solid #069; border-top-width: 1px; vertical-align: top;background: white;opacity: 1; color: black; text-align: left; margin: 0 0 10px; padding: 1em;padding-top: .3em;">
<div style="float:left;margin-right:1em"><a href="/wiki/File:HCl_molecule_model-VdW_surface.svg" class="image" title="Molecular model of hydrogen chloride"><img alt="Molecular model of hydrogen chloride" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/88/HCl_molecule_model-VdW_surface.svg/150px-HCl_molecule_model-VdW_surface.svg.png" width="150" height="150" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/88/HCl_molecule_model-VdW_surface.svg/225px-HCl_molecule_model-VdW_surface.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/88/HCl_molecule_model-VdW_surface.svg/300px-HCl_molecule_model-VdW_surface.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="230" data-file-height="230" /></a></div>
<b><a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_chloride" title="Hydrogen chloride">Hydrogen chloride</a></b>, also known under the name <b>HCl</b>, is a highly <a href="/wiki/Corrosive" class="mw-redirect" title="Corrosive">corrosive</a> and <a href="/wiki/Toxic" class="mw-redirect" title="Toxic">toxic</a> colorless <a href="/wiki/Gas" title="Gas">gas</a> that forms white fumes on contact with <a href="/wiki/Humidity" title="Humidity">humidity</a>. These fumes consist of <a href="/wiki/Hydrochloric_acid" title="Hydrochloric acid">hydrochloric acid</a> which forms when hydrogen chloride <a href="/wiki/Solution" title="Solution">dissolves</a> in <a href="/wiki/Water_(molecule)" class="mw-redirect" title="Water (molecule)">water</a>. Hydrogen chloride gas as well as hydrochloric acid are important <a href="/wiki/Chemical" class="mw-redirect" title="Chemical">chemicals</a> in <a href="/wiki/Chemistry" title="Chemistry">chemistry</a>, <a href="/wiki/Science" title="Science">science</a>, <a href="/wiki/Technology" title="Technology">technology</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Industry" title="Industry">industry</a>. The name HCl often refers somewhat misleadingly to hydrochloric acid instead of the gaseous hydrogen chloride.
<p>Hydrogen chloride forms corrosive hydrochloric acid on contact with body tissue. <a href="/wiki/Inhalation" title="Inhalation">Inhalation</a> of the fumes can cause <a href="/wiki/Cough" title="Cough">coughing</a>, <a href="/wiki/Choking" title="Choking">choking</a>, <a href="/wiki/Inflammation" title="Inflammation">inflammation</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Nose" title="Nose">nose</a>, <a href="/wiki/Throat" title="Throat">throat</a>, and upper <a href="/wiki/Respiratory_tract" title="Respiratory tract">respiratory tract</a>, and in severe cases, <a href="/wiki/Pulmonary_edema" title="Pulmonary edema">pulmonary edema</a>, <a href="/wiki/Circulatory_system" title="Circulatory system">circulatory system</a> failure, and <a href="/wiki/Death" title="Death">death</a>. <a href="/wiki/Skin" title="Skin">Skin</a> contact can cause redness, <a href="/wiki/Pain" title="Pain">pain</a>, and severe skin <a href="/wiki/Burn_(injury)" class="mw-redirect" title="Burn (injury)">burns</a>. Hydrogen chloride may cause severe burns to the <a href="/wiki/Eye" title="Eye">eye</a> and permanent eye damage.</p>

<h2 style="font-family:sans-serif; font-size:100%; font-weight:bold; border: none; margin: 0; padding:0; padding-bottom:.1em; color:#069;"><span class="mw-headline" id="Selected_biography"><i>Selected biography</i></span></h2>
</div>
<div style="border: 1px solid #069; border-top-width: 1px; vertical-align: top;background: white;opacity: 1; color: black; text-align: left; margin: 0 0 10px; padding: 1em;padding-top: .3em;">
<p><b><a href="/wiki/Michio_Kaku" title="Michio Kaku">Michio Kaku</a></b> (<a href="/wiki/Kanji" title="Kanji">Kanji</a>: 加来 道雄) (born January 24, 1947 in the United States) is a <a href="/wiki/Japanese_American" class="mw-redirect" title="Japanese American">Japanese American</a> <a href="/wiki/Theoretical_physicist" class="mw-redirect" title="Theoretical physicist">theoretical physicist</a>, tenured professor, and co-creator of <a href="/wiki/String_field_theory" title="String field theory">string field theory</a>, a branch of <a href="/wiki/String_theory" title="String theory">string theory</a>.</p>
<p>Dr. Kaku is the author of several scholarly, Ph.D.-level <a href="/wiki/Textbook" title="Textbook">textbooks</a> and has had more than 70 articles published in physics journals covering topics such as <a href="/wiki/Superstring_theory" title="Superstring theory">superstring theory</a>, <a href="/wiki/Supergravity" title="Supergravity">supergravity</a>, <a href="/wiki/Supersymmetry" title="Supersymmetry">supersymmetry</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Hadron" title="Hadron">hadronic</a> physics. He is also known as an author of popular science books, including the best-sellers <i>Beyond Einstein</i>, <i>Visions</i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Hyperspace_(book)" title="Hyperspace (book)">Hyperspace</a></i>, and <i>Parallel Worlds</i>, and the host of several radio shows, as well as being a popular figure in science television shows due to his accessible approach to the layman on explaining complex physics.</p>
