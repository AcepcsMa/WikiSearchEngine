<p>The <b>Hohenstaufen</b>, also called the <b>Staufer</b> or <b>Staufen</b>, were a dynasty of <a href="/wiki/List_of_German_Kings_and_Emperors" class="mw-redirect" title="List of German Kings and Emperors">German kings</a> (1138–1254) during the <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">Middle Ages</a>. Besides Germany, they also ruled the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Sicily" title="Kingdom of Sicily">Kingdom of Sicily</a> (1194–1268). In Italian historiography, they are known as the <i>Svevi</i> (Swabians), since they were (successive) <a href="/wiki/Duke_of_Swabia" title="Duke of Swabia">dukes of Swabia</a> from 1079. Three members of the dynasty—<a href="/wiki/Frederick_I,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Frederick I, Holy Roman Emperor">Frederick I</a>, <a href="/wiki/Henry_VI,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Henry VI, Holy Roman Emperor">Henry VI</a> and <a href="/wiki/Frederick_II,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Frederick II, Holy Roman Emperor">Frederick II</a>—were crowned <a href="/wiki/Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Holy Roman Emperor">Holy Roman Emperor</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Name">Name</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hohenstaufen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Name">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Hohenstaufen_Castle_Ruins.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/20/Hohenstaufen_Castle_Ruins.jpg/220px-Hohenstaufen_Castle_Ruins.jpg" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/20/Hohenstaufen_Castle_Ruins.jpg/330px-Hohenstaufen_Castle_Ruins.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/20/Hohenstaufen_Castle_Ruins.jpg/440px-Hohenstaufen_Castle_Ruins.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1024" data-file-height="684" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Hohenstaufen_Castle_Ruins.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Ruins of <a href="/wiki/Hohenstaufen_Castle" title="Hohenstaufen Castle">Hohenstaufen Castle</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The name "Hohenstaufen", meaning "high Staufen", originates in the 14th century, when it was first used to distinguish the <a href="/wiki/Conical_hill" title="Conical hill">conical hill</a> named <a href="/wiki/Hohenstaufen_(mountain)" title="Hohenstaufen (mountain)">Staufen</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Swabian_Jura" title="Swabian Jura">Swabian Jura</a>, in the district of <a href="/wiki/G%C3%B6ppingen_(district)" title="Göppingen (district)">Göppingen</a>, from the village of the same name in the valley below. The name "Staufen" derives from <i>Stauf</i> (formerly <i>stouf</i> and akin to <a href="/wiki/Early_Modern_English" title="Early Modern English">Early Modern English</a> <i><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/stoup" class="extiw" title="wiktionary:stoup">stoup</a></i>), meaning "<a href="/wiki/Chalice" title="Chalice">chalice</a>", and was commonly applied to conical hills in Swabia in the Middle Ages. The family derives its name from the castle which the first Swabian duke of the lineage built there in the latter half of the 11th century. <a href="/wiki/Hohenstaufen_Castle" title="Hohenstaufen Castle">Staufen castle</a> was only finally called Hohenstaufen by historians in the 19th century, to distinguish it from other castles of the same name. The name of the dynasty followed, but in recent decades the trend in German historiography has been to prefer the name Staufer.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Origins">Origins</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hohenstaufen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Origins">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The noble family first appeared in the late 10th century in the <a href="/wiki/Duchy_of_Swabia" title="Duchy of Swabia">Swabian</a> <i>Riesgau</i> region around the former <a href="/wiki/Carolingian_Empire" title="Carolingian Empire">Carolingian</a> court of <a href="/wiki/N%C3%B6rdlingen" title="Nördlingen">Nördlingen</a>. A local count Frederick (d. about 1075) is mentioned as progenitor in a pedigree drawn up by Abbot <a href="/wiki/Wibald" title="Wibald">Wibald of Stavelot</a> at the behest of Emperor Frederick Barbarossa in 1153. He held the office of a Swabian <a href="/wiki/Count_palatine" title="Count palatine">count palatine</a>; his son Frederick of <a href="/wiki/W%C3%A4schenbeuren" title="Wäschenbeuren">Buren</a> (c.1020–1053) married Hildegard of <a href="/wiki/Eguisheim" title="Eguisheim">Egisheim</a>-<a href="/wiki/County_of_Dagsburg" title="County of Dagsburg">Dagsburg</a> (d. 1094/95), a niece of <a href="/wiki/Pope_Leo_IX" title="Pope Leo IX">Pope Leo IX</a>. Their son <a href="/wiki/Frederick_I,_Duke_of_Swabia" title="Frederick I, Duke of Swabia">Frederick I</a> was appointed <a href="/wiki/Duke_of_Swabia" title="Duke of Swabia">Duke of Swabia</a> at Hohenstaufen Castle by the <a href="/wiki/Salian_dynasty" title="Salian dynasty">Salian</a> king <a href="/wiki/Henry_IV,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Henry IV, Holy Roman Emperor">Henry IV of Germany</a> in 1079.</p>
<p>At the same time, Duke Frederick I was engaged to the king's approximately seventeen-year-old daughter, <a href="/wiki/Agnes_of_Germany" class="mw-redirect" title="Agnes of Germany">Agnes</a>. Nothing is known about Frederick's life before this event, but he proved to be an imperial ally throughout Henry's struggles against other Swabian lords, namely <a href="/wiki/Rudolf_of_Rheinfelden" title="Rudolf of Rheinfelden">Rudolf of Rheinfelden</a>, Frederick's predecessor, and the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Z%C3%A4hringen" title="House of Zähringen">Zähringen</a> and <a href="/wiki/House_of_Welf" title="House of Welf">Welf</a> lords. Frederick's brother Otto was elevated to the <a href="/wiki/Bishop_of_Strasbourg" class="mw-redirect" title="Bishop of Strasbourg">Strasbourg bishopric</a> in 1082.</p>
<p>Upon Frederick's death, he was succeeded by his son, Duke <a href="/wiki/Frederick_II,_Duke_of_Swabia" title="Frederick II, Duke of Swabia">Frederick II</a>, in 1105. Frederick II remained a close ally of the Salians, he and his younger brother <a href="/wiki/Conrad_III_of_Germany" title="Conrad III of Germany">Conrad</a> were named the king's representatives in Germany when the king was in Italy. Around 1120, Frederick II married Judith of Bavaria from the rival <a href="/wiki/House_of_Welf" title="House of Welf">House of Welf</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ruling_in_Germany">Ruling in Germany</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hohenstaufen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Ruling in Germany">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>When the last male member of the Salian dynasty, Emperor <a href="/wiki/Henry_V,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Henry V, Holy Roman Emperor">Henry V</a>, died without heirs in 1125, a controversy arose about the succession. Duke <a href="/wiki/Frederick_II,_Duke_of_Swabia" title="Frederick II, Duke of Swabia">Frederick II</a> and <a href="/wiki/Conrad_III_of_Germany" title="Conrad III of Germany">Conrad</a>, the two current male Staufers, by their mother Agnes, were grandsons of late Emperor Henry IV and nephews of Henry V. Frederick attempted to succeed to the throne of the <a href="/wiki/Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Holy Roman Emperor">Holy Roman Emperor</a> (formally known as the <a href="/wiki/King_of_the_Romans" title="King of the Romans">King of the Romans</a>) through a customary election, but lost to the <a href="/wiki/Duchy_of_Saxony" title="Duchy of Saxony">Saxon</a> duke <a href="/wiki/Lothair_III,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" class="mw-redirect" title="Lothair III, Holy Roman Emperor">Lothair of Supplinburg</a>. A civil war between Frederick's dynasty and Lothair's ended with Frederick's submission in 1134. After Lothair's death in 1137, Frederick's brother Conrad was elected King as Conrad III.</p>

<p>Because the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Welf" title="House of Welf">Welf duke</a> <a href="/wiki/Henry_the_Proud" class="mw-redirect" title="Henry the Proud">Henry the Proud</a>, son-in-law and heir of Lothair and the most powerful prince in Germany, who had been passed over in the election, refused to acknowledge the new king, Conrad III deprived him of all his territories, giving the <a href="/wiki/Duchy_of_Saxony" title="Duchy of Saxony">Duchy of Saxony</a> to <a href="/wiki/Albert_the_Bear" title="Albert the Bear">Albert the Bear</a> and that of <a href="/wiki/Duchy_of_Bavaria" title="Duchy of Bavaria">Bavaria</a> to <a href="/wiki/Leopold_IV,_Margrave_of_Austria" class="mw-redirect" title="Leopold IV, Margrave of Austria">Leopold&#160;IV, Margrave of Austria</a>. In 1147, Conrad heard <a href="/wiki/Bernard_of_Clairvaux" title="Bernard of Clairvaux">Bernard of Clairvaux</a> preach the <a href="/wiki/Second_Crusade" title="Second Crusade">Second Crusade</a> at <a href="/wiki/Speyer" title="Speyer">Speyer</a>, and he agreed to join King <a href="/wiki/Louis_VII_of_France" title="Louis VII of France">Louis VII of France</a> in a great <a href="/wiki/Second_Crusade" title="Second Crusade">expedition</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Holy_Land" title="Holy Land">Holy Land</a> which failed.</p>
<p>Conrad's brother Duke Frederick II died in 1147, and was succeeded in Swabia by his son, Duke <a href="/wiki/Frederick_I,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Frederick I, Holy Roman Emperor">Frederick III</a>. When King Conrad III died without adult heir in 1152, Frederick also succeeded him, taking both German royal and Imperial titles.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Frederick_Barbarossa">Frederick Barbarossa</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hohenstaufen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Frederick Barbarossa">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Frederick I, known as Frederick Barbarossa because of his red beard, struggled throughout his reign to restore the power and prestige of the German monarchy against the dukes, whose power had grown both before and after the <a href="/wiki/Investiture_Controversy" title="Investiture Controversy">Investiture Controversy</a> under his Salian predecessors. As royal access to the resources of the church in Germany was much reduced, Frederick was forced to go to <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Italy_(medieval)" class="mw-redirect" title="Kingdom of Italy (medieval)">Italy</a> to find the finances needed to restore the king's power in Germany. He was soon crowned emperor in Italy, but decades of warfare on the peninsula yielded scant results. The <a href="/wiki/Papal_States" title="Papal States">Papacy</a> and the prosperous city-states of the <a href="/wiki/Lombard_League" title="Lombard League">Lombard League</a> in northern Italy were traditional enemies, but the fear of Imperial domination caused them to join ranks to fight Frederick. Under the skilled leadership of <a href="/wiki/Pope_Alexander_III" title="Pope Alexander III">Pope Alexander III</a>, the alliance suffered many defeats but ultimately was able to deny the emperor a complete victory in Italy. Frederick returned to Germany. He had vanquished one notable opponent, his Welf cousin, Duke <a href="/wiki/Henry_the_Lion" title="Henry the Lion">Henry the Lion</a> of Saxony and <a href="/wiki/Duchy_of_Bavaria" title="Duchy of Bavaria">Bavaria</a> in 1180, but his hopes of restoring the power and prestige of the monarchy seemed unlikely to be met by the end of his life.</p>
<p>During Frederick's long stays in Italy, the German princes became stronger and began a successful colonization of Slavic lands. Offers of reduced taxes and manorial duties enticed many Germans to settle in the east in the course of the <i><a href="/wiki/Ostsiedlung" title="Ostsiedlung">Ostsiedlung</a></i>. In 1163 Frederick waged a successful campaign against the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Poland_(1025%E2%80%931385)" title="Kingdom of Poland (1025–1385)">Kingdom of Poland</a> in order to re-install the <a href="/wiki/Duchy_of_Silesia" title="Duchy of Silesia">Silesian</a> dukes of the <a href="/wiki/Silesian_Piasts" title="Silesian Piasts">Piast</a> dynasty. With the German colonization, the Empire increased in size and came to include the <a href="/wiki/Duchy_of_Pomerania" title="Duchy of Pomerania">Duchy of Pomerania</a>. A quickening economic life in Germany increased the number of towns and <a href="/wiki/Free_imperial_city" title="Free imperial city">Imperial cities</a>, and gave them greater importance. It was also during this period that castles and courts replaced monasteries as centers of culture. Growing out of this courtly culture, <a href="/wiki/Middle_High_German" title="Middle High German">Middle High German</a> literature reached its peak in lyrical love poetry, the <a href="/wiki/Minnesang" title="Minnesang">Minnesang</a>, and in narrative epic poems such as <i><a href="/wiki/Tristan" title="Tristan">Tristan</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Parzival" title="Parzival">Parzival</a></i>, and the <i><a href="/wiki/Nibelungenlied" title="Nibelungenlied">Nibelungenlied</a></i>.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Henry_VI">Henry VI</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hohenstaufen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Henry VI">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Frederick died in 1190 while on the <a href="/wiki/Third_Crusade" title="Third Crusade">Third Crusade</a> and was succeeded by his son, <a href="/wiki/Henry_VI,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Henry VI, Holy Roman Emperor">Henry VI</a>. Elected king even before his father's death, Henry went to <a href="/wiki/Rome" title="Rome">Rome</a> to be crowned emperor. He married Queen <a href="/wiki/Constance,_Queen_of_Sicily" title="Constance, Queen of Sicily">Constance of Sicily</a>, and deaths in his wife's family gave him claim of succession and possession of the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Sicily" title="Kingdom of Sicily">Kingdom of Sicily</a> in 1189 and 1194 respectively, a source of vast wealth. Henry failed to make royal and Imperial succession hereditary, but in 1196 he succeeded in gaining a pledge that his infant son <a href="/wiki/Frederick_II,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Frederick II, Holy Roman Emperor">Frederick</a> would receive the German crown. Faced with difficulties in Italy and confident that he would realize his wishes in Germany at a later date, Henry returned to the south, where it appeared he might unify the peninsula under the Hohenstaufen name. After a series of military victories, however, he fell ill and died of natural causes in Sicily in 1197. His underage son Frederick could only succeed him in Sicily and Malta, while in the Empire the struggle between the House of Staufen and the House of Welf erupted once again.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Philip_of_Swabia">Philip of Swabia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hohenstaufen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Philip of Swabia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Because the election of a three-year-old boy to be German king appeared likely to make orderly rule difficult, the boy's uncle, Duke <a href="/wiki/Philip_of_Swabia" title="Philip of Swabia">Philip of Swabia</a>, brother of late Henry VI, was designated to serve in his place. Other factions however favoured a Welf candidate. In 1198, two rival kings were chosen: the Hohenstaufen Philip of Swabia and the son of the deprived Duke <a href="/wiki/Henry_the_Lion" title="Henry the Lion">Henry the Lion</a>, the Welf <a href="/wiki/Otto_IV,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Otto IV, Holy Roman Emperor">Otto IV</a>. A long civil war began; Philip was about to win when he was murdered by the Bavarian <a href="/wiki/Count_palatine" title="Count palatine">count palatine</a> Otto VIII of <a href="/wiki/Wittelsbach" class="mw-redirect" title="Wittelsbach">Wittelsbach</a> in 1208. <a href="/wiki/Pope_Innocent_III" title="Pope Innocent III">Pope Innocent III</a> initially had supported the Welfs, but when Otto, now sole elected monarch, moved to appropriate Sicily, Innocent changed sides and accepted young Frederick II<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="Please clarify the following statement or statements with a good explanation from a reliable source. (July 2014)">how?</span></a></i>]</sup> and his ally, King <a href="/wiki/Philip_II_of_France" title="Philip II of France">Philip II of France</a>, who defeated Otto at the 1214 <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Bouvines" title="Battle of Bouvines">Battle of Bouvines</a>. Frederick had returned to Germany in 1212 from Sicily, where he had grown up, and was elected king in 1215. When Otto died in 1218, Fredrick became the undisputed ruler, and in 1220 was crowned <a href="/wiki/Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Holy Roman Emperor">Holy Roman Emperor</a>.</p>
<p>Philip changed the coat of arms from a black lion on a gold shield to three leopards,<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> probably derived from the arms of his Welf rival <a href="/wiki/Otto_IV,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Otto IV, Holy Roman Emperor">Otto IV</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ruling_in_Italy">Ruling in Italy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hohenstaufen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Ruling in Italy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The conflict between the Staufer dynasty and the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Welf" title="House of Welf">Welf</a> had irrevocably weakened the Imperial authority and the <a href="/wiki/Norman_conquest_of_southern_Italy#Conquest_of_Sicily.2C_1061.E2.80.931091" title="Norman conquest of southern Italy">Norman kingdom of Sicily</a> became the base for Staufer rule.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Frederick_II">Frederick II</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hohenstaufen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Frederick II">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Emperor_Frederick_II" class="mw-redirect" title="Emperor Frederick II">Emperor Frederick II</a> spent little time in Germany as his main concerns lay in <a href="/wiki/Southern_Italy" title="Southern Italy">Southern Italy</a>. He founded the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Naples" class="mw-redirect" title="University of Naples">University of Naples</a> in 1224 to train future state officials and reigned over Germany primarily through the allocation of royal prerogatives, leaving the sovereign authority and imperial estates to the ecclesiastical and secular princes. He made significant concessions to the German nobles, such as those put forth in an imperial statute of 1232, which made princes virtually independent rulers within their territories. These measures favoured the further fragmentation of the Empire.</p>

<p>By the 1226 <a href="/wiki/Golden_Bull_of_Rimini" title="Golden Bull of Rimini">Golden Bull of Rimini</a>, Frederick had assigned the military order of the <a href="/wiki/Teutonic_Knights" class="mw-redirect" title="Teutonic Knights">Teutonic Knights</a> to complete the conquest and conversion of the <a href="/wiki/Prussia_(region)" title="Prussia (region)">Prussian</a> lands. A reconciliation with the Welfs took place in 1235, whereby <a href="/wiki/Otto_I,_Duke_of_Brunswick-L%C3%BCneburg" title="Otto I, Duke of Brunswick-Lüneburg">Otto the Child</a>, grandson of the late Saxon duke Henry the Lion, was named <a href="/wiki/Duchy_of_Brunswick-L%C3%BCneburg" title="Duchy of Brunswick-Lüneburg">Duke of Brunswick and Lüneburg</a>. The power struggle with the popes continued and resulted in Fredrick's <a href="/wiki/Excommunication" title="Excommunication">excommunication</a> in 1227. In 1239, <a href="/wiki/Pope_Gregory_IX" title="Pope Gregory IX">Pope Gregory IX</a> excommunicated Fredrick again, and in 1245 he was condemned as a heretic by a church council. Although Frederick was one of the most energetic, imaginative, and capable rulers of the time, he was not concerned with drawing the disparate forces in Germany together. His legacy was thus that local rulers had more authority after his reign than before it. The clergy also had become more powerful.</p>

<p>By the time of Frederick's death in 1250, little centralized power remained in Germany. The Great <a href="/wiki/Interregnum" title="Interregnum">Interregnum</a>, a period in which there were several elected rival kings, none of whom was able to achieve any position of authority, followed the death of Frederick's son King <a href="/wiki/Conrad_IV_of_Germany" title="Conrad IV of Germany">Conrad IV of Germany</a> in 1254. The German princes vied for individual advantage and managed to strip many powers away from the diminished monarchy. Rather than establish sovereign states however, many nobles tended to look after their families. Their many male heirs created more and smaller estates, and from a largely free class of officials previously formed, many of these assumed or acquired hereditary rights to administrative and legal offices. These trends compounded political fragmentation within Germany. The period was ended in 1273 with the election of <a href="/wiki/Rudolph_I_of_Germany" class="mw-redirect" title="Rudolph I of Germany">Rudolph of Habsburg</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Godparent" title="Godparent">godson</a> of Frederick.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="End_of_the_Staufer_dynasty">End of the Staufer dynasty</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hohenstaufen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: End of the Staufer dynasty">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Conrad IV was succeeded as duke of Swabia by his only son, two-year-old <a href="/wiki/Conradin" title="Conradin">Conradin</a>. By this time, the office of duke of Swabia had been fully subsumed into the office of the king, and without royal authority had become meaningless. In 1261, attempts to elect young Conradin king were unsuccessful. He also had to defend Sicily against an invasion, sponsored by <a href="/wiki/Pope_Urban_IV" title="Pope Urban IV">Pope Urban IV</a> (Jacques Pantaléon) and <a href="/wiki/Pope_Clement_IV" title="Pope Clement IV">Pope Clement IV</a> (Guy Folques), by <a href="/wiki/Charles_of_Anjou" class="mw-redirect" title="Charles of Anjou">Charles of Anjou</a>, a brother of the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_France" title="Kingdom of France">French</a> king. Charles had been promised by the popes the Kingdom of Sicily, where he would replace the relatives of Frederick II. Charles had defeated Conradin's uncle <a href="/wiki/Manfred,_King_of_Sicily" title="Manfred, King of Sicily">Manfred, King of Sicily</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Benevento" title="Battle of Benevento">Battle of Benevento</a> on 26 February 1266. The king himself, refusing to flee, rushed into the midst of his enemies and was killed. Conradin's campaign to retake control ended with his defeat in 1268 at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Tagliacozzo" title="Battle of Tagliacozzo">Battle of Tagliacozzo</a>, after which he was handed over to Charles, who had him publicly <a href="/wiki/Executed" class="mw-redirect" title="Executed">executed</a> at <a href="/wiki/Naples" title="Naples">Naples</a>. With Conradin, the direct line of the Dukes of Swabia finally ceased to exist, though most of the later emperors were descended from the Staufer dynasty indirectly.</p>
<p>During the political decentralization of the late Staufer period, the population had grown from an estimated 8 million in 1200 to about 14 million in 1300, and the number of towns increased tenfold. The most heavily urbanized areas of Germany were located in the south and the west. Towns often developed a degree of independence, but many were subordinate to local rulers if not <a href="/wiki/Imperial_immediacy" title="Imperial immediacy">immediate</a> to the emperor. Colonization of the east also continued in the thirteenth century, most notably through the efforts of the Teutonic Knights. German merchants also began trading extensively on the <a href="/wiki/Baltic_region" title="Baltic region">Baltic</a>.</p>
