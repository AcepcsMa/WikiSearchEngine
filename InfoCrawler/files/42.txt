<p><b>Gerard A. "Gerry" Salton</b> (8 March 1927 in <a href="/wiki/Nuremberg" title="Nuremberg">Nuremberg</a> – 28 August 1995), was a Professor of <a href="/wiki/Computer_Science" class="mw-redirect" title="Computer Science">Computer Science</a> at <a href="/wiki/Cornell_University" title="Cornell University">Cornell University</a>. Salton was perhaps the leading computer scientist working in the field of <a href="/wiki/Information_retrieval" title="Information retrieval">information retrieval</a> during his time, and "the father of information retrieval".<sup id="cite_ref-father-IR_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-father-IR-1">[1]</a></sup> His group at Cornell developed the <a href="/wiki/SMART_Information_Retrieval_System" title="SMART Information Retrieval System">SMART Information Retrieval System</a>, which he initiated when he was at Harvard.</p>
<p>Salton was born Gerhard Anton Sahlmann on March 8, 1927 in <a href="/wiki/Nuremberg,_Germany" class="mw-redirect" title="Nuremberg, Germany">Nuremberg, Germany</a>. He received a Bachelor's (1950) and Master's (1952) degree in mathematics from <a href="/wiki/Brooklyn_College" title="Brooklyn College">Brooklyn College</a>, and a Ph.D. from <a href="/wiki/Harvard_University" title="Harvard University">Harvard</a> in <a href="/wiki/Applied_Mathematics" class="mw-redirect" title="Applied Mathematics">Applied Mathematics</a> in 1958, the last of <a href="/wiki/Howard_Aiken" class="mw-redirect" title="Howard Aiken">Howard Aiken</a>'s doctoral students, and taught there until 1965, when he joined <a href="/wiki/Cornell_University" title="Cornell University">Cornell University</a> and co-founded its department of Computer Science.</p>
<p>Salton was perhaps most well known for developing the now widely used <a href="/wiki/Vector_space_model" title="Vector space model">vector space model</a> for Information Retrieval.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> In this model, both documents and queries are represented as vectors of term counts, and the similarity between a document and a query is given by the cosine between the term vector and the document vector. In this paper, he also introduced <a href="/wiki/TF-IDF" class="mw-redirect" title="TF-IDF">TF-IDF</a>, or term-frequency-inverse-document frequency, a model in which the score of a term in a document is the ratio of the number of terms in that document divided by the frequency of the number of documents in which that term occurs. (The concept of inverse document frequency, a measure of specificity, had been introduced in 1972 by <a href="/wiki/Karen_Sp%C3%A4rck_Jones" title="Karen Spärck Jones">Karen Sparck-Jones</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>) Later in life, he became interested in automatic text summarization and analysis,<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> as well as automatic hypertext generation.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> He published over 150 research articles and 5 books during his life.</p>
<p>Salton was editor-in-chief of the <a href="/wiki/Communications_of_the_ACM" title="Communications of the ACM">Communications of the ACM</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Journal_of_the_ACM" title="Journal of the ACM">Journal of the ACM</a>, and chaired <a href="/wiki/Special_Interest_Group_on_Information_Retrieval" title="Special Interest Group on Information Retrieval">Special Interest Group on Information Retrieval</a> (SIGIR). He was an associate editor of the <a href="/wiki/ACM_Transactions_on_Information_Systems" title="ACM Transactions on Information Systems">ACM Transactions on Information Systems</a>. He was an <a href="/wiki/List_of_Fellows_of_the_Association_for_Computing_Machinery" title="List of Fellows of the Association for Computing Machinery">ACM Fellow</a> (elected 1995),<sup id="cite_ref-fellow-acm_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fellow-acm-6">[6]</a></sup> received an Award of Merit from the <a href="/wiki/American_Society_for_Information_Science" class="mw-redirect" title="American Society for Information Science">American Society for Information Science</a> (1989), and was the first recipient of the SIGIR Award for outstanding contributions to study of information retrieval (1983) -- now called the <a href="/wiki/Gerard_Salton_Award" title="Gerard Salton Award">Gerard Salton Award</a>.</p>

<li><a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.ideals.uiuc.edu/bitstream/2142/1697/2/Dubin748764.pdf">The Most Influential Paper Gerard Salton Never Wrote</a> - This 2004 Library Trends paper by David Dubin serves as a historical review of the metamorphosis of the term discrimination value model (TDV) into the vector space model as an information retrieval model (VSM as an IR model). This paper calls into question what the Information Retrieval research community believed Salton's vector space model was originally intended to model. What much later became an information retrieval model was originally a data-centric mathematical–computational model used as an explanatory device. In addition, Dubin's paper points out that a 1975 Salton paper oft cited does not exist but is probably a combination of two other papers, neither of which actually refers to the VSM as an IR model.</li>
</ul>
