<p>The <b>Bavarian Ludwig Railway</b> (<i>Bayerische Ludwigseisenbahn</i> or <i>Ludwigsbahn</i>) was the <a href="/wiki/List_of_the_first_German_railways_to_1870" title="List of the first German railways to 1870">first steam-hauled railway opened in Germany</a>. The <i>Königlich privilegierte Ludwigs-Eisenbahn-Gesellschaft</i> ("Royal Privileged Ludwig Railway Company", later called the <i>Ludwigs-Eisenbahn-Gesellschaft</i>) received a concession to build a railway from <a href="/wiki/Nuremberg" title="Nuremberg">Nuremberg</a> to <a href="/wiki/F%C3%BCrth" title="Fürth">Fürth</a> in the <a href="/wiki/States_of_Germany" title="States of Germany">state</a> of <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Bavaria" title="Kingdom of Bavaria">Bavaria</a> on 19 February 1834.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Background">Background</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bavarian_Ludwig_Railway&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The first reports from England over the planning of railways attracted great attention in Germany, particularly in Bavaria, where the road between the important commercial cities of Nuremberg and Fürth was the busiest road connection in the kingdom. Bavarian interest was also stimulated by <a href="/wiki/Friedrich_List" title="Friedrich List">Friedrich List</a>’s advocacy of an all-German railway system and the reports of Joseph von Baader, whom <a href="/wiki/Ludwig_I_of_Bavaria" title="Ludwig I of Bavaria">King Ludwig</a> had sent to <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a> to study railways. After a discussion of this topic in the first Bavarian parliament in 1825, it authorised the king to build an experimental railway in the <a href="/wiki/Nymphenburg_Palace" title="Nymphenburg Palace">Nymphenburg Palace park</a>. As the king’s 1828 request for Franconian merchants to begin building a railway line led to no action, he turned his attention to his favourite project, the building of the <a href="/wiki/Ludwig_Canal" title="Ludwig Canal">Ludwig Canal</a> between the <a href="/wiki/Danube" title="Danube">Danube</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Main_(river)" title="Main (river)">Main</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Establishment">Establishment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bavarian_Ludwig_Railway&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Establishment">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>After railways had been operating in England for a few years, local businesspeople decided to build a railway line along the Nürnberg-Fürth road. On 14 May 1833 they founded the <i>Gesellschaft zur Errichtung einer Eisenbahn mit Dampffahrt zwischen Nürnberg und Fürth</i> ("company for the establishment of a steam railway between Nuremberg and Fürth") to develop the railway. Within six months the two main instigators from Nuremberg, the merchant and market chief, George Zacharias Platner, and the head of the poly-technical school, Johannes Scharrer, had successfully raised the planned share capital of 132,000 <a href="/wiki/South_German_gulden" title="South German gulden">guilders</a>. The proposed dividend of 12<span class="nowrap"> </span><span class="frac nowrap"><sup>2</sup>⁄<sub>3</sub></span>% was met with skepticism, although the company did, in fact, pay a dividend of 20% in 1836.</p>
<p>King Ludwig was an unenthusiastic supporter of railways because of his preference for building the <a href="/wiki/Ludwig_Canal" title="Ludwig Canal">Ludwig Canal</a> between the <a href="/wiki/Main_(river)" title="Main (river)">Main</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Danube" title="Danube">Danube</a>; this was actually built between 1836 and 1846. The canal was relatively unsuccessful because of its profusion of locks, its narrowness and early competition from railways, but it foreshadowed the more successful <a href="/wiki/Rhine-Main-Danube_Canal" class="mw-redirect" title="Rhine-Main-Danube Canal">Rhine-Main-Danube Canal</a> built on a similar route and completed in 1992. Ludwig allowed the railway company to use his name and authorized his government to buy a token two shares in it. Significantly for the construction of the railway, the king made available the Bavarian road builder, <a href="/wiki/Paul_Camille_von_Denis" title="Paul Camille von Denis">Paul Camille von Denis</a>, for the railway construction. Von Denis adopted the English <a href="/wiki/Rail_gauge" class="mw-redirect" title="Rail gauge">rail gauge</a> of <a href="/wiki/Standard_gauge" title="Standard gauge">1435 mm</a> for the nearly dead-straight 6.04&#160;km-long single-track line next to the Fürth-Nuremberg road.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Start_of_services">Start of services</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bavarian_Ludwig_Railway&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Start of services">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>On 7 December 1835 the company opened the first German steam-powered railway line for passenger and freight traffic before a large public gathering. The <a href="/wiki/Steam_locomotive" title="Steam locomotive">steam locomotive</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Adler_(locomotive)" title="Adler (locomotive)">Adler</a></i> ("eagle") had been supplied with its driver by <a href="/wiki/George_Stephenson" title="George Stephenson">Stephenson</a>’s company from <a href="/wiki/Newcastle_upon_Tyne" title="Newcastle upon Tyne">Newcastle</a>. The <i>Remy &amp; Co aus Rasselstein</i> company of <a href="/wiki/Neuwied" title="Neuwied">Neuwied</a>, supplied only the 15-foot (4.6&#160;m)-long rails of rolled wrought iron. The carriages were supplied by local wagon-builders.</p>
<p>Already on 20 September 1831 the private narrow gauge, <a href="/wiki/Wagonway" title="Wagonway">horse-drawn</a> <a href="/wiki/Prince_William_Railway" class="mw-redirect" title="Prince William Railway">Prince William Railway</a> coal railway had opened between Hinsbeck (Ruhr) and Nierenhof, but it did not excite the public attention of a steam-hauled and passenger railway. Nevertheless, King Ludwig did not visit the railway named after him until August 1836. The cost of building the railway, which had been estimated at 132,000 guilders, actually reached 170,000 guilders as a result of lack of railway building experience and, in particular, the high price of land acquisition in the absence of a law providing for <a href="/wiki/Eminent_domain" title="Eminent domain">compulsory purchase</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Operations">Operations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bavarian_Ludwig_Railway&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Operations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Beginning on 8 December 1835 a horse-drawn services operated on the line from Nuremberg to Fürth once an hour. The <i>Adler</i> only operated at 13:00 and 14:00 daily. The high cost of importing hard coal from <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Saxony" title="Kingdom of Saxony">Saxony</a>, which in the beginning still had to be brought by horse cart, prevented in the early years regular use of the <i>Adler</i> or the <i>Pfeil</i> ("arrow"). With the acquisition of more locomotives, only the early and late services were horses-drawn. Finally in 1863 horse operations were abandoned to reduce maintenance costs (especially the provision of horse tracks) and to raise speeds.</p>
<p>Goods traffic at first consisted of the carriage of newspapers and beer. General freight traffic only started in 1839 and mail traffic in 1840. The success of the line is shown by the fact that up to 1855 dividends were never less than 12%. At the time this was considered a magnificent capital return. Nevertheless, the government refused permission to extend the line to <a href="/wiki/W%C3%BCrzburg" title="Würzburg">Würzburg</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Closure">Closure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bavarian_Ludwig_Railway&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Closure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Competition developed with the building of <a href="/wiki/Horsecar" title="Horsecar">horse trams</a> between Nuremberg and Fürth, particularly once they were electrified in 1898. From 1893, part of the line was double-tracked but this was never completed. Traffic and profits fell constantly.</p>
<p>The <i>Ludwigsbahn</i> closed on 31 October 1922. The old station building in Fürth was torn down in 1938, to make room for a <a href="/wiki/National_Socialist_German_Workers_Party" class="mw-redirect" title="National Socialist German Workers Party">Nazi Party</a> parade ground, now a plaza called <i>Fürther Freiheit</i> ("Furth Liberty"). The Nuremberg station was demolished in 1952 to allow the construction of a new multistory building. The line was leased to the Nuremberg tramways and trams operated on it until 1981. It was proposed to develop it as a high-speed tramway between Nürnberg and Fürth, but this was only achieved in the 1970s and 1980s with the construction of part of line U1 of the <a href="/wiki/Nuremberg_U-Bahn" title="Nuremberg U-Bahn">Nuremberg U-Bahn</a> along the route, partly underground and partly elevated. This was opened to <a href="/wiki/F%C3%BCrth_Hauptbahnhof" title="Fürth Hauptbahnhof">Fürth station</a> on 5 December 1985, 150 years after the opening of the original line.</p>
<p>The line ran from the Nuremberg station in Plärrer place, along today's <i>Fürther Straße</i>, past the boundary with Fürth and then followed the present <i>Hornschuchpromenade</i> to the Ludwig station at <i>Fürther Freiheit</i>, 100 m north of Fürth station.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Locomotives_and_carriages">Locomotives and carriages</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bavarian_Ludwig_Railway&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Locomotives and carriages">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <i>Ludwigsbahn</i> possessed many locomotives during its 87 years of operation. Some were bought second-hand, many were sold when it was closed.</p>

<p>In 1935 a replica of the <i>Adler</i> was constructed on old plans for the 100-year anniversary of the German railways, but it was seriously damaged on 17 October 2005 together with many other preserved locomotives in the great fire at the Nuremberg shed. However it was painstakingly restored in 2007 at a cost of € 1M and made operational again. The greatest number of carriages during the line’s existence was in 1893: 44 coaches, 1 luggage van and 10 wagons.</p>
