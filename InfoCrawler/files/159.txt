<p>The <b>white-crowned forktail</b> (<i>Enicurus leschenaulti</i>) is a species of <a href="/wiki/Forktail" title="Forktail">forktail</a> in the family <a href="/wiki/Muscicapidae" class="mw-redirect" title="Muscicapidae">Muscicapidae</a>. <a href="/wiki/Species_description" title="Species description">Scientifically described</a> in 1818, it has six subspecies, each occupying a different geographic range. The largest of the forktails, <i>Enicurus leschenaulti</i>, is between 25 and 28 centimetres (9.8 and 11.0&#160;in) long. It has a black throat and breast, black mantle, and largely black wings. The rump and lower back are white, and the bird has a prominent white <a href="/wiki/Crown_(anatomy)" title="Crown (anatomy)">crown</a>, from which it gets its name. As with other forktails, the tail is long, deeply forked, and banded in black and white. A variety of whistling and clicking calls have been described. Slight morphological differences have been observed between subspecies.</p>
<p>A shy bird, the white-crowned forktail stays near water, and forages on the edges of rivers and streams for invertebrates. Its <a href="/wiki/Breeding_season" class="mw-redirect" title="Breeding season">breeding season</a> is between March and September, and possibly extends till October. Its nests are also built near the water, and are constructed of plant material. The eggs are between two and five in number, though there is latitudinal variation. Multiple broods in a year have been observed in China. The white-crowned forktail is found in <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a>, <a href="/wiki/Southeast_Asia" title="Southeast Asia">Southeast Asia</a> and also in northeastern parts of the <a href="/wiki/Indian_subcontinent" title="Indian subcontinent">Indian subcontinent</a>. Its natural <a href="/wiki/Habitat" title="Habitat">habitats</a> are subtropical or tropical moist lowland <a href="/wiki/Forest" title="Forest">forests</a> and subtropical or tropical moist <a href="/wiki/Montane_forest" class="mw-redirect" title="Montane forest">montane forests</a>. Its elevational range varies across its range, from a minimum of 185 metres (607&#160;ft) above sea level to a maximum of 2,400 metres (7,900&#160;ft). It is categorized as a species of least concern by the <a href="/wiki/International_Union_for_Conservation_of_Nature" title="International Union for Conservation of Nature">International Union for Conservation of Nature</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy">Taxonomy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=White-crowned_forktail&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Taxonomy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The white-crowned forktail was <a href="/wiki/Species_description" title="Species description">scientifically described</a> in 1818 by French ornithologist <a href="/wiki/Louis_Jean_Pierre_Vieillot" title="Louis Jean Pierre Vieillot">Louis Jean Pierre Vieillot</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-IUCN_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IUCN-1">[1]</a></sup> The specimen described came from <a href="/wiki/Java" title="Java">Java</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-HBW_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HBW-2">[2]</a></sup> It is closely related to the <a href="/wiki/Bornean_forktail" class="mw-redirect" title="Bornean forktail">Bornean forktail</a> (<i>Enicurus borneensis</i>) which replaces it in mountain areas of <a href="/wiki/Borneo" title="Borneo">Borneo</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Phillipps_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Phillipps-3">[3]</a></sup> Six subspecies have been described. <i>E. l. indicus</i> is found from <a href="/wiki/Sikkim" title="Sikkim">Sikkim</a> to Myanmar, the Chinese province of <a href="/wiki/Yunnan" title="Yunnan">Yunnan</a>, northern Thailand, and Vietnam. <i>E. l. sinensis</i> is found in southern and eastern China, including in the regions of <a href="/wiki/Qinghai" title="Qinghai">Qinghai</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gansu" title="Gansu">Gansu</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sichuan" title="Sichuan">Sichuan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Zhejiang" title="Zhejiang">Zhejiang</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Guangdong" title="Guangdong">Guangdong</a>. <i>E. l. frontalis</i> is seen in the southern and central parts of the <a href="/wiki/Malay_Peninsula" title="Malay Peninsula">Malay Peninsula</a>, on the islands of <a href="/wiki/Nias_island" class="mw-redirect" title="Nias island">Nias</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sumatra" title="Sumatra">Sumatra</a>, and in the lowland regions of <a href="/wiki/Borneo" title="Borneo">Borneo</a>. <i>E. l. chaseni</i> is found only on <a href="/wiki/Batu_Islands" title="Batu Islands">Batu island</a>. <i>E. l. leschenaulti</i> is found in <a href="/wiki/Java" title="Java">Java</a> and on the island of <a href="/wiki/Bali" title="Bali">Bali</a>. <i>E. l. borneensis</i> is found in the highland regions of Borneo.<sup id="cite_ref-HBW_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HBW-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>The two sub-species found in Borneo, which have different elevational ranges, are genetically relatively distant. The upland subspecies <i>E. l. borneensis</i> may be a separate species, but is morphologically not very distinct from the other subspecies of <i>E. leschenaulti</i>. Birds of the <i>borneensis</i> subspecies appeared to breed between mountaintops, but not with individuals of the subspecies <i>frontalis</i> in the valleys in between.<sup id="cite_ref-Phylogeny_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Phylogeny-4">[4]</a></sup> The species was found to be more distantly related to the <a href="/wiki/Slaty-backed_forktail" title="Slaty-backed forktail">slaty-backed forktail</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Little_forktail" title="Little forktail">little forktail</a> than to other forktail species.<sup id="cite_ref-Phylogeny_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Phylogeny-4">[4]</a></sup> The precise geographic delineation between <i>E. l. borneensis</i> and <i>E. l. frontalis</i> is not known.<sup id="cite_ref-HBW_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HBW-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<p>The white-crowned forktail is the largest of the forktails.<sup id="cite_ref-Clement2016_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Clement2016-5">[5]</a></sup> Between 25–28 centimetres (9.8–11.0&#160;in) long, the white-crowned forktail typically weighs between 27–38 grams (0.95–1.34&#160;oz), though specimens weighing up to 53 grams (1.9&#160;oz) have been reported.<sup id="cite_ref-HBW_2-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HBW-2">[2]</a></sup> It has black feathering on its throat extending down to its breast, as well as a black face and scapulars. The crown and forehead have a prominent white patch, sometimes visible as a slight crest, from which the bird gets its name.<sup id="cite_ref-Clement2016_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Clement2016-5">[5]</a></sup> The belly of the bird is white,<sup id="cite_ref-GrimmettInskipp2011_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GrimmettInskipp2011-6">[6]</a></sup> and is sharply delineated from the throat and face. The lower back and rump of the bird are white.<sup id="cite_ref-Myers2016_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Myers2016-7">[7]</a></sup> The tail of the species is long and evenly graduated, with a deep fork. It has white tips, and the outer feathers are white.<sup id="cite_ref-HBW_2-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HBW-2">[2]</a></sup> It also has three narrower white bands created by the tips of shorter tail feathers. The wings of this species are largely black with a prominent white band across the greater coverts.<sup id="cite_ref-Clement2016_5-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Clement2016-5">[5]</a></sup> Juveniles of the species have brownish black upperparts, breast, and throat, brown flanks, and brown mottling on the belly. They also lack the prominent white crown.<sup id="cite_ref-GrimmettInskipp2011_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GrimmettInskipp2011-6">[6]</a></sup> The bill of the species is black, while the feet are pinkish in color.<sup id="cite_ref-HBW_2-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HBW-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Its mantle is completely black, a feature used to distinguish the species from the <a href="/wiki/Spotted_forktail" title="Spotted forktail">spotted forktail</a>, which has a speckled mantle, and from the <a href="/wiki/Slaty-backed_forktail" title="Slaty-backed forktail">slaty-backed forktail</a>, which has a slate-grey mantle. It is distinguished from the <a href="/wiki/Black-backed_forktail" title="Black-backed forktail">black-backed forktail</a> by its longer tail and larger size.<sup id="cite_ref-GrimmettInskipp2011_6-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GrimmettInskipp2011-6">[6]</a></sup> The Indian subspecies <i>E. l. indicus</i> has a bill slightly longer than the nominate subspecies <i>E. l. leschenaulti</i>, while the bill of the Chinese subspecies <i>E. l. sinensis</i> is slightly shorter than that of the nominate. The subspecies <i>frontalis</i> is somewhat smaller than the others, and the extent of white on its crown is smaller: <i>E. l. borneensis</i> is similar to <i>frontalis</i> but has a longer tail. <i>E. l. chaseni</i> is larger than birds from the rest of Sumatra, and also has a longer tail.<sup id="cite_ref-HBW_2-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HBW-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Multiple calls have been observed. The <a href="/wiki/Alarm_call" class="mw-redirect" title="Alarm call">alarm call</a> and <a href="/wiki/Contact_call" title="Contact call">contact calls</a> are high-pitched, ringing lengthy whistles, described as "tseee, tseee" or "zweeet": these calls are repeated multiple times, with pauses in between. The alarm call is harsher and more emphatic, described as "scree" or "scree chit chit". Males use a long and complex whistled song when displaying or exhibiting territorial behavior; this often consists of a long whistle that fades away, followed by shorter whistles, clicking or chacking noises, or bell-like sounds. The call of the <i>borneensis</i> subspecies is slightly different from the others.<sup id="cite_ref-Clement2016_5-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Clement2016-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution_and_habitat">Distribution and habitat</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=White-crowned_forktail&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Distribution and habitat">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The habitat of the white-crowned forktail is subtropical or tropical regions in moist lowland forests and moist broadleaf <a href="/wiki/Montane_forest" class="mw-redirect" title="Montane forest">montane forests</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Phillipps_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Phillipps-3">[3]</a></sup> As is the case with other <a href="/wiki/Forktail" title="Forktail">forktail</a> species, the white-crowned forktail frequents fast-flowing rivers, waterfalls, and streams within the forests, though it may move to slower moving water sources in the winter.<sup id="cite_ref-GrimmettInskipp2011_6-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GrimmettInskipp2011-6">[6]</a></sup> Its elevational range has been observed to vary seasonally, and in the northeastern regions of the <a href="/wiki/Indian_subcontinent" title="Indian subcontinent">Indian subcontinent</a>, it is likely to migrate seasonally; all records from Bangladesh are from winter months.<sup id="cite_ref-HBW_2-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HBW-2">[2]</a></sup> It also frequents damp areas and pools within the forest, including animal wallows, swampy areas, and water ditches. It may be found along slower-moving rivers and streams in the lowland regions of its distribution. It prefers areas screened by dense vegetation. On the island of Borneo the white-crowned forktail is sometimes found in drier areas, including along tracks and ridges in the forest, and in <a href="/wiki/Heathland" class="mw-redirect" title="Heathland">heathland</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Clement2016_5-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Clement2016-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>The white-crowned forktail has a wide geographic distribution across south and south-east Asia, including in parts of India, Bangladesh, China, Myanmar, Bhutan, Thailand, Laos, Vietnam, Malaysia, Indonesia, and Brunei.<sup id="cite_ref-IUCN_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IUCN-1">[1]</a></sup> The elevational range of the species varies across its geographic distribution. In the Eastern Himalayas the species is generally found below 800 metres (2,600&#160;ft) above sea level, though occasionally found till 1,250 metres (4,100&#160;ft), and exceptionally at 2,400 metres (7,900&#160;ft) in the state of <a href="/wiki/Arunachal_Pradesh" title="Arunachal Pradesh">Arunachal Pradesh</a>. In <a href="/wiki/Sumatra" title="Sumatra">Sumatra</a> and Borneo it is generally seen up to an elevation of 1,400 metres (4,600&#160;ft). The subspecies <i>borneensis</i> is found at 900–1,950 metres (2,950–6,400&#160;ft), but occasionally as low as 185 metres (607&#160;ft).<sup id="cite_ref-Clement2016_5-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Clement2016-5">[5]</a></sup> It is common through most of its range but uncommon in the Himalayas.<sup id="cite_ref-HBW_2-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HBW-2">[2]</a></sup> In the Chinese portion of its range, it is reported to be the most common forktail.<sup id="cite_ref-Walters2008_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Walters2008-8">[8]</a></sup> Though the population of the species is not precisely known, it is thought to be greater than 10,000 individuals, and is estimated to be stable. It is categorized as a species of least concern by the <a href="/wiki/International_Union_for_Conservation_of_Nature" title="International Union for Conservation of Nature">International Union for Conservation of Nature</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-IUCN_1-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IUCN-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Behavior_and_ecology">Behavior and ecology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=White-crowned_forktail&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Behavior and ecology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>As with other forktails, the white-crowned forktail keeps close to water. It has been observed to frequently wag its tail.<sup id="cite_ref-GrimmettInskipp2011_6-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GrimmettInskipp2011-6">[6]</a></sup> It forages along the edges of streams and in the water, primarily for insects, such as black beetles, water crickets, <a href="/wiki/Springtail" title="Springtail">springtails</a>, and caterpillars.<sup id="cite_ref-HBW_2-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HBW-2">[2]</a></sup> Described as a shy bird, it flies close to the ground, usually calling as it does.<sup id="cite_ref-Myers2016_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Myers2016-7">[7]</a></sup> It is thought to shift its elevational range slightly with the time of year.<sup id="cite_ref-HBW_2-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HBW-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>The white-crowned forktail breeds between the months of March and September, and possibly till October. The breeding period varies slightly across its range. Eggs have been recorded as early as March in Borneo, and on one occasion a parent with a fledgling was recorded in February.<sup id="cite_ref-Clement2016_5-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Clement2016-5">[5]</a></sup> The nest is constructed of moss, plants, leaves, and wood fibre, and is in the shape of a large cup. It is usually located near or over the water, and occasionally in forest gullies nearby. It is usually placed in a hole in a bank or cliff, or among tree roots, and has been observed to have been constructed behind waterfalls,<sup id="cite_ref-Clement2016_5-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Clement2016-5">[5]</a></sup> with the birds flying through the water to reach their nest. The location of the nest is always damp.<sup id="cite_ref-HBW_2-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HBW-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>The species usually lays between two and five eggs, which are creamy, pinkish, or greyish white, and covered in speckles of red-brown, salmon, and lilac. Birds in southern China have been observed to have two broods in a year, a pattern which may hold true elsewhere.<sup id="cite_ref-Clement2016_5-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Clement2016-5">[5]</a></sup> The number of eggs in a brood varies with latitude, with individuals in China regularly being recorded laying four to five eggs. The nests of the species have been observed in Myanmar to be parasitized by the <a href="/wiki/Drongo_cuckoo" class="mw-redirect" title="Drongo cuckoo">Drongo cuckoo</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Johnsgard1997_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Johnsgard1997-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
