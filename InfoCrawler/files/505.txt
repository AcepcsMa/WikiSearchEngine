			<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Peter Henlein</h1>
									<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
									<div id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
								<div id="contentSub"></div>
												<div id="jump-to-nav" class="mw-jump">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>
				</div>
				<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Peter_Henlein%2B.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e1/Peter_Henlein%2B.jpg/220px-Peter_Henlein%2B.jpg" width="220" height="293" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e1/Peter_Henlein%2B.jpg/330px-Peter_Henlein%2B.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e1/Peter_Henlein%2B.jpg/440px-Peter_Henlein%2B.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2736" data-file-height="3648" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Peter_Henlein%2B.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Monument to Henlein by Max Meißner, in Hefnersplatz, Nuremberg</div>
</div>
</div>
<p><b>Peter Henlein</b> (also spelled Henle or Hele)<sup id="cite_ref-Milham_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Milham-1">[1]</a></sup> (1485 - August 1542), a locksmith and clockmaker of <a href="/wiki/Nuremberg" title="Nuremberg">Nuremberg</a>, Germany, is often considered the inventor of the <a href="/wiki/Watch" title="Watch">watch</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Rossum_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rossum-2">[2]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Cipolla_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cipolla-3">[3]</a></sup> He was one of the first craftsmen to make small ornamental <i>Taschenuhren</i>, portable clocks which were often worn as pendants or attached to clothing,<sup id="cite_ref-Carlisle_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Carlisle-4">[4]</a></sup> regarded as the first watches. Many sources also erroneously credit him as the inventor of the <a href="/wiki/Mainspring" title="Mainspring">mainspring</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Milham_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Milham-1">[1]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Levy_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Levy-5">[5]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Britannica_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Britannica-6">[6]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Anzovin_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Anzovin-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Little is known about Henlein's life. He apparently apprenticed in his youth as a locksmith. At the time, locksmiths were among the few craftsmen with the skills and tools to enter the new field of clockmaking,<sup id="cite_ref-Farr_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Farr-8">[8]</a></sup> and Henlein also became a <a href="/wiki/Clockmaker" title="Clockmaker">clockmaker</a>. On September 7, 1504, he was involved in a brawl in which a fellow locksmith, George Glaser, was killed. He sought asylum at a local Franciscan monastery, where he stayed for four years, until 1508. In 1509 he became a master in the city's locksmith guild.<sup id="cite_ref-Rossum_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rossum-2">[2]</a></sup> He became known as a maker of small portable ornamental spring-powered brass clocks, very rare and expensive,<sup id="cite_ref-Rossum_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rossum-2">[2]</a></sup> which were fashionable among the nobility of the time. These were sometimes worn as pendants or attached to clothing,<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> and so may be considered the first watches, although at over 3 inches long<sup id="cite_ref-Carlisle_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Carlisle-4">[4]</a></sup> they were bigger than the first true <a href="/wiki/Pocketwatch" class="mw-redirect" title="Pocketwatch">pocketwatches</a> which appeared about a century later, and were not able to fit in pockets. He is mentioned in the city's records as the supplier of small spring-driven clocks, which were given as gifts to important people.<sup id="cite_ref-Rossum_2-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rossum-2">[2]</a></sup> He was supposedly the first craftsman to build clockworks into "Bisamköpfe", small containers fashioned from precious metals for fragrances or disinfectants.<sup id="cite_ref-Rossum_2-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rossum-2">[2]</a></sup> For example a Nuremberg paper records that in 1524 he was paid 15 florins for a gilt musk-ball watch.<sup id="cite_ref-Campbell_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Campbell-10">[10]</a></sup> He also built a <a href="/wiki/Tower_clock" class="mw-redirect" title="Tower clock">tower clock</a> for <a href="/wiki/Lichtenau,_Bavaria" title="Lichtenau, Bavaria">Lichtenau</a> castle in 1541, and was known as a maker of scientific instruments.<sup id="cite_ref-Rossum_2-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rossum-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Nuernberger_Ei_offen.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e7/Nuernberger_Ei_offen.jpg/220px-Nuernberger_Ei_offen.jpg" width="220" height="287" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/e/e7/Nuernberger_Ei_offen.jpg 1.5x" data-file-width="278" data-file-height="363" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Nuernberger_Ei_offen.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
An early "clock-watch" <i>(Taschenuhr)</i>, 2nd half of the 16th century.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Henlein's fame is mostly due to a passage by <a href="/wiki/Johann_Cochl%C3%A4us" class="mw-redirect" title="Johann Cochläus">Johann Cochläus</a> in the 1511 <i>Cosmographia</i> by Pomponius Mela:<sup id="cite_ref-Milham_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Milham-1">[1]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Rossum_2-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rossum-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<blockquote>
<p>Peter Hele, still a young man, fashions works which even the most learned mathematicians admire. He shapes many-wheeled clocks out of small bits of iron, which run and chime the hours without weights for forty hours, whether carried at the breast or in a handbag</p>
</blockquote>
<p>His reputation as the inventor of the watch came after his rise to popular consciousness in the 19th century, through a novel by Karl Spindler, <i>Der Nürnberger Sophokles</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-Rossum_2-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rossum-2">[2]</a></sup> This was made into <a href="/wiki/The_Immortal_Heart" title="The Immortal Heart">a 1939 film</a>, and his likeness appeared on a 1942 German stamp.<sup id="cite_ref-Rossum_2-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rossum-2">[2]</a></sup> However, although he was a notable and talented clockmaker, there were other clockmakers making small clocks at the time,<sup id="cite_ref-Cipolla_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cipolla-3">[3]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Farr_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Farr-8">[8]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Campbell_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Campbell-10">[10]</a></sup> and no contemporary source from his time credits him with inventing anything.<sup id="cite_ref-Rossum_2-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rossum-2">[2]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Mainspring" title="Mainspring">mainspring</a> which made portable clocks possible, often attributed to him,<sup id="cite_ref-Milham_1-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Milham-1">[1]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Levy_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Levy-5">[5]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Britannica_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Britannica-6">[6]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Anzovin_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Anzovin-7">[7]</a></sup> actually appeared in the early 15th century, almost a century before his work.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-White_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-White-12">[12]</a></sup> Perhaps the most that was said of him by his peers comes from Johann Neudorfer in 1547 shortly after his death:<sup id="cite_ref-Rossum_2-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rossum-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<blockquote>
<p>This . . . Henlein was very nearly the first of those who invented how to put small clocks into little boxes.</p>
</blockquote>
