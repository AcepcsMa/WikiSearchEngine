<p>A <b>fief</b> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="//: primary stress follows"></span><span title="'f' in 'find'">f</span><span title="/i/: 'ee' in 'fleece'">i</span><span title="'f' in 'find'">f</span></span>/</a></span></span>; <a href="/wiki/Latin_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Latin language">Latin</a>: <span lang="la" xml:lang="la"><i>feudum</i></span>) was the central element of <a href="/wiki/Feudalism" title="Feudalism">feudalism</a> and consisted of <a href="/wiki/Inheritance" title="Inheritance">heritable</a> property or rights granted by an <a href="/wiki/Lord" title="Lord">overlord</a> to a <a href="/wiki/Vassal" title="Vassal">vassal</a> who held it in <a href="/wiki/Fealty" title="Fealty">fealty</a> (or "in fee") in return for a form of feudal allegiance and service, usually given by the personal ceremonies of <a href="/wiki/Homage_(medieval)" class="mw-redirect" title="Homage (medieval)">homage</a> and fealty. The fees were often lands or revenue-producing real property held in feudal <a href="/wiki/Land_tenure" title="Land tenure">land tenure</a>: these are typically known as <b>fiefs</b> or <b>fiefdoms</b>. However, not only land but anything of value could be held in fee, including governmental office, rights of exploitation such as hunting or fishing, monopolies in trade, and <a href="/wiki/Tax_farm" class="mw-redirect" title="Tax farm">tax farms</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Terminology">Terminology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fief&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Terminology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Feudalism" title="Feudalism">Feudalism</a></div>
<table role="presentation" class="mbox-small plainlinks sistersitebox" style="background-color:#f9f9f9;border:1px solid #aaa;color:#000">
<tr>
<td class="mbox-image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/99/Wiktionary-logo-en-v2.svg/40px-Wiktionary-logo-en-v2.svg.png" width="40" height="40" class="noviewer" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/99/Wiktionary-logo-en-v2.svg/60px-Wiktionary-logo-en-v2.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/99/Wiktionary-logo-en-v2.svg/80px-Wiktionary-logo-en-v2.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="391" data-file-height="391" /></td>
<td class="mbox-text plainlist">Look up <i><b><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Special:Search/fief" class="extiw" title="wiktionary:Special:Search/fief">fief</a></b></i> in Wiktionary, the free dictionary.</td>
</tr>
</table>
<p>In ancient Rome a "<a href="/wiki/Benefice" title="Benefice">benefice</a>" (from the Latin noun <i>beneficium</i>, meaning "benefit") was a gift of land (<i><a href="/wiki/Precaria" class="mw-redirect" title="Precaria">precaria</a></i>) for life as a reward for services rendered, originally, to the state. In medieval Latin European documents, a land grant in exchange for service continued to be called a <i>beneficium</i> (Latin).<sup id="cite_ref-lubetski_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-lubetski-1">[1]</a></sup> Later, the term <i>feudum</i>, or <i>feodum</i>, began to replace <i>beneficium</i> in the documents.<sup id="cite_ref-lubetski_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-lubetski-1">[1]</a></sup> The first attested instance of this is from 984, although more primitive forms were seen up to one hundred years earlier.<sup id="cite_ref-lubetski_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-lubetski-1">[1]</a></sup> The origin of the <i>feudum</i> and why it replaced <i>beneficium</i> has not been well established, but there are multiple theories, described below.<sup id="cite_ref-lubetski_1-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-lubetski-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>The most widely held theory is put forth by <a href="/wiki/Marc_Bloch" title="Marc Bloch">Marc Bloch</a><sup id="cite_ref-lubetski_1-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-lubetski-1">[1]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-bloch-ety1_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bloch-ety1-2">[2]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-bloch-ety2_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bloch-ety2-3">[3]</a></sup> that it is related to the Frankish term <i>*fehu-d</i>, in which <i>*fehu</i> means "cattle" and <i>-d</i> means "goods", implying "a moveable object of value."<sup id="cite_ref-bloch-ety1_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bloch-ety1-2">[2]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-bloch-ety2_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bloch-ety2-3">[3]</a></sup> When land replaced currency as the primary <a href="/wiki/Store_of_value" title="Store of value">store of value</a>, the Germanic word <i>*fehu-d</i> replaced the Latin word <i>beneficium</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-bloch-ety1_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bloch-ety1-2">[2]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-bloch-ety2_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bloch-ety2-3">[3]</a></sup> This Germanic origin theory was also shared by <a href="/wiki/William_Stubbs" title="William Stubbs">William Stubbs</a> in the nineteenth century.<sup id="cite_ref-lubetski_1-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-lubetski-1">[1]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>A theory put forward by <a href="/wiki/Archibald_R._Lewis" class="mw-redirect" title="Archibald R. Lewis">Archibald R. Lewis</a><sup id="cite_ref-lubetski_1-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-lubetski-1">[1]</a></sup> that the origin of 'fief' is not <i>feudum</i> (or <i>feodum</i>), but rather <i>foderum</i>, the earliest attested use being in <a href="/wiki/Astronomus" class="mw-redirect" title="Astronomus">Astronomus</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Vita_Hludovici" title="Vita Hludovici">Vita Hludovici</a></i> (840).<sup id="cite_ref-arlewis_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-arlewis-5">[5]</a></sup> In that text is a passage about <a href="/wiki/Louis_the_Pious" title="Louis the Pious">Louis the Pious</a> which says <i>annona militaris quas vulgo foderum vocant</i>, which can be translated as "(Louis forbade that) military provender which they popularly call 'fodder' (be furnished)."<sup id="cite_ref-lubetski_1-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-lubetski-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>A theory by <a href="/w/index.php?title=Alauddin_Samarrai&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Alauddin Samarrai (page does not exist)">Alauddin Samarrai</a> suggests an Arabic origin, from <i>fuy</i> (the plural of <i>fay</i>, which literally means "the returned", and was used especially for 'land that has been conquered from enemies that did not fight').<sup id="cite_ref-lubetski_1-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-lubetski-1">[1]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-samarrai_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-samarrai-6">[6]</a></sup> Samarrai's theory is that early forms of 'fief' include <i>feo</i>, <i>feu</i>, <i>feuz</i>, <i>feuum</i> and others, the plurality of forms strongly suggesting origins from a <a href="/wiki/Loanword" title="Loanword">loanword</a>. First use of these terms was in <a href="/wiki/Languedoc" title="Languedoc">Languedoc</a>, one of the least-Germanized areas of Europe and bordered Muslim Spain, where the earliest use of <i>feuum</i> as a replacement for <i>beneficium</i> can be dated to 899, the same year a Muslim base at <a href="/wiki/Fraxinetum" class="mw-redirect" title="Fraxinetum">Fraxinetum</a> (<a href="/wiki/La_Garde-Freinet" title="La Garde-Freinet">La Garde-Freinet</a>) in <a href="/wiki/Provence" title="Provence">Provence</a> was established. It is possible, Samarrai says, that French scribes, writing in Latin, attempted to <a href="/wiki/Transliteration" title="Transliteration">transliterate</a> the Arabic word <i>fuy</i> (the plural of <i>fay</i>), which was being used by the Muslim invaders and occupiers at the time, resulting in a plurality of forms - <i>feo, feu, feuz, feuum</i> and others - from which eventually <i>feudum</i> derived. Samarrai, however, also advises medieval and early modern Muslim scribes often used etymologically "fanciful roots" in order to claim the most outlandish things to be of Arabian or Muslim origin.<sup id="cite_ref-samarrai_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-samarrai-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the 10th and 11th centuries the Latin terms for fee could be used either to describe dependent tenure held by a man from his lord, as the term is used now by historians, or it could mean simply "property" (the <a href="/wiki/Manor" title="Manor">manor</a> was, in effect, a small fief). It lacked a precise meaning until the middle of the 12th century, when it received formal definition from land lawyers.</p>
<p>In English usage, the word "fee" is first attested around 12501300 (Middle English); the word <i>fief</i> from around 160515. In French, the term <i>fief</i> is found from the middle of the 13th century (Old French), derived from the 11th-century terms <i>feu</i>, <i>fie</i>. The odd appearance of the second <i>f</i> in the form <i>fief</i> may be due to influence from the verb <i>fiever</i> 'to grant in fee'.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> In French, one also finds "seigneurie" (land and rights possessed by a "seigneur" or "lord", 12th-century), which gives rise to the expression "seigneurial system" to describe feudalism.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_feudal_grants">Early feudal grants</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fief&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Early feudal grants">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Originally, <a href="/wiki/Vassal" title="Vassal">vassalage</a> did not imply the giving or receiving of landholdings (which were granted only as a reward for loyalty), but by the eighth century the giving of a landholding was becoming standard.<sup id="cite_ref-Cantor_1993.2C_pp._198-199_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cantor_1993.2C_pp._198-199-8">[8]</a></sup> The granting of a landholding to a vassal did not relinquish the lord's property rights, but only the use of the lands and their income; the granting lord retained ultimate ownership of the fee and could, technically, recover the lands in case of disloyalty or death.<sup id="cite_ref-Cantor_1993.2C_pp._198-199_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cantor_1993.2C_pp._198-199-8">[8]</a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/Francia" title="Francia">8th-century France</a>, <a href="/wiki/Charles_Martel" title="Charles Martel">Charles Martel</a> was the first to make large-scale and systematic use (the practice had remained until then sporadic) of the remuneration of vassals by the concession of the usufruct of lands (a <i>beneficatium</i> or "<a href="/wiki/Benefice" title="Benefice">benefice</a>" in the documents) for the life of the vassal, or, sometimes extending to the second or third generation.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>By the middle of the 10th century, fee had largely become hereditary.<sup id="cite_ref-Cantor.2C_200_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cantor.2C_200-10">[10]</a></sup> The eldest son of a deceased vassal would inherit, but first he had to do homage and fealty to the lord and pay a "<a href="/wiki/Feudal_relief" title="Feudal relief">relief</a>" for the land (a monetary recognition of the lord's continuing proprietary rights over the property).</p>
<p>Historically, the fees of the 11th and the 12th century derived from two separate sources. The first was land carved out of the estates of the upper nobility. The second source was <a href="/wiki/Allod" title="Allod">allodial</a> land transformed into dependent tenures.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> During the 10th century in northern France and the 11th century in France south of the <a href="/wiki/Loire" title="Loire">Loire</a>, local magnates either recruited or forced the owners of allodial holdings into dependent relationships and they were turned into fiefs. The process occurred later in Germany, and was still going on in the 13th century.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>In England, <a href="/wiki/Henry_II_of_England" title="Henry II of England">Henry II</a> transformed them into important sources of royal income and patronage. The discontent of <a href="/wiki/Baron" title="Baron">barons</a> with royal claims to arbitrarily assessed "reliefs" and other feudal payments under Henry's son <a href="/wiki/John_of_England" class="mw-redirect" title="John of England">King John</a> resulted in <a href="/wiki/Magna_Carta" title="Magna Carta">Magna Carta</a> of 1215.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Eventually, great feudal lords sought also to seize governmental and legal authority (the collection of taxes, the right of high justice, etc.) in their lands, and some passed these rights to their own vassals.<sup id="cite_ref-Cantor.2C_200_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cantor.2C_200-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>The privilege of minting official coins developed into the concept of <a href="/wiki/Seigniorage" title="Seigniorage">seigniorage</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Later_feudal_grants_and_knightly_service">Later feudal grants and knightly service</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fief&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Later feudal grants and knightly service">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 13th-century Germany, Italy, England, France, and Spain the term "feodum" was used to describe a dependent tenure held from a lord by a vassal in return for a specified amount of <a href="/wiki/Knight" title="Knight">knight</a> service and occasional financial payments (<a href="/w/index.php?title=Feudal_incidents&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Feudal incidents (page does not exist)">feudal incidents</a>).</p>
<p>However, knight service in war was far less common than:</p>
<ul>
<li>castle-guard (called <i><a href="/wiki/Burghut" class="mw-redirect" title="Burghut">Burghut</a></i> in the Holy Roman Empire) the obligation of a vassal to serve in a <a href="/wiki/Castle" title="Castle">castle</a> <a href="/wiki/Garrison" title="Garrison">garrison</a> of the lord),</li>
<li>suit in court (the vassal's obligation to attend the lord's <a href="/wiki/Court_(royal)" title="Court (royal)">court</a>, to give him counsel, and to help him judge disputes)</li>
<li>attendance in the lord's entourage (accompanying the lord when he travelled or attended the court of his lord so as to increase the social status of the lord),</li>
<li>hospitality to the lord or to his servants (accommodation).</li>
</ul>

<p>A lord in late 12th-century England and France could also claim the right of:</p>
<ul>
<li>wardship and marriage - right to control descent of fee by choosing husband for female heir and guardian of minors (preferably in consultation with heir's closest male adult kinsmen);</li>
<li>"aids" - payments to aid the lord in times of need (customarily given to the lord to cover the cost of knighting of eldest son, marriage of eldest daughter, and for <a href="/wiki/Ransom" title="Ransom">ransoming</a> of lord if required);</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Escheat" title="Escheat">escheat</a> - the reversion of the fief to the lord in default of an heir.<sup id="cite_ref-abels_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-abels-11">[11]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<p>In northern France in the 12th and 13th centuries, military service for fiefs was limited for offensive campaigns to 40 days for a knight. By the 12th century, English and French kings and barons began to commute military service for cash payments (<a href="/wiki/Scutage" title="Scutage">scutages</a>), with which they could purchase the service of <a href="/wiki/Mercenaries" class="mw-redirect" title="Mercenaries">mercenaries</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-abels_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-abels-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Feudal_registers">Feudal registers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fief&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Feudal registers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A list of several hundred such fees held <a href="/wiki/Tenant-in-chief" title="Tenant-in-chief">in chief</a> between 1198 and 1292, along with their holders' names and form of tenure, was published in three volumes between 1920 and 1931 and is known as <i><a href="/wiki/Book_of_Fees" title="Book of Fees">The Book of Fees</a></i>; it was developed from the 1302 <i><a href="/wiki/Testa_de_Nevill" class="mw-redirect" title="Testa de Nevill">Testa de Nevill</a></i>.</p>
