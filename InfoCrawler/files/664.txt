			<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Sister city</h1>

<p><b>Twin towns</b> or <b>sister cities</b> are <a href="/wiki/International_relations" title="International relations">a form of legal or social agreement</a> between towns, cities, counties, <a href="/wiki/Oblast" title="Oblast">oblasts</a>, <a href="/wiki/Prefecture" title="Prefecture">prefectures</a>, provinces, regions, states, and even countries in geographically and politically distinct areas to promote cultural and commercial ties.<sup id="cite_ref-Clarke_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Clarke-1">[1]</a></sup> The modern concept of town twinning, conceived after the <a href="/wiki/Second_World_War" class="mw-redirect" title="Second World War">Second World War</a> in 1947, was intended to foster friendship and understanding between different cultures and between former foes as an act of peace and reconciliation,<sup id="cite_ref-Origins_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Origins-2">[2]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Limousin_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Limousin-3">[3]</a></sup> and to encourage trade and tourism.<sup id="cite_ref-Clarke_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Clarke-1">[1]</a></sup> In recent times, town twinning has increasingly been used to form strategic international business links between member cities.<sup id="cite_ref-BBC_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BBC-4">[4]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Partners_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Partners-5">[5]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Terminology">Terminology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sister_city&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Terminology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Gemellaggio_Castellabate-Blieskastel.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/58/Gemellaggio_Castellabate-Blieskastel.JPG/220px-Gemellaggio_Castellabate-Blieskastel.JPG" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/58/Gemellaggio_Castellabate-Blieskastel.JPG/330px-Gemellaggio_Castellabate-Blieskastel.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/58/Gemellaggio_Castellabate-Blieskastel.JPG/440px-Gemellaggio_Castellabate-Blieskastel.JPG 2x" data-file-width="800" data-file-height="600" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Gemellaggio_Castellabate-Blieskastel.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
An example of a 'gemellaggio' (twinning) agreement between <a href="/wiki/Castellabate" title="Castellabate">Castellabate</a>, Italy and <a href="/wiki/Blieskastel" title="Blieskastel">Blieskastel</a>, Germany</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>In the United Kingdom, the term "twin towns" is most commonly used; the term "sister cities" is generally used for agreements with towns and cities in the Americas.<sup id="cite_ref-Clarke_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Clarke-1">[1]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-EU_Commission_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EU_Commission-6">[6]</a></sup> In mainland Europe, the most commonly used terms are "twin towns", "partnership towns", "partner towns", and "friendship towns". The <a href="/wiki/European_Commission" title="European Commission">European Commission</a> uses the term "twinned towns" and refers to the process as "town twinning".<sup id="cite_ref-Clarke_1-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Clarke-1">[1]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-EU_Commission_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EU_Commission-6">[6]</a></sup> Spain uses the term "ciudades hermanadas", which means "sister cities". Germany, Poland, and the Czech Republic use <i>Partnerstadt</i> (De) / <i>Miasto Partnerskie</i> (Pl) / <i>Partnerské město</i> (Cz), which translate as "Partner Town or City". France uses <i>Ville Jumelée</i> (Twinned Town or City), and Italy has <i>Gemellaggio</i> (twinning) and <i>Comune gemellato</i> (twinned municipality).<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> In the Netherlands, the term is <i>Stedenband</i> (Cities-bond). In Greece, the word αδελφοποίηση (<i>adelfopiisi</i>&#160;– fraternisation) has been adopted. In Iceland, the terms <i>vinabæir</i> (friend towns) and <i>vinaborgir</i> (friend cities) are used. In the former <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Bloc" title="Eastern Bloc">Soviet Bloc</a>, "twin towns" and "twin cities" are used,<sup id="cite_ref-Mogilev_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mogilev-8">[8]</a></sup> along with <i>города-побратимы</i> (Ru) (sworn brother cities).<sup id="cite_ref-Trend_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Trend-9">[9]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Americas, South Asia, and Australasia use the term "sister cities" or "twin cities". In China, the term is 友好城市 (yǒuhǎo chéngshì&#160;– friendship city).<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> Sometimes, other government bodies enter into a twinning relationship, such as the agreement between the provinces of Hainan in China and Jeju-do in South Korea. The <a href="/wiki/Douzelage" title="Douzelage">Douzelage</a> is a town twinning association with one town from each of the member states of the European Union.<sup id="cite_ref-Clarke_1-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Clarke-1">[1]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Douzelage_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Douzelage-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="City_diplomacy">City diplomacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sister_city&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: City diplomacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In recent years, the term "city diplomacy" has gained increased usage and acceptance, particularly as a strand of <a href="/wiki/Paradiplomacy" title="Paradiplomacy">paradiplomacy</a> and <a href="/wiki/Public_diplomacy" title="Public diplomacy">public diplomacy</a>. It is formally used in the workings of the <a href="/wiki/United_Cities_and_Local_Governments" title="United Cities and Local Governments">United Cities and Local Governments</a> and the <a href="/wiki/C40_Cities_Climate_Leadership_Group" title="C40 Cities Climate Leadership Group">C40 Cities Climate Leadership Group</a> and recognised by the <a href="/wiki/USC_Center_on_Public_Diplomacy" title="USC Center on Public Diplomacy">USC Center on Public Diplomacy</a>. A March 2014 debate in the British <a href="/wiki/House_of_Lords" title="House of Lords">House of Lords</a> acknowledged the evolution of town twinning into city diplomacy, particularly around trade and tourism, but also in culture and post-conflict reconciliation.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> The importance of cities developing "their own foreign economic policies on trade, foreign investment, tourism and attracting foreign talent" has also been highlighted by the <a href="/wiki/World_Economic_Forum" title="World Economic Forum">World Economic Forum</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Europe">Europe</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sister_city&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Europe">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">For a more comprehensive list, see <a href="/wiki/List_of_twin_towns_and_sister_cities_in_Europe" class="mw-redirect" title="List of twin towns and sister cities in Europe">List of twin towns and sister cities in Europe</a>.</div>

<p>The earliest known town twinning in Europe was between <a href="/wiki/Paderborn" title="Paderborn">Paderborn</a>, Germany, and <a href="/wiki/Le_Mans" title="Le Mans">Le Mans</a>, France, in 836.<sup id="cite_ref-Origins_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Origins-2">[2]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Balavoine1994_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Balavoine1994-15">[15]</a></sup> Starting in 1905, <a href="/wiki/Keighley" title="Keighley">Keighley</a> in West Yorkshire, England, had a twinning arrangement with French communities <a href="/wiki/Suresnes" title="Suresnes">Suresnes</a> and <a href="/wiki/Puteaux" title="Puteaux">Puteaux</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-France_Magazine_-_Twin_Towns_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-France_Magazine_-_Twin_Towns-17">[17]</a></sup> The first recorded modern twinning agreement was between Keighley and <a href="/wiki/Poix-du-Nord" title="Poix-du-Nord">Poix-du-Nord</a> in Nord, France, in 1920 following the end of the <a href="/wiki/First_World_War" class="mw-redirect" title="First World War">First World War</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Partners_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Partners-5">[5]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-France_Magazine_-_Twin_Towns_17-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-France_Magazine_-_Twin_Towns-17">[17]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-lga_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-lga-18">[18]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-East_Street_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-East_Street-19">[19]</a></sup> This was initially referred to as an adoption of the French town; formal twinning charters were not exchanged until 1986.<sup id="cite_ref-telegraph.26argus_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-telegraph.26argus-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>The practice was continued after the Second World War as a way to promote mutual understanding and cross-border projects of mutual benefit.<sup id="cite_ref-Clarke_1-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Clarke-1">[1]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Origins_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Origins-2">[2]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Twinning1_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Twinning1-21">[21]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Twin_Towns_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Twin_Towns-22">[22]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Regional_Dev_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Regional_Dev-23">[23]</a></sup> For example, <a href="/wiki/Coventry" title="Coventry">Coventry</a> twinned with <a href="/wiki/Volgograd" title="Volgograd">Stalingrad</a> and later with <a href="/wiki/Dresden" title="Dresden">Dresden</a> as an act of peace and reconciliation, all three cities having been heavily bombed during the war.<sup id="cite_ref-Clarke_1-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Clarke-1">[1]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-East_Street_19-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-East_Street-19">[19]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Coventry_twinnings_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Coventry_twinnings-24">[24]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Coventry_twins_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Coventry_twins-25">[25]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> The City of <a href="/wiki/Bath,_Somerset" title="Bath, Somerset">Bath</a> formed an "<a href="/wiki/Alkmaar" title="Alkmaar">Alkmaar</a> Adoption committee" in March 1945, when the Dutch city was still occupied by the German Army in the final months of the war, and children from each city took part in exchanges in 1945 and 1946.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> Similarly, in 1947, <a href="/wiki/Bristol" title="Bristol">Bristol</a> Corporation (later Bristol City Council) sent five 'leading citizens' on a goodwill mission to <a href="/wiki/Hanover" title="Hanover">Hanover</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Partners_5-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Partners-5">[5]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-East_Street_19-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-East_Street-19">[19]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Reading,_Berkshire" title="Reading, Berkshire">Reading</a> in 1947 was the first British town to form links with a former "enemy" city – <a href="/wiki/D%C3%BCsseldorf" title="Düsseldorf">Düsseldorf</a>. The link still exists (Reading-Düsseldorf Association: <a rel="nofollow" class="external free" href="http://www.reading-dusseldorf.org.uk/">http://www.reading-dusseldorf.org.uk/</a>). Since 9 April 1956 Rome and Paris have been exclusively and reciprocally twinned with each other, following the motto: "Only Paris is worthy of Rome; only Rome is worthy of Paris."<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Paris1_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Paris1-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<p>Within Europe, town twinning is supported by the European Union.<sup id="cite_ref-Clarke_1-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Clarke-1">[1]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Origins_2-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Origins-2">[2]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Partners_5-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Partners-5">[5]</a></sup> The support scheme was established in 1989. In 2003 an annual budget of about <a href="/wiki/Euro" title="Euro">€</a>12&#160;million was allocated to about 1,300 projects. The <a href="/wiki/Council_of_European_Municipalities_and_Regions" title="Council of European Municipalities and Regions">Council of European Municipalities and Regions</a> also works closely with the Commission (DG Education and Culture) to promote modern, high quality twinning initiatives and exchanges that involve all sections of the community. It has launched a website dedicated to town twinning.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> As of 1995, the European Union had more than 7,000 bilateral relationships involving almost 10,000 European municipalities, primarily French (2837 twinnings) and German (2485 twinnings).<sup id="cite_ref-Regional_Dev_23-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Regional_Dev-23">[23]</a></sup></p>

<p><a href="/wiki/Public_art" title="Public art">Public art</a> has been used to celebrate twin town links, for instance in the form of seven mural paintings in the centre of the town of <a href="/wiki/Sutton,_London" title="Sutton, London">Sutton</a>, Greater London. The five main paintings show a number of the main features of the <a href="/wiki/London_Borough_of_Sutton" title="London Borough of Sutton">London Borough of Sutton</a> and its four twin towns, along with the <a href="/wiki/Escutcheon_(heraldry)" title="Escutcheon (heraldry)">heraldic shield</a> of each above the other images. Each painting also features a plant as a visual representation of its town's <a href="/wiki/Environmentalism" title="Environmentalism">environmental awareness</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> In the case of Sutton this is in a separate smaller painting (above its main one) showing a beech tree, intended as a symbol of prosperity and from which <a href="/wiki/Carshalton" title="Carshalton">Carshalton Beeches</a> in the borough derives its name.<sup id="cite_ref-Press_release_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Press_release-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<p>Another example of the use of public art is the wall sculpture of the partner cities of <a href="/wiki/Munich" title="Munich">Munich</a>, Germany.</p>
<p>A recent study has concluded that geographical distance has very little, if any, influence upon communities' selections of a twin town.<sup id="cite_ref-Arxiv_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Arxiv-33">[33]</a></sup> Twinned towns are often chosen because of similarities between them; thus about 15 towns in Wales are twinned with towns in <a href="/wiki/Brittany" title="Brittany">Brittany</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Oxford" title="Oxford">Oxford</a> is with <a href="/wiki/Bonn" title="Bonn">Bonn</a>, <a href="/wiki/Leiden" title="Leiden">Leiden</a>, <a href="/wiki/Grenoble" title="Grenoble">Grenoble</a> and other university cities.<sup id="cite_ref-Clarke_1-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Clarke-1">[1]</a></sup> Many former <a href="/wiki/West_Germany" title="West Germany">West German</a> cities are twinned with former East German cities; these twinning links were established before the fall of the <a href="/wiki/Iron_Curtain" title="Iron Curtain">Iron Curtain</a>. Famous examples are the partnerships of Hanover and <a href="/wiki/Leipzig" title="Leipzig">Leipzig</a>, both of which have important <a href="/wiki/Trade_fair" title="Trade fair">trade fair grounds</a>, or between <a href="/wiki/Hamburg" title="Hamburg">Hamburg</a> and Dresden. The first US-German town twinning was in 1947 between <a href="/wiki/Worthington,_Minnesota" title="Worthington, Minnesota">Worthington, Minnesota</a> and <a href="/wiki/Crailsheim" title="Crailsheim">Crailsheim</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Clarke_1-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Clarke-1">[1]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/St_Petersburg#Twin_towns_and_sister_cities" class="mw-redirect" title="St Petersburg">St Petersburg</a> in Russia holds the record for the largest number of partnership arrangements with other communities.<sup id="cite_ref-Arxiv_33-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Arxiv-33">[33]</a></sup> In June 2012, the Scottish village of <a href="/wiki/Dull,_Perth_and_Kinross" title="Dull, Perth and Kinross">Dull</a> and the US town of <a href="/wiki/Boring,_Oregon" title="Boring, Oregon">Boring, Oregon</a>, agreed to twin their municipalities to promote tourism in both places, playing on their names.<sup id="cite_ref-Dull_and_Boring_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dull_and_Boring-34">[34]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
<p>Recently some towns have made novelty twinning arrangements with fictional or virtual locations. <a href="/wiki/Wincanton" title="Wincanton">Wincanton</a>, England is partnered with <a href="/wiki/Ankh-Morpork" title="Ankh-Morpork">Ankh-Morpork</a> from <a href="/wiki/Terry_Pratchett" title="Terry Pratchett">Terry Pratchett</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Discworld" title="Discworld">Discworld books</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-BBC.2FPratchett_37-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BBC.2FPratchett-37">[37]</a></sup></p>
<p>Town twinning has increasingly been used to form strategic international business links. For example, in the 1990s, when the <a href="/wiki/Nottingham_City_Council" title="Nottingham City Council">Nottingham City Council</a> in the UK considered installing a tram network, it consulted experts from its twin city of <a href="/wiki/Karlsruhe" title="Karlsruhe">Karlsruhe</a>, which has one of the most extensive and efficient tram networks in Germany. With assistance from Karlsruhe's specialist engineers, Nottingham completed its second tram line in 2013.<sup id="cite_ref-BBC_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BBC-4">[4]</a></sup> More recently Bristol and New Orleans have announced their intention to form a 'tuning' partnership based on a shared musical heritage and culture offer, at the initiative of Bristol Mayor <a href="/wiki/George_Ferguson_(architect)" class="mw-redirect" title="George Ferguson (architect)">George Ferguson</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Post.2FFerguson_38-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Post.2FFerguson-38">[38]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Annecy" title="Annecy">Annecy</a>, France and <a href="/wiki/Nerima" title="Nerima">Nerima</a>, Tokyo have for several years shared a partnership based around their "co-existent animation industry".<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="North_America">North America</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sister_city&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: North America">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">For a more comprehensive list, see <a href="/wiki/List_of_twin_towns_and_sister_cities_in_North_America" title="List of twin towns and sister cities in North America">List of twin towns and sister cities in North America</a>.</div>

<p>The first city in North America to engage in town twinning was <a href="/wiki/Toledo,_Ohio" title="Toledo, Ohio">Toledo</a>, Ohio, which twinned with <a href="/wiki/Toledo,_Spain" title="Toledo, Spain">Toledo</a>, Spain in 1931. <a href="/wiki/Vancouver" title="Vancouver">Vancouver</a>, British Columbia entered into an intercontinental twinning arrangement in 1944 with <a href="/wiki/Odessa" title="Odessa">Odessa</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ukraine" title="Ukraine">Ukraine</a>, which was part of the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet Union</a> at the time. The initiative was based on Canada's aid to the allied port city during the Second World War. <a href="/wiki/Liberal,_Kansas" title="Liberal, Kansas">Liberal</a>, Kansas was twinned with <a href="/wiki/Olney,_Buckinghamshire" title="Olney, Buckinghamshire">Olney</a>, Buckinghamshire in 1950, and the cities have run a joint <a href="/wiki/Pancake#Pancake_Day" title="Pancake">Pancake Day</a> race ever since.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Olney_race_42-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Olney_race-42">[42]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Tashkent" title="Tashkent">Tashkent</a>, the capital city of Uzbekistan and also formerly part of the Soviet Union, was twinned with <a href="/wiki/Seattle" title="Seattle">Seattle</a>, Washington in 1973. Another first for twin towns occurred in 1967 between <a href="/wiki/Rochester,_Minnesota" title="Rochester, Minnesota">Rochester, Minnesota</a> and <a href="/wiki/Knebworth" title="Knebworth">Knebworth</a>, UK, both centres for primary medical research.</p>
<p>Town twinning begins for a variety of reasons. Generally, partner towns share similar demographics and size. They may arise from business connections, travel, similar industries, diaspora communities, or shared history. For example, the partnership between <a href="/wiki/Portland,_Oregon" title="Portland, Oregon">Portland, Oregon</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bologna" title="Bologna">Bologna</a>, Italy arose from shared industries in <a href="/wiki/Biotechnology" title="Biotechnology">biotechnology</a> and education, appreciation for the arts, and a "similar attitude towards food";<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> whereas Chicago's link with <a href="/wiki/Warsaw" title="Warsaw">Warsaw</a>, Poland began with Chicago's historic <a href="/wiki/Poles_in_Chicago" title="Poles in Chicago">Polish community</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> and the twinning of <a href="/wiki/Indianapolis" title="Indianapolis">Indianapolis</a> with <a href="/wiki/Monza" title="Monza">Monza</a>, Italy is due to both cities' long association with <a href="/wiki/Auto_racing" title="Auto racing">auto racing</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sister_city&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft">

<p>A twin towns program was instituted in the United States in 1956 when President <a href="/wiki/Dwight_D._Eisenhower" title="Dwight D. Eisenhower">Dwight D. Eisenhower</a> proposed a people-to-people, citizen diplomacy initiative. Originally a program of the <a href="/wiki/National_League_of_Cities" title="National League of Cities">National League of Cities</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sister_Cities_International" title="Sister Cities International">Sister Cities International</a> (SCI) became a separate corporation in 1967 due to the growth and popularity of the US program.<sup id="cite_ref-sister-cities.org_45-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sister-cities.org-45">[45]</a></sup> SCI is now a nonprofit citizen diplomacy network that creates and strengthens partnerships between communities in the US and other countries, organises cultural exchanges, and provides support and funding. Under its administration, more than 2,000 cities, states and counties are partnered in 136 countries.<sup id="cite_ref-sister-cities.org_45-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sister-cities.org-45">[45]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-mission_46-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mission-46">[46]</a></sup></p>

<a href="/wiki/Thessaloniki" title="Thessaloniki">Thessaloniki</a> <a href="/wiki/Stele" title="Stele">stele</a>, in twin town <a href="/wiki/Melbourne" title="Melbourne">Melbourne</a><sup id="cite_ref-Greek_twinnings_47-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Greek_twinnings-47">[47]</a></sup></div>

<p>According to the SCI website, these exchanges include "musical performances, art exhibits, construction of peace parks or tea gardens, international cultural festivals, and teacher exchanges".<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup></p>
<p>Twin town cultural events include the annual <a href="/wiki/National_Cherry_Blossom_Festival" title="National Cherry Blossom Festival">National Cherry Blossom Festival</a> in Washington, D.C., honouring Washington's twin relationship with <a href="/wiki/Tokyo_City" title="Tokyo City">Tokyo City</a>. Capitalizing on the growing world economy, many twinned towns developed business agreements with their partners. For example, <a href="/wiki/Vermont" title="Vermont">Vermont</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Ben_%26_Jerry%27s" title="Ben &amp; Jerry's">Ben &amp; Jerry's</a> Ice Cream company opened a factory in <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Karelia" title="Republic of Karelia">Karelia, Russia</a>, and offered the same profit-sharing plan to its Russian employees as its American employees enjoyed. While not a primary goal, business relationships are a natural by-product of twin town exchanges.</p>
<p>In 1995, the <a href="/wiki/US_House_of_Representatives" class="mw-redirect" title="US House of Representatives">US House of Representatives</a> passed legislation to eliminate the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Information_Agency" title="United States Information Agency">United States Information Agency</a> (USIA) with vocal support from the <a href="/wiki/US_Conference_of_Mayors" class="mw-redirect" title="US Conference of Mayors">US Conference of Mayors</a> International Affairs Committee and Sister City members. The broadcasting functions of the USIA were maintained by the now independent <a href="/wiki/Broadcasting_Board_of_Governors" title="Broadcasting Board of Governors">Broadcasting Board of Governors</a> (BBG); all other continuing functions became part of the organizational structure overseen by the purposefully established <a href="/wiki/Under_Secretary_of_State_for_Public_Diplomacy_and_Public_Affairs" title="Under Secretary of State for Public Diplomacy and Public Affairs">Under Secretary of State for Public Diplomacy and Public Affairs</a> within the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_State" title="United States Department of State">United States Department of State</a> (DoS). SCI is a leading member of a consortium of non-profit NGOs that partner with the DoS <a href="/wiki/Bureau_of_Educational_and_Cultural_Affairs" title="Bureau of Educational and Cultural Affairs">Bureau of Educational and Cultural Affairs</a> (ECA), which was created as part of this new organizational structure.</p>
<p>Through the grant funding, logistical support and other resources made available through the ECA, SCI and other organisations develop, administer and promote the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Cultural_Exchange_Programs" title="United States Cultural Exchange Programs">United States Cultural Exchange Programs</a> (CEPs). One such CEP is the Kennedy-Lugar <a href="/wiki/Youth_Exchange_and_Study_Programs" title="Youth Exchange and Study Programs">Youth Exchange and Study Programs</a> (Yes Programs), which sponsor students predominantly from the Middle East to study for a year in the US. On a 2004 exchange, Arab students from Gaza, Jordan, Kuwait, Lebanon, Syria, West Bank, Tunisia and Yemen lived in the US for a year with host families and attended a leadership summit in <a href="/wiki/Boulder,_Colorado" title="Boulder, Colorado">Boulder, Colorado</a>. To further the Yes Program's goals, Sister Cities International developed the Youth and Education Network in 2004.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Asia">Asia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sister_city&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Asia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">For a more comprehensive list, see <a href="/wiki/List_of_twin_towns_and_sister_cities_in_Asia" title="List of twin towns and sister cities in Asia">List of twin towns and sister cities in Asia</a>.</div>

<p>Town twinning is supported in Japan by the <a href="/wiki/Council_of_Local_Authorities_for_International_Relations" title="Council of Local Authorities for International Relations">Council of Local Authorities for International Relations</a>, a joint agency of local governments established by the Japanese government in 1988 (similar to <a href="/wiki/Sister_Cities_International" title="Sister Cities International">Sister Cities International</a>, its counterpart in the US). More recently, Tokyo has begun to actively promote 'city diplomacy' with other global cities at the initiative of its governor <a href="/wiki/Yoichi_Masuzoe" class="mw-redirect" title="Yoichi Masuzoe">Yoichi Masuzoe</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Daman,_Daman_and_Diu" title="Daman, Daman and Diu">Daman</a>, <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a> is a twin town of the city of <a href="/wiki/Coimbra" title="Coimbra">Coimbra</a>, Portugal.<sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Linguistic_reasons">Linguistic reasons</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sister_city&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Linguistic reasons">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Relationships between communities can also arise because of shared names; they may be named after one community (as in the case of <a href="/wiki/C%C3%B3rdoba,_Andalusia#Twin_towns_.E2.80.93_sister_cities" class="mw-redirect" title="Córdoba, Andalusia">Córdoba</a>), they may share names (as in the case of <a href="/wiki/Santiago_de_Compostela#Twin_towns_.E2.80.93_Sister_cities" title="Santiago de Compostela">Santiago de Compostela</a>), or their names may have a common <a href="/wiki/Etymology" title="Etymology">etymology</a>.</p>

<p>These similarities usually arise from sharing the same or related language, or from having been a colony or previously conquered.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Political_purposes">Political purposes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sister_city&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Political purposes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Twinning towns and cities is sometimes done for political purposes. The Hungarian city <a href="/wiki/Gy%C3%B6ngy%C3%B6s" title="Gyöngyös">Gyöngyös</a> was twinned with the Azerbaijani city of <a href="/wiki/Shusha" title="Shusha">Shusha</a> in 2013, signing the twinning agreement with representatives from the Azerbaijani government; Hungary recognises Shusha as part of Azerbaijan, even though since the end of the <a href="/wiki/Karabakh_War" class="mw-redirect" title="Karabakh War">Karabakh War</a> it has been controlled by the military forces of <a href="/wiki/Armenia" title="Armenia">Armenia</a> and the unrecognised <a href="/wiki/Nagorno-Karabakh_Republic" class="mw-redirect" title="Nagorno-Karabakh Republic">Nagorno-Karabakh Republic</a> (<i><a href="/wiki/De_jure" title="De jure">de jure</a></i> part of Azerbaijan).<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup> An attempt was made in 2003 by <a href="/wiki/Preston,_Lancashire" title="Preston, Lancashire">Preston</a> city councillors in England to twin with the Palestinian town of <a href="/wiki/Nablus" title="Nablus">Nablus</a> in the name of solidarity.<sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup></p>
<p>Recently, city partnership termination—not just creation—started to be used for political purposes. In 2013, <a href="/wiki/Milan" title="Milan">Milan</a> and <a href="/wiki/Venice" title="Venice">Venice</a>, formerly twinned with <a href="/wiki/Saint_Petersburg" title="Saint Petersburg">Saint Petersburg</a>, suspended their links due to St Petersburg's ban on "promotion of homosexuality to minors".<sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup> Other cities considered similar steps,<sup id="cite_ref-Bay_Area_News_54-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bay_Area_News-54">[54]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-National_Geographic_55-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-National_Geographic-55">[55]</a></sup> namely Los Angeles and <a href="/wiki/City_of_Melbourne" title="City of Melbourne">Melbourne</a>. The cities of <a href="/wiki/Glendale,_California" title="Glendale, California">Glendale, California</a> and <a href="/wiki/Higashiosaka" class="mw-redirect" title="Higashiosaka">Higashiosaka</a>, Japan came close to terminating their twinning in 2013/14 on account of an ongoing dispute over Glendale's support for the erection of a statue dedicated to Korean <a href="/wiki/Comfort_women" title="Comfort women">comfort women</a> in a city park.<sup id="cite_ref-56" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup> In 2014, <a href="/wiki/Prague" title="Prague">Prague</a> terminated its partnership with <a href="/wiki/Saint_Petersburg" title="Saint Petersburg">Saint Petersburg</a> and Moscow because of the <a href="/wiki/2014_Russian_military_intervention_in_Ukraine" class="mw-redirect" title="2014 Russian military intervention in Ukraine">Russian military intervention</a> in Ukraine. Moscow had been Prague's partner city since 1995.<sup id="cite_ref-Prague_Post_57-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Prague_Post-57">[57]</a></sup></p>
