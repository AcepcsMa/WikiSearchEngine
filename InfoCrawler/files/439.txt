<p><b>Julius Streicher</b> (12 February 1885 – 16 October 1946) was a prominent member of the <a href="/wiki/Nazi_Party" title="Nazi Party">Nazi Party</a> (NSDAP) prior to <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>. He was the founder and publisher of the <a href="/wiki/Anti-Semitic" class="mw-redirect" title="Anti-Semitic">anti-Semitic</a> newspaper <i><a href="/wiki/Der_St%C3%BCrmer" title="Der Stürmer">Der Stürmer</a></i>, which became a central element of the <a href="/wiki/Nazi_propaganda" class="mw-redirect" title="Nazi propaganda">Nazi propaganda</a> machine. His publishing firm also released three anti-Semitic books for children, including the 1938 <i><a href="/wiki/Der_Giftpilz" title="Der Giftpilz">Der Giftpilz</a></i> (translated into English as <i>The Toadstool</i> or <i>The Poisonous Mushroom</i><sup id="cite_ref-calvin_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-calvin-1">[1]</a></sup>), one of the most widespread pieces of propaganda, which warned about insidious dangers <a href="/wiki/Jew" class="mw-redirect" title="Jew">Jews</a> posed by using the <a href="/wiki/Metaphor" title="Metaphor">metaphor</a> of an attractive yet deadly mushroom. The publishing firm was financially very successful and made Streicher a multi-millionaire.<sup id="cite_ref-bayern_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bayern-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>After the war, Streicher was convicted of <a href="/wiki/Crime_against_humanity" class="mw-redirect" title="Crime against humanity">crimes against humanity</a> and executed.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAvalon_Project.E2.80.93Yale_University.2C_.27.27Judgement:_Streicher.27.27_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAvalon_Project.E2.80.93Yale_University.2C_.27.27Judgement:_Streicher.27.27-3">[3]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Julius_Streicher&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Streicher was born in <a href="/wiki/Fleinhausen" title="Fleinhausen">Fleinhausen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Bavaria" title="Kingdom of Bavaria">Kingdom of Bavaria</a>, one of nine children of the teacher Friedrich Streicher and his wife Anna (née Weiss). He worked as an elementary school teacher like his father, and in 1921 he began his political career, joining the NSDAP.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAvalon_Project.E2.80.93Yale_University.2C_.27.27Judgement:_Streicher.27.27_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAvalon_Project.E2.80.93Yale_University.2C_.27.27Judgement:_Streicher.27.27-3">[3]</a></sup> He would later claim that because his political work brought him into contact with <a href="/wiki/German_Jews" class="mw-redirect" title="German Jews">German Jews</a>, he "must therefore have been fated to become later-on, a writer and speaker on racial politics."<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFriedman1998300_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFriedman1998300-4">[4]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[a]</a></sup> In 1913 Streicher married Kunigunde Roth, a baker's daughter, in <a href="/wiki/Nuremberg" title="Nuremberg">Nuremberg</a>. They had two sons, Lothar (born 1915) and Elmar (born 1918).<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBytwerk20015_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBytwerk20015-7">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>Streicher joined the <a href="/wiki/German_Army_(German_Empire)" title="German Army (German Empire)">German Army</a> in 1914. For his outstanding combat performance during the <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">First World War</a>, he was awarded the <a href="/wiki/Iron_Cross" title="Iron Cross">Iron Cross</a> 1st and 2nd Class, as well as earning a battlefield commission as an officer (<a href="/wiki/Lieutenant" title="Lieutenant">lieutenant</a>), despite having several reported instances of poor behavior in his military record,<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESnyder1976336_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESnyder1976336-8">[7]</a></sup> and at a time when officers were primarily from aristocratic families. Following the end of World War I, Streicher was demobilized and returned to Nuremberg.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBytwerk20016_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBytwerk20016-9">[8]</a></sup> Upon his return, Steicher took up another teaching position there but something happened (unknown) in 1919, which turned him into a "radical anti-Semite."<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBytwerk20018_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBytwerk20018-10">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_politics">Early politics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Julius_Streicher&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Early politics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In February 1919 Streicher became active in the <a href="/wiki/Antisemitism" title="Antisemitism">anti-Semitic</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Deutschv%C3%B6lkischer_Schutz_und_Trutzbund" class="mw-redirect" title="Deutschvölkischer Schutz und Trutzbund">Deutschvölkischer Schutz und Trutzbund</a></i> (German Nationalist Protection and Defense Federation), one of the various radical-nationalist organizations that sprang up in the wake of the failed <a href="/wiki/German_Revolution_of_1918%E2%80%9319" title="German Revolution of 1918–19">German Communist revolution of 1918</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBracher197081.E2.80.9382_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBracher197081.E2.80.9382-11">[10]</a></sup> Such groups fostered the view that Jews and "<a href="/wiki/Bolshevik" class="mw-redirect" title="Bolshevik">Bolsheviks</a>" were synonymous, and that they were traitors trying to subject Germany to Communist rule.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELongerich201012.E2.80.9313_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELongerich201012.E2.80.9313-12">[11]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKershaw2000137.E2.80.93138_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKershaw2000137.E2.80.93138-13">[12]</a></sup> In 1920 Streicher turned to the <i>Deutschsozialistische Partei</i> (German-Socialist Party), a group whose platform was close to that of the young <a href="/wiki/NSDAP" class="mw-redirect" title="NSDAP">NSDAP</a>, or <i>Nationalsozialistische Deutsche Arbeiter Partei</i> (National Socialist German Workers' Party). The <i>German Socialist Party</i> (Deutsch-Sozialistische Partei, DSP) was created in May 1919 as an initiative of <a href="/wiki/Rudolf_von_Sebottendorf" title="Rudolf von Sebottendorf">Rudolf von Sebottendorf</a> as a child of the <a href="/wiki/Thule_Society" title="Thule Society">Thule Society</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKershaw2000138.E2.80.93139_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKershaw2000138.E2.80.93139-14">[13]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBracher197093_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBracher197093-15">[14]</a></sup> and its program was based on the ideas of the mechanical engineer Alfred Brunner (1881–1936)<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKershaw2000138_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKershaw2000138-16">[15]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[b]</a></sup> Leading members of the DSP were Hans Georg Müller, Max Sesselmann and Dr. Friedrich Wiesel, the first two editors of the <a href="/wiki/V%C3%B6lkischer_Beobachter" title="Völkischer Beobachter">Münchner Beobachter</a>. Julius Streicher founded his local branch in 1919 in <a href="/wiki/Nuremberg" title="Nuremberg">Nuremberg</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFranz-Willing196289_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFranz-Willing196289-18">[16]</a></sup> The DSP was officially inaugurated in 1919 in <a href="/wiki/Hanover" title="Hanover">Hanover</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBracher197093_15-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBracher197093-15">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>By the end of 1919, the DSP had branches in Düsseldorf, Kiel, Frankfurt am Main, Dresden, Nuremberg and Munich.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKershaw2000138_16-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKershaw2000138-16">[15]</a></sup> Streicher sought to move the German Socialists in a more virulently anti-Semitic direction – an effort which aroused enough opposition that he left the group and brought his now-substantial following to yet another organization in 1921, the <i>Deutsche Werkgemeinschaft</i> (German Working Community), which hoped to unite the various anti-Semitic <i>völkisch</i> movements.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBytwerk200112.E2.80.9314_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBytwerk200112.E2.80.9314-19">[17]</a></sup> Meanwhile, Streicher's rhetoric against the Jews continued to intensify to such a degree that the leadership of the <i>Deutsche Werkgemeinschaft</i> thought he was dangerous and criticized him for his obsessive "hatred of the Jews and foreign races."<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERees201722_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERees201722-20">[18]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Nazism">Nazism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Julius_Streicher&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Nazism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1921, Streicher found his mentor. He visited <a href="/wiki/Munich" title="Munich">Munich</a> in order to hear <a href="/wiki/Adolf_Hitler" title="Adolf Hitler">Adolf Hitler</a> speak, an experience that he later said left him transformed. When asked about that moment, Streicher stated:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>It was on a winter's day in 1922. I sat unknown in the large hall of the Bürgerbräuhaus...suspense was in the air. Everyone seemed tense with excitement, with anticipation. Then suddenly a shout. "Hitler is coming!" Thousands of men and women jumped to their feet as if propelled by a mysterious power...they shouted, "Heil Hitler! Heil Hitler!"...And then he stood on the podium...Then I knew that in this Adolf Hitler was someone extraordinary...Here was one who could wrest out of the German spirit and the German heart the power to break the chains of slavery. Yes! Yes! This man spoke as a messenger from heaven at a time when the gates of hell were opening to pull down everything. And when he finally finished, and while the crowd raised the roof with the singing of the "Deutschland" song, I rushed to the stage.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDolibois2000114_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDolibois2000114-21">[19]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>Nearly religiously converted by this speech, Streicher believed from this point forward that, "it was his destiny to serve Hitler."<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERees201722.E2.80.9323_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERees201722.E2.80.9323-22">[20]</a></sup> That same year, Streicher joined the Nazi Party (NSDAP) and merged his personal following with Hitler's, more than doubling the party membership.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERees201723_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERees201723-23">[21]</a></sup> In May 1923 Streicher founded the newspaper <i><a href="/wiki/Der_St%C3%BCrmer" title="Der Stürmer">Der Stürmer</a></i> (<i>The Stormer</i>, or, loosely, <i>The Attacker</i>).<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBytwerk200151.E2.80.9352_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBytwerk200151.E2.80.9352-24">[22]</a></sup> From the outset, the chief aim of the paper was to promulgate anti-Semitic <a href="/wiki/Propaganda" title="Propaganda">propaganda</a>. For example, the first issue had an excerpt that stated, "As long as the Jew is in the German household, we will be Jewish slaves. Therefore he must go."<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBytwerk200152_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBytwerk200152-25">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>In November 1923, Streicher participated in Hitler’s first effort to seize power, the failed <a href="/wiki/Beer_Hall_Putsch" title="Beer Hall Putsch">Beer Hall Putsch</a> in <a href="/wiki/Munich" title="Munich">Munich</a>. Streicher marched with Hitler in the front row of the would-be <a href="/wiki/Revolutionary" title="Revolutionary">revolutionaries</a>. As a result of his participation in the attempted Putsch, Streicher was suspended from teaching school.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEZentnerBed.C3.BCrftig1991921_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEZentnerBed.C3.BCrftig1991921-26">[24]</a></sup> His loyalty to the cause earned him Hitler's lifelong trust and protection; in the years that followed, Streicher would be one of the dictator's few true intimates.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEShowalter_1997.2C_.27.27Jews.2C_Nazis.2C_and_the_Law:_The_Case_of_Julius_Streicher.27.27_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEShowalter_1997.2C_.27.27Jews.2C_Nazis.2C_and_the_Law:_The_Case_of_Julius_Streicher.27.27-27">[25]</a></sup> Within the pages of <i>Mein Kampf</i>, Hitler even praised Streicher for subordinating the German Socialist Party to the Nazi Party, a move Hitler believed was essential to the success of the National Socialists.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBullock1962124_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBullock1962124-28">[26]</a></sup> When Hitler was released from his sentence at Landsberg am Lech on 20 December 1924 for his role in the Putsch, Streicher was one of the few remaining followers waiting for him at his Munich apartment.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFest1974219_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFest1974219-29">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p>As a reward for his dedication, when the Nazi Party was again legalized and re-organized in 1925 Streicher was appointed <i><a href="/wiki/Gauleiter" title="Gauleiter">Gauleiter</a></i> of the Bavarian region of <a href="/wiki/Franconia" title="Franconia">Franconia</a> (which included his home town of <a href="/wiki/Nuremberg" title="Nuremberg">Nuremberg</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEZentnerBed.C3.BCrftig1991922_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEZentnerBed.C3.BCrftig1991922-30">[28]</a></sup> In the early years of the party’s rise, <i>Gauleiter</i> were essentially party functionaries without real power; but in the final years of the <a href="/wiki/Weimar_Republic" title="Weimar Republic">Weimar Republic</a>, as the Nazi Party grew, so did their power as regional party leaders. An early confidant of Streicher, who in October 1923 had founded one of the earliest Nazi student fraternities at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Erlangen-Nuremberg" title="University of Erlangen-Nuremberg">University of Erlangen-Nuremberg</a>, was <a href="/w/index.php?title=Ludwig_Franz_Gengler&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Ludwig Franz Gengler (page does not exist)">Ludwig Franz Gengler</a><span class="noprint" style="font-size:85%; font-style: normal;">&#160;(<a href="https://de.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ludwig_Franz_Gengler" class="extiw" title="de:Ludwig Franz Gengler">de</a>)</span>, and he was considered by party comrades to be the "intellectual leader of the Nuremberg Gau".<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERuault2006267_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERuault2006267-31">[29]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">[c]</a></sup> During the 12 years of the Nazi regime, party <i>Gauleiter</i> like Streicher would wield immense power, and be in large measure untouchable by legal authority. Streicher was also elected to the Bavarian "<a href="/wiki/Landtag" title="Landtag">Landtag</a>" or legislature,<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEZentnerBed.C3.BCrftig1991922_30-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEZentnerBed.C3.BCrftig1991922-30">[28]</a></sup> a position which gave him a margin of <a href="/wiki/Parliamentary_immunity" title="Parliamentary immunity">parliamentary immunity</a> – a safety net that would help him resist efforts to silence his <a href="/wiki/Racism" title="Racism">racist</a> message.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEShowalter_1997.2C_.27.27Jews.2C_Nazis.2C_and_the_Law:_The_Case_of_Julius_Streicher.27.27_27-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEShowalter_1997.2C_.27.27Jews.2C_Nazis.2C_and_the_Law:_The_Case_of_Julius_Streicher.27.27-27">[25]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span id="Rise_of_Der_Stürmer"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Rise_of_Der_St.C3.BCrmer">Rise of <i>Der Stürmer</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Julius_Streicher&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Rise of Der Stürmer">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Bundesarchiv_Bild_133-075,_Worms,_Antisemitische_Presse,_%22St%C3%BCrmerkasten%22.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ab/Bundesarchiv_Bild_133-075%2C_Worms%2C_Antisemitische_Presse%2C_%22St%C3%BCrmerkasten%22.jpg/200px-Bundesarchiv_Bild_133-075%2C_Worms%2C_Antisemitische_Presse%2C_%22St%C3%BCrmerkasten%22.jpg" width="200" height="139" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ab/Bundesarchiv_Bild_133-075%2C_Worms%2C_Antisemitische_Presse%2C_%22St%C3%BCrmerkasten%22.jpg/300px-Bundesarchiv_Bild_133-075%2C_Worms%2C_Antisemitische_Presse%2C_%22St%C3%BCrmerkasten%22.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ab/Bundesarchiv_Bild_133-075%2C_Worms%2C_Antisemitische_Presse%2C_%22St%C3%BCrmerkasten%22.jpg/400px-Bundesarchiv_Bild_133-075%2C_Worms%2C_Antisemitische_Presse%2C_%22St%C3%BCrmerkasten%22.jpg 2x" data-file-width="784" data-file-height="544" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Bundesarchiv_Bild_133-075,_Worms,_Antisemitische_Presse,_%22St%C3%BCrmerkasten%22.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>

</div>

<p>Beginning in 1924, Streicher used <i><a href="/wiki/Der_St%C3%BCrmer" title="Der Stürmer">Der Stürmer</a></i> as a mouthpiece not only for general antisemitic attacks, but for calculated <a href="/wiki/Smear_campaign" title="Smear campaign">smear campaigns</a> against specific Jews, such as the <a href="/wiki/Nuremberg" title="Nuremberg">Nuremberg</a> city official Julius Fleischmann, who worked for Streicher's nemesis, mayor <a href="/w/index.php?title=Hermann_Luppe&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Hermann Luppe (page does not exist)">Hermann Luppe</a><span class="noprint" style="font-size:85%; font-style: normal;">&#160;(<a href="https://de.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hermann_Luppe" class="extiw" title="de:Hermann Luppe">de</a>)</span>. <i><a href="/wiki/Der_St%C3%BCrmer" title="Der Stürmer">Der Stürmer</a></i> accused Fleischmann of stealing socks from his quartermaster during combat in <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a>. Fleischmann sued Streicher and disproved the allegations in court, where Streicher was fined 900 <a href="/wiki/German_Reichsmark" class="mw-redirect" title="German Reichsmark">marks</a> but the detailed testimony exposed less-than-glorious details of Fleischmann's record, and his reputation was badly damaged. It was proof that Streicher's unofficial motto for his tactics was correct: "Something always sticks."<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEShowalter_1997.2C_.27.27Jews.2C_Nazis.2C_and_the_Law:_The_Case_of_Julius_Streicher.27.27_27-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEShowalter_1997.2C_.27.27Jews.2C_Nazis.2C_and_the_Law:_The_Case_of_Julius_Streicher.27.27-27">[25]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">[d]</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Der_St%C3%BCrmer" title="Der Stürmer">Der Stürmer</a>'s</i> infamous official slogan, <i>Die Juden sind unser Unglück</i> (the Jews are our misfortune) was deemed non-actionable under German statutes, since it was not a direct incitement to violence.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEShowalter_1997.2C_.27.27Jews.2C_Nazis.2C_and_the_Law:_The_Case_of_Julius_Streicher.27.27_27-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEShowalter_1997.2C_.27.27Jews.2C_Nazis.2C_and_the_Law:_The_Case_of_Julius_Streicher.27.27-27">[25]</a></sup></p>
<p>Streicher's opponents complained to authorities that <i>Der Stürmer</i> violated a statute against religious offense with his constant promulgation of the "<a href="/wiki/Blood_libel_against_Jews" class="mw-redirect" title="Blood libel against Jews">blood libel</a>" – the <a href="/wiki/Medieval" class="mw-redirect" title="Medieval">medieval</a> accusation that Jews killed <a href="/wiki/Christian" title="Christian">Christian</a> children to use their blood to make <a href="/wiki/Matzoh" class="mw-redirect" title="Matzoh">matzoh</a>. Streicher argued that his accusations were based on <a href="/wiki/Race_(classification_of_human_beings)" class="mw-redirect" title="Race (classification of human beings)">race</a>, not religion, and that his communications were political speech, and therefore protected by the German constitution.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEShowalter_1997.2C_.27.27Jews.2C_Nazis.2C_and_the_Law:_The_Case_of_Julius_Streicher.27.27_27-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEShowalter_1997.2C_.27.27Jews.2C_Nazis.2C_and_the_Law:_The_Case_of_Julius_Streicher.27.27-27">[25]</a></sup></p>
<p>Streicher orchestrated his early campaigns against Jews to make the most extreme possible claims, short of violating a law that might get the paper shut down. He insisted in the pages of his newspaper that the Jews had caused the worldwide <a href="/wiki/Great_Depression" title="Great Depression">Depression</a>, and were responsible for the crippling <a href="/wiki/Unemployment" title="Unemployment">unemployment</a> and <a href="/wiki/Inflation" title="Inflation">inflation</a> which afflicted Germany during the 1920s. He claimed that Jews were <a href="/wiki/Sexual_slavery" title="Sexual slavery">white-slavers</a> responsible for Germany's prostitution rings. Real unsolved killings in Germany, especially of children or women, were often confidently explained in the pages of <i>Der Stürmer</i> as cases of "Jewish <a href="/wiki/Ritual_murder" class="mw-redirect" title="Ritual murder">ritual murder</a>."<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESnyder198947.E2.80.9351_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESnyder198947.E2.80.9351-34">[30]</a></sup></p>
<p>One of Streicher's constant themes was the sexual violation of ethnically German women by Jews, a subject which he used to publish semi-<a href="/wiki/Pornography" title="Pornography">pornographic</a> tracts and images detailing degrading sexual acts.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBytwerk2001143.E2.80.93150_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBytwerk2001143.E2.80.93150-35">[31]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWistrich200142_36-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWistrich200142-36">[32]</a></sup> The fascination with the pornographic aspects of the propaganda in <i>Der Stürmer</i> was an important feature for many anti-Semites.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWelch200275_37-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWelch200275-37">[33]</a></sup> With the help of his notorious cartoonist <a href="/wiki/Philipp_Rupprecht" title="Philipp Rupprecht">Phillip "Fips" Rupprecht</a>, Streicher published image after image of Jewish <a href="/wiki/Stereotype" title="Stereotype">stereotypes</a> and sexually-charged encounters.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKoonz2005232.E2.80.93233_38-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKoonz2005232.E2.80.93233-38">[34]</a></sup> His portrayal of Jews as subhuman and evil is widely considered to have played a critical role in the dehumanization and marginalization of the Jewish minority in the eyes of common Germans – creating the necessary conditions for the later perpetration of the <a href="/wiki/Holocaust" class="mw-redirect" title="Holocaust">Holocaust</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFischer1995135.E2.80.93136_39-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFischer1995135.E2.80.93136-39">[35]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWelch200276.E2.80.9377_40-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWelch200276.E2.80.9377-40">[36]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">[e]</a></sup> To protect himself from accountability, Streicher relied on Hitler's protection. Hitler declared that <i>Der Stürmer</i> was his favorite newspaper, and saw to it that each weekly issue was posted for public reading in special glassed-in display cases known as <a href="/wiki/File:USHMM_64415.jpg" title="File:USHMM 64415.jpg">"Stürmerkasten"</a>. The newspaper reached a peak circulation of 600,000 in 1935.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESnyder198950_42-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESnyder198950-42">[37]</a></sup> One of the possible solutions to the Jewish problem Streicher mentioned within the pages of <i>Der Stürmer</i> before it was officially tabled as a possibility, was shipping all of them to <a href="/wiki/Madagascar_Plan" title="Madagascar Plan">Madagascar</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKershaw2001320_43-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKershaw2001320-43">[38]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Streicher_in_power">Streicher in power</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Julius_Streicher&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Streicher in power">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In April 1933, after Nazi control of the German state apparatus gave the <a href="/wiki/Gauleiter" title="Gauleiter">Gauleiters</a> enormous power, Streicher organised a one-day <a href="/wiki/Boycott" title="Boycott">boycott</a> of Jewish businesses which was used as a dress-rehearsal for other anti-Semitic commercial measures. As he consolidated his hold on power, he came to more or less rule the city of Nuremberg and his <i><a href="/wiki/Gau_Franken" class="mw-redirect" title="Gau Franken">Gau Franken</a></i>. Among the nicknames provided by his enemies were "King of Nuremberg" and the "Beast of <a href="/wiki/Franconia" title="Franconia">Franconia</a>." Because of his role as Gauleiter of Franconia, he also gained the nickname of <i><a href="/wiki/F%C3%BChrer" title="Führer">Frankenführer</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTENadler19695_44-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTENadler19695-44">[39]</a></sup></p>

<p>Streicher later claimed that he was only "indirectly responsible" for passage of the anti-Jewish <a href="/wiki/Nuremberg_Laws" title="Nuremberg Laws">Nuremberg Laws</a> of 1935, and that he felt slighted because he was not directly consulted. Perhaps epitomizing the "profound anti-intellectualism" of the Nazi party, Streicher once opined that, "If the brains of all university professors were put at one end of the scale, and the brains of the <i>Führer</i> at the other, which end do you think would tip?"<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWall199798_45-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWall199798-45">[40]</a></sup></p>
<p>Streicher was ordered to take part in the establishment of the <a href="/wiki/Institute_for_the_Study_and_Elimination_of_Jewish_Influence_on_German_Church_Life" title="Institute for the Study and Elimination of Jewish Influence on German Church Life">Institute for the Study and Elimination of Jewish Influence on German Church Life</a>, that was to be organized together with the <a href="/wiki/German_Christians" title="German Christians">German Christians</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Ministry_of_Public_Enlightenment_and_Propaganda" class="mw-redirect" title="Ministry of Public Enlightenment and Propaganda">Ministry of Public Enlightenment and Propaganda</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Reichserziehungsministerium" class="mw-redirect" title="Reichserziehungsministerium">Reich Ministry of Education</a> and the Reich Ministry of the Churches. A surgical operation prevented Streicher from being able to fully participate and engage in this endeavor.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKaterMommsenPapen1999151_46-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKaterMommsenPapen1999151-46">[41]</a></sup> This anti-Semitic standpoint concerning the bible can be traced back to the earliest time of the Nazi movement, e.g., <a href="/wiki/Dietrich_Eckart" title="Dietrich Eckart">Dietrich Eckart</a>'s (Hitler's early mentor) book <i>Bolshevism from Moses to Lenin: A Dialogue Between Adolf Hitler and Me</i>, where it was claimed that "Jewish forgeries" had been <a href="/wiki/Positive_Christianity" title="Positive Christianity">added</a> to the <a href="/wiki/New_Testament" title="New Testament">New Testament</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESteigmann-Gall200317.E2.80.9324_47-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESteigmann-Gall200317.E2.80.9324-47">[42]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1938, Streicher ordered the Great Synagogue of Nuremberg destroyed as part of his contribution to <a href="/wiki/Kristallnacht" title="Kristallnacht">Kristallnacht</a>; he later claimed that his decision was based on his disapproval of its architectural design, which in his opinion "disfigured the beautiful German townscape."<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKershaw2001132_48-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKershaw2001132-48">[43]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Fall_from_power">Fall from power</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Julius_Streicher&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Fall from power">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Streicher's excesses brought condemnation even from other Nazis. His behaviour was viewed as so irresponsible that he was embarrassing the party leadership;<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESnyder198952.E2.80.9353_49-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESnyder198952.E2.80.9353-49">[44]</a></sup> chief among his enemies in Hitler's hierarchy was Reichsmarschall <a href="/wiki/Hermann_G%C3%B6ring" title="Hermann Göring">Hermann Göring</a>, who loathed him and later claimed that he forbade his own staff to read <i>Der Stürmer</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMaser2000282_50-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMaser2000282-50">[45]</a></sup></p>
<p>Despite his special relationship with Hitler, after 1938 Streicher's position began to unravel. He was accused of keeping Jewish property seized after <a href="/wiki/Kristallnacht" title="Kristallnacht">Kristallnacht</a> in November 1938; he was charged with spreading untrue stories about Göring – such as alleging that Göring's daughter Edda was conceived by <a href="/wiki/Artificial_insemination" title="Artificial insemination">artificial insemination</a>; and he was confronted with his excessive personal behaviour, including unconcealed adultery, several furious verbal attacks on other <i>Gauleiters</i> and striding through the streets of Nuremberg cracking a bullwhip.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESnyder198947.2C_50.E2.80.9353_51-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESnyder198947.2C_50.E2.80.9353-51">[46]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">[f]</a></sup> In February 1940 he was stripped of his party offices and withdrew from the public eye, although he was permitted to continue publishing <i>Der Stürmer</i>. Hitler remained committed to Streicher, whom he considered a loyal friend, despite his unsavory reputation.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWistrich1995251.E2.80.93252_53-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWistrich1995251.E2.80.93252-53">[47]</a></sup></p>
<p>Streicher's wife, Kunigunde Streicher, died in 1943 after 30 years of marriage.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDavidson199743_54-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDavidson199743-54">[48]</a></sup></p>
<p>When Germany surrendered to the <a href="/wiki/Allies_of_World_War_II" title="Allies of World War II">Allied</a> armies in May 1945, Streicher said later, he decided to commit <a href="/wiki/Suicide" title="Suicide">suicide</a>. Instead, he married his former secretary, Adele Tappe.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDavidson199744_55-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDavidson199744-55">[49]</a></sup> Days later, on 23 May 1945, Streicher was captured in the town of <a href="/wiki/Waidring" title="Waidring">Waidring</a>, <a href="/wiki/Austria" title="Austria">Austria</a>, by a group of American officers led by Major Henry Plitt.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTETofahrn2008163_56-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTETofahrn2008163-56">[50]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-57" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-57">[g]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Trial_and_execution">Trial and execution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Julius_Streicher&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Trial and execution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Julius_Streicher_72-920_crop.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/03/Julius_Streicher_72-920_crop.jpg/220px-Julius_Streicher_72-920_crop.jpg" width="220" height="292" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/0/03/Julius_Streicher_72-920_crop.jpg 1.5x" data-file-width="267" data-file-height="354" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Julius_Streicher_72-920_crop.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Julius Streicher in custody</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>During his trial, Streicher claimed that he had been mistreated by Allied soldiers after his capture.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBytwerk200142_58-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBytwerk200142-58">[51]</a></sup> Julius Streicher was not a member of the military and did not take part in planning the <a href="/wiki/Holocaust" class="mw-redirect" title="Holocaust">Holocaust</a>, or the invasion of other nations. Yet his pivotal role in inciting the extermination of Jews was significant enough, in the prosecutors' judgment, to include him in the indictment of Major War Criminals before the <a href="/wiki/Nuremberg_Trials" class="mw-redirect" title="Nuremberg Trials">International Military Tribunal</a> which sat in Nuremberg, where Streicher had once been an unchallenged authority. He complained throughout the process that all his judges were Jews.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESnyder198954.E2.80.9356_59-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESnyder198954.E2.80.9356-59">[52]</a></sup> Most of the evidence against Streicher came from his numerous speeches and articles over the years.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESnyder198956.E2.80.9357_60-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESnyder198956.E2.80.9357-60">[53]</a></sup> In essence, prosecutors contended that Streicher's articles and speeches were so incendiary that he was an <a href="/wiki/Accessory_(legal_term)" title="Accessory (legal term)">accessory</a> to murder, and therefore as culpable as those who actually ordered the mass extermination of Jews (such as <a href="/wiki/Hans_Frank" title="Hans Frank">Hans Frank</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ernst_Kaltenbrunner" title="Ernst Kaltenbrunner">Ernst Kaltenbrunner</a>). They further argued that he kept them up when he was well aware Jews were being slaughtered.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESnyder198957_61-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESnyder198957-61">[54]</a></sup></p>
<p>He was acquitted of <a href="/wiki/Crime_against_peace" title="Crime against peace">crimes against peace</a>, but found guilty of <a href="/wiki/Crimes_against_humanity" title="Crimes against humanity">crimes against humanity</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Capital_punishment" title="Capital punishment">sentenced to death</a> on 1 October 1946. The judgment against him read, in part:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>For his 25 years of speaking, writing and preaching hatred of the Jews, Streicher was widely known as 'Jew-Baiter Number One.' In his speeches and articles, week after week, month after month, he infected the German mind with the <a href="/wiki/Virus" title="Virus">virus</a> of anti-Semitism, and incited the German people to active persecution. ... Streicher's incitement to murder and extermination at the time when Jews in the East were being killed under the most horrible conditions clearly constitutes persecution on political and racial grounds in connection with war crimes, as defined by the Charter, and constitutes a crime against humanity.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAvalon_Project.E2.80.93Yale_University.2C_.27.27Judgement:_Streicher.27.27_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAvalon_Project.E2.80.93Yale_University.2C_.27.27Judgement:_Streicher.27.27-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>During his trial, Streicher displayed for the last time the flair for courtroom theatrics that had made him famous in the 1920s. He answered questions from his own defence lawyer with diatribes against Jews, the Allies, and the court itself, and was frequently silenced by the court officers. Streicher was largely shunned by all of the other Nuremberg defendants. He also peppered his testimony with references to passages of Jewish texts he had so often carefully selected and inserted into the pages of <i>Der Stürmer</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEConot2000381.E2.80.93389_62-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEConot2000381.E2.80.93389-62">[55]</a></sup></p>

<p>Streicher was <a href="/wiki/Hanging" title="Hanging">hanged</a> in the early hours of 16 October 1946, along with the nine other condemned defendants from the first Nuremberg trial (Göring, Streicher's nemesis, committed <a href="/wiki/Suicide" title="Suicide">suicide</a> only hours earlier). Streicher's was the most melodramatic of the hangings carried out that night. At the bottom of the scaffold he cried out "<a href="/wiki/Heil_Hitler" class="mw-redirect" title="Heil Hitler">Heil Hitler</a>!". When he mounted the platform, he delivered his last sneering reference to Jewish scripture, snapping "<i>Purimfest</i>!"<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWistrich1995252_63-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWistrich1995252-63">[56]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-64" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-64">[h]</a></sup> Streicher's final declaration before the hood went over his head was, "The <a href="/wiki/Bolsheviks" title="Bolsheviks">Bolsheviks</a> will hang you one day!"<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEConot2000506_65-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEConot2000506-65">[57]</a></sup> Joseph Kingsbury-Smith, who covered the executions,<sup id="cite_ref-66" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-66">[i]</a></sup> said in his filed report that after the hood descended over Streicher's head, he also apparently said "Adele, meine liebe Frau!" ("Adele, my dear wife!").<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERadlmeier2001345.E2.80.93346_67-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERadlmeier2001345.E2.80.93346-67">[58]</a></sup></p>
<p>The consensus among eyewitnesses was that Streicher's hanging did not proceed as planned, and that he did not receive the quick death from <a href="/wiki/Spinal_cord" title="Spinal cord">spinal</a> severing typical of the other executions at Nuremberg. Kingsbury-Smith, who covered the executions for the <a href="/wiki/International_News_Service" title="International News Service">International News Service</a>, reported that Streicher "went down kicking," which may have dislodged the hangman's knot from its optimum position. Smith stated that Streicher could be heard groaning under the scaffold after he dropped through the trap-door, and that the executioner intervened under the gallows, which was screened by wood panels and a black curtain, to finish the job.<sup id="cite_ref-68" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-68">[j]</a></sup> U.S. Army <a href="/wiki/Master_Sergeant" class="mw-redirect" title="Master Sergeant">Master Sergeant</a> <a href="/wiki/John_C._Woods" title="John C. Woods">John C. Woods</a> was the main executioner, and not only insisted he had performed all executions correctly, but stated he was very proud of his work.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDuff1999130_69-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDuff1999130-69">[59]</a></sup></p>
<p>Streicher's body, as those of the other nine executed men and the corpse of Hermann Göring, was cremated at <a href="/wiki/Ostfriedhof_(Munich)" title="Ostfriedhof (Munich)">Ostfriedhof (Munich)</a> and the ashes were scattered.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEManvellFraenkel2011393_70-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEManvellFraenkel2011393-70">[60]</a></sup></p>
