<p><b>Konzentrationslager Flossenbürg</b> was a <a href="/wiki/Nazi_concentration_camp" class="mw-redirect" title="Nazi concentration camp">Nazi German concentration camp</a> built in May 1938 by the <a href="/wiki/Schutzstaffel" title="Schutzstaffel">Schutzstaffel (SS)</a> Economic-Administrative Main Office at <a href="/wiki/Flossenb%C3%BCrg" title="Flossenbürg">Flossenbürg</a>, in the <a href="/wiki/Upper_Palatinate" title="Upper Palatinate">Upper Palatinate</a> region of <a href="/wiki/Bavaria" title="Bavaria">Bavaria</a>, <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a>, near the border with <a href="/wiki/Czechoslovakia" title="Czechoslovakia">Czechoslovakia</a>. Until its liberation in April 1945, more than 96,000 prisoners passed through the camp, around 30,000 of whom died there.<sup id="cite_ref-w_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-w-1">[1]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Flossenb%C3%BCrg_concentration_camp&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pre.E2.80.93World_War_II">Pre–World War II</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Flossenb%C3%BCrg_concentration_camp&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Pre–World War II">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Before <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>, Flossenbürg was a men's camp primarily for "antisocial" or "criminal" prisoners. The camp's site was chosen so that the inmates could be used as slave labor to quarry the granite found in the nearby hills. The quarries belonged to the SS-owned and -operated German Earth and Stone Works (<a href="/wiki/DEST" title="DEST">DEST</a>) company. On 24 March 1938, SS authorities determined a site near the small town of Flossenbürg to be suitable for the establishment of a concentration camp due to its potential for extracting granite for construction purposes.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="During_World_War_II">During World War II</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Flossenb%C3%BCrg_concentration_camp&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: During World War II">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>During World War II, most of the inmates sent to Flossenbürg, or to one of about 100 sub-camps, came from the German-occupied eastern territories. The inmates in Flossenbürg were housed in 16 huge wooden barracks, its <a href="/wiki/Crematorium" class="mw-redirect" title="Crematorium">crematorium</a> was built in a valley straight outside the camp. In September 1939, the SS transferred 1,000 <a href="/wiki/Political_prisoner" title="Political prisoner">political prisoners</a> to Flossenbürg from <a href="/wiki/Dachau_concentration_camp" title="Dachau concentration camp">Dachau</a>.</p>
<p>In 1941 to 1942, about 1,500 <a href="/wiki/Poland" title="Poland">Polish</a> prisoners, mostly members of the <a href="/wiki/Polish_resistance_movement_in_World_War_II" title="Polish resistance movement in World War II">Polish resistance</a>, were deported to Flossenbürg. In July 1941, SS guards shot 40 Polish prisoners at the SS firing range outside the Flossenbürg concentration camp. Between February and September 1941 the SS executed about one-third of the Polish political prisoners deported to Flossenbürg.</p>
<p>During World War II, the German army turned tens of thousands of <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet</a> prisoners over to the SS for execution. More than 1,000 Soviet prisoners of war were executed in Flossenbürg by the end of 1941. The SS also established a special camp for Soviet prisoners of war within Flossenbürg. <a href="/wiki/Execution" class="mw-redirect" title="Execution">Executions</a> of Soviet prisoners of war continued sporadically through 1944. Soviet prisoners of war in <a href="/wiki/M%C3%BClsen" title="Mülsen">Mülsen St. Micheln</a>, a subcamp of Flossenbürg, staged an uprising and mass escape attempt on 1 May 1944. They set their bunks on fire and killed some of the camp's <a href="/wiki/Kapo_(Arbeitslager)" class="mw-redirect" title="Kapo (Arbeitslager)">Kapos</a> - prisoner trustees who carried out SS orders. SS guards crushed the revolt and none of the prisoners escaped. Almost 200 prisoners died from burns and wounds sustained in the uprising. The SS transferred about 40 leaders of the revolt to Flossenbürg itself, where they were later executed in the camp jail.</p>
<p>There were over 4,000 prisoners in the main camp of Flossenbürg in February 1943. More than half of these prisoners were political prisoners (mainly Soviet, <a href="/wiki/Czech_Republic" title="Czech Republic">Czech</a>, <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Dutch</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">German</a>). Almost 800 were German criminals, more than 100 were <a href="/wiki/Homosexuals" class="mw-redirect" title="Homosexuals">homosexuals</a>, and 7 were <a href="/wiki/Jehovah%27s_Witnesses" title="Jehovah's Witnesses">Jehovah's Witnesses</a>.</p>
<p>During the war, prisoner forced labor became increasingly important in German arms production. As a result, the Flossenbürg camp system expanded to include approximately 100 subcamps concentrated mainly around armaments industries in southern Germany and western Czechoslovakia.</p>
<p>On 1 September 1944, Flossenbürg became a training camp for extremely large numbers of female guards (<a href="/wiki/Aufseherin" class="mw-redirect" title="Aufseherin">Aufseherinnen</a>) who were recruited by force from factories all over Germany and Poland. All together, over 500 women were trained in the camp and in time went on to its subcamps. Women matrons staffed the Flossenbürg subcamps, such as <a href="/w/index.php?title=Dresden_Ilke_Werke&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Dresden Ilke Werke (page does not exist)">Dresden Ilke Werke</a>, <a href="/wiki/Freiberg_subcamp" title="Freiberg subcamp">Freiberg</a>, <a href="/wiki/Helmbrechts_concentration_camp" title="Helmbrechts concentration camp">Helmbrechts</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hol%C3%BD%C5%A1ov" title="Holýšov">Holleischen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Leitmeritz" class="mw-redirect" title="Leitmeritz">Leitmeritz</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mehltheuer" title="Mehltheuer">Mehltheuer</a>, <a href="/wiki/Neustadt_bei_Coburg" title="Neustadt bei Coburg">Neustadt</a> (near <a href="/wiki/Coburg" title="Coburg">Coburg</a>), <a href="/wiki/N%C3%BCrnberg" class="mw-redirect" title="Nürnberg">Nürnberg</a>-Siemens, <a href="/wiki/Oederan" title="Oederan">Oederan</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Svatava_(Sokolov_District)" title="Svatava (Sokolov District)">Zwodau</a>, and it is known that six <a href="/wiki/Schutzstaffel#Helferinnen_Corps" title="Schutzstaffel">SS women</a> <i>(SS-Helferinnenkorps)</i> staffed the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Gundelsdorf&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Gundelsdorf (page does not exist)">Gundelsdorf</a> subcamp in <a href="/wiki/Czechoslovakia" title="Czechoslovakia">Czechoslovakia</a>.</p>
<p>By 1945, there were almost 40,000 inmates held in the whole Flossenbürg camp system, including almost 11,000 women. Inmates were made to work in the Flossenbürg camp quarry and in armaments making. Underfeeding, sickness, and overwork were rife among the inmates. Combined with the harshness of the guards, this deliberate maltreatment killed many thousands of inmates.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>It is estimated that between April 1944 and April 1945, more than 1500 death sentences were carried out there. To this end, six new <a href="/wiki/Gallows" title="Gallows">gallows</a> hooks were installed. In the last months the rate of daily executions overtook the capacity of the crematorium. As a solution, the SS began stacking the bodies in piles, drenching them with gasoline, and setting them alight. Incarcerated in what was called the "<i>Bunker</i>," those who had been condemned to death were kept alone in dark rooms with no food for days until they were executed.</p>
<p>Amongst the <a href="/wiki/Allies_of_World_War_II" title="Allies of World War II">Allied</a> military officers executed at Flossenbürg were <a href="/wiki/Special_Operations_Executive" title="Special Operations Executive">Special Operations Executive</a> (SOE) agent <a href="/wiki/Gustave_Bi%C3%A9ler" title="Gustave Biéler">Gustave Daniel Alfred Biéler</a> (executed 6 September 1944). As Germany's defeat loomed, a number of the SOE agents whom the SS had tortured repeatedly in order to extract information, were executed on the same day. On 29 March 1945 13 SOE agents were hanged, including <a href="/wiki/Jack_Agazarian" title="Jack Agazarian">Jack Charles Stanmore Agazarian</a>. Together with his deputy General <a href="/wiki/Hans_Oster" title="Hans Oster">Hans Oster</a>, military jurist General Karl Sack, theologian Rev. <a href="/wiki/Dietrich_Bonhoeffer" title="Dietrich Bonhoeffer">Dietrich Bonhoeffer</a><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> and Ludwig Gehre, <a href="/wiki/Wilhelm_Canaris" title="Wilhelm Canaris">Wilhelm Canaris</a> was humiliated before witnesses and then executed on 9 April 1945. At the time of his execution, Canaris had been decorated with the Iron Cross First and Second Class, and the German Cross in Silver. On 1 August 2007 a memorial was unveiled at Flossenbürg to the memory of the seven members of the German resistance who had conspired to assassinate Hitler and who were executed on 9 April 1945.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Death_march_and_liberation">Death march and liberation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Flossenb%C3%BCrg_concentration_camp&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Death march and liberation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Flossenb%C3%BCrg_April_9_1945_Memorial.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c2/Flossenb%C3%BCrg_April_9_1945_Memorial.JPG/220px-Flossenb%C3%BCrg_April_9_1945_Memorial.JPG" width="220" height="338" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c2/Flossenb%C3%BCrg_April_9_1945_Memorial.JPG/330px-Flossenb%C3%BCrg_April_9_1945_Memorial.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c2/Flossenb%C3%BCrg_April_9_1945_Memorial.JPG/440px-Flossenb%C3%BCrg_April_9_1945_Memorial.JPG 2x" data-file-width="1458" data-file-height="2241" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Flossenb%C3%BCrg_April_9_1945_Memorial.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Memorial to members of German resistance executed on 9 April 1945</div>

<p>In early April 1945, as <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">American</a> forces were approaching the camp, the SS staged a perfunctory "trial" and executed General <a href="/wiki/Hans_Oster" title="Hans Oster">Hans Oster</a>, Admiral <a href="/wiki/Wilhelm_Canaris" title="Wilhelm Canaris">Wilhelm Canaris</a>, Rev. Dr. <a href="/wiki/Dietrich_Bonhoeffer" title="Dietrich Bonhoeffer">Dietrich Bonhoeffer</a>, Dr. <a href="/wiki/Karl_Sack" title="Karl Sack">Karl Sack</a>, Dr. <a href="/wiki/Theodor_Str%C3%BCnck" title="Theodor Strünck">Theodor Strünck</a> and General <a href="/wiki/Friedrich_von_Rabenau" title="Friedrich von Rabenau">Friedrich von Rabenau</a>, who were involved in the <a href="/wiki/20_July_plot" title="20 July plot">20 July plot</a> against <a href="/wiki/Adolf_Hitler" title="Adolf Hitler">Adolf Hitler</a> or previous plots against Hitler, along with the French Resistance worker <a href="/wiki/Simone_Michel-L%C3%A9vy" title="Simone Michel-Lévy">Simone Michel-Lévy</a>, who had managed to organize an uprising in the camp. On 20 April 1945, they began the forced evacuation of 22,000 inmates, including 1,700 Jews, leaving behind only those too sick to walk. On the death march to the <a href="/wiki/Dachau_concentration_camp" title="Dachau concentration camp">Dachau concentration camp</a>, SS guards shot any inmate too sick to keep up.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Before they reached Dachau, more than 7,000 inmates had been shot or had collapsed and died.</p>
<p>By the time the <a href="/wiki/U.S._Army" class="mw-redirect" title="U.S. Army">U.S. Army</a> freed the camp on 23 April 1945, more than 30,000 inmates had died at Flossenbürg. Troops from the <a href="/wiki/2nd_Cavalry_Group,_Mechanized" class="mw-redirect" title="2nd Cavalry Group, Mechanized">2nd Cavalry Group, Mechanized</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template noprint Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:NOTRS" class="mw-redirect" title="Wikipedia:NOTRS"><span title="This claim needs references to better sources. (July 2016)">better&#160;source&#160;needed</span></a></i>]</sup> the <a href="/wiki/90th_Infantry_Division_(United_States)" title="90th Infantry Division (United States)">90th Infantry Division</a> and the <a href="/wiki/97th_Infantry_Division_(United_States)" title="97th Infantry Division (United States)">97th Infantry Division</a><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> found about 1,600 ill and weak prisoners, mostly in the camp's hospital barracks. Members of the army's <a href="/wiki/Counter-Intelligence_Corps" class="mw-redirect" title="Counter-Intelligence Corps">Counter-Intelligence Corps</a>, including <a href="/wiki/Ib_Melchior" title="Ib Melchior">Ib Melchior</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Anthony_Hecht" title="Anthony Hecht">Anthony Hecht</a><sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Robie_Macauley" title="Robie Macauley">Robie Macauley</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> conducted extensive interviews with survivors to gather evidence for the 1946 <a href="/wiki/List_of_Axis_personnel_indicted_for_war_crimes#Flossenberg" title="List of Axis personnel indicted for war crimes">Flossenbürg War Crimes Trial</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Aftermath">Aftermath</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Flossenb%C3%BCrg_concentration_camp&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Aftermath">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Flossenb.C3.BCrg_Trial">Flossenbürg Trial</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Flossenb%C3%BCrg_concentration_camp&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Flossenbürg Trial">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:TAL_DES_TODES,_Gesamtansicht.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e5/TAL_DES_TODES%2C_Gesamtansicht.jpg/220px-TAL_DES_TODES%2C_Gesamtansicht.jpg" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e5/TAL_DES_TODES%2C_Gesamtansicht.jpg/330px-TAL_DES_TODES%2C_Gesamtansicht.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e5/TAL_DES_TODES%2C_Gesamtansicht.jpg/440px-TAL_DES_TODES%2C_Gesamtansicht.jpg 2x" data-file-width="750" data-file-height="562" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:TAL_DES_TODES,_Gesamtansicht.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<i>Tal des Todes</i> (Valley of the Dead) with memorials</div>

<p>The Flossenbürg War Crimes Trial took place in Dachau, Germany, from 12 June 1946 until 22 January 1947.</p>
<p>An American military tribunal tried 46 former staff from Flossenbürg concentration camp for crimes of murder, torturing, and starving the inmates in their custody. All but 5 of the defendants were found guilty, 15 of whom were condemned to death, 11 were given <a href="/wiki/Life_sentence" class="mw-redirect" title="Life sentence">life sentences</a>, and 14 were jailed for terms of 1 to 30 years.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Holocaust_museum">Holocaust museum</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Flossenb%C3%BCrg_concentration_camp&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Holocaust museum">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On 22 July 2007, the sixty-second anniversary of the camp's liberation, a Holocaust museum was opened at Flossenbürg. The ceremony was attended by 84 former inmates, in addition to the president of Ukraine, <a href="/wiki/Viktor_Yushchenko" title="Viktor Yushchenko">Viktor Yushchenko</a>, whose father was among the camp's inmates during World War II for five months.<sup id="cite_ref-w_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-w-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
