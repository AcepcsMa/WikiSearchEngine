<p><b>Hadera</b> (<a href="/wiki/Hebrew_language" title="Hebrew language">Hebrew</a>: <span class="script-hebrew" style="font-size: 115%; font-family: Alef, 'SBL BibLit', 'SBL Hebrew', 'David CLM', 'Frenk Ruehl CLM', 'Hadasim CLM', Shofar, 'Ezra SIL', 'Ezra SIL SR', Cardo, 'Noto Sans Hebrew', David, FreeSerif, 'Times New Roman', FreeSans, Arial;" dir="rtl">חֲדֵרָה</span>‎ <i><span title="he transliteration" class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none">Ḥadera </span></i>; <a href="/wiki/Arabic_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Arabic language">Arabic</a>: <span lang="ar" dir="rtl" xml:lang="ar">الخضيرة</span>‎‎ <i><span title="DIN 31635 Arabic" class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none">al-Ḫuḍayrah </span></i>) is a city located in the <a href="/wiki/Haifa_District" title="Haifa District">Haifa District</a> of <a href="/wiki/Israel" title="Israel">Israel</a>, in the northern <a href="/wiki/Sharon_plain" title="Sharon plain">Sharon</a> region, approximately 45 kilometers (28&#160;miles) from the major cities of <a href="/wiki/Tel_Aviv" title="Tel Aviv">Tel Aviv</a> and <a href="/wiki/Haifa" title="Haifa">Haifa</a>. The city is located along 7&#160;km (5&#160;mi) of the <a href="/wiki/Israeli_coastal_plain" title="Israeli coastal plain">Israeli Mediterranean Coastal Plain</a>. The city has a population of about 91,634, which includes a high proportion of immigrants arriving since 1990, notably from <a href="/wiki/Ethiopia" title="Ethiopia">Ethiopia</a> and the former <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet Union</a>. In 2015 it had a population of 88,783.<sup id="cite_ref-Israelpopulations_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Israelpopulations-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Hadera was established in 1891 as a farming colony by members of the Zionist group, <a href="/wiki/Hovevei_Zion" title="Hovevei Zion">Hovevei Zion</a>, from Lithuania and Latvia. By 1948, it was a regional center with a population of 11,800. In 1952, Hadera was declared a city, with jurisdiction over an area of 53,000 <a href="/wiki/Dunam" title="Dunam">dunams</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Haaretz_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Haaretz-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<p>Hadera was founded in 1891, in the early days modern <a href="/wiki/Zionism" title="Zionism">Zionism</a> by Jewish immigrants from <a href="/wiki/Lithuania" title="Lithuania">Lithuania</a> and <a href="/wiki/Latvia" title="Latvia">Latvia</a> on land purchased by <a href="/wiki/Yehoshua_Hankin" title="Yehoshua Hankin">Yehoshua Hankin</a>, known as the Redeemer of the Valley.<sup id="cite_ref-Haaretz_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Haaretz-2">[2]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-jvl_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-jvl-3">[3]</a></sup> The land was purchased from a Christian <a href="/wiki/Effendi" title="Effendi">effendi</a>, Selim Khuri. This was the largest purchase of land in <a href="/wiki/Eretz_Israel" class="mw-redirect" title="Eretz Israel">Eretz Israel</a> by a Zionist group, although the land was of low quality and mostly swampland. The only inhabitants prior to the purchase were a few families raising water buffaloes and selling reeds.<sup id="cite_ref-dispossession_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dispossession-4">[4]</a></sup> The town may derive its name from the <a href="/wiki/Arabic" title="Arabic">Arabic</a> word <i>khadra</i>, meaning "green" in reference to the wild weeds which covered the marshes on which the town is built.<sup id="cite_ref-jvl_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-jvl-3">[3]</a></sup> Historical geographer, Mordechai Eshel, citing a geographical work written in 1913, claims that the existing site whereon is now built Hadera was formerly called in Arabic <i>Ǧadera</i>, being the site of the town, <i>Gadera of Caesarea</i> (<a href="/wiki/Hebrew_language" title="Hebrew language">Hebrew:</a> <b>גדרה של קיסרין</b>), mentioned in <a href="/wiki/Tosefta" title="Tosefta">Tosefta</a> <i>Shevi'it</i>, ch. 7.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Others say that the ancient Gadera should be identified with <i>Tel Um Qeis</i>.</p>
<p>The first modern settlers lived in a house known as the Khan near Hadera's main synagogue.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Initially, Hadera was a lonely outpost of 10 families and 4 guards. In its early years, however, the town had issues with land ownership having drained the swamps with the aid of Egyptian workers sent to them by <a href="/wiki/Edmond_James_de_Rothschild" title="Edmond James de Rothschild">Baron Edmond de Rothschild</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Old tombstones in the local cemetery reveal that out of a population of 540, 210 died of malaria.<sup id="cite_ref-handbook_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-handbook-8">[8]</a></sup> Therefore a Bible verse from the Psalms (Tehillim) was inscribed in the city's logo: "Those who sow in tears, will reap with songs of joy." (Ps 126:5)</p>
<p>Relations between the residents of Hadera and neighboring <a href="/wiki/Bedouin" title="Bedouin">Bedouins</a> were poor and <a href="/wiki/HaShomer" class="mw-redirect" title="HaShomer">HaShomer</a> organization was assigned to guarding the fields. By the early twentieth century, the town had become a regional economic center.<sup id="cite_ref-Haaretz_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Haaretz-2">[2]</a></sup> Land disputes in the area were resolved by the 1930s, by which time, the population had grown to 2,002 in 1931.<sup id="cite_ref-jvl_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-jvl-3">[3]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-dispossession_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dispossession-4">[4]</a></sup> Free schooling was introduced in the city in 1937 in all schools apart from the <a href="/wiki/Histadrut" title="Histadrut">Histadrut</a> school.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="After_1948">After 1948</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hadera&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: After 1948">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Hadera's population began to grow dramatically after Israeli independence in 1948 as immigrants flocked to the country. Among the immigrants to the city were Russian and other European immigrants and also 40 Yemenite families.<sup id="cite_ref-handbook_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-handbook-8">[8]</a></sup> In 1953, Israel's first paper mill opened in the town. Sponsored by investors from within Israel as well as from the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>, <a href="/wiki/Brazil" title="Brazil">Brazil</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a>, the mill was designed to meet all of Israel's paper needs.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> It was also at this time that the Givat Olga neighborhood was constructed on the coast, and Beit Eliezer in the east of the city. In 1964, Hadera was declared a city.<sup id="cite_ref-Haaretz_2-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Haaretz-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="From_the_1990s">From the 1990s</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hadera&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: From the 1990s">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The city grew dramatically during the 1990s as it absorbed large numbers of <a href="/wiki/Russians" title="Russians">Russian</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ethiopia" title="Ethiopia">Ethiopian</a> immigrants.<sup id="cite_ref-Haaretz_2-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Haaretz-2">[2]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-jvl_3-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-jvl-3">[3]</a></sup> Hadera, considered a safe place by its inhabitants, was jolted by several acts of <a href="/wiki/Terrorism" title="Terrorism">terrorism</a>, including a <a href="/wiki/Bat_Mitzvah_massacre" title="Bat Mitzvah massacre">massacre</a> of six civilians at a Bat Mitzvah<sup id="cite_ref-independent_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-independent-11">[11]</a></sup> and a suicide bomber who blew himself up at a <a href="/wiki/Falafel" title="Falafel">falafel</a> stand on October 26, 2005, killing seven civilians,<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated2_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated2-12">[12]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated3_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated3-13">[13]</a></sup> and 55 were injured, of them five in severe condition.<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated4_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated4-14">[14]</a></sup> In addition, four civilians were killed when a terrorist opened fire on pedestrians at a bus stop on October 28, 2001.<sup id="cite_ref-mfa.gov.il_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mfa.gov.il-15">[15]</a></sup> However, since the construction of the nearby <a href="/wiki/Israeli_West_Bank_barrier" title="Israeli West Bank barrier">West Bank barrier</a>, the frequency of such incidents has dropped drastically. On August 4, 2006, three rockets fired by <a href="/wiki/Hezbollah" title="Hezbollah">Hezbollah</a> hit Hadera. Hadera is 50 miles (80&#160;km) south of the Lebanese border and marked the farthest point inside Israel hit by Hezbollah.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>Hadera has traditionally been known as a backwater. However, in the 2000s, several projects were launched to improve the quality of life in Hadera. The city center was regenerated, a high-tech business park was constructed, and the world's largest <a href="/wiki/Desalination" title="Desalination">desalination</a> plant was built.<sup id="cite_ref-Haaretz_2-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Haaretz-2">[2]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> In addition, the city has slowly but steadily attracted new home-buyers. Currently, a development plan is underway to turn Hadera into a major center of home ownership, business, and vacation. Some 18,000 new homes will be built, including 2,500 villas and cottages and 1,300 luxury apartments on the coast. These housing units will be built in new neighborhoods that will be built in the underdeveloped northeastern part of the city, and 2.5&#160;km (1.6&#160;mi) of the coastline will be developed. A large park and several major shopping malls will be built. Ten new hotels with a total of 1,800 rooms will also be built; the city is planned to become a future vacation destination due to its closeness to the <a href="/wiki/Galilee" title="Galilee">Galilee</a>, beaches, and access to major highways.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geography">Geography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hadera&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Geography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>Hadera is located on the <a href="/wiki/Israeli_Coastal_Plain" class="mw-redirect" title="Israeli Coastal Plain">Israeli Mediterranean coastal plain</a>, 45&#160;km (28&#160;mi) north of <a href="/wiki/Tel_Aviv" title="Tel Aviv">Tel Aviv</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> The city's jurisdiction covers 53,000 dunams (53.0&#160;km<sup>2</sup>; 20.5&#160;sq&#160;mi), making it the fourth largest city in the country. <a href="/wiki/Nahal_Hadera_Park" class="mw-redirect" title="Nahal Hadera Park">Nahal Hadera Park</a>, a eucalyptus forest covering 1,300 dunams (1.3&#160;km<sup>2</sup>; 0.5&#160;sq&#160;mi) and Hasharon Park are located on the outskirts of Hadera.<sup id="cite_ref-Haaretz_2-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Haaretz-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Transportation">Transportation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hadera&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Transportation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Hadera lies along two main <a href="/wiki/Israel_Railways" title="Israel Railways">Israel Railways</a> lines: the <a href="/wiki/Coastal_railway_line,_Israel" title="Coastal railway line, Israel">Coastal Line</a> and the nowadays freight-only <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Railway_(Israel)" title="Eastern Railway (Israel)">Eastern Line</a>. The city's <a href="/wiki/Hadera_West_Railway_Station" title="Hadera West Railway Station">railway station</a> is located in the west of the city and is on the Tel Aviv suburban line which runs between <a href="/wiki/Binyamina" class="mw-redirect" title="Binyamina">Binyamina</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ashkelon" title="Ashkelon">Ashkelon</a>. The city center of Hadera is located near Israel's two main north-south highways; <a href="/wiki/Highway_2_(Israel)" title="Highway 2 (Israel)">Highway 2</a>, linking Tel Aviv to Haifa, and <a href="/wiki/Highway_4_(Israel)" title="Highway 4 (Israel)">Highway 4</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Haaretz_2-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Haaretz-2">[2]</a></sup> This made Hadera an important junction for all coastal bus transportation after 1948 and into the 1950s.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Economy">Economy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hadera&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Economy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>Hadera's importance as an economic center was first recognised when Israel's first paper mill was opened here in 1953. <a href="/w/index.php?title=Hadera_Paper&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Hadera Paper (page does not exist)">Hadera Paper</a> continues to be a large employer in the city. In December 2009 the world's largest desalination plant of its type,<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> located within the municipal borders, was inaugurated.<sup id="cite_ref-Haaretz_2-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Haaretz-2">[2]</a></sup> Hadera is the location of the <a href="/wiki/Orot_Rabin" title="Orot Rabin">Orot Rabin</a> Power Plant, Israel's largest power station.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Demographics">Demographics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hadera&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Demographics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>According to the <a href="/wiki/Israel_Central_Bureau_of_Statistics" title="Israel Central Bureau of Statistics">Israel Central Bureau of Statistics</a>, as of October 2013, Hadera had a population of 91,634 which is growing at an annual rate of 1.2%.<sup id="cite_ref-cbs_populations_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cbs_populations-22">[22]</a></sup> As of 2003, the city had a population density of 1,516.6 per km<sup>2</sup>.<sup id="cite_ref-cbs_2002_pop_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cbs_2002_pop-23">[23]</a></sup> Of the city's population of 2013 of 91,634, approximately 23,407 were immigrants, many from <a href="/wiki/Ethiopia" title="Ethiopia">Ethiopia</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-cbs_2002_pop_23-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cbs_2002_pop-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>According to a <a href="/wiki/1922_census_of_Palestine" title="1922 census of Palestine">census</a> conducted in 1922 by the <a href="/wiki/British_Mandate_of_Palestine" class="mw-redirect" title="British Mandate of Palestine">British Mandate authorities</a>, Hadera had a population of 540 inhabitants, consisting of 450 Jews, 89 Muslims and 1 Christian.<sup id="cite_ref-Census1922_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Census1922-24">[24]</a></sup> Hadera has grown steadily since 1948, when the city had a population of 11,800. In 1955, the population almost doubled to 22,500. In 1961 it rose to 25,600, 1972 to 32,200, and 1983, to 38,700.<sup id="cite_ref-cbs_2002_pop_23-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cbs_2002_pop-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>The median age in Hadera is 32.8, with 23,200 people 19 years of age or younger, 12.1% between 20 and 29, 14,100 between 30 and 44, 17,600 from 45 to 64, and 9,700, 65 or older.<sup id="cite_ref-statabstract_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-statabstract-25">[25]</a></sup> As of 2007<sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Hadera&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, there were 37,500 males and 39,200 females.<sup id="cite_ref-statabstract_25-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-statabstract-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2001, the ethnic makeup was 99.2% <a href="/wiki/Jew" class="mw-redirect" title="Jew">Jewish</a> and other non-Arab, with no significant <a href="/wiki/Arab" class="mw-redirect" title="Arab">Arab</a> population. In 2000, there were 27,920 salaried workers and 1,819 self-employed. The mean monthly wage in 2000 for a salaried worker was <a href="/wiki/New_Israeli_Shekel" class="mw-redirect" title="New Israeli Shekel">ILS</a> 5,135, a real change of 8.0% over the course of 2000. Salaried males had a mean monthly wage of ILS 6,607 (a real change of 9.0%) compared with ILS 3,598 for females (a real change of 3.1%). The mean income for the self-employed was 6,584. A total of 1,752 people received unemployment benefits and 6,753 received income supplements.</p>

<p>In 2001, there were 15,622 students studying at 42 schools (24 elementary schools with 7,933 students, and 21 high schools with 7,689 students). A total of 57.5% of 12th graders were entitled to a matriculation certificate.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Democratic_School_of_Hadera" title="Democratic School of Hadera">Democratic School of Hadera</a>, which opened in 1987, was the first of its kind in Israel. The <a href="/w/index.php?title=Technoda&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Technoda (page does not exist)">Technoda</a>, an educational center for science and technology equipped with a state-of-the-art telescope and planetarium, is located in Hadera's Givat Olga neighborhood.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Medical_facilities">Medical facilities</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hadera&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Medical facilities">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Neighborhoods">Neighborhoods</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hadera&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Neighborhoods">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Neighborhoods of Hadera include Givat Olga,<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> Beit Eliezer, <a href="/wiki/Kfar_Brandeis" title="Kfar Brandeis">Kfar Brandeis</a>, Haotzar, Hephzibah, Neve Haim, Nissan, Ephraim, Bilu, Klarin, Nahaliel, Shimshon, Shlomo, Pe'er, Bialik, Beitar and The Park.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sports">Sports</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hadera&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Sports">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Hadera is home to four current <a href="/wiki/Association_football" title="Association football">football</a> clubs: <a href="/wiki/Hapoel_Hadera_F.C." title="Hapoel Hadera F.C.">Hapoel Hadera</a>, <a href="/wiki/F.C._Givat_Olga" title="F.C. Givat Olga">F.C. Givat Olga</a> (both playing in <a href="/wiki/Liga_Alef" title="Liga Alef">Liga Alef</a> North), <a href="/w/index.php?title=Beitar_Hadera_F.C.&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Beitar Hadera F.C. (page does not exist)">Beitar Hadera</a> (playing in <a href="/wiki/Liga_Gimel" title="Liga Gimel">Liga Gimel</a> Shomron) and the women's football club <a href="/wiki/Maccabi_Kishronot_Hadera_F.C." title="Maccabi Kishronot Hadera F.C.">Maccabi Kishronot Hadera</a> (playing in <a href="/wiki/Ligat_Nashim_Rishona" class="mw-redirect" title="Ligat Nashim Rishona">Ligat Nashim Rishona</a>). In the past the city was also home to <a href="/wiki/Maccabi_Hadera_F.C." title="Maccabi Hadera F.C.">Maccabi Hadera</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hapoel_Nahliel_F.C." title="Hapoel Nahliel F.C.">Hapoel Nahliel</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hapoel_Beit_Eliezer_F.C." title="Hapoel Beit Eliezer F.C.">Hapoel Beit Eliezer</a>.</p>
<p>The city is also represented in the <a href="/wiki/Israeli_Beach_Soccer_League" title="Israeli Beach Soccer League">Israeli Beach Soccer League</a>. Its team, Hapoel Hadera, won the championship (under its previous name, Hadera's Princes) in 2008.<sup id="cite_ref-champs_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-champs-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Basketball" title="Basketball">Basketball</a>, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Maccabi_Hadera_(women%27s_basketball)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Maccabi Hadera (women's basketball) (page does not exist)">Maccabi Hadera</a>'s women's basketball team plays in second tier <a href="/w/index.php?title=Israeli_Female_Basketball_Liga_Leumit&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Israeli Female Basketball Liga Leumit (page does not exist)">Liga Leumit</a>, while the club's <a href="/wiki/Maccabi_Hadera_B.C." class="mw-redirect" title="Maccabi Hadera B.C.">Maccabi Hadera</a> men's basketball team plays in third tier <a href="/wiki/Liga_Artzit_(basketball)" title="Liga Artzit (basketball)">Liga Artzit</a>.</p>
