<p><b>Heinz Bernhard Löwenstein</b>, known as <b>Heinz Bernard</b> (born 22 December 1923 – died 18 December 1994) was a British actor and director and theatre manager. Of Polish-Jewish and German-Jewish descent, he lived and worked in Israel from 1971-81. He trained at the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Academy_of_Dramatic_Arts" class="mw-redirect" title="Royal Academy of Dramatic Arts">Royal Academy of Dramatic Arts</a> (RADA), graduating in 1951.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>After graduation, he worked in a group of travelling players throughout Britain, performing every night in different towns and villages. He went on to become the manager of the famous leftist <a href="/wiki/Unity_Theatre,_London" title="Unity Theatre, London">Unity Theatre, London</a>. As manager of Unity Theatre he staged the first professional British production of a <a href="/wiki/Brecht" class="mw-redirect" title="Brecht">Brecht</a> play, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Visions_of_Simone_Machard" title="The Visions of Simone Machard">The Visions of Simone Machard</a></i>. <a href="/wiki/Lionel_Bart" title="Lionel Bart">Lionel Bart</a>, who later gained fame as the author of the musical <a href="/wiki/Oliver!_(film)" title="Oliver! (film)"><i>Oliver!</i></a>, designed the poster. Bernard also acted and directed in the travelling <a href="/wiki/Century_Theatre_(UK)" class="mw-redirect" title="Century Theatre (UK)">Century Theatre</a> and taught at RADA, where he was director of admissions.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Heinz's surname at birth was Messinger. He was adopted as a baby by a family called <a href="/wiki/L%C3%B6wenstein" title="Löwenstein">Löwenstein</a>. After leaving <a href="/wiki/RADA" class="mw-redirect" title="RADA">RADA</a> he worked under the professional name Harry Bernard, later becoming Heinz Bernard.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Childhood">Childhood</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Heinz_Bernard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Childhood">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p><b>Heinz Messinger</b> grew up in a Jewish family in <a href="/wiki/Nuremberg" title="Nuremberg">Nuremberg</a> in Nazi Germany. He was adopted by the Lowenstein family after his biological father died of tuberculosis. His biological father was the <a href="/wiki/Hazzan" title="Hazzan">hazzan</a> of the city's Orthodox synagogue. As was usual at the time, he was not told by his parents that he was adopted. In March 1932, when Heinz was nine years old, his adoptive father Max Lowenstein committed suicide following the collapse of his business. In 1933 the Nazis came to power in Germany and began persecuting the Jews. Jews were banned from state schools and only one Jewish school, the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Israelitische_Realschule&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Israelitische Realschule (page does not exist)">Israelitische Realschule</a>, in neighbouring <a href="/wiki/F%C3%BCrth" title="Fürth">Fürth</a>, was allowed to operate (it was closed in 1939<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>) where Heinz attended.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>In November 1935, the <a href="/wiki/Nuremberg_Laws" title="Nuremberg Laws">Nuremberg Laws</a> deprived Heinz and his family of their German citizenship. In December 1936 Heinz had had his <a href="/wiki/Bar_mitzvah" class="mw-redirect" title="Bar mitzvah">bar mitzvah</a> in the main Orthodox synagogue of Nuremberg where his biological father had been the hazzan.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> In August 1938 <a href="/wiki/Julius_Streicher" title="Julius Streicher">Julius Streicher</a>, editor of <a href="/wiki/Der_St%C3%BCrmer" title="Der Stürmer">Der Stürmer</a>, ordered the large Nuremberg Reform Synagogue torn down. In October 1938, during <a href="/wiki/Kristallnacht" title="Kristallnacht">Kristallnacht</a>, the synagogue where Heinz had had his <a href="/wiki/Bar_mitzvah" class="mw-redirect" title="Bar mitzvah">bar mitzvah</a> was burnt down along with most of Germany's synagogues. He hid with relatives in Frankfurt.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>In August 1938, Poland issued an order depriving Polish Jews residing outside of Poland of their citizenship. Although Heinz was born in Germany and adopted by native German Jews, his real father had emigrated from Poland. In return, Germany began expelling Jews of Polish origin to Poland. The Poles refused to admit them and some 17,000 Jews were trapped between the two countries at <a href="/wiki/Zb%C4%85szy%C5%84" title="Zbąszyń">Zbąszyń</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> At some point the Germans began tracing adopted children of Polish emigrants and in 1939 began proceedings to expel Heinz.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Jews in Nazi Germany were eligible for one-way passports only, to leave the country; other countries would not admit them due to this. Heinz was ineligible even for this kind of passport due to his Polish origins. He was also ineligible for a Polish passport. His mother eventually procured a forged Polish passport for him. In July 1939 the MP <a href="/wiki/Josiah_Wedgwood,_1st_Baron_Wedgwood" title="Josiah Wedgwood, 1st Baron Wedgwood">Josiah Wedgwood</a>, who dedicated his life to saving Jews, asked a parliamentary question directed at the Home Secretary:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>"Colonel Wedgwood asked the Home Secretary whether he will authorise a visa to Frau Betty Loewenstein, [sic] and her son, Heinz, Nurenberg, guarantee and application for whom was made by Walter Block, Alsager, Stoke-on-Trent, on 10th May, in view of the fact that the boy, aged 16 years, is under expulsion orders for 10th July?"<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>As a result of this question Heinz and his mother, Betty (maiden name Ehrlich) were granted transit visas to pass through England and join his uncles, who had migrated to the United States. On 28 August 1939, Bernard was sent to England by plane by his mother, who meant to join him a few days later. On the day she planned to leave Germany, war broke out trapping her on the continent. In mid-1940, she received a U.S. visa and took the <a href="/wiki/Trans-Siberian_Railway" title="Trans-Siberian Railway">Trans-Siberian Railway</a>, travelling through <a href="/wiki/Manchukuo" title="Manchukuo">Japanese-occupied China</a> to Japan and sailing from there to Seattle. From Seattle she took a bus to Pennsylvania.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Heinz remained trapped in England and learned English by sitting in cinemas watching movies. He eventually joined a home for Jewish orphans run by <a href="/wiki/Free_German_Youth" title="Free German Youth">exiles from the German Communist Party</a> where he organized weekly plays. Heinz worked in a variety of low-paid jobs, including as a rabbit skinner and a waiter, saving enough money to attend the Royal Academy of Dramatic Arts (RADA) in London.<sup id="cite_ref-independent1_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-independent1-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>After the war, his mother informed him that he was adopted. A biological brother and sister had reached Palestine before the war and now made contact with him. Heinz's brother was a co-founder of Kibbutz <a href="/wiki/Ramat_Yochanan" class="mw-redirect" title="Ramat Yochanan">Ramat Yochanan</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Heinz was a UN-registered refugee with no citizenship of any country, eventually taking British citizenship in 1969<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Career">Career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Heinz_Bernard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In the 1950s, Bernard was manager of <a href="/wiki/Unity_Theatre" class="mw-disambig" title="Unity Theatre">Unity Theatre</a>, a radical theatre in London where he staged British Premieres of plays by <a href="/wiki/Bertolt_Brecht" title="Bertolt Brecht">Bertolt Brecht</a><sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup>. In the early 1960s, Heinz was director of a travelling theatre in the Lake District in Cumbria called the <a href="/wiki/Theatre_by_the_Lake" title="Theatre by the Lake">Century Theatre</a>. At the time television was rare and the travelling theatre provided a major form of local entertainment. In the late 1960s he played the role of the Rabbi in the West End production of <i><a href="/wiki/Fiddler_on_the_Roof" title="Fiddler on the Roof">Fiddler on the Roof</a></i> (at Her Majesty's Theatre).<sup id="cite_ref-independent1_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-independent1-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>From 1971 to 1981, Barnard lived in Israel, where he worked at the National Theatre <a href="/wiki/Habima" class="mw-redirect" title="Habima">Habima</a> <sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> and appeared in the educational TV series, Here We Are and Neighbours. The shows aired twice a week on <a href="/wiki/Israeli_Educational_Television" title="Israeli Educational Television">Israeli_Educational_Television</a> for 17 years, making him a familiar face to a generation of Israelis and leading to parts in a wide variety of mainstream Israeli cinema. In 1981, he returned to England where he had to restart his career. He continued to work until his death of a rare blood disease in 1994. He died four days before his 71st birthday.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
