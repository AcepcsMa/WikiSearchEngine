			<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Tinsel</h1>

<p><b>Tinsel</b>, is a type of decorative material that mimics the effect of ice, consisting of thin strips of sparkling material attached to a thread. When in long narrow strips not attached to thread, it is called "<b>lametta</b>", and emulates icicles. It was originally a metallic <a href="/wiki/Garland_(decoration)" class="mw-redirect" title="Garland (decoration)">garland</a> for <a href="/wiki/Christmas_decoration" title="Christmas decoration">Christmas decoration</a>. The modern production of tinsel typically involves plastic, and is used particularly to decorate <a href="/wiki/Christmas_tree" title="Christmas tree">Christmas trees</a>. It may be hung from ceilings or wrapped around statues, lampposts, and so on. Modern tinsel was invented in <a href="/wiki/Nuremberg" title="Nuremberg">Nuremberg</a>, <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a>, in 1610, and was originally made of shredded <a href="/wiki/Silver" title="Silver">silver</a>.</p>
<p>According to the <i><a href="/wiki/Concise_Oxford_Dictionary" class="mw-redirect" title="Concise Oxford Dictionary">Concise Oxford Dictionary</a></i>, the word is from the <a href="/wiki/Old_French" title="Old French">Old French</a> word <i>estincele</i>, meaning “<a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/sparkle" class="extiw" title="wiktionary:sparkle">sparkle</a>”.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tinsel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Tinsel was invented in <a href="/wiki/Nuremberg" title="Nuremberg">Nuremberg</a> around 1610.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Tinsel was originally made from <a href="/wiki/Extruded" class="mw-redirect" title="Extruded">extruded</a> strands of <a href="/wiki/Silver" title="Silver">silver</a>. Because silver <a href="/wiki/Tarnish" title="Tarnish">tarnishes</a> quickly, other shiny metals were substituted. Before the 16th century, tinsel was used for adorning <a href="/wiki/Sculptures" class="mw-redirect" title="Sculptures">sculptures</a> rather than Christmas trees. It was added to <a href="/wiki/Christmas_trees" class="mw-redirect" title="Christmas trees">Christmas trees</a> to enhance the flickering of the candles on the tree. Tinsel was used to represent the starry sky over a Nativity scene.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>By the early 20th century, manufacturing advances allowed cheap aluminium-based tinsel, and until <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a>, <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a> was the world leader in its manufacture. Production was curtailed during the First World War as a result of wartime demand for <a href="/wiki/Copper" title="Copper">copper</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>During the 1950s, tinsel and tinsel garlands were so popular that they frequently were used more than Christmas lights, as tinsel was much less of a fire hazard than lights were for the then-popular <a href="/wiki/Aluminum_Christmas_tree" title="Aluminum Christmas tree">aluminum Christmas trees</a>, which were made from flammable aluminized paper.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Lead" title="Lead">Lead</a> <a href="/wiki/Foil_(metal)" title="Foil (metal)">foil</a> was a popular material for tinsel manufacture for several decades of the 20th century. Unlike silver, lead tinsel did not tarnish, so it retained its shine. However, use of lead tinsel was phased out after the 1960s due to concern that it exposed children to a risk of <a href="/wiki/Lead_poisoning" title="Lead poisoning">lead poisoning</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-DecorationsSpanned_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DecorationsSpanned-3">[3]</a></sup> In the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Food_and_Drug_Administration" title="Food and Drug Administration">Food and Drug Administration</a> (FDA) concluded in August 1971 that lead tinsel caused an unnecessary risk to <a href="/wiki/Children" class="mw-redirect" title="Children">children</a>, and convinced manufacturers and importers to voluntarily stop producing or importing lead tinsel after January 1, 1972. The FDA did not actually ban the product because the agency did not have the evidence needed to declare lead tinsel a "health <a href="/wiki/Hazard" title="Hazard">hazard</a>."<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Modern tinsel is typically made from <a href="/wiki/Polyvinyl_chloride" title="Polyvinyl chloride">polyvinyl chloride</a> (PVC) film coated with a metallic finish.<sup id="cite_ref-BriteStar1994_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BriteStar1994-5">[5]</a></sup> Coated <a href="/wiki/Mylar" class="mw-redirect" title="Mylar">mylar</a> film also has been used.<sup id="cite_ref-DecorationsSpanned_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DecorationsSpanned-3">[3]</a></sup> These plastic forms of tinsel do not hang as well as tinsel made from heavy metals such as silver and lead.<sup id="cite_ref-BriteStar1994_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BriteStar1994-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Figurative_use">Figurative use</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tinsel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Figurative use">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Germans refer to a row of <a href="/wiki/Awards_and_decorations_of_the_German_Armed_Forces" title="Awards and decorations of the German Armed Forces">military Awards and decorations</a> as <i>Lametta</i> (<a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a> for tinsel), similar to dressing in full regalia or with a high level of formality. The expression was coined earlier to describe the appearance of <a href="/wiki/Hermann_G%C3%B6ring" title="Hermann Göring">Hermann Göring</a>, e.g. in a <i><a href="/wiki/Chanson" title="Chanson">Chanson</a></i> by <a href="/wiki/Claire_Waldoff" title="Claire Waldoff">Claire Waldoff</a>, <i>Rechts Lametta, links Lametta, Und der Bauch wird imma fetta</i> (right tinsel, left tinsel, and the belly gets immense).<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>Humorist <a href="/wiki/Vicco_von_B%C3%BClow" title="Vicco von Bülow">Loriot</a>'s 1977 film <i>Weihnachten bei Hoppenstedts</i> about a family Christmas involved a <a href="/wiki/Prussian_education_system" title="Prussian education system">Prussian educated</a> grandfather humming the <i><a href="https://de.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helenenmarsch" class="extiw" title="de:Helenenmarsch">Helenenmarsch</a></i> with "ra-da-buff" and deploring the lack of tinsel („<i>Früher war mehr Lametta!</i>“ "There used to be more tinsel!" has become proverbial).<sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_uses">Other uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tinsel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Other uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Tinsel has many traditional uses in <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>, including decorations on images, garlands for weddings and other ceremonies, and ornamental trappings for horses and elephants.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Tinsels of various types are popular materials used in <a href="/wiki/Fly_tying" title="Fly tying">fly tying</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
