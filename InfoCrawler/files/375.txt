<p><b>Peter Parler</b> (<a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a>: <span lang="de" xml:lang="de"><i>Peter von Gemünd</i></span>, <a href="/wiki/Czech_language" title="Czech language">Czech</a>: <span lang="cs" xml:lang="cs"><i>Petr Parléř</i></span>, <a href="/wiki/Latin_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Latin language">Latin</a>: <span lang="la" xml:lang="la"><i>Petrus de Gemunden in Suevia</i></span>; 1333 – 13 July 1399) was a German-<a href="/wiki/Bohemian" title="Bohemian">Bohemian</a> architect and sculptor from the <a href="/wiki/Parler_family" title="Parler family">Parler family</a> of master builders. Along with his father, <a href="/wiki/Heinrich_Parler" title="Heinrich Parler">Heinrich Parler</a>, he is one of the most prominent and influential craftsmen of the <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">Middle Ages</a>. Born and apprenticed in the town of <a href="/wiki/Schw%C3%A4bisch_Gm%C3%BCnd" title="Schwäbisch Gmünd">Schwäbisch Gmünd</a>, Peter worked at several important late Medieval building sites, including Strasbourg, Cologne, and Nuremberg. After 1356 he lived in <a href="/wiki/Prague" title="Prague">Prague</a>, capital of the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Bohemia" title="Kingdom of Bohemia">Kingdom of Bohemia</a> and seat of the <a href="/wiki/Holy_Roman_Empire" title="Holy Roman Empire">Holy Roman Empire</a>, where he created his most famous works: <a href="/wiki/St._Vitus_Cathedral" title="St. Vitus Cathedral">St. Vitus Cathedral</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Charles_Bridge" title="Charles Bridge">Charles Bridge</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Peter_Parler&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:M%C3%BCnster-GD-Vorderansicht.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/86/M%C3%BCnster-GD-Vorderansicht.JPG/220px-M%C3%BCnster-GD-Vorderansicht.JPG" width="220" height="288" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/86/M%C3%BCnster-GD-Vorderansicht.JPG/330px-M%C3%BCnster-GD-Vorderansicht.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/86/M%C3%BCnster-GD-Vorderansicht.JPG/440px-M%C3%BCnster-GD-Vorderansicht.JPG 2x" data-file-width="1956" data-file-height="2563" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:M%C3%BCnster-GD-Vorderansicht.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The choir of Holy Cross Minster, <a href="/wiki/Schw%C3%A4bisch_Gm%C3%BCnd" title="Schwäbisch Gmünd">Schwäbisch Gmünd</a>, Germany</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Little is known about the earliest youth of Peter Parler. His father, <a href="/wiki/Heinrich_Parler" title="Heinrich Parler">Heinrich Parler the Elder</a>, was a stonemason at <a href="/wiki/Cologne_Cathedral" title="Cologne Cathedral">Cologne Cathedral</a>. Around 1333, when Peter was born, Heinrich was invited by the <a href="/wiki/Free_imperial_city" title="Free imperial city">free imperial city</a> of <a href="/wiki/Schw%C3%A4bisch_Gm%C3%BCnd" title="Schwäbisch Gmünd">Schwäbisch Gmünd</a> to take over construction of a large new parish church, Holy Cross Minster.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Peter was already working alongside his father on the church when the cornerstone of the <a href="/wiki/Choir_(architecture)" title="Choir (architecture)">choir</a> was laid in 1351. This is presumably also where his apprenticeship was completed. The division of the choir by a continuous balustrade demonstrates what would become his trademark style. The arched section at the southern portal of the church can also be attributed to Peter.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> The designs developed by the father and son team at Holy Cross were unique and mark an important milestone in late Gothic architecture and sculpture.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Peter then set off on the so-called "<a href="/wiki/Journeyman_years" title="Journeyman years">Wandering Years</a>" (<a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a>: <span lang="de" xml:lang="de"><i>Wanderjahre</i></span>), the Medieval German tradition of working as a travelling craftsman after completing apprenticeship. On these visits he learned the most important concepts of Gothic construction techniques in Central Europe. He visited Cologne, perhaps <a href="/wiki/Paris" title="Paris">Paris</a>, and certainly spent time in <a href="/wiki/Strasbourg" title="Strasbourg">Strasbourg</a> – precise knowledge of <a href="/wiki/Strasbourg_Cathedral" title="Strasbourg Cathedral">Strasbourg Cathedral</a> can be seen in his subsequent buildings.<sup id="cite_ref-Baragli.2C_p._363_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Baragli.2C_p._363-4">[4]</a></sup> While working in Cologne he met Druda (Gertrud), daughter of the resident architect Bartholomäus von Hamm. They eventually married and had four children: three sons, of which two went on to become known master builders in their own right, and a daughter.<sup id="cite_ref-Grueber.2C_p._275_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Grueber.2C_p._275-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>While undocumented, some scholars believe Parler traveled to <a href="/wiki/England_in_the_Late_Middle_Ages" title="England in the Late Middle Ages">England</a> during this period, while others find the possibility unlikely. The idea arises because certain detailed and concealed technical devices appear in his later work which could have only been obtained though first-hand experience and not from any architectural drawings.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> What is definitely known is that masons at Strasbourg implemented specific craft methods from western England in the late 1200s, which points to knowledge of <a href="/wiki/English_Gothic_architecture" title="English Gothic architecture">English Gothic</a> architectural methods in the <a href="/wiki/Rhineland" title="Rhineland">Rhineland</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Since Peter Parler spent time working at Strasbourg, it is much more likely his knowledge of these methods came from there.<sup id="cite_ref-Schock-Werner.2C_p._72_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Schock-Werner.2C_p._72-8">[8]</a></sup> In particular, the vaults he later designed at St. Vitus Cathedral were most probably modeled after the St. Catherine Chapel of Strasbourg Cathedral.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Nuremberg">Nuremberg</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Peter_Parler&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Nuremberg">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Nuremberg_Frauenkirche_edit.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e6/Nuremberg_Frauenkirche_edit.jpg/220px-Nuremberg_Frauenkirche_edit.jpg" width="220" height="291" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e6/Nuremberg_Frauenkirche_edit.jpg/330px-Nuremberg_Frauenkirche_edit.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e6/Nuremberg_Frauenkirche_edit.jpg/440px-Nuremberg_Frauenkirche_edit.jpg 2x" data-file-width="4744" data-file-height="6267" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Nuremberg_Frauenkirche_edit.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Frauenkirche,_Nuremberg" title="Frauenkirche, Nuremberg">Frauenkirche, Nuremberg</a>, Germany, where the first sculpture by Peter Parler can be directly identified (1352–1356)</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Sometime after 1352 Peter joined the construction works at the <a href="/wiki/Frauenkirche,_Nuremberg" title="Frauenkirche, Nuremberg">Frauenkirche</a> in <a href="/wiki/Nuremberg" title="Nuremberg">Nuremberg</a> as the <i>parlier</i>, or chief site assistant alongside his father. The exact date and his precise role are unclear. The foundations of the church had already been laid in 1351 on the initiative of <a href="/wiki/Charles_IV,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Charles IV, Holy Roman Emperor">Charles IV, Holy Roman Emperor</a>, who envisioned it as a center for holding imperial ceremonies. This is reflected in the carved <a href="/wiki/Coats_of_arms" class="mw-redirect" title="Coats of arms">coats of arms</a> of the Emperor, the seven <a href="/wiki/Prince-elector" title="Prince-elector">Prince-electors</a>, and the city of <a href="/wiki/Rome" title="Rome">Rome</a> where the <a href="/wiki/Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Holy Roman Emperor">Holy Roman Emperors</a> were <a href="/wiki/Coronation_of_the_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Coronation of the Holy Roman Emperor">crowned</a>. Here the first sculptures can be directly ascribed to Peter, among them a prominent figure of <a href="/wiki/Zechariah_(Hebrew_prophet)" title="Zechariah (Hebrew prophet)">Zacharias</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Schock-Werner.2C_p._72_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Schock-Werner.2C_p._72-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>Charles also commissioned the building of <a href="/wiki/Lauf_Castle" title="Lauf Castle">Wenzelsburg Castle</a> at <a href="/wiki/Lauf_an_der_Pegnitz" title="Lauf an der Pegnitz">Lauf an der Pegnitz</a>, near Nuremberg.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Built on the ruins of an older castle, its ambitious architecture can be directly connected to the Parlers and Peter probably worked there as a sculptor.<sup id="cite_ref-Baragli.2C_p._363_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Baragli.2C_p._363-4">[4]</a></sup> By 1355 their sophisticated style had come to the attention of Charles IV and Peter was called to work in Prague, the capitol of the new Kingdom of Bohemia and seat of the Emperor. The year of this appointment is not entirely clear since the documentary inscription in Prague was partially painted over in the 15th century. Today research usually sets the date to 1356,<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> which accounts for a pause in construction in both Nuremberg and Prague, as well as information that he was at least 23 years old at the time of his appointment.<sup id="cite_ref-Schock-Werner.2C_p._72_8-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Schock-Werner.2C_p._72-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Prague">Prague</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Peter_Parler&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Prague">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:St_Vitus_Prague_September_2016-2.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b6/St_Vitus_Prague_September_2016-2.jpg/220px-St_Vitus_Prague_September_2016-2.jpg" width="220" height="330" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b6/St_Vitus_Prague_September_2016-2.jpg/330px-St_Vitus_Prague_September_2016-2.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b6/St_Vitus_Prague_September_2016-2.jpg/440px-St_Vitus_Prague_September_2016-2.jpg 2x" data-file-width="4792" data-file-height="7197" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:St_Vitus_Prague_September_2016-2.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Interior of St. Vitus Cathedral, clearly showing the Parler-style balustrade</div>
</div>
</div>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/History_of_Prague#Renaissance" title="History of Prague">History of Prague §&#160;Renaissance</a></div>
<p>Upon his ascension to the throne as <a href="/wiki/King_of_Bohemia" class="mw-redirect" title="King of Bohemia">King of Bohemia</a> in 1347, Charles IV established his capitol at Prague. At the time it consisted of two autonomous Medieval towns on each side of the <a href="/wiki/Vltava_river" class="mw-redirect" title="Vltava river">Vltava river</a> (<a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a>: <span lang="de" xml:lang="de"><i>Moldau</i></span>): the <a href="/wiki/Old_Town_(Prague)" title="Old Town (Prague)">Old Town</a> on the right and the <a href="/wiki/Mal%C3%A1_Strana" title="Malá Strana">Malá Strana</a> with <a href="/wiki/Prague_Castle" title="Prague Castle">Prague Castle</a> above it on the left. In 1348 he ordered construction of the <a href="/wiki/New_Town,_Prague" title="New Town, Prague">New Town</a> adjacent to the existing <a href="/wiki/Old_Town_(Prague)" title="Old Town (Prague)">Old Town</a>, founded <a href="/wiki/Charles_University" title="Charles University">Charles University</a>, began reconstruction of the castle, and restarted work on the cathedral. To accomplish this ambitious building program he recruited the best architects he could find. First was <a href="/wiki/Matthias_of_Arras" title="Matthias of Arras">Matthias of Arras</a>, who died in 1352. When elected <a href="/wiki/Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Holy Roman Emperor">Holy Roman Emperor</a> in 1355, Charles determined to make Prague the showcase capitol of <a href="/wiki/Central_Europe" title="Central Europe">Central Europe</a> and recruited Peter Parler to execute this grand vision. The period would launch what became known as Prague's "golden era" and it soon was the largest European city after <a href="/wiki/Constantinople" title="Constantinople">Constantinople</a>, <a href="/wiki/Paris" title="Paris">Paris</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Granada" title="Granada">Granada</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="St._Vitus_Cathedral">St. Vitus Cathedral</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Peter_Parler&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: St. Vitus Cathedral">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/St._Vitus_Cathedral" title="St. Vitus Cathedral">St. Vitus Cathedral</a></div>
<p>When Peter arrived in Prague in 1356 at the age of 23, his immediate assignment was to take over the construction site at St. Vitus Cathedral, which had languished since the death of Matthias of Arras in 1352. It is indicative of Peter's talents that the most important building in the Empire was then entrusted to such a young architect.<sup id="cite_ref-Baragli.2C_p._363_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Baragli.2C_p._363-4">[4]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Bork.2C_p._201_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bork.2C_p._201-13">[13]</a></sup> Peter continued the works at St. Vitus from the ambulatory and chapels, which were partially complete, and gradually changed the Matthias plan while keeping continual references to the original vision.<sup id="cite_ref-Schock-Werner.2C_p._72_8-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Schock-Werner.2C_p._72-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Charles_Bridge">Charles Bridge</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Peter_Parler&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Charles Bridge">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Prague_07-2016_View_from_Petrinska_Tower_img2.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/20/Prague_07-2016_View_from_Petrinska_Tower_img2.jpg/220px-Prague_07-2016_View_from_Petrinska_Tower_img2.jpg" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/20/Prague_07-2016_View_from_Petrinska_Tower_img2.jpg/330px-Prague_07-2016_View_from_Petrinska_Tower_img2.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/20/Prague_07-2016_View_from_Petrinska_Tower_img2.jpg/440px-Prague_07-2016_View_from_Petrinska_Tower_img2.jpg 2x" data-file-width="5021" data-file-height="3347" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Prague_07-2016_View_from_Petrinska_Tower_img2.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Charles Bridge and the Old Town Bridge Tower</div>
</div>
</div>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Charles_Bridge" title="Charles Bridge">Charles Bridge</a></div>
<p>In 1342, just prior to the reign of Charles IV, the <a href="/wiki/Romanesque_architecture" title="Romanesque architecture">Romanesque</a> Judith Bridge across the Vltava was severely damaged in a flood. It had been the first stone bridge to connect each bank of the river. In 1357, Charles commissioned Peter to build a new structure to reconnect the growing city, now consisting of 4 districts. The first foundation stone was ceremonially laid in the presence of the Emperor on 9 July 1357 at 5:31 am. The exact time is known because the architect had the <a href="/wiki/Palindromic_number" title="Palindromic number">palindromic number</a> 135797531 carved into the bridge's tower, having been chosen by the royal astrologists as the most auspicious for starting construction. Due to the complexity of the engineering project, and the large number of other projects underway, construction lasted until 1402.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>The east tower of the Bridge, Old Town Bridge Tower, was also built by Peter Parler. Its arch contains a <a href="/wiki/Net_vault" class="mw-redirect" title="Net vault">net vault</a> that was the first of its kind in Bohemia.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_Works">Other Works</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Peter_Parler&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Other Works">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Apart from the cathedral and the bridge, Peter was also the main designer of the <a href="/wiki/New_Town,_Prague" title="New Town, Prague">New Town of Prague</a>. He also built the <i>All Saints' Chapel</i> inside the Royal Palace of Prague Castle. After a fire in 1541 it was redecorated in the <a href="/wiki/Baroque_architecture" title="Baroque architecture">Baroque</a> style. Between 1360–78 Parler built the chancel of the <a href="/wiki/Bartholomew_the_Apostle" title="Bartholomew the Apostle">St. Bartholomew</a> church in <a href="/wiki/Kol%C3%ADn" title="Kolín">Kolín</a>. Peter is also responsible for various tombs, shrines and sculptures at various sites in and around Prague, including at <a href="/wiki/Kutn%C3%A1_Hora" title="Kutná Hora">Kutná Hora</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Family_and_personal_life">Family and personal life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Peter_Parler&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Family and personal life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Petr_Parler_nahrdeska.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/08/Petr_Parler_nahrdeska.jpg/220px-Petr_Parler_nahrdeska.jpg" width="220" height="330" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/08/Petr_Parler_nahrdeska.jpg/330px-Petr_Parler_nahrdeska.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/08/Petr_Parler_nahrdeska.jpg/440px-Petr_Parler_nahrdeska.jpg 2x" data-file-width="600" data-file-height="900" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Petr_Parler_nahrdeska.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Tomb of Peter Parler in St. Vitus Cathedral</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Parler_family" title="Parler family">Parler family</a> grew large and its many members worked at Gothic construction sites all over <a href="/wiki/Central_Europe" title="Central Europe">Central Europe</a> and <a href="/wiki/Northern_Italy" title="Northern Italy">Northern Italy</a>. Peter's branch of the family began with his marriage to his first wife, Gertrude. Although their wedding date is not exactly known, by 1360 they were raising three sons and a daughter:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Johann_Parler" title="Johann Parler">Johann Parler the Younger</a>: born around 1359, educated in Prague, worked alongside his father on St. Vitus. Johann took over his father's role as the cathedral's master builder in 1398 and also became the master builder of <a href="/wiki/St._Barbara%27s_Church,_Kutn%C3%A1_Hora" title="St. Barbara's Church, Kutná Hora">St. Barbara's Church, Kutná Hora</a>, now a <a href="/wiki/UNESCO_World_Heritage_Site" class="mw-redirect" title="UNESCO World Heritage Site">UNESCO World Heritage Site</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></li>
<li>Nikolaus Parler: became a clergyman and served as a canon in Prague from 1380–1398. Little else is known about him.<sup id="cite_ref-Grueber.2C_p._275_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Grueber.2C_p._275-5">[5]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Wenzel_Parler" title="Wenzel Parler">Wenzel Parler</a>: born around 1360, also educated in Prague, worked with both his father and brother Johann at St. Vitus through the 1390s. He then went on to become master stonemason at <a href="/wiki/St._Stephen%27s_Cathedral,_Vienna" title="St. Stephen's Cathedral, Vienna">St. Stephen's Cathedral, Vienna</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></li>
<li>Their daughter appears around 1383 as the wife of a Michael from Cologne, possibly the son of another cathedral architect named Michael (1364–1387).<sup id="cite_ref-Grueber.2C_p._275_5-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Grueber.2C_p._275-5">[5]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<p>After 1360, Peter owned a house in <a href="/wiki/St._George%27s_Square_(Prague_Castle)" title="St. George's Square (Prague Castle)">Prague Castle Square</a> and had been elected city alderman. His first wife Gertrude died in the late 1360s and he returned to Cologne to dispose of her inheritance in 1370. He then married Elisabeth Agnes von Bur in 1380 and in the same year bought a second house on Castle Square. From this marriage two more children were born: Johann, who possibly became a stonemason in <a href="/wiki/Zagreb" title="Zagreb">Zagreb</a>; and Paul, about whom no information survives.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> He may have had other children as well.<sup id="cite_ref-Grueber.2C_p._275_5-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Grueber.2C_p._275-5">[5]</a></sup> The purchase of the houses, the marriage of his daughter, and the number of his children led to some legal disputes. Peter Parler is known to have relinquished both houses to his second wife and their children.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>Peter was granted citizenship in Prague in 1379.<sup id="cite_ref-Baragli.2C_p._363_4-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Baragli.2C_p._363-4">[4]</a></sup> By his old age he had become a wealthy man and was still active as an architect. His name appears as Cathedral Master (<a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a>: <span lang="de" xml:lang="de"><i>Dombaumeister</i></span>) on a panel at St. Vitus as late as 1396.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> He died in Prague in 1399 and was buried at St. Vitus Cathedral, with his sons Peter and Wenzel carrying on his work.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legacy">Legacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Peter_Parler&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Legacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Peter Parler was one of the most well-known and influential craftsman of the Middle Ages.<sup id="cite_ref-Bork.2C_p._201_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bork.2C_p._201-13">[13]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> The designs of both he and his father became known as the "Parler style" and spread throughout Central Europe. Significant examples include: <a href="/wiki/St._Martin%27s_Church,_Landshut" title="St. Martin's Church, Landshut">St. Martin's Church, Landshut</a> (begun 1389); <a href="/wiki/St._Lorenz,_Nuremberg" title="St. Lorenz, Nuremberg">St. Lorenz, Nuremberg</a> (nave begun 1400); St. George's Minster, <a href="/wiki/Dinkelsb%C3%BChl" title="Dinkelsbühl">Dinkelsbühl</a> (begun 1448); <a href="/wiki/St._Stephen%27s_Cathedral,_Vienna" title="St. Stephen's Cathedral, Vienna">St. Stephen's Cathedral, Vienna</a> (south tower begun 1368); and numerous other examples across the <a href="/wiki/Hanseatic_League" title="Hanseatic League">Hanseatic League</a> from the <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Netherlands</a> to <a href="/wiki/Poland" title="Poland">Poland</a>. Examples can also be found in <a href="/wiki/Scandinavia" title="Scandinavia">Scandinavia</a>, such as at St. Mary's, <a href="/wiki/Helsing%C3%B8r" title="Helsingør">Helsingør</a>, <a href="/wiki/Denmark" title="Denmark">Denmark</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>A demonstration of just how far his ideas went can be found at <a href="/wiki/Seville_Cathedral" title="Seville Cathedral">Seville Cathedral</a>, begun in 1402 and today still the largest Gothic cathedral in the world.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> The standard Gothic verticality of the central space is broken up by a <a href="/wiki/Balustrade" class="mw-redirect" title="Balustrade">balustrade</a> at the boundary with the <a href="/wiki/Clerestory" title="Clerestory">clerestory</a> windows. More importantly, the balustrade's widely spaced vertical bars and its position flowing outside of the outermost ribs are exactly like Peter Parler's original design of the western bays at St. Vitus’ Cathedral.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> Construction at Seville Cathedral continued until 1506.</p>
<p>During the <a href="/wiki/Hussite_wars" class="mw-redirect" title="Hussite wars">Hussite wars</a> (1419–1434) the <a href="/wiki/Lands_of_the_Bohemian_Crown" title="Lands of the Bohemian Crown">lands of the Bohemian Crown</a> were totally ravaged. Although his buildings still stand, much of Peter Parler's sculptural works were destroyed. A portrait of him, carved out of his hand, remains in the <i><span lang="de" xml:lang="de">Domgalerie</span></i> at St. Vitus Cathedral.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Asteroid">Asteroid</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Peter_Parler&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Asteroid">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>An <a href="/wiki/Asteroid" title="Asteroid">Asteroid</a> named in honor of Peter Parler, <a href="/wiki/6550_Parl%C3%A9%C5%99" class="mw-redirect" title="6550 Parléř">6550 Parléř</a>, was discovered by <a href="/wiki/Anton%C3%ADn_Mrkos" title="Antonín Mrkos">Antonín Mrkos</a> at <a href="/wiki/Kle%C5%A5_Observatory" title="Kleť Observatory">Kleť Observatory</a> on 4 November 1988.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Parler_Prize">Parler Prize</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Peter_Parler&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Parler Prize">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The "Peter Parler Prize" was established in 1994 by the <a href="/wiki/Deutsche_Stiftung_Denkmalschutz" title="Deutsche Stiftung Denkmalschutz">German Foundation for Monument Protection</a> (<a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a>: <span lang="de" xml:lang="de"><i>Deutsche Stiftung Denkmalschutz</i></span>) and the Federal Association of German Stonemasons (<a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a>: <span lang="de" xml:lang="de"><i>Bundesverband Deutscher Steinmetze</i></span>). It is awarded every two years at the International Exhibition of Natural Stone and Stone Processing in Nuremberg for outstanding service in the preservation of cultural heritage. The prize is accompanied by a commemorative bust of Peter Parler and EUR 15,000.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> It was most recently awarded in 2015 to August Weber and Helmut Schneider for restoration work at St. George's church in <a href="/wiki/Ulm" title="Ulm">Ulm</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
