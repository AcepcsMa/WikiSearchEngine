			<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Michael Wolgemut</h1>

<p><b>Michael Wolgemut</b> (formerly spelt <i>Wohlgemuth</i>; 1434 – 30 November 1519) was a German painter and <a href="/wiki/Printmaker" class="mw-redirect" title="Printmaker">printmaker</a>, who was born and ran a workshop in <a href="/wiki/Nuremberg" title="Nuremberg">Nuremberg</a>. He taught <a href="/wiki/Albrecht_D%C3%BCrer" title="Albrecht Dürer">Albrecht Dürer</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biography">Biography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Michael_Wolgemut&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Biography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Michael_Wolgemut_-_Portrait_of_Ursula_Tucher_-_WGA25863.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f6/Michael_Wolgemut_-_Portrait_of_Ursula_Tucher_-_WGA25863.jpg/220px-Michael_Wolgemut_-_Portrait_of_Ursula_Tucher_-_WGA25863.jpg" width="220" height="296" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f6/Michael_Wolgemut_-_Portrait_of_Ursula_Tucher_-_WGA25863.jpg/330px-Michael_Wolgemut_-_Portrait_of_Ursula_Tucher_-_WGA25863.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f6/Michael_Wolgemut_-_Portrait_of_Ursula_Tucher_-_WGA25863.jpg/440px-Michael_Wolgemut_-_Portrait_of_Ursula_Tucher_-_WGA25863.jpg 2x" data-file-width="800" data-file-height="1077" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Michael_Wolgemut_-_Portrait_of_Ursula_Tucher_-_WGA25863.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Portrait of Ursula Tucher, 1478</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Wolgemut trained with his father Valentin Wolgemut (who died in 1469 or 1470) and is thought to have been an assistant to <a href="/wiki/Hans_Pleydenwurff" title="Hans Pleydenwurff">Hans Pleydenwurff</a> in Nuremberg. He worked with Gabriel Malesskircher in Munich early in 1471,<sup id="cite_ref-rowlands_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rowlands-1">[1]</a></sup> leaving the city after unsuccessfully suing Malesskircher's daughter for breach of contract, claiming she had broken off their engagement.<sup id="cite_ref-sinners_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sinners-2">[2]</a></sup> He then returned to his late father's workshop in Nuremberg, which his mother had maintained since Valentin's death.<sup id="cite_ref-rowlands_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rowlands-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1472 he married Pleydenwurff's widow and took over his workshop;<sup id="cite_ref-sinners_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sinners-2">[2]</a></sup> her son <a href="/wiki/Wilhelm_Pleydenwurff" class="mw-redirect" title="Wilhelm Pleydenwurff">Wilhelm Pleydenwurff</a> worked as an assistant, and from 1491 a partner, to Wolgemut. Some consider Wilhelm a finer artist than Wolgemut, however he died in January 1494, when he was probably still in his thirties. Wilhelm's oeuvre remains unclear, though works in various media have been attributed to him.</p>
<p>The importance of Wolgemut as an artist rests not only on his own individual works, but also on the fact that he was the head of a large workshop, in which many different branches of the fine arts were carried on by a great number of pupil-assistants, including <a href="/wiki/Albrecht_D%C3%BCrer" title="Albrecht Dürer">Albrecht Dürer</a>, who completed an apprenticeship with him between 1486-9. In his <i>atelier</i> large altar-pieces and other sacred paintings were executed, and also elaborate carved painted wood <a href="/wiki/Retable" title="Retable">retables</a>, consisting of crowded subjects in high relief, richly decorated with gold and colour.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Woodcuts">Woodcuts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Michael_Wolgemut&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Woodcuts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Danse_macabre_by_Michael_Wolgemut.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bf/Danse_macabre_by_Michael_Wolgemut.png/220px-Danse_macabre_by_Michael_Wolgemut.png" width="220" height="187" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bf/Danse_macabre_by_Michael_Wolgemut.png/330px-Danse_macabre_by_Michael_Wolgemut.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bf/Danse_macabre_by_Michael_Wolgemut.png/440px-Danse_macabre_by_Michael_Wolgemut.png 2x" data-file-width="705" data-file-height="599" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Danse_macabre_by_Michael_Wolgemut.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Michael Wolgemut, <i><a href="/wiki/Dance_of_Death" class="mw-redirect" title="Dance of Death">Danse Macabre</a>,</i> 1493</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Wolgemut was a leader among the artists reviving the standards of German <a href="/wiki/Woodcut" title="Woodcut">woodcut</a> at this time. The production of woodcuts was a large part of the work of the workshop, the blocks being cut from Wolgemut's designs. They were mostly made to supply the many publishers in Nuremberg with book illustrations, with the most attractive also being sold separately. Wolgemut's woodcuts followed the advances in <a href="/wiki/Engraving" title="Engraving">engraving</a>, depicting volume and shading to a much greater extent than before. Many are remarkable for their vigour and clever adaptation to the special necessities of the technique of woodcut. Nonetheless, they were very often hand-coloured before or after sale. His pupil Dürer was to build on and to so surpass his achievement that it is often overlooked.</p>
<p>Two large and copiously illustrated books have woodcuts supplied by Wolgemut and his stepson Wilhelm Pleydenwurff; both were printed and published by Germany's largest publisher, the Nuremberger <a href="/wiki/Anton_Koberger" title="Anton Koberger">Anton Koberger</a>, who was also Dürer's godfather. The first is the <i>Schatzbehalter der wahren Reichthumer des Heils</i> (1491); the other is the <i>Historia mundi</i>, by <a href="/wiki/Hartmann_Schedel" title="Hartmann Schedel">Schedel</a> (1493), usually known as the <i><a href="/wiki/Nuremberg_Chronicle" title="Nuremberg Chronicle">Nuremberg Chronicle</a></i>, which is highly valued, not for the text, but for its remarkable collection of 1,809 spirited illustrations. Wolgemut and Wilhelm Pleydenwurff were first commissioned to provide the illustrations in 1487-1488, and a further contract of 29 December 1491 commissioned manuscript layouts of the text and illustrations. A further contact of 1492 stipulated that Koberger should provide a locked room for the blocks to be kept safely. A drawing by Wolgemut for the elaborate frontispiece, dated 1490, is in the <a href="/wiki/British_Museum" title="British Museum">British Museum</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-bm1_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bm1-3">[3]</a></sup> As with other books of the period, many of the woodcuts, showing towns, battles or kings were used more than once in the book, with the text labels merely changed. The book is large, with a double-page woodcut measuring about 342x500mm.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Paintings">Paintings</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Michael_Wolgemut&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Paintings">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Wolgemut's paintings show Flemish influence, and he may have traveled within <a href="/wiki/Flanders" title="Flanders">Flanders</a> (modern Belgium and surrounding areas). The earliest known work by Wolgemut is a <a href="/wiki/Retable" title="Retable">retable</a> consisting of four panels, dated 1465, now in the <a href="/wiki/Munich" title="Munich">Munich</a> gallery, a decorative work of much beauty. In 1479 he painted the retable of the high altar in the church of St Mary at <a href="/wiki/Zwickau" title="Zwickau">Zwickau</a>, which still exists, receiving for it the large sum of 1400 gulden. One of his finest and largest works is the great retable painted for the church of the <a href="/wiki/Augustinian" class="mw-redirect" title="Augustinian">Augustinian</a> friars at Nuremberg, now moved into the museum; it consists of a great many panels, with figures of locally venerated saints.</p>
<p>In 1501 Wolgemut was employed to decorate the town hall at <a href="/wiki/Goslar" title="Goslar">Goslar</a> with a large series of paintings; some on the ceiling are on panel, and others on the walls are painted thinly in <a href="/wiki/Tempera" title="Tempera">tempera</a> on canvas. As a portrait-painter he enjoyed much repute, and some of his works of this class are very admirable for their realistic vigour and minute finish.</p>
<p>Outside Germany Wolgemut's paintings are scarce: the Royal Institution at <a href="/wiki/Liverpool" title="Liverpool">Liverpool</a> possesses two good examples--<i>Pilate washing his Hands</i>, and <i>The Deposition from the Cross</i>, parts probably of a large altar-piece. During the last ten years of his life Wohlgemut appears to have produced little by his own hand. One of his latest paintings is the retable at <a href="/wiki/Schwabach" title="Schwabach">Schwabach</a>, executed in 1508, the contract for which still exists. He died at Nuremberg in 1519.</p>
