<p>The <b>Nuremberg Toy Museum</b> (also known as <b>Lydia Bayer Museum</b><sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup>) in <a href="/wiki/Nuremberg" title="Nuremberg">Nuremberg</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bavaria" title="Bavaria">Bavaria</a>, is a municipal <a href="/wiki/Museum" title="Museum">museum</a>, which was founded in 1971. It is considered to be one of the most well known <a href="/wiki/Toy_museums" class="mw-redirect" title="Toy museums">toy museums</a> in the world, depicting the cultural history of <a href="/wiki/Toys" class="mw-redirect" title="Toys">toys</a> from <a href="/wiki/Classical_antiquity" title="Classical antiquity">antiquity</a> to the present.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nuremberg_Toy_Museum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Hallersches_Haus">Hallersches Haus</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nuremberg_Toy_Museum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Hallersches Haus">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The toy museum's building, located in KarlstraÃe 13â15, can be dated back to 1517 as being the property of Wilhelm Haller, senior, member of a patrician family. Jeweler, Paul Kandler bought the house in 1611 and had the front rebuilt for the first time (probably by Jakob Wolff senior). The <a href="/wiki/Bay_window" title="Bay window">oriel</a> (this type of oriel is called a chÃ¶rlein) was constructed roughly around 1720. A distinctive feature of the Hallersches Haus, but also of many other houses in Nuremberg, is the <i>Dockengalerie</i>, which is a wooden gallery built around an inner <a href="/wiki/Courtyard" title="Courtyard">courtyard</a>, connecting the adjacent buildings. 'Docken' refers to turned wooden <a href="/wiki/Baluster" title="Baluster">balusters</a> used for construction galleries and limbless wooden dolls.</p>
<p>The estate was badly damaged during the World War II, but was rebuilt in the following years. Furthermore, the building is one of the stops of the âHistorical Mile Nurembergâ.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Lydia_and_Paul_Bayer">Lydia and Paul Bayer</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nuremberg_Toy_Museum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Lydia and Paul Bayer">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The core of the museumâs collection is approximately 12,000 toys, which have been collected over decades by Lydia (1897â1961) and Paul Bayer (1896â1982). The Bayers had begun to put together a comprehensive collection of toys by the early 1920s although toys had not yet been recognized as having historico-cultural value. The private Lydia Bayer Museum, located in NeubaustraÃe in <a href="/wiki/W%C3%BCrzburg" title="WÃ¼rzburg">WÃ¼rzburg</a>, was open to the public.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Museum">Museum</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nuremberg_Toy_Museum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Museum">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The city of Nuremberg took over the Bayers' stocks in 1966. Thanks to the support of the aid association, the Hallersches Haus in KarlstraÃe was ready to open in 1971.</p>
<p>The toy museum has turned into an extraordinarily successful museum with international recognition. The exhibits area was expanded to 1,200&#160;m<sup>2</sup> (13,000&#160;sq&#160;ft) in 1989 and to 1,400&#160;m<sup>2</sup> (15,000&#160;sq&#160;ft), due to roof constructions, in 1998. The Toy Museum and the German Games Archive in Nuremberg are part of the network Nuremberg Municipal Museums founded in 1994. Other places that are part of the network are the <a href="/wiki/Albrecht_D%C3%BCrer%27s_House" title="Albrecht DÃ¼rer's House">DÃ¼rer-Haus</a>, the City Museum Fembohaus, the Tucher Mansion, the Museum for Industrial Culture, the Documentation Centre <a href="/wiki/Nazi_party_rally_grounds" title="Nazi party rally grounds">Nazi Party Rally Grounds</a> and the Memorium <a href="/wiki/Nuremberg_trials" title="Nuremberg trials">Nuremberg Trials</a>.</p>
<h3><span id="Gockelreiterbrunnen_(Rooster_Rider_Fountain)"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Gockelreiterbrunnen_.28Rooster_Rider_Fountain.29">Gockelreiterbrunnen (Rooster Rider Fountain)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nuremberg_Toy_Museum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Gockelreiterbrunnen (Rooster Rider Fountain)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On the occasion of the <a href="/wiki/Inauguration" title="Inauguration">inauguration</a> of the museum in 1971, which incidentally marked the three hundred-year anniversary of <a href="/wiki/Albrecht_D%C3%BCrer" title="Albrecht DÃ¼rer">DÃ¼rer</a>âs death, the <i>Gockelreiterbrunnen</i> (Rooster Rider Fountain), designed by Nuremberg artist, <a href="/wiki/Michael_Mathias_Prechtl" title="Michael Mathias Prechtl">Michael Mathias Prechtl</a>, was erected in front of the toy museum. The figure depicting a rooster rider is on top of a pipe rising up out of the fountainâs washed-concrete basin. The colorfully painted <a href="/wiki/Ceramic" title="Ceramic">ceramic</a> figure, which is surrounded by iron bars, fits well into the location in two respects: not only is its shape reminiscent of a wooden toy referring to the function of the museum, but it also recalls Nuremberg as the city of toys.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Exhibitions">Exhibitions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nuremberg_Toy_Museum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Exhibitions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The collection, which contains around 87,000 objects, of which only about five percent are visible in the museum, spans the time from antiquity to the present. It focuses on the development of the toy over the past two hundred years. The great majority of the toys are located in the museum depot, but can also be viewed on the museumâs website, which gives an overview of the cultural history of the toy. Nurembergâs special role as a metropolis of toys in the <a href="/wiki/Industrialisation" title="Industrialisation">industrial age</a> becomes particularly apparent due to the local toy industry.</p>
