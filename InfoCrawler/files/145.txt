			<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Advowson</h1>
									<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
									<div id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
								<div id="contentSub"></div>
												<div id="jump-to-nav" class="mw-jump">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>
				</div>
				<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div class="mw-parser-output"><dl>
<dd><i>For the process for appointing a parish priest in the Church of England, see <a href="/wiki/Parish#England" title="Parish">Parish</a>.</i></dd>
</dl>
<p><b>Advowson</b> (or "patronage") is the right in <a href="/wiki/English_law" title="English law">English law</a> of a patron (avowee) to present to the diocesan bishop (or in some cases the <a href="/wiki/Ordinary_(officer)" title="Ordinary (officer)">ordinary</a> if not the same person) a nominee for appointment to a vacant <a href="/wiki/Ecclesiastical" class="mw-redirect" title="Ecclesiastical">ecclesiastical</a> <a href="/wiki/Benefice" title="Benefice">benefice</a> or church living, a process known as <i>presentation</i> (<i>jus praesentandi</i>, Latin: "the right of presenting"). The word derives, via French, from the Latin <i>advocare</i>, from <i>vocare</i> "to call" plus <i>ad</i>, "to, towards", thus a "summoning".<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> In effect, an advowson is the right to nominate a person to be <a href="/wiki/Parish" title="Parish">parish</a> priest (subject to episcopal approval), and such right was often originally held by the <a href="/wiki/Lord_of_the_manor" title="Lord of the manor">lord of the manor</a> of the principal <a href="/wiki/Manor" title="Manor">manor</a> within the parish.<sup id="cite_ref-Saul11_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Saul11-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legal_status">Legal status</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Advowson&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Legal status">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Legally, advowsons were treated as <a href="/wiki/Real_property" title="Real property">real property</a> that could be held or <a href="/wiki/Conveyancing" title="Conveyancing">conveyed</a>, and conversely could be taken or <a href="/wiki/Encumbrance" title="Encumbrance">encumbered</a>, in the same general manner as a parcel of land. Advowsons were one of the earliest <a href="/wiki/Incorporeal_hereditament" class="mw-redirect" title="Incorporeal hereditament">incorporeal hereditaments</a>, and often held in <a href="/wiki/Fee_tail" title="Fee tail">fee tail</a>. Litigation on the basis of an advowson was somewhat specialized, with unique <a href="/wiki/Form_of_action" title="Form of action">forms of action</a> inapplicable to other realty, such as the writ of <i><a href="/wiki/Quare_impedit" title="Quare impedit">quare impedit</a></i>, by which a patron sued a bishop in support of his presented candidate being unreasonably hindered from succeeding to a benefice.</p>
<p>Following reforms of parish administration in the late 19th century (principally the imposition of secular parishes and wider county and district authorities) the advowson had negligible commercial value. The Benefices Act 1898 (Amendment) Measure 1923 phased out advowsons so that they could not be sold or inherited after two vacancies occurred after 14 July 1924 and enabled their elimination earlier by direction of the current patron.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTENeepEdinger19288_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTENeepEdinger19288-3">[3]</a></sup> It had however the effect, in the words of the then Archbishop of Canterbury, <a href="/wiki/Cosmo_Gordon_Lang" title="Cosmo Gordon Lang">Cosmo Lang</a>, in 1933, "that the sale of advowsons very rapidly increased. Patrons were eager to sell, and were advised to sell, while their proprietary rights had still some financial value, and there can be no doubt the opportunity was used very largely to secure advowsons for party purposes and for party trusts.".<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> The purchase of advowsons to ensure that a parish became an Anglo-Catholic, or more often Evangelical, stronghold, had begun in the 19th century. It led to a number of situations where an incumbent was imposed on a parish, and had been partly dealt with by the establishment of <a href="/wiki/List_of_Church_of_England_Measures" title="List of Church of England Measures">Diocesan Boards of Patronage</a> in 1932.</p>
<p>The rich body of common law regarding advowsons can sometimes become relevant in modern times when dealing with disputes over modern incorporeal hereditaments, such as farming allotments, that are not handled by statute or adequately settled by other common law. This took place in the 1980 case of <i>First Victoria National Bank v. United States.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></i></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Value">Value</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Advowson&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Value">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Advowsons were valuable <a href="/wiki/Asset" title="Asset">assets</a> for a number of reasons, principally as a means for the <a href="/wiki/Patronage#Anglican" title="Patronage">patron</a> to exert moral influence on the <a href="/wiki/Parish#England" title="Parish">parishioners</a>, who were his manorial tenants, through the teaching and sermons of the parish priest. The <a href="/wiki/Manor" title="Manor">manor</a> was a business enterprise, and it was important for its commercial success that all who lived there should live and work in harmony for a common purpose, and should obey the law of the land and of the <a href="/wiki/Manorial_court" title="Manorial court">manorial court</a>. Such a law-abiding attitude could be fostered by a suitable parish priest, and clearly the appointment of a priest who preached against this would be a disaster for the interests of the <a href="/wiki/Lord_of_the_manor" title="Lord of the manor">lord of the manor</a>. An appointment could also be used to provide an income for a valued servant in holy orders (such as a chaplain or secretary), or as a reward for past services rendered to the patron by the appointee. A benefice generally included use of a house, i.e. a <a href="/wiki/Vicarage" class="mw-redirect" title="Vicarage">vicarage</a>, <a href="/wiki/Parsonage" class="mw-redirect" title="Parsonage">parsonage</a> or <a href="/wiki/Rectory" class="mw-redirect" title="Rectory">rectory</a>, as well as the income from the <a href="/wiki/Glebe" title="Glebe">glebe</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tithes" class="mw-redirect" title="Tithes">tithes</a>, which would provide for the living expenses of the <a href="/wiki/Incumbent_(ecclesiastical)" title="Incumbent (ecclesiastical)">incumbent</a>, and the value of the advowson would thus vary according to how richly endowed the glebe had been out of the lord of the manor's manorial lands. For example: the advowson of the parish of <a href="/wiki/Paulerspury" title="Paulerspury">Paulerspury</a> in Northamptonshire (Peterborough diocese) was bought by New College, Oxford, in 1750 for the sum of £1,300.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>Advowsons were frequently used by lords and landowners as a means of providing a career and income for a younger son who, due to the custom of <a href="/wiki/Primogeniture" title="Primogeniture">primogeniture</a>, would not inherit any of the paternal lands. If the father did not already own a suitable advowson, he might purchase one for this purpose. Appointments however were subject to the approval of the <a href="/wiki/Ordinary_(officer)" title="Ordinary (officer)">ordinary</a> (usually the <a href="/wiki/Bishop" title="Bishop">bishop</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Diocese" title="Diocese">diocese</a>) who could refuse for good reason although since the <a href="/wiki/English_Reformation" title="English Reformation">Reformation</a> the refusal could be tested in the civil court.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTENeepEdinger192810_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTENeepEdinger192810-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Origin">Origin</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Advowson&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Origin">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The creation of an advowson was a secondary development arising from the process of creating <a href="/wiki/Parish" title="Parish">parishes</a> across England in the 11th and 12th centuries, with their associated parish churches. A major impetus to this development was the legal exaction of agricultural <a href="/wiki/Tithe" title="Tithe">tithes</a> specific to the support of churches and their clergy; landowners needed to establish parish churches on their lands in order to retain tithe income within their estates, and to this purpose sought to raise former <a href="/wiki/Minster_(church)" title="Minster (church)">field churches</a> to parish church status. This was generally performed by a lord of a manor by rebuilding a church within the boundary of his manor, or within that of a newly <a href="/wiki/Subinfeudation" title="Subinfeudation">subinfeudated</a> manor, and then transferring proprietary rights of certain individual named fields, mills or <a href="/wiki/Messuage" class="mw-redirect" title="Messuage">messuages</a> (i.e. houses on the manor which earned rents) to establish a glebe. Where parish churches existed on manors established before the <a href="/wiki/Norman_Conquest" class="mw-redirect" title="Norman Conquest">Norman Conquest</a> of 1066, the position is less clear and records generally are scarce from which to reconstruct a history. However Britain was split into dioceses and many parish churches were established long before that time, and the process of their creation was likely similarly performed by Anglo-Saxon <a href="/wiki/Thane" title="Thane">thanes</a> as by Norman <a href="/wiki/Lord_of_the_manor" title="Lord of the manor">lords</a> and <a href="/wiki/English_feudal_barony" title="English feudal barony">feudal barons</a>. Initially feudal lords exercised <a href="/wiki/Seigneur" title="Seigneur">seigneural</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Dominium" title="Dominium">dominium</a></i> over these new parish churches, exacting an <i>introit</i> fee on appointment, and an annual rent thereafter. The restriction of these seigneural rights to that of 'advowson', purely a right of presentation, developed with the process of <a href="/wiki/Gregorian_Reform" title="Gregorian Reform">Gregorian Reform</a>; in consequence of which payments from spiritual income to lay lords were forbidden.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> The new process was performed in conjunction with the bishop of the diocese in which the manor was situated. The lord of the manor, having incurred a very great expense in building the church and parsonage and having suffered a loss of income due to his donation of property to the glebe, quite reasonably insisted on the right to select the individual who would act as parish priest, from which office he could not be ejected by the lord until the priest's death. The bishop, without whose <a href="/wiki/Consecration" title="Consecration">consecration</a> the new church would have no religious and spiritual stature, in turn demanded the right of confirmation of the appointment. Thus from the earliest time the advowson was "appurtenant to" the manor, that is to say it appertained to the manor and was exercisable by the lord. The advowson, being real property could be "alienated" (i.e. disposed of) by sale or gift of the patron, but with special licence from the <a href="/wiki/Lord" title="Lord">overlord</a> as was required for the alienation of the manor itself.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Presentation_by_turns">Presentation by turns</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Advowson&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Presentation by turns">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Where a manor was split into <a href="/wiki/Moiety_title" title="Moiety title">moieties</a> due to inheritance by co-heiresses, the advowson was also split. Before the 13th century, this would commonly result in the rectory (and its advowson) being split into two or more portions, with the successors of each co-heiress from then on separately nominating a parish priest as their 'portioner'. Emerging Canon Law however, strongly deprecated dividing the <a href="/wiki/Cure_of_souls" class="mw-redirect" title="Cure of souls">cure of souls</a> for a single parish in this way, and bishops ceased to allow such devices; except in the case of <a href="/wiki/Collegiate_church" title="Collegiate church">portionary collegiate foundations</a>. Henceforth therefore, if a lord of a manor died without male issue but with two daughters, the manorial lands would be split into two moieties, still however within the original undivided parish, controlled by the husbands of each daughter, and the advowson would be held by each daughter's husband <i><a href="/wiki/Jure_uxoris" title="Jure uxoris">jure uxoris</a></i> in turn. The husband of the elder daughter would have the right to the first presentation, that is to say the right to appoint a new priest to the first vacancy, whilst the husband of the second daughter – or more usually, given the life tenure of priests, their descendant – would hold the right to the second presentation. Where manors were split into three or more moieties or "purparties", the turns of presentation expanded accordingly. Bishops then sought to convert existing portionary churches into presentation by turns; but portionary arrangements nevertheless survived in several parishes into the 19th century; and a few still operate (though in name only, the portions being united into a single incumbent). Patrons in need of ready cash might seek to capitalize on the value of their advowsons by selling turns of presentation; either the next turn singly, or alternate future turns as a long-term asset.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Encroachment_of_canon_law">Encroachment of canon law</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Advowson&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Encroachment of canon law">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Canon law, however, by the 12th century, decreed that the right to present belonged to the saint to whom the church was dedicated and that only ecclesiastical courts could rule on cases involving advowsons. King <a href="/wiki/Henry_II_of_England" title="Henry II of England">Henry II</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Constitutions_of_Clarendon" title="Constitutions of Clarendon">Constitutions of Clarendon</a> held otherwise, and after <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Becket" title="Thomas Becket">Thomas Becket</a>'s murder, the king once more ruled that cases involving advowsons should be heard by secular courts.<sup id="cite_ref-Saul11_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Saul11-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Effect_of_the_Reformation">Effect of the Reformation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Advowson&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Effect of the Reformation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Reformation" title="Reformation">Reformation</a> in the 16th century, the <a href="/wiki/Dissolution_of_the_Monasteries" title="Dissolution of the Monasteries">Dissolution of the Monasteries</a> led to the transfer of much monastic property to laymen, and with the properties passed the advowsons which the monasteries had held: thus creating a large group of lay patrons (see <a href="/wiki/Impropriation" title="Impropriation">impropriation</a>). In 1603, there were an estimated 3849 livings in the hands of lay impropriators out of a total of 9284.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>There were also many lords of manors and patrons of appurtenant livings who were <a href="/wiki/Recusant" class="mw-redirect" title="Recusant">recusants</a>, that is to say who remained <a href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic" class="mw-redirect" title="Roman Catholic">Roman Catholics</a> and refused to adopt the new <a href="/wiki/Protestant" class="mw-redirect" title="Protestant">Protestant</a> religion. Such patrons were disbarred from making presentations by the <a href="/wiki/Presentation_of_Benefices_Act_1605" title="Presentation of Benefices Act 1605">Presentation of Benefices Act 1605</a> (3 Jac. 1 c 5), which transferred the right for the time being to the Universities of Oxford or Cambridge according to the geographical location of the parish.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> The university was allowed to assign this right to a third party adherent to the new religion, for <a href="/wiki/Consideration" title="Consideration">consideration</a> or otherwise.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Value_as_historical_records">Value as historical records</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Advowson&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Value as historical records">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The records of historic presentations to manors are held amongst the <a href="/wiki/Muniment" title="Muniment">muniments</a> or archives of their dioceses. Often such records exist unbroken for several centuries, and record the names of the patron and the appointee, with related information. These records are thus of great value to non-ecclesiastical historians of the manors concerned, and also to <a href="/wiki/Genealogist" class="mw-redirect" title="Genealogist">genealogists</a>, for example by confirming the name of the lord of the manor at a certain date, confirming the dates of his life, and the name of his heir, spouse or widow. Most of the secular historical records relating to manors are contained within <a href="/wiki/Manorial_roll" title="Manorial roll">manorial rolls</a> or in the archives of the ancient departments of state, for example the records of the <a href="/wiki/Exchequer" title="Exchequer">Exchequer</a>, royal courts etc. The existence of an entirely independent set of records within the diocesan archives, generally published in the calendars of the bishops of the <a href="/wiki/Episcopal_see" title="Episcopal see">see</a> in question, is thus a valuable resource.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Current_position">Current position</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Advowson&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Current position">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In the 20th century it became the policy of many English sees to acquire the advowsons to the parish churches within their ecclesiastical jurisdictions. This gave the bishop total control over the selection of parish priests, which might give him political as well as religious influence over a parish. Thus the days of the fiercely independent, often <a href="/wiki/Right-wing" class="mw-redirect" title="Right-wing">right-wing</a> "fox-hunting parson" like <a href="/wiki/John_Russell_(parson)" class="mw-redirect" title="John Russell (parson)">Jack Russell</a> (d. 1883), are now historical. With the abolition of <a href="/wiki/Manorial_court" title="Manorial court">manorial courts</a> in the 19th century, the powers, but not the vestigial office, of lord of the manor, were largely eliminated, yet owners of former manorial estates, often inherited, still held the historic advowsons appurtenant to their lands. In most instances, such holders of advowsons were willing to donate them without payment to the diocese, on being asked to do so in a polite letter from the bishop.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> Advowsons had lost any value to their private holders, since few landowners any longer wished to exert a proxy influence over the morals of their neighbours, formerly manorial tenants, and few wished their younger sons to be parish priests, which carries a lower status than formerly. It is however still a sign of a great aristocratic past for an individual to hold one or more advowsons. In <i><a href="/wiki/Debrett%27s_Peerage" class="mw-redirect" title="Debrett's Peerage">Debrett's Peerage</a></i> of 1968, <a href="/wiki/Victor_Hervey,_6th_Marquess_of_Bristol" title="Victor Hervey, 6th Marquess of Bristol">Victor Hervey, 6th Marquess of Bristol</a> (d. 1985), then aged 53, was listed as being "patron of thirty livings". Sizable numbers of advowsons are also held by organizations such as the <a href="/wiki/Church_Society" title="Church Society">Church Society</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
