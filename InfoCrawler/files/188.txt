			<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Saffir–Simpson scale</h1>

<p>The <b>Saffir–Simpson hurricane wind scale</b> (<b>SSHWS</b>), formerly the <b>Saffir–Simpson hurricane scale</b> (<b>SSHS</b>), classifies <a href="/wiki/Hurricanes" class="mw-redirect" title="Hurricanes">hurricanes</a>&#160;– <a href="/wiki/Western_Hemisphere" title="Western Hemisphere">Western Hemisphere</a> <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone" title="Tropical cyclone">tropical cyclones</a> that exceed the intensities of <a href="/wiki/Tropical_depression" class="mw-redirect" title="Tropical depression">tropical depressions</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tropical_storm" class="mw-redirect" title="Tropical storm">tropical storms</a>&#160;– into five categories distinguished by the intensities of their sustained <a href="/wiki/Wind" title="Wind">winds</a>. To be classified as a hurricane, a <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone" title="Tropical cyclone">tropical cyclone</a> must have <a href="/wiki/Maximum_sustained_wind" title="Maximum sustained wind">maximum sustained winds</a> of at least 74&#160;mph (33&#160;<a href="/wiki/Metre_per_second" title="Metre per second">m/s</a>; 64&#160;<a href="/wiki/Knot_(unit)" title="Knot (unit)">kn</a>; 119&#160;<a href="/wiki/Kilometres_per_hour" title="Kilometres per hour">km/h</a>) (Category 1). The highest classification in the scale, <a href="#Category_5">Category&#160;5</a>, contains storms with sustained winds exceeding 156&#160;mph (70&#160;m/s; 136&#160;kn; 251&#160;km/h).</p>
<p>The classifications can provide some indication of the potential damage and <a href="/wiki/Flooding" class="mw-redirect" title="Flooding">flooding</a> a hurricane will cause upon <a href="/wiki/Landfall" title="Landfall">landfall</a>.</p>
<p>Officially, the Saffir–Simpson hurricane wind scale is used <i>only</i> to describe <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_hurricane" title="Atlantic hurricane">hurricanes forming in the Atlantic Ocean</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pacific_hurricane" title="Pacific hurricane">northern Pacific Ocean</a> east of the <a href="/wiki/International_Date_Line" title="International Date Line">International Date Line</a>. Other areas use <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone_scales" title="Tropical cyclone scales">different scales</a> to label these storms, which are called "cyclones" or "<a href="/wiki/Typhoon" title="Typhoon">typhoons</a>", depending on the area.</p>
<p>There is some <a href="#Criticism">criticism</a> of the SSHS for not taking rain, <a href="/wiki/Storm_surge" title="Storm surge">storm surge</a>, and other important factors into consideration, but SSHS defenders say that part of the goal of SSHS is to be straightforward and simple to understand.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Saffir%E2%80%93Simpson_scale&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The scale was developed in 1971 by <a href="/wiki/Civil_engineering" title="Civil engineering">civil engineer</a> <a href="/wiki/Herbert_Saffir" title="Herbert Saffir">Herbert Saffir</a> and meteorologist <a href="/wiki/Robert_Simpson_(meteorologist)" title="Robert Simpson (meteorologist)">Robert Simpson</a>, who at the time was director of the U.S. <a href="/wiki/National_Hurricane_Center" title="National Hurricane Center">National Hurricane Center</a> (NHC).<sup id="cite_ref-USA_Today_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-USA_Today-1">[1]</a></sup> The scale was introduced to the general public in 1973,<sup id="cite_ref-Bridgeport_Post_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bridgeport_Post-2">[2]</a></sup> and saw widespread use after <a href="/wiki/Neil_Frank" title="Neil Frank">Neil Frank</a> replaced Simpson at the helm of the NHC in 1974.<sup id="cite_ref-Simpson_interview_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Simpson_interview-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>The initial scale was developed by Saffir, a <a href="/wiki/Structural_engineering" title="Structural engineering">structural engineer</a>, who in 1969 went on commission for the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations" title="United Nations">United Nations</a> to study low-cost housing in hurricane-prone areas.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-4">[4]</a></sup> While performing the study, Saffir realized there was no simple scale for describing the likely effects of a hurricane. Mirroring the utility of the <a href="/wiki/Richter_magnitude_scale" title="Richter magnitude scale">Richter magnitude scale</a> in describing earthquakes, he devised a 1–5 scale based on <a href="/wiki/Wind_speed" title="Wind speed">wind speed</a> that showed expected damage to structures. Saffir gave the scale to the NHC, and Simpson added the effects of <a href="/wiki/Storm_surge" title="Storm surge">storm surge</a> and flooding.</p>
<p>In 2009, the NHC made moves to eliminate pressure and storm surge ranges from the categories, transforming it into a pure wind scale, called the Saffir–Simpson Hurricane Wind Scale (Experimental) [SSHWS].<sup id="cite_ref-NHC_SSHS_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NHC_SSHS-5">[5]</a></sup> The new scale became operational on May 15, 2010.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> The scale excludes flood ranges, storm surge estimations, rainfall, and location, which means a Category&#160;2 hurricane which hits a major city will likely do far more cumulative damage than a Category&#160;5 hurricane that hits a rural area.<sup id="cite_ref-LiveScience_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-LiveScience-7">[7]</a></sup> The agency cited various hurricanes as reasons for removing the "scientifically inaccurate" information, including <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Katrina" title="Hurricane Katrina">Hurricane Katrina</a> (2005) and <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Ike" title="Hurricane Ike">Hurricane Ike</a> (2008), which both had stronger than estimated storm surges, and <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Charley" title="Hurricane Charley">Hurricane Charley</a> (2004), which had weaker than estimated storm surge.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Since removed from the Saffir–Simpson hurricane wind scale, storm surge predicting and modeling is now handled with the use of a computerized numerical model developed by the National Weather Service called "<a href="/wiki/Sea,_Lake,_and_Overland_Surge_from_Hurricanes" title="Sea, Lake, and Overland Surge from Hurricanes">Sea, Lake, and Overland Surge from Hurricanes</a>" (SLOSH).</p>
<p>In 2012, the NHC expanded the windspeed range for Category 4 by 1&#160;mph in both directions, to 130–156&#160;mph, with corresponding changes in the other units (113–136 kn, 209–251&#160;km/h), instead of 131–155&#160;mph (114–135 kn, 210–249&#160;km/h). The NHC and the <a href="/wiki/Central_Pacific_Hurricane_Center" title="Central Pacific Hurricane Center">Central Pacific Hurricane Center</a> assign tropical cyclone intensities in 5 knot increments, and then convert to mph and km/h with a similar rounding for other reports. So an intensity of 115 knots is rated Category 4, but the conversion to miles per hour (132.3&#160;mph) would round down to 130&#160;mph, making it appear to be a Category 3 storm. Likewise, an intensity of 135 knots (~155&#160;mph, and thus Category 4) is 250.02&#160;km/h, which according to the definition used before the change would be Category 5. To resolve these issues, the NHC had been obliged to incorrectly report storms with wind speeds of 115 kn as 135&#160;mph, and 135 kn as 245&#160;km/h. The change in definition allows storms of 115 kn to be correctly rounded down to 130&#160;mph, and storms of 135 kn to be correctly reported as 250&#160;km/h, and still qualify as Category 4. Since the NHC had previously rounded incorrectly to keep storms in Category 4 in each unit of measure, the change does not affect the classification of storms from previous years.<sup id="cite_ref-NHC_SSHS_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NHC_SSHS-5">[5]</a></sup> The new scale became operational on May 15, 2012.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Categories">Categories</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Saffir%E2%80%93Simpson_scale&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Categories">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The scale separates hurricanes into five different categories based on wind. The U.S. National Hurricane Center classifies hurricanes of Category&#160;3 and above as <i>major hurricanes</i>, and the Joint Typhoon Warning Center classifies typhoons of 150&#160;mph or greater (strong Category 4 and Category 5) as <i>super typhoons</i> (although all tropical cyclones can be very dangerous). Most weather agencies use the definition for sustained winds recommended by the <a href="/wiki/World_Meteorological_Organization" title="World Meteorological Organization">World Meteorological Organization</a> (WMO), which specifies measuring winds at a height of 33&#160;ft (10.1&#160;m) for 10&#160;minutes, and then taking the average. By contrast, the U.S. <a href="/wiki/National_Weather_Service" title="National Weather Service">National Weather Service</a>, <a href="/wiki/Central_Pacific_Hurricane_Center" title="Central Pacific Hurricane Center">Central Pacific Hurricane Center</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Joint_Typhoon_Warning_Center" title="Joint Typhoon Warning Center">Joint Typhoon Warning Center</a> define sustained winds as average winds over a period of one minute, measured at the same 33&#160;ft (10.1&#160;m) height,<sup id="cite_ref-NWSM_Defs_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NWSM_Defs-10">[10]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FEMA_glossary_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FEMA_glossary-11">[11]</a></sup> and that is the definition used for this scale. Intensity of example hurricanes is from both the time of landfall and the maximum intensity.</p>
<p>The scale is roughly logarithmic in wind speed, and the top wind speed for Category "<i>c</i>" (<i>c</i>=1 to 4, as there is no upper limit for category 5) can be expressed as 83x10^(c/15) miles per hour rounded to the nearest multiple of 5 – except that after the change mentioned above, Category 4 is now widened by 1&#160;mph in each direction.</p>

<p><i><b>Very dangerous winds will produce some damage</b></i></p>
<p>Category&#160;1 storms usually cause no significant structural <a href="/wiki/Property_damage" title="Property damage">damage</a> to most well-constructed permanent structures; however, they can topple unanchored <a href="/wiki/Mobile_home" title="Mobile home">mobile homes</a>, as well as uproot or snap weak trees. Poorly attached roof shingles or tiles can blow off. Coastal flooding and <a href="/wiki/Pier" title="Pier">pier</a> damage are often associated with Category&#160;1 storms. Power outages are typically widespread to extensive, sometimes lasting several days. Even though it is the least intense type of hurricane, the storm can still produce widespread damage and can be a life-threatening storm.<sup id="cite_ref-NHC_SSHS_5-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NHC_SSHS-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Hurricanes that peaked at Category&#160;1 intensity, and made landfall at that intensity include: <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Flossy_(1956)" title="Hurricane Flossy (1956)">Flossy</a> (1956), <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Gladys_(1968)" title="Hurricane Gladys (1968)">Gladys</a> (1968), <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Agnes" title="Hurricane Agnes">Agnes</a> (1972), <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Juan_(1985)" title="Hurricane Juan (1985)">Juan</a> (1985), <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Ismael" title="Hurricane Ismael">Ismael</a> (1995), <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Claudette_(2003)" title="Hurricane Claudette (2003)">Claudette</a> (2003), <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Gaston_(2004)" title="Hurricane Gaston (2004)">Gaston</a> (2004), <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Stan" title="Hurricane Stan">Stan</a> (2005), <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Humberto_(2007)" title="Hurricane Humberto (2007)">Humberto</a> (2007), <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Isaac_(2012)" title="Hurricane Isaac (2012)">Isaac</a> (2012), <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Manuel" title="Hurricane Manuel">Manuel</a> (2013), <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Earl_(2016)" title="Hurricane Earl (2016)">Earl</a> (2016), <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Hermine" title="Hurricane Hermine">Hermine</a> (2016), <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Newton_(2016)" title="Hurricane Newton (2016)">Newton</a> (2016), <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Franklin" title="Hurricane Franklin">Franklin</a> (2017), and <a href="/wiki/2017_Pacific_hurricane_season#Hurricane_Max" title="2017 Pacific hurricane season">Max</a> (2017).</p>

<p><i><b>Extremely dangerous winds will cause extensive damage</b></i></p>
<p>Storms of Category&#160;2 intensity often damage roofing material (sometimes exposing the roof) and inflict damage upon poorly constructed doors and windows. Poorly constructed signs and piers can receive considerable damage and many trees are uprooted or snapped. Mobile homes, whether anchored or not, are typically damaged and sometimes destroyed, and many <a href="/wiki/Manufactured_housing" title="Manufactured housing">manufactured homes</a> also suffer structural damage. Small craft in unprotected anchorages may break their <a href="/wiki/Mooring_(watercraft)" title="Mooring (watercraft)">moorings</a>. Extensive to near-total power outages and scattered loss of potable water are likely, possibly lasting many days.<sup id="cite_ref-NHC_SSHS_5-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NHC_SSHS-5">[5]</a></sup></p>

<p><i><b>Devastating damage will occur</b></i></p>
<p>Tropical cyclones of Category 3 and higher are described as <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone#Classifications.2C_terminology.2C_and_naming" title="Tropical cyclone">major hurricanes</a> in the Atlantic or Eastern Pacific <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone_basins" title="Tropical cyclone basins">basins</a>. These storms can cause some structural damage to small residences and utility buildings, particularly those of wood frame or manufactured materials with minor <a href="/wiki/Curtain_wall_(architecture)" title="Curtain wall (architecture)">curtain wall</a> failures. Buildings that lack a solid foundation, such as mobile homes, are usually destroyed, and <a href="/wiki/Gable" title="Gable">gable</a>-end roofs are peeled off. Manufactured homes usually sustain severe and irreparable damage. Flooding near the coast destroys smaller structures, while larger structures are struck by floating debris. A large number of trees are uprooted or snapped, isolating many areas. Additionally, terrain may be flooded well inland. Near-total to total power loss is likely for up to several weeks and water will likely also be lost or contaminated.<sup id="cite_ref-NHC_SSHS_5-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NHC_SSHS-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Hurricanes that peaked at Category&#160;3 intensity, and made landfall at that intensity include: <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Easy_(1950)" title="Hurricane Easy (1950)">Easy</a> (1950), <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Carol" title="Hurricane Carol">Carol</a> (1954), <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Hilda" title="Hurricane Hilda">Hilda</a> (1955), <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Celia" title="Hurricane Celia">Celia</a> (1970), <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Ella_(1970)" title="Hurricane Ella (1970)">Ella</a> (1970), <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Eloise" title="Hurricane Eloise">Eloise</a> (1975), <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Olivia_(1975)" title="Hurricane Olivia (1975)">Olivia</a> (1975), <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Alicia" title="Hurricane Alicia">Alicia</a> (1983), <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Elena" title="Hurricane Elena">Elena</a> (1985), <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Roxanne" title="Hurricane Roxanne">Roxanne</a> (1995), <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Fran" title="Hurricane Fran">Fran</a> (1996), <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Isidore" title="Hurricane Isidore">Isidore</a> (2002), <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Lane_(2006)" title="Hurricane Lane (2006)">Lane</a> (2006), <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Karl" title="Hurricane Karl">Karl</a> (2010), <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Sandy" title="Hurricane Sandy">Sandy</a> (2012). and <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Otto" title="Hurricane Otto">Otto</a> (2016).</p>

<p><i><b>Catastrophic damage will occur</b></i></p>
<p>Category&#160;4 hurricanes tend to produce more extensive curtainwall failures, with some complete <a href="/wiki/Structural_failure" class="mw-redirect" title="Structural failure">structural failure</a> on small residences. Heavy, irreparable damage and near complete destruction of gas station canopies and other wide span overhang type structures are common. Mobile and manufactured homes are often flattened. Most trees, except for the heartiest, are uprooted or snapped, isolating many areas. These storms cause extensive beach erosion, while terrain may be flooded far inland. Total and long-lived electrical and water losses are to be expected, possibly for many weeks.<sup id="cite_ref-NHC_SSHS_5-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NHC_SSHS-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/1900_Galveston_hurricane" title="1900 Galveston hurricane">1900 Galveston hurricane</a>, the deadliest natural disaster to hit the United States, peaked at an intensity that corresponds to a modern-day Category&#160;4 storm. <p><i><b>Catastrophic damage will occur</b></i></p>
<p>Category&#160;5 is the highest category of the Saffir–Simpson scale. These storms cause complete roof failure on many residences and industrial buildings, and some complete building failures with small utility buildings blown over or away. Collapse of many wide-span roofs and walls, especially those with no interior supports, is common. Very heavy and irreparable damage to many wood frame structures and total destruction to mobile/manufactured homes is prevalent. Only a few types of structures are capable of surviving intact, and only if located at least 3 to 5 miles (5 to 8&#160;km) inland. They include office, condominium and apartment buildings and hotels that are of solid concrete or steel frame construction, public multi-story concrete parking garages, and residences that are made of either reinforced brick or <a href="/wiki/Concrete" title="Concrete">concrete</a>/<a href="/wiki/Cement" title="Cement">cement</a> block and have <a href="/wiki/Hip_roof" title="Hip roof">hipped roofs</a> with slopes of no less than 35&#160;degrees from horizontal and no overhangs of any kind, and if the windows are either made of hurricane-resistant safety glass or covered with shutters. Unless all of these requirements are met, the absolute destruction of a structure is certain.<sup id="cite_ref-NHC_SSHS_5-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NHC_SSHS-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>The storm's flooding causes major damage to the lower floors of all structures near the shoreline, and many coastal structures can be completely flattened or washed away by the storm surge. Virtually all trees are uprooted or snapped and some may be debarked, isolating most affected communities. Massive <a href="/wiki/Emergency_evacuation" title="Emergency evacuation">evacuation</a> of residential areas may be required if the hurricane threatens populated areas. Total and extremely long-lived power outages and water losses are to be expected, possibly for up to several months.<sup id="cite_ref-NHC_SSHS_5-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NHC_SSHS-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Historical examples of storms that made landfall at Category 5 status include: <a href="/wiki/1924_Cuba_hurricane" title="1924 Cuba hurricane">"Cuba"</a> (1924), <a href="/wiki/1928_Okeechobee_hurricane" title="1928 Okeechobee hurricane">"Okeechobee"</a> (1928), <a href="/wiki/1932_Bahamas_hurricane" title="1932 Bahamas hurricane">"Bahamas"</a> (1932), <a href="/wiki/1933_Cuba%E2%80%93Brownsville_hurricane" title="1933 Cuba–Brownsville hurricane">"Cuba–Brownsville"</a> (1933), <a href="/wiki/1935_Labor_Day_hurricane" title="1935 Labor Day hurricane">"Labor Day"</a> (1935), <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Janet" title="Hurricane Janet">Janet</a> (1955), <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Camille" title="Hurricane Camille">Camille</a> (1969), <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Edith_(1971)" title="Hurricane Edith (1971)">Edith</a> (1971), <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Anita" title="Hurricane Anita">Anita</a> (1977), <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_David" title="Hurricane David">David</a> (1979), <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Gilbert" title="Hurricane Gilbert">Gilbert</a> (1988), <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Andrew" title="Hurricane Andrew">Andrew</a> (1992), <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Dean" title="Hurricane Dean">Dean</a> (2007), <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Felix" title="Hurricane Felix">Felix</a> (2007), <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Irma" title="Hurricane Irma">Irma</a> (2017),<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Maria" title="Hurricane Maria">Maria</a> (2017).<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> No Category 5 hurricane is known to have made landfall at that strength in the eastern Pacific basin.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Criticism">Criticism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Saffir%E2%80%93Simpson_scale&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Criticism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Some scientists, including <a href="/wiki/Kerry_Emanuel" title="Kerry Emanuel">Kerry Emanuel</a> and Lakshmi Kantha, have criticized the scale as being simplistic, indicating that the scale takes into account neither the physical size of a storm nor the amount of <a href="/wiki/Precipitation" title="Precipitation">precipitation</a> it produces.<sup id="cite_ref-LiveScience_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-LiveScience-7">[7]</a></sup> Additionally, they and others point out that the Saffir–Simpson scale, unlike the Richter scale used to measure <a href="/wiki/Earthquakes" class="mw-redirect" title="Earthquakes">earthquakes</a>, is not continuous, and is <a href="/wiki/Quantization_(signal_processing)" title="Quantization (signal processing)">quantized</a> into a small number of categories. Proposed replacement classifications include the Hurricane Intensity Index, which is based on the <a href="/wiki/Dynamic_pressure" title="Dynamic pressure">dynamic pressure</a> caused by a storm's winds, and the Hurricane Hazard Index, which bases itself on surface wind speeds, the <a href="/wiki/Radius_of_maximum_wind" title="Radius of maximum wind">radius of maximum winds</a> of the storm, and its <a href="/wiki/Translation_(geometry)" title="Translation (geometry)">translational</a> velocity.<sup id="cite_ref-Kantha_HHI_HII_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kantha_HHI_HII-15">[15]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Kantha_BAMS_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kantha_BAMS-16">[16]</a></sup> Both of these scales are continuous, akin to the Richter scale;<sup id="cite_ref-UCL_alternates_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-UCL_alternates-17">[17]</a></sup> however, neither of these scales have been used by officials.</p>
<h3><span id="&quot;Category_6&quot;"></span><span class="mw-headline" id=".22Category_6.22">"Category 6"</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Saffir%E2%80%93Simpson_scale&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: &quot;Category 6&quot;">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>After the series of powerful storm systems of the <a href="/wiki/2005_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="2005 Atlantic hurricane season">2005 Atlantic hurricane season</a>, a few newspaper columnists and scientists brought up the suggestion of introducing Category&#160;6, and they have suggested pegging Category&#160;6 to storms with winds greater than 174 or 180&#160;mph (78 or 80&#160;m/s; 151 or 156&#160;kn; 280 or 290&#160;km/h).<sup id="cite_ref-LiveScience_7-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-LiveScience-7">[7]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> Only a few storms of this intensity have been recorded. Of the 33 hurricanes currently considered to have attained Category 5 status in the Atlantic, 17 had wind speeds at 175&#160;mph (78&#160;m/s; 152&#160;kn; 282&#160;km/h) or greater and only seven had wind speeds at 180&#160;mph (80&#160;m/s; 160&#160;kn; 290&#160;km/h) or greater (the <a href="/wiki/1935_Labor_Day_hurricane" title="1935 Labor Day hurricane">1935 Labor Day hurricane</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Allen" title="Hurricane Allen">Allen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Gilbert" title="Hurricane Gilbert">Gilbert</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Mitch" title="Hurricane Mitch">Mitch</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Rita" title="Hurricane Rita">Rita</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Wilma" title="Hurricane Wilma">Wilma</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Irma" title="Hurricane Irma">Irma</a>). Of the 15 hurricanes currently considered to have attained Category 5 status in the eastern Pacific, only six had wind speeds at 175&#160;mph (78&#160;m/s; 152&#160;kn; 282&#160;km/h) or greater (<a href="/wiki/1959_Pacific_hurricane_season#Hurricane_Patsy" title="1959 Pacific hurricane season">Patsy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_John_(1994)" title="Hurricane John (1994)">John</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Linda_(1997)" title="Hurricane Linda (1997)">Linda</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Rick_(2009)" title="Hurricane Rick (2009)">Rick</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Patricia" title="Hurricane Patricia">Patricia</a>), and only three had wind speeds at 180&#160;mph (80&#160;m/s; 160&#160;kn; 290&#160;km/h) or greater (Linda, Rick, and Patricia). However, most storms which would be eligible for this category were <a href="/wiki/Typhoon" title="Typhoon">typhoons</a> in the western Pacific, most notably <a href="/wiki/Typhoon_Tip" title="Typhoon Tip">Typhoon Tip</a> in 1979 with sustained winds of 190&#160;mph (310&#160;km/h) and typhoons <a href="/wiki/Typhoon_Haiyan" title="Typhoon Haiyan">Haiyan</a> and <a href="/wiki/Typhoon_Meranti" title="Typhoon Meranti">Meranti</a> in 2013 and 2016, respectively, with sustained winds of 195&#160;mph (314&#160;km/h).<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>According to <a href="/wiki/Robert_Simpson_(meteorologist)" title="Robert Simpson (meteorologist)">Robert Simpson</a>, there are no reasons for a Category&#160;6 on the Saffir–Simpson Scale because it is designed to measure the potential damage of a hurricane to human-made structures. Simpson stated that "...when you get up into winds in excess of 155&#160;mph (249&#160;km/h) you have enough damage if that extreme wind sustains itself for as much as six seconds on a building it's going to cause rupturing damages that are serious no matter how well it's engineered."<sup id="cite_ref-Simpson_interview_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Simpson_interview-3">[3]</a></sup> Despite his statements, the counties of Broward and Miami-Dade in Florida have building codes which require critical infrastructure buildings to be able to withstand hurricane winds of 156 and up,<sup id="cite_ref-Irma_could_test_strength_of_Florida.E2.80.99s_strict_building_codes_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Irma_could_test_strength_of_Florida.E2.80.99s_strict_building_codes-20">[20]</a></sup> which contradicts Mr. Simpson's assessment of building strength. Absent a "Category 6", governments have no guidance as to where "up" should end.</p>
