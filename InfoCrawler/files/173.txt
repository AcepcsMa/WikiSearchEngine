<p><b>Alan R Duffy</b> (born 1983) is a professional <a href="/wiki/Astronomer" title="Astronomer">astronomer</a> and <a href="/wiki/Science_communication" title="Science communication">science communicator</a>. He was born in England, raised in Ireland, and is currently based in Australia. He is a Research Fellow and Associate Professor at the <a href="/wiki/Centre_for_Astrophysics_and_Supercomputing" title="Centre for Astrophysics and Supercomputing">Centre for Astrophysics and Supercomputing</a> at <a href="/wiki/Swinburne_University_of_Technology" title="Swinburne University of Technology">Swinburne University of Technology</a>.</p>
<p>His research is focused on using <a href="/wiki/Super-computer" class="mw-redirect" title="Super-computer">super-computers</a> to build and test models of the growth of galaxies within vast dark matter halos, and in particular focuses on the formation of the first galaxies in the early universe during the "Epoch of <a href="/wiki/Reionisation" class="mw-redirect" title="Reionisation">Reionisation</a>". These models aim to improve our understanding of the nature of <a href="/wiki/Dark_matter" title="Dark matter">dark matter</a>, and the large scale properties of the universe.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life_and_studies">Early life and studies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alan_Duffy_(astronomer)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life and studies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Duffy was born in <a href="/wiki/Peterborough" title="Peterborough">Peterborough</a>, England. His family migrated to Northern Ireland when he was four years old, where he attended <a href="/wiki/Ballyclare_High_School" title="Ballyclare High School">Ballyclare High School</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Ballyclare_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ballyclare-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>His undergraduate studies in physics were conducted at the <a href="/wiki/Manchester_University" class="mw-redirect" title="Manchester University">University of Manchester</a>. He incorporated periods of study in the Netherlands on an EU scholarship, working on supercomputers at Europe's oldest observatory in <a href="/wiki/Leiden_University" title="Leiden University">Leiden University</a>, and undertook physics at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Amsterdam" title="University of Amsterdam">University of Amsterdam</a>, even though he spoke no Dutch when he started.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> He graduated with a <a href="/wiki/Master_of_Physics" title="Master of Physics">MPhys</a> (1st) in 2005. He completed his <a href="/wiki/Doctor_of_Philosophy" title="Doctor of Philosophy">PhD</a> in Astronomy and Astrophysics at <a href="/wiki/Jodrell_Bank" class="mw-redirect" title="Jodrell Bank">Jodrell Bank</a> Observatory based in the University of Manchester in 2009, with his thesis entitled "Investigation of large scale structure in the Universe".<sup id="cite_ref-Ballyclare_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ballyclare-1">[1]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Green_2016_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Green_2016-3">[3]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Academic_career">Academic career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alan_Duffy_(astronomer)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Academic career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>As Duffy was completing his doctorate, work was starting on the Australian component of the world's largest astronomical facility, the <a href="/wiki/Square_Kilometer_Array" class="mw-redirect" title="Square Kilometer Array">Square Kilometer Array</a>. His doctoral work had covered similar topics, and he was invited to join the first stage of this telescope; the Australian Square Kilometre Array Pathfinder (<a href="/wiki/Australian_Square_Kilometre_Array_Pathfinder" title="Australian Square Kilometre Array Pathfinder">ASKAP</a>). He moved to <a href="/wiki/Perth" title="Perth">Perth</a>, Western Australia in 2009 to take up the position at the <a href="/wiki/International_Centre_for_Radio_Astronomy_Research" title="International Centre for Radio Astronomy Research">International Centre for Radio Astronomy Research</a>. Three years later, he moved to <a href="/wiki/Melbourne" title="Melbourne">Melbourne</a> to accept an academic post as Postdoctoral Research Fellow at the <a href="/wiki/University_Of_Melbourne" class="mw-redirect" title="University Of Melbourne">University Of Melbourne</a>, where he investigated the formation of the first galaxies in the early universe.<sup id="cite_ref-Ballyclare_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ballyclare-1">[1]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Green_2016_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Green_2016-3">[3]</a></sup> Then in 2014 he took up the position of Associate Professor at Swinburne University of Technology, still based in Melbourne.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>

<p>His supercomputer simulations have shown that in very early galaxies, the rate of star formation was not enough to consume&#160;the infalling cold gas. Earlier models had assumed this was essentially molecular hydrogen, but the model from Duffy and the DRAGONS ("Dark-ages, Reionization And Galaxy-formation Observables Numerical Simulation") consortium also accounted for&#160;atomic (non-molecular) hydrogen. As a result, there was a deficit in the amount of gas that could form&#160;stars compared to the amount flowing in.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> By modelling the galaxies like an economy, Duffy was able to show that the early galaxies were in a "Great Galactic Recession". Those simulations focusing on the dark matter around galaxies demonstrate that without the dark matter, there would not have been enough time since the start of the universe for our galaxy to form.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>He has attempted to directly detect this dark matter as part of SABRE ("Sodium-iodide with Active Background REjection"), an international research consortium with teams in Italy, USA and Australia. SABRE is constructing the southern hemisphere's first dark matter detector 1 kilometre (0.62&#160;mi) underground in a gold mine in <a href="/wiki/Stawell,_Victoria" title="Stawell, Victoria">Stawell, Victoria</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Conversation_2016_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Conversation_2016-10">[10]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> He is also part of two <a href="/wiki/Australian_Research_Council" title="Australian Research Council">Australian Research Council</a> Centres of Excellence, investigating the origin of matter (<a href="/wiki/ARC_Centre_of_Excellence_for_All-Sky_Astrophysics" title="ARC Centre of Excellence for All-Sky Astrophysics">ASTRO-3D</a>) and seeing the Universe with <a href="/wiki/Gravitational_wave" title="Gravitational wave">gravitational waves</a> (OzGrav),<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> and he was a member of the worldwide OWLS (OverWhelmingly Large Simulations) collaboration.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Science_communications">Science communications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alan_Duffy_(astronomer)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Science communications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Duffy appears regularly on <a href="/wiki/ABC_News_Breakfast" class="mw-redirect" title="ABC News Breakfast">ABC's Breakfast News</a> TV, <a href="/wiki/ABC_Radio_Sydney" title="ABC Radio Sydney">ABC Radio Sydney</a>, <a href="/wiki/ABC_Radio_Melbourne" title="ABC Radio Melbourne">ABC Radio Melbourne</a>, Ten’s <i><a href="/wiki/The_Project_(Australian_TV_program)" title="The Project (Australian TV program)">The Project</a></i>, Nine’s <i><a href="/wiki/Today_(Australian_TV_program)" title="Today (Australian TV program)">Today Weekends</a></i> and TripleJ’s <i><a href="/wiki/Hack_(radio_program)" title="Hack (radio program)">Hack</a></i> show, where he explains developments in science and space.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> He writes a regular column in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Conversation_(TV_series)" class="mw-redirect" title="The Conversation (TV series)">The Conversation</a></i><sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> and the science magazine <i><a href="/wiki/Cosmos_(magazine)" title="Cosmos (magazine)">Cosmos</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>He has presented at <a href="/wiki/TEDx" class="mw-redirect" title="TEDx">TEDx</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Sydney_Opera_House" title="Sydney Opera House">Sydney Opera House</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> He was the Ambassador for the Sydney Science Festival 2016,<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> and host for <a href="/wiki/Cheltenham_Science_Festival#FameLab" title="Cheltenham Science Festival">Famelab</a> showcasing Australian research and achievement in science, technology, engineering and mathematics.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> He hosted an Evening with&#160;<a href="/wiki/Neil_deGrasse_Tyson" title="Neil deGrasse Tyson">Neil DeGrasse Tyson</a> at the Melbourne Convention Centre, interviewed onstage <a href="/wiki/Lawrence_M._Krauss" title="Lawrence M. Krauss">Lawrence Krauss</a><sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> as well as <a href="/wiki/Brian_Greene" title="Brian Greene">Brian Greene</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> and presented in a national tour for <a href="/wiki/BBC_Worldwide" title="BBC Worldwide">BBC Worldwide</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/The_Science_of_Doctor_Who" title="The Science of Doctor Who">The Science of Doctor Who</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>He wrote and starred in a 2012 science documentary about dark matter, <i>Dark</i>. Since December 2012 he has co-hosted the YouTube series <i>Pint in the Sky</i> with <a href="/wiki/Katie_Mack_(astronomer)" title="Katie Mack (astronomer)">Katie Mack</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> and in March 2017 he started a new podcast, <i>Cosmic Vertigo</i> with co-host <a href="/wiki/Amanda_Bauer" title="Amanda Bauer">Amanda Bauer</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> As part of <a href="/wiki/National_Science_Week#Australia" title="National Science Week">Science Week</a> 2017, Duffy and <a href="/wiki/Katie_Mack_(astronomer)" title="Katie Mack (astronomer)">Katie Mack</a> launched a virtual reality tour of the Universe, using custom-made headsets and a free app.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<p>Duffy's good looks have helped to attract media attention – <i><a href="/wiki/Mamamia_(website)" title="Mamamia (website)">MamaMia</a></i> commented that "Unfortunately, I couldn’t hear what he was saying over the sound of his cheekbones."<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life">Personal life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alan_Duffy_(astronomer)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Personal life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In January 2016, Duffy married Sarah Clarke. He qualified as an Australian citizen in October 2014.<sup id="cite_ref-Ballyclare_1-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ballyclare-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Duffy has a keen interest in science fiction, telling the <i>Belfast Telegraph</i>:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>Sci-fi was a big inspiration. My mum had remarried and my stepdad at the time was a ferocious nerd like me. We watched everything – Star Trek, Star Wars, all the classic sci-fi books by Asimov and Arthur C Clarke – and it opened up this world to me of all these possibilities... And, of course, Stephen Hawking's books – how could you not want to study physics? There are these incredible concepts – black holes, the universe expanding – that are so bizarre and yet are actually part of our world.<sup id="cite_ref-Ballyclare_1-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ballyclare-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
