<p><b>Thaumaturgy</b> (<span class="nowrap"><small><a href="/wiki/American_English" title="American English">US</a>:</small> <span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="/θ/: 'th' in 'thigh'">θ</span><span title="/ɔː/: 'au' in 'fraud'">ɔː</span><span title="'m' in 'my'">m</span><span title="/ə/: 'a' in 'about'">ə</span><span title="/ˌ/: secondary stress follows">ˌ</span><span title="'t' in 'tie'">t</span><span title="/ɜːr/: 'ur' in 'fur'">ɜːr</span><span title="/dʒ/: 'j' in 'jam'">dʒ</span><span title="/iː/ or /ɪ/: 'y' in 'happy'">i</span></span>/</a></span><small class="nowrap metadata">&#160;(<span class="unicode haudio"><span class="fn"><span style="white-space:nowrap"><a href="/wiki/File:En-us-thaumaturgy.ogg" title="About this sound"><img alt="About this sound" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/11px-Loudspeaker.svg.png" width="11" height="11" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/17px-Loudspeaker.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/22px-Loudspeaker.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="20" data-file-height="20" /></a>&#160;</span><a href="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/3/3e/En-us-thaumaturgy.ogg" class="internal" title="En-us-thaumaturgy.ogg">listen</a></span></span>)</small></span>, from <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a> <span lang="el" xml:lang="el">θαῦμα <i>thaûma</i></span>, meaning "miracle" or "marvel" and <span lang="el" xml:lang="el">ἔργον <i>érgon</i></span>, meaning "work") is the capability of a <a href="/wiki/Magic_(paranormal)" title="Magic (paranormal)">magician</a> or a <a href="/wiki/Saint" title="Saint">saint</a> to work <a href="/wiki/Magic_(paranormal)" title="Magic (paranormal)">magic</a> or <a href="/wiki/Miracle" title="Miracle">miracles</a>. <a href="/wiki/Isaac_Bonewits" title="Isaac Bonewits">Isaac Bonewits</a> defined thaumaturgy as "the use of magic for nonreligious purposes; the art and science of 'wonder working;' using magic to actually change things in the physical world".<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> It is sometimes translated into English as <b>wonderworking</b>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> A practitioner of thaumaturgy is a <b>thaumaturge</b>, <b>thaumaturgist</b> or <b>miracle-worker</b>.</p>

<p>In original Greek writings, the term <i>thaumaturge</i> referred to several Christian <a href="/wiki/Saint" title="Saint">saints</a>. This is usually translated into English as "wonderworker": a saint through whom God works miracles, not just occasionally, but as a matter of course. It was even said<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag may use weasel words or too-vague attribution. (October 2016)">by whom?</span></a></i>]</sup> that God raises up not more than one every century. Famous ancient Christian thaumaturges include <a href="/wiki/Saint_Gregory_of_Neocaesarea" class="mw-redirect" title="Saint Gregory of Neocaesarea">Saint Gregory of Neocaesarea</a>, also known as Saint Gregory Thaumaturgus (c. 213–270), <a href="/wiki/Saint_Menas" title="Saint Menas">Saint Menas</a> of Egypt (285 – c. 309), <a href="/wiki/Saint_Nicholas_of_Myra" class="mw-redirect" title="Saint Nicholas of Myra">Saint Nicholas of Myra</a> (270–343), <a href="/wiki/Saint_Anthony_of_Padua" class="mw-redirect" title="Saint Anthony of Padua">Saint Anthony of Padua</a> (1195–1231), <a href="/wiki/Saint_Philomena" class="mw-redirect" title="Saint Philomena">Saint Philomena</a>, <a href="/wiki/Saint_Ambrose_of_Optina" class="mw-redirect" title="Saint Ambrose of Optina">Saint Ambrose of Optina</a> (1812–1891), <a href="/wiki/Gerard_Majella" title="Gerard Majella">Saint Gerard Majella</a> (1726–1755) and <a href="/wiki/Saint_John_of_Kronstadt" class="mw-redirect" title="Saint John of Kronstadt">Saint John of Kronstadt</a> (1829–1908). The <a href="/wiki/Carmelite" class="mw-redirect" title="Carmelite">Carmelite</a> <a href="/wiki/Bishop_of_Fiesole" class="mw-redirect" title="Bishop of Fiesole">Bishop of Fiesole</a>, <a href="/wiki/Saint_Andrew_Corsini" class="mw-redirect" title="Saint Andrew Corsini">Saint Andrew Corsini</a> (1302–1373), was also called a thaumaturge during his lifetime.</p>
<p>Kings of France and England were also called thaumaturges, as they were traditionally considered able to <a href="/wiki/Royal_touch" title="Royal touch">heal scrofula</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Islam">Islam</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Thaumaturgy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Islam">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Islamic_view_of_miracles" title="Islamic view of miracles">Islamic view of miracles</a></div>
<p><b>Miracle</b> in the <a href="/wiki/Qur%27an" class="mw-redirect" title="Qur'an">Qur'an</a> can be defined as a supernatural intervention in the life of human beings.<sup id="cite_ref-EoQ_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EoQ-3">[3]</a></sup> According to this definition, <a href="/wiki/Miracle" title="Miracle">miracles</a> are present "in a threefold sense: in <a href="/wiki/Sacred_history" title="Sacred history">sacred history</a>, in connection with the <a href="/wiki/Prophets_of_Islam" class="mw-redirect" title="Prophets of Islam">Islamic prophet</a> <a href="/wiki/Muhammad" title="Muhammad">Muhammad</a> himself and in relation to revelation".<sup id="cite_ref-EoQ_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EoQ-3">[3]</a></sup> The Qur'an does not use the technical <a href="/wiki/Arabic_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Arabic language">Arabic</a> word for miracle (<i>Muʿdjiza</i>) literally meaning "that by means of which [the Prophet] confounds, overwhelms, his opponents". It rather uses the term <i><a href="/wiki/Ayah" title="Ayah">Ayah</a></i> (literally meaning <a href="/wiki/Omen" title="Omen">sign</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-EoI_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EoI-4">[4]</a></sup> The term <i>Ayah</i> is used in the Qur'an in the above mentioned threefold sense: it refers to the "verses" of the Qur'an (believed to be the divine speech in <a href="/wiki/Natural_language" title="Natural language">human language</a>; presented by Muhammad as his chief miracle); as well as to miracles of it and the signs (particularly those of creation).<sup id="cite_ref-EoQ_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EoQ-3">[3]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-EoI_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EoI-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Judaism">Judaism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Thaumaturgy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Judaism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See: <a href="/wiki/Practical_Kabbalah" title="Practical Kabbalah">Practical Kabbalah</a> and <a href="/wiki/Baal_Shem" title="Baal Shem">Baal Shem</a>.</div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Buddhism">Buddhism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Thaumaturgy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Buddhism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See: <a href="/wiki/Abhij%C3%B1%C4%81" title="Abhijñā">Abhijñā</a>.</div>
<p>In the introduction of his translation of the "Spiritual Powers (神通 <i>Jinzū</i>)" chapter of <a href="/wiki/D%C5%8Dgen" title="Dōgen">Dōgen</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Sh%C5%8Db%C5%8Dgenz%C5%8D" title="Shōbōgenzō">Shōbōgenzō</a>, Carl Bielefel refers to the powers developed by adepts of Buddhist meditation as belonging to the "thaumaturgical tradition".<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Magic">Magic</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Thaumaturgy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Magic">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In the 16th century, the word <i>thaumaturgy</i> entered the English language meaning miraculous or magical powers.</p>
<p>The word was first anglicized and used in the magical sense in <a href="/wiki/John_Dee_(mathematician)" class="mw-redirect" title="John Dee (mathematician)">John Dee</a>'s book <i>Mathematicall Praeface to Euclid's Elements</i> (1570). He mentions an "art mathematical" called "thaumaturgy... which giveth certain order to make strange works, of the sense to be perceived and of men greatly to be wondered at".</p>
<p>In Dee's time, "the Mathematicks" referred not merely to the abstract computations associated with the term today, but to physical mechanical devices which employed mathematical principles in their design. These devices, operated by means of compressed air, springs, strings, pulleys or levers, were seen by unsophisticated people (who did not understand their working principles) as magical devices which could only have been made with the aid of demons and devils.<sup id="cite_ref-Zetterburg_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Zetterburg-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>By building such mechanical devices, Dee earned a reputation as a conjurer "dreaded" by neighborhood children.<sup id="cite_ref-Zetterburg_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Zetterburg-6">[6]</a></sup> He complained of this assessment in his "Mathematicall Praeface": "And for these, and such like marvellous Actes and Feates, Naturally, and Mechanically, wrought and contrived: ought any honest Student and Modest Christian Philosopher, be counted, &amp; called a Conjurer? Shall the folly of Idiotes, and the Malice of the Scornfull, so much prevaille ... Shall that man, be (in hugger mugger) condemned, as a Companion of the hellhoundes, and a Caller, and Conjurer of wicked and damned Spirites?"<sup id="cite_ref-Zetterburg_6-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Zetterburg-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Hermetic_Qabalah">Hermetic Qabalah</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Thaumaturgy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Hermetic Qabalah">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Hermetic_Qabalah" title="Hermetic Qabalah">Hermetic Qabalah</a> mystical tradition, a person titled a magician has the power to make subtle changes in higher realms, which in turn produce physical results. For instance, if a Magician made slight changes in the world of formation (<a href="/wiki/Olam_Yetzirah" class="mw-redirect" title="Olam Yetzirah">Olam Yetzirah</a>), such as within the Sefirah of <a href="/wiki/Yesod_(Kabbalah)" class="mw-redirect" title="Yesod (Kabbalah)">Yesod</a> upon which <a href="/wiki/Malkuth" title="Malkuth">Malkuth</a> (the material realm) is based and within which all former <a href="/wiki/Sephirot_(Kabbalah)" class="mw-redirect" title="Sephirot (Kabbalah)">Sephiroth</a> are brought together, then these alterations would appear in the world of action (<a href="/wiki/Olam_Assiah" class="mw-redirect" title="Olam Assiah">Olam Assiah</a>).</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Philosophy">Philosophy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Thaumaturgy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Philosophy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In his book, <i>The Gift of Death</i>, <a href="/wiki/Deconstruction" title="Deconstruction">deconstructionist philosopher</a> <a href="/wiki/Jacques_Derrida" title="Jacques Derrida">Jacques Derrida</a> refers to <a href="/wiki/Philosophy" title="Philosophy">philosophy</a> as thaumaturgy. The idea is taken from the fifth essay of Jan Patočka's work, <i>Heretical Essays in the History of Philosophy</i><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Derrida's reading is based on a deconstruction of the origin of the concepts of responsibility, faith, and gift.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>

<p>The term <b>thaumaturgy</b> is used in various novels and games as a synonym for <a href="/wiki/Magic_(paranormal)" title="Magic (paranormal)">magic</a>, a particular sub-school (often mechanical) of magic, or as the "science" of magic.</p>
<ul>
<li>Thaumaturgy is often used as a name for the magic in <a href="/wiki/Terry_Pratchett" title="Terry Pratchett">Terry Pratchett</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Discworld" title="Discworld">Discworld</a></i> novels.</li>
<li>Thaumaturgy is defined as the "science" or "physics" of magic in RPGs such as <i><a href="/wiki/GURPS" title="GURPS">GURPS</a></i>,<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> novels such as China Miéville's <a href="/wiki/Perdido_Street_Station" title="Perdido Street Station">Perdido Street Station</a>, and real world works such as Marcus Cordey's <i>Magical Theory and Tradition</i> and <a href="/wiki/Isaac_Bonewits" title="Isaac Bonewits">Isaac Bonewits</a>' 1971 <i>Real Magic</i> and his 2005 <i>Authentic Thaumaturgy</i></li>
<li>In <a href="/wiki/Lyndon_Hardy" title="Lyndon Hardy">Lyndon Hardy</a>'s <i>Magics</i> trilogy, thaumaturgy is one of the five disciplines of magic. It figures most prominently in the first book, <i><a href="/wiki/Master_of_the_Five_Magics" title="Master of the Five Magics">Master of the Five Magics</a></i>.</li>
<li>In the roleplaying game <i><a href="/wiki/Dungeons_%26_Dragons" title="Dungeons &amp; Dragons">Dungeons &amp; Dragons</a></i> the thaumaturgist (sic) is a prestige class which specifically summons <a href="/wiki/Outsider_(Dungeons_%26_Dragons)" title="Outsider (Dungeons &amp; Dragons)">outsiders</a>.</li>
<li>In <a href="/wiki/White_Wolf_Publishing" title="White Wolf Publishing">White Wolf Publishing</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Role-playing_game" title="Role-playing game">role-playing game</a>, <i><a href="/wiki/Vampire:_The_Masquerade" title="Vampire: The Masquerade">Vampire: The Masquerade</a>,</i> Thaumaturgy is a magical Discipline encompassing the Hermetic style of blood magic the Tremere converted from their old practices when they became vampires.</li>
<li>In the <i><a href="/wiki/Elder_Scrolls" class="mw-redirect" title="Elder Scrolls">Elder Scrolls</a></i> games <i><a href="/wiki/Daggerfall" class="mw-redirect" title="Daggerfall">Daggerfall</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Battlespire" class="mw-redirect" title="Battlespire">Battlespire</a></i>, thaumaturgy is a character skill, which is loosely defined as "focus[ing] on manipulating known forces and objects within their natural laws".</li>
<li>In <i><a href="/wiki/Final_Fantasy_XIV" title="Final Fantasy XIV">Final Fantasy XIV</a></i>, thaumaturge is the title of a playable character class.</li>
<li>In <i><a href="/wiki/Dominions_3:_The_Awakening" title="Dominions 3: The Awakening">Dominions 3: The Awakening</a></i>, thaumaturgy is one of the paths of magic that can be researched.</li>
<li>In <a href="/wiki/Jim_Butcher" title="Jim Butcher">Jim Butcher</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/The_Dresden_Files" title="The Dresden Files">The Dresden Files</a></i>, thaumaturgy is the creation of a magical link between an object and a person (as when using voodoo dolls).</li>
<li>In <i><a href="/wiki/The_Lunar_Chronicles" title="The Lunar Chronicles">The Lunar Chronicles</a></i> by <a href="/wiki/Marissa_Meyer" title="Marissa Meyer">Marissa Meyer</a>, thaumaturges are the bodyguards and police force of the Lunar Queen Levana.</li>
<li>In <i><a href="/wiki/Magic:_The_Gathering" title="Magic: The Gathering">Magic: The Gathering</a></i>, many cards in the plane of <a href="/wiki/Theros" title="Theros">Theros</a> feature Thaumaturges, most notably the Battle Thaumaturge. In the planeswalker's guide to the setting, they are flavoured more akin to <a href="/wiki/Theurgy" title="Theurgy">theurgists</a>, having their magic stem from the gods.</li>
<li>In <i><a href="/wiki/Minecraft" title="Minecraft">Minecraft</a></i>, the player Azanor is the developer of the popular modification Thaumcraft, which brings many extensions to the game based around Thaumaturgy.</li>
<li>In <i><a href="/wiki/Path_of_Exile" title="Path of Exile">Path of Exile</a></i>, the witch makes mention of thaumaturgy in several places along the storyline and in various dungeons.</li>
<li>In <a href="/wiki/Patrick_Rothfuss" title="Patrick Rothfuss">Patrick Rothfuss</a>' trilogy, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Kingkiller_Chronicle" title="The Kingkiller Chronicle">The Kingkiller Chronicle</a></i>, a "Thaum" is the unit used to describe heat energy at the University.</li>
<li>In <i>Wizard101</i>, Thaumaturges are wizards who specialize in ice magic. These wizards specialize in tanking hits, having spells that do not deal a lot of damage, but can provide shielding spells and taunt enemies, drawing them away from more important players.</li>
<li>In <i>Ultima VIII&#160;: Pagan,</i> Thaumaturgy is one of the five schools of magic along with Sorcery, Necromancy, Theurgy and Tempestry. Thaumaturgy contains miscellenaous spells of usually non-elemental nature. Its practice is not tied to one of the titans.</li>
<li>In <i><a href="/wiki/Beautiful_Creatures_(novel)" title="Beautiful Creatures (novel)">Beautiful Creatures</a></i>, the book by Kami Garcia and Margaret Stohl, Ryan, a cousin of the main character's love interest, Lena, is discovered as being a Thaumaturge when she heals the main character after an accident. When asked "I'm guessing that's a fancy Caster name for healer?" Lena replies, "Something like that."</li>
<li>In <i><a href="/wiki/The_Elder_Scrolls_V:_Skyrim" title="The Elder Scrolls V: Skyrim">The Elder Scrolls V: Skyrim</a></i> A man who has the ability to cure vampirism calls himself a thaumaturge in Morthal.</li>
</ul>
