			<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Death of Linda Norgrove</h1>
									<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
									<div id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
								<div id="contentSub"></div>
												<div id="jump-to-nav" class="mw-jump">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>
				</div>
				<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Linda_Norgrove.jpg" class="image"><img alt="Aid worker Linda Norgrove, northeastern Afghanistan" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/5/51/Linda_Norgrove.jpg/220px-Linda_Norgrove.jpg" width="220" height="250" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/5/51/Linda_Norgrove.jpg 1.5x" data-file-width="259" data-file-height="294" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Linda_Norgrove.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Linda Norgrove</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>On 26 September 2010, British aid worker Linda Norgrove and three Afghan colleagues were kidnapped by members of the <a href="/wiki/Taliban" title="Taliban">Taliban</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Kunar_Province" title="Kunar Province">Kunar Province</a> of eastern Afghanistan. At the time, she was working in the country as regional director for <a href="/wiki/Development_Alternatives_Incorporated" title="Development Alternatives Incorporated">Development Alternatives Incorporated</a>, a contractor for U.S. and other government agencies. After their capture, the group was taken to the nearby Dewegal Valley area. United States and Afghan forces began a search of the area, placing roadblocks to prevent the group from being moved east into Pakistan.</p>
<p>Norgrove's captors demanded the release of <a href="/wiki/Aafia_Siddiqui" title="Aafia Siddiqui">Aafia Siddiqui</a> in exchange for her return. During negotiations, on 3 October 2010, the Taliban released the three Afghans. Five days later, amid concerns that Norgrove would be killed or moved by her kidnappers, the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Naval_Special_Warfare_Development_Group" class="mw-redirect" title="United States Naval Special Warfare Development Group">United States Naval Special Warfare Development Group</a> conducted a predawn rescue attempt on the Taliban mountain hideout where she was held captive. U.S. forces killed several kidnappers and three local farmers during the assault. They subsequently located a badly wounded Norgrove in a nearby gully; she died later from her injuries.</p>
<p>Initial reports said that she had been killed by an explosion set off by one of her captors. However, a joint official investigation by the United Kingdom and the United States concluded that Norgrove's fatal injuries were inflicted by a grenade thrown by one of her rescuers. A February 2011 <a href="/wiki/Inquests_in_England_and_Wales" title="Inquests in England and Wales">coroner's narrative verdict</a> reported that Norgrove died during the failed rescue attempt. In October 2012, one of her colleagues said in an interview that the captors had told Norgrove they had no intention of killing her.</p>

<h2><span id="Early_life,_education_and_work"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life.2C_education_and_work"><span id="Linda_Norgrove"></span>Early life, education and work</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Death_of_Linda_Norgrove&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life, education and work">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Lewis_abandoned.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/c/c7/Lewis_abandoned.jpg/220px-Lewis_abandoned.jpg" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/c/c7/Lewis_abandoned.jpg/330px-Lewis_abandoned.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/c/c7/Lewis_abandoned.jpg/440px-Lewis_abandoned.jpg 2x" data-file-width="900" data-file-height="600" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Lewis_abandoned.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Isle of Lewis, Scotland, where Norgrove grew up</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Norgrove was born in <a href="/wiki/Altnaharra" title="Altnaharra">Altnaharra</a>, Scotland, in 1974 to John and Lorna Norgrove. She grew up on a <a href="/wiki/Croft_(land)" title="Croft (land)">croft</a> on the <a href="/wiki/Isle_of_Lewis" class="mw-redirect" title="Isle of Lewis">Isle of Lewis</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Western_Isles" class="mw-redirect" title="Western Isles">Western Isles</a>, attending a primary school in <a href="/wiki/Uig,_Lewis" title="Uig, Lewis">Uig</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-SCOTS2010-10-10_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SCOTS2010-10-10-1">[1]</a></sup> She later attended the <a href="/wiki/Nicolson_Institute" title="Nicolson Institute">Nicolson Institute</a> in <a href="/wiki/Stornoway" title="Stornoway">Stornoway</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-BBC2010-10-09_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BBC2010-10-09-2">[2]</a></sup> Norgrove attended the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Aberdeen" title="University of Aberdeen">University of Aberdeen</a>, receiving a first-class honours degree in tropical environmental science; her coursework included postgraduate research at the University of <a href="/wiki/Chiapas" title="Chiapas">Chiapas</a> in Mexico and a year of study at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Oregon" title="University of Oregon">University of Oregon</a> (1993–94).<sup id="cite_ref-KVAL2010-10-12_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-KVAL2010-10-12-3">[3]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-MAIL2010-10-14_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MAIL2010-10-14-4">[4]</a></sup> She attended the <a href="/wiki/University_of_London" title="University of London">University of London</a>, receiving a <a href="/wiki/Master%27s_degree" title="Master's degree">MA</a> with distinction in rural resources and environmental policy in 1997.<sup id="cite_ref-SKY2010-10-09_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SKY2010-10-09-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2002, Norgrove received a <a href="/wiki/Doctor_of_Philosophy" title="Doctor of Philosophy">Ph.D.</a> from the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Manchester" title="University of Manchester">University of Manchester</a> in development policy and management.<sup id="cite_ref-BBC2010-10-09_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BBC2010-10-09-2">[2]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-TELE2010-10-10_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TELE2010-10-10-6">[6]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-SCOTS2010-10-10-2_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SCOTS2010-10-10-2-7">[7]</a></sup> From 2002 to 2005 she worked for the <a href="/wiki/World_Wide_Fund_for_Nature" title="World Wide Fund for Nature">World Wide Fund</a> in Peru, supporting (and later supervising) the WWF's Forest Program in the Peruvian <a href="/wiki/Andes" title="Andes">Andes</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-WWF2010-10-10_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WWF2010-10-10-8">[8]</a></sup> At the time of her death, in addition to her aid work, Norgrove was working towards an <a href="/wiki/Master_of_Business_Administration" title="Master of Business Administration">MBA</a> from the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Warwick" title="University of Warwick">University of Warwick</a> through <a href="/wiki/Distance_learning" class="mw-redirect" title="Distance learning">distance learning</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-MAIL2010-10-10_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MAIL2010-10-10-9">[9]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-PAJ2010-10-11_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PAJ2010-10-11-10">[10]</a></sup> She worked in Afghanistan for the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations" title="United Nations">United Nations</a> from 2005 to 2008, and as regional director of an international development company based in <a href="/wiki/Jalalabad" title="Jalalabad">Jalalabad</a><sup id="cite_ref-PAJ2010-10-11_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PAJ2010-10-11-10">[10]</a></sup> beginning in February 2010. She also worked in Laos as an environmental specialist for the U.N. in 2008–09, Mexico, and Uganda where Norgrove researched the effects of national park management on the indigenous population near <a href="/wiki/Mount_Elgon_National_Park" title="Mount Elgon National Park">Mount Elgon National Park</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-CNN2010-10-09_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CNN2010-10-09-11">[11]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-SCOTS2010-10-14-2_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SCOTS2010-10-14-2-12">[12]</a></sup> In the investigative article "The Crimes of SEAL Team Six" the journalist Matthew Cole states that Linda Norgrove actually worked for the British Secret Intelligence Service (MI6) but the intelligence service has neither confirmed or denied this.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Kidnapping">Kidnapping</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Death_of_Linda_Norgrove&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Kidnapping">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:162px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Afghanistan-Konar.png" class="image"><img alt="Pink map of Afghanistan, with Kunar Province in red in the northeast" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e4/Afghanistan-Konar.png/160px-Afghanistan-Konar.png" width="160" height="123" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e4/Afghanistan-Konar.png/240px-Afghanistan-Konar.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/e/e4/Afghanistan-Konar.png 2x" data-file-width="300" data-file-height="230" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Afghanistan-Konar.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Map of Afghanistan, with <a href="/wiki/Kunar_Province" title="Kunar Province">Kunar Province</a> in red</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>On 26 September 2010, Norgrove and three Afghan colleagues were travelling in the <a href="/wiki/Chawkay_District" title="Chawkay District">Chawkay District</a> (also known as Tsawkay and Sawkay) of eastern <a href="/wiki/Kunar_Province" title="Kunar Province">Kunar Province</a> when they were kidnapped by local insurgents.<sup id="cite_ref-BBC2010-10-11_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BBC2010-10-11-13">[13]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-BBC2010-10-11-2_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BBC2010-10-11-2-14">[14]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-NPR2010-10-11_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NPR2010-10-11-15">[15]</a></sup> They were ambushed whilst driving on the main highway from <a href="/wiki/Jalalabad" title="Jalalabad">Jalalabad</a> to <a href="/wiki/Asadabad,_Afghanistan" title="Asadabad, Afghanistan">Asadabad</a>, in the Dewagal valley,<sup id="cite_ref-GD2010-10-11_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GD2010-10-11-16">[16]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-SCOTS2010-10-14_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SCOTS2010-10-14-17">[17]</a></sup> in two unarmored, unmarked <a href="/wiki/Toyota_Corolla" title="Toyota Corolla">Toyota Corollas</a>. A U.S. military convoy was ambushed two months earlier on the same stretch of road.<sup id="cite_ref-CNN2010-10-20_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CNN2010-10-20-18">[18]</a></sup> Norgrove wore a <a href="/wiki/Burqa" title="Burqa">burqa</a> to disguise her foreign appearance.<sup id="cite_ref-TELE2010-10-09_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TELE2010-10-09-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>Dressed in men's clothing by her captors, she was taken first into the mountains<sup id="cite_ref-NPR2010-10-11_15-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NPR2010-10-11-15">[15]</a></sup> and then brought to the Dewegal Valley in Chowkai District (which crosses the <a href="/wiki/Korengal_Valley" class="mw-redirect" title="Korengal Valley">Korengal Valley</a>). U.S. Army troops from Bravo Company, 2/327 Infantry, 1st Brigade Combat Team, 101st Airborne Division began a 12-day search supported by Afghan army, police and commando units under the codename "Enterprise". A house-to-house search was conducted and roadblocks posted at the valley entrance to prevent Norgrove's captors from transferring her eastward into Pakistan. The difficult terrain (with few roads) complicated and slowed the process; however, the search efforts succeeded in containing the kidnappers in the vicinity and several local Taliban members were killed.<sup id="cite_ref-SCOTS2010-10-10-3_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SCOTS2010-10-10-3-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Negotiations"><span id="Negotiation_with_captors"></span>Negotiations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Death_of_Linda_Norgrove&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Negotiations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>It was unclear at first who had kidnapped Norgrove and her colleagues. A Taliban commander, the Pakistan-based Mohammed Osman, was reported to demand the release of Aafia Siddiqui in return for Norgrove's freedom. Siddiqui, known as "Lady al-Qaeda", had received an 86-year prison sentence in the U.S. on 26 September.<sup id="cite_ref-REUT2010-10-10_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-REUT2010-10-10-21">[21]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-WSJ2010-10-09_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WSJ2010-10-09-22">[22]</a></sup> "We are lucky that we abducted this British woman soon after the ruthless ruling by an American court on Aafia Siddiqui. We will demand the release of Aafia Siddiqui in exchange for her", said Osman.<sup id="cite_ref-TELE2010-09-28_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TELE2010-09-28-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>However, other Afghan sources denied any link to Osman.<sup id="cite_ref-SCOTS2010-10-10-3_20-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SCOTS2010-10-10-3-20">[20]</a></sup> U.S. military sources identified Norgrove's captors as Kunar Taliban, and <a href="/wiki/Secretary_of_State_for_Foreign_and_Commonwealth_Affairs" title="Secretary of State for Foreign and Commonwealth Affairs">British Foreign Secretary</a> <a href="/wiki/William_Hague" title="William Hague">William Hague</a> said they were from a <a href="/wiki/Salafi" class="mw-redirect" title="Salafi">Salafist</a> group affiliated with the local Taliban,<sup id="cite_ref-BBC2010-10-11-2_14-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BBC2010-10-11-2-14">[14]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-NYT2010-10-12_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NYT2010-10-12-24">[24]</a></sup> known as <a href="/wiki/Jamaat_al_Dawa_al_Quran" title="Jamaat al Dawa al Quran">Jamaat al Dawa al Quran</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-roots_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-roots-25">[25]</a></sup> An Afghan intelligence official later identified her captors as local commanders Mullah Basir and Mullah Keftan.<sup id="cite_ref-CNN2010-10-09_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CNN2010-10-09-11">[11]</a></sup> Negotiations for Norgrove's release were conducted through local tribal elders.<sup id="cite_ref-SCOTS2010-10-10-3_20-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SCOTS2010-10-10-3-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>The three Afghans captured with Norgrove were released on 3 October.<sup id="cite_ref-BBC2010-10-11-2_14-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BBC2010-10-11-2-14">[14]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-SMH2010-10-11_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SMH2010-10-11-26">[26]</a></sup> British Prime Minister <a href="/wiki/David_Cameron" title="David Cameron">David Cameron</a> said that the primary fear was that she "was going to be passed up the terrorist chain, which would increase further the already high risk that she would be killed."<sup id="cite_ref-TIME2010-10-12_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TIME2010-10-12-27">[27]</a></sup> The British foreign office asked the media not to release details about Norgrove's personal life while she was in captivity to avoid attaching "trophy value" to her kidnapping.<sup id="cite_ref-REUT2010-10-10_21-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-REUT2010-10-10-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template"><span style="white-space: nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Link_rot" title="Wikipedia:Link rot"><span title="&#160;Dead link since March 2016">dead link</span></a></i>]</span></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Rescue_attempt_and_death">Rescue attempt and death</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Death_of_Linda_Norgrove&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Rescue attempt and death">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Intelligence reports indicated that a group of local elders were calling for Norgrove to be executed "like the Russian" (a possible reference to the Russian war in Afghanistan).<sup id="cite_ref-TELE2010-10-17_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TELE2010-10-17-28">[28]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-GD2010-10-13_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GD2010-10-13-29">[29]</a></sup> There were concerns that she might be moved into <a href="/wiki/North_Waziristan" title="North Waziristan">North Waziristan</a> in Pakistan, about 10 miles (16&#160;km) away.<sup id="cite_ref-MAIL2010-10-13_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MAIL2010-10-13-30">[30]</a></sup> The intelligence prompted British Prime Minister Cameron and William Hague to approve a United States special operations effort to rescue Norgrove during her 13th night of captivity. The operation was spearheaded by "SEAL Team Six", <a href="/wiki/United_States_Navy_SEALs" title="United States Navy SEALs">Navy SEALs</a> from the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Naval_Special_Warfare_Development_Group" class="mw-redirect" title="United States Naval Special Warfare Development Group">Naval Special Warfare Development Group</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-GD2010-10-13_29-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GD2010-10-13-29">[29]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-SMH2010-10-14_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SMH2010-10-14-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<p>The SEALs staged a predawn raid on the Taliban hillside <a href="/wiki/Compound_(enclosure)" title="Compound (enclosure)">compound</a> hideout, where Norgrove was held in a shack, on 8 October 2010. The stronghold was surrounded by 16-foot (4.9&#160;m) high, 2-foot (0.61&#160;m) thick perimeter walls in a densely wooded area<sup id="cite_ref-TELE2010-10-17_28-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TELE2010-10-17-28">[28]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-AFP2010-10-22_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AFP2010-10-22-32">[32]</a></sup> in the village of Dineshgal, 7,000 feet (2,100&#160;m) up a steep mountain in the <a href="/wiki/Korengal_Valley" class="mw-redirect" title="Korengal Valley">Korengal Valley</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-IND2010-10-12_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IND2010-10-12-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<p>At approximately 3:30&#160;am 20 SEALs and about 24 U.S. Army Rangers from the <a href="/wiki/75th_Ranger_Regiment" class="mw-redirect" title="75th Ranger Regiment">75th Ranger Regiment</a> (wearing <a href="/wiki/Night_vision_device" title="Night vision device">night-vision goggles</a>) approached the compound, <a href="/wiki/Fast_roping" class="mw-redirect" title="Fast roping">fast roping</a> from two <a href="/wiki/CH-47_Chinook" class="mw-redirect" title="CH-47 Chinook">CH-47 Chinook</a> helicopters. They were fired on from the compound and from a nearby position by Taliban armed with <a href="/wiki/AK-47" title="AK-47">AK-47s</a>, rocket-propelled grenades, and suicide vests.<sup id="cite_ref-TELE2010-10-17_28-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TELE2010-10-17-28">[28]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-MAIL2010-10-13_30-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MAIL2010-10-13-30">[30]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-TELE2010-10-12_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TELE2010-10-12-34">[34]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-TIME2010-10-12-2_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TIME2010-10-12-2-35">[35]</a></sup> Two American snipers aboard a helicopter killed two guards using sound-suppressed rifles.<sup id="cite_ref-TELE2010-10-17_28-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TELE2010-10-17-28">[28]</a></sup> An <a href="/wiki/Lockheed_AC-130" title="Lockheed AC-130">AC-130 Spectre</a> <a href="/wiki/Gunship" title="Gunship">gunship</a> provided the U.S. troops on the ground with <a href="/wiki/Close_air_support" title="Close air support">close air support</a>, killing two fleeing Taliban.<sup id="cite_ref-BLOOM2010-10-15_36-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BLOOM2010-10-15-36">[36]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-TELE2010-10-15_37-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TELE2010-10-15-37">[37]</a></sup> The Rangers secured enemy positions on the nearby hills,<sup id="cite_ref-TELE2010-10-17_28-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TELE2010-10-17-28">[28]</a></sup> and all six Taliban gunmen who fought the U.S. forces were killed.<sup id="cite_ref-GD2010-10-13_29-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GD2010-10-13-29">[29]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-IND2010-10-12_33-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IND2010-10-12-33">[33]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-TIME2010-10-12-2_35-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TIME2010-10-12-2-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
<p>During the gunfight Norgrove's captors dragged her outside the building where she was being held, but she apparently broke away from them. Video footage of the raid showed an explosion in her vicinity; Norgrove was then found, injured, in a <a href="/wiki/Fetal" class="mw-redirect" title="Fetal">fetal</a> position in a gully.<sup id="cite_ref-BBC2010-10-10_38-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BBC2010-10-10-38">[38]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-BBC2010-12-2_39-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BBC2010-12-2-39">[39]</a></sup></p>
<p>Norgrove was removed from the scene via helicopter and received medical care, but she died.<sup id="cite_ref-IND2010-10-12_33-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IND2010-10-12-33">[33]</a></sup> It was reported initially that she had been killed by one of her captors setting off a <a href="/wiki/Suicide_vest" class="mw-redirect" title="Suicide vest">suicide vest</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-GD2010-10-13_29-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GD2010-10-13-29">[29]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-TELE2010-10-12_34-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TELE2010-10-12-34">[34]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-WSJ2010-10-12_40-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WSJ2010-10-12-40">[40]</a></sup> According to <i><a href="/wiki/The_Guardian" title="The Guardian">The Guardian</a></i>, insurgents often put on suicide vests if they think they are in danger of being attacked.<sup id="cite_ref-GD2010-10-13_29-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GD2010-10-13-29">[29]</a></sup> Taliban commanders Mullah Basir and Mullah Keftan (who were holding Norgrove) were among those killed in the raid, according to an Afghan intelligence official.<sup id="cite_ref-CNN2010-10-09_11-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CNN2010-10-09-11">[11]</a></sup> Other women and children in the compound were uninjured, and no members of the rescue team were wounded.<sup id="cite_ref-TIME2010-10-12-2_35-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TIME2010-10-12-2-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Joint_investigation">Joint investigation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Death_of_Linda_Norgrove&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Joint investigation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:162px;"><a href="/wiki/File:LTG_Joseph_Votel_official_portrait.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f6/LTG_Joseph_Votel_official_portrait.png/160px-LTG_Joseph_Votel_official_portrait.png" width="160" height="200" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f6/LTG_Joseph_Votel_official_portrait.png/240px-LTG_Joseph_Votel_official_portrait.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f6/LTG_Joseph_Votel_official_portrait.png/320px-LTG_Joseph_Votel_official_portrait.png 2x" data-file-width="373" data-file-height="467" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:LTG_Joseph_Votel_official_portrait.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Major General, now General, <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Votel" title="Joseph Votel">Joseph Votel</a>, led the investigation</div>

</div>
<p>British Foreign Secretary William Hague announced Norgrove's death. In a written statement, he said that after receiving information on her location it was "decided that, given the danger she was facing, her best chance of safe release was to act on that information."<sup id="cite_ref-CNN2010-10-09_11-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CNN2010-10-09-11">[11]</a></sup> Prime Minister Cameron defended the rescue attempt: "Decisions on operations to free hostages are always difficult. But where a British life is in such danger, and where we and our allies can act, I believe it is right to try".<sup id="cite_ref-SKY2010-10-10_41-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SKY2010-10-10-41">[41]</a></sup></p>
<p>On 10 October, an unnamed Afghan intelligence officer said that Norgrove was killed by a <a href="/wiki/Grenade" title="Grenade">grenade</a> thrown by one of her captors.<sup id="cite_ref-BBC2010-12-2_39-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BBC2010-12-2-39">[39]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-SKY2010-10-10_41-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SKY2010-10-10-41">[41]</a></sup> The following day, Cameron said that new information indicated Norgrove may have accidentally been killed by a U.S. grenade.<sup id="cite_ref-BBC2010-10-11-2_14-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BBC2010-10-11-2-14">[14]</a></sup> The Prime Minister said in an interview: "Linda could have died as a result of a grenade detonated by the task force during the assault. However, this is not definite".<sup id="cite_ref-BLOOM2010-10-11_42-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BLOOM2010-10-11-42">[42]</a></sup> A U.S. military statement read: "Subsequent review of surveillance footage and discussions with members of the rescue team do not conclusively determine the cause of her death".<sup id="cite_ref-BBC2010-10-11-2_14-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BBC2010-10-11-2-14">[14]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-WSJ2010-10-12_40-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WSJ2010-10-12-40">[40]</a></sup></p>
<p>U.S. President <a href="/wiki/Barack_Obama" title="Barack Obama">Barack Obama</a> promised "to get to the bottom" of the failed rescue attempt.<sup id="cite_ref-TELE2010-10-12-2_43-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TELE2010-10-12-2-43">[43]</a></sup> General <a href="/wiki/David_Petraeus" title="David Petraeus">David Petraeus</a>, commander of the <a href="/wiki/International_Security_Assistance_Force" title="International Security Assistance Force">NATO security force</a> in Afghanistan, ordered an investigation into the incident. <a href="/wiki/Major_general_(United_States)" title="Major general (United States)">U.S. Major General</a> <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Votel" title="Joseph Votel">Joseph Votel</a> (then Chief of Staff of the <a href="/wiki/U.S._Special_Operations_Command" class="mw-redirect" title="U.S. Special Operations Command">U.S. Special Operations Command</a>) and <a href="/wiki/Brigadier_(United_Kingdom)" title="Brigadier (United Kingdom)">British Brigadier</a> <a href="/wiki/Robert_Nitsch" title="Robert Nitsch">Robert Nitsch</a> (Head of Joint Force Support, UK Forces Afghanistan) were appointed to lead a joint UK and U.S. investigation.<sup id="cite_ref-BBC2010-10-11-3_44-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BBC2010-10-11-3-44">[44]</a></sup> It was reported on 12 October that the results were expected within days,<sup id="cite_ref-TIME2010-10-12_27-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TIME2010-10-12-27">[27]</a></sup> and Norgrove's family was kept informed of the investigation's progress.<sup id="cite_ref-BBC2010-10-11-3_44-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BBC2010-10-11-3-44">[44]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-GD2010-10-15_45-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GD2010-10-15-45">[45]</a></sup></p>
<p>Whilst the military investigation was conducted, Norgrove's body was returned to the United Kingdom on 14 October on a <a href="/wiki/Royal_Air_Force" title="Royal Air Force">Royal Air Force</a> flight to <a href="/wiki/RAF_Lyneham" class="mw-redirect" title="RAF Lyneham">RAF Lyneham</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-GD2010-10-14_46-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GD2010-10-14-46">[46]</a></sup> A <a href="/wiki/Humanism" title="Humanism">humanist</a> funeral ceremony, attended by hundreds of people, was held on 26 October at the Uig Community Centre in the <a href="/wiki/Western_Isles" class="mw-redirect" title="Western Isles">Western Isles</a>. Norgrove was buried at <a href="/wiki/Ardroil" title="Ardroil">Ardroil</a> cemetery.<sup id="cite_ref-TELE2010-10-26_47-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TELE2010-10-26-47">[47]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-SCOTS2010-10-26_48-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SCOTS2010-10-26-48">[48]</a></sup></p>
<p>On 2 December Hague announced the results of the joint investigation, which concluded that Norgrove was accidentally killed by a grenade thrown by a U.S sailor. Hague said to the House of Commons: "A grenade was thrown by a member of the rescue team who feared for his own life and those of his team towards a gully from where some of the insurgents had emerged. When the grenade was thrown no member of the team had seen, or heard, Linda Norgrove".<sup id="cite_ref-BBC2010-12-2_39-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BBC2010-12-2-39">[39]</a></sup> Navy SEALs did not immediately notify senior officers about throwing the grenade; this breached military law,<sup id="cite_ref-BBC2010-12-2_39-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BBC2010-12-2-39">[39]</a></sup> and a number of sailors were disciplined.<sup id="cite_ref-BBC2010-12-2_39-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BBC2010-12-2-39">[39]</a></sup></p>
<p>A <a href="/wiki/Post-mortem_examination" class="mw-redirect" title="Post-mortem examination">post-mortem examination</a> of Norgrove's body was conducted by British coroner Russell Delaney on 19 October 2010. <a href="/wiki/Detective_Chief_Inspector" class="mw-redirect" title="Detective Chief Inspector">Detective Chief Inspector</a> Colin Smith of the Metropolitan Police told an inquest, opened 22 October in the Salisbury coroner's court, that the examination identified the cause of death as "penetrating fragment injuries to the head and chest."<sup id="cite_ref-AFP2010-10-22_32-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AFP2010-10-22-32">[32]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template"><span style="white-space: nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Link_rot" title="Wikipedia:Link rot"><span title="&#160;Dead link since March 2016">dead link</span></a></i>]</span></sup> In February 2011 the coroner recorded a <a href="/wiki/Narrative_verdict" title="Narrative verdict">narrative verdict</a> confirming the earlier military investigations' findings that Norgrove was killed by a member of the U.S. rescue team, noting that a gunshot wound to the leg Norgrove received during the rescue did not contribute to her death.<sup id="cite_ref-BBC2011-02-15_49-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BBC2011-02-15-49">[49]</a></sup> In October 2012 Abdul Wadood, Norgrove's colleague and fellow captive, told the BBC that she asked the kidnappers if they were going to kill her:<sup id="cite_ref-bbc.co.uk_50-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bbc.co.uk-50">[50]</a></sup> "Linda asked the abductors when they were planning to kill her and then the abductors talked to her in a very soft language and they told her and assured her that she would not be killed, because that was not their purpose to kill her—all they wanted was to release some of their prisoners, in the end she would be released when this deed is done".<sup id="cite_ref-bbc.co.uk_50-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bbc.co.uk-50">[50]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Tributes">Tributes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Death_of_Linda_Norgrove&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Tributes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>James Boomgard, regional director for <a href="/wiki/Development_Alternatives_Incorporated" title="Development Alternatives Incorporated">Development Alternatives Inc.</a> (the company employing Norgrove when she was kidnapped), released a statement: "We are saddened beyond words by the death of a wonderful woman whose sole purpose in Afghanistan was to do good – to help the Afghan people achieve a measure of prosperity and stability in their everyday lives as they set about rebuilding their country".<sup id="cite_ref-GD2010-10-09_51-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GD2010-10-09-51">[51]</a></sup> United Nations Humanitarian Coordinator Robert Watkins praised Norgrove: "She was a true advocate for the people of Afghanistan and was dedicated to bringing improvements to their lives", and "her spirit and compassion will be greatly missed".<sup id="cite_ref-UN2010-10-10_52-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-UN2010-10-10-52">[52]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/First_Minister_of_Scotland" title="First Minister of Scotland">First Minister of Scotland</a> <a href="/wiki/Alex_Salmond" title="Alex Salmond">Alex Salmond</a> said: "Ms. Norgrove was a dedicated aid worker who was doing everything she could to help people in Afghanistan—hopefully that legacy of service in a humanitarian cause can be of some comfort to her loved ones in their time of grief".<sup id="cite_ref-GD2010-10-10_53-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GD2010-10-10-53">[53]</a></sup></p>
<p>Norgrove received the 2011 <a href="/wiki/Robert_Burns_Humanitarian_Award" title="Robert Burns Humanitarian Award">Robert Burns Humanitarian Award</a> posthumously for her work in Afghanistan.<sup id="cite_ref-BURNS2011-01-22_54-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BURNS2011-01-22-54">[54]</a></sup> Her family has established the Linda Norgrove Foundation to continue her relief work.<sup id="cite_ref-BBC2010-12-28_55-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BBC2010-12-28-55">[55]</a></sup></p>
