			<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Maria Sibylla Merian</h1>

<p><b>Maria Sibylla Merian</b> (2 April 1647&#160;– 13 January 1717) was a <a href="/wiki/German_people" class="mw-redirect" title="German people">German</a>-born <a href="/wiki/Naturalist" class="mw-redirect" title="Naturalist">naturalist</a> and scientific <a href="/wiki/Illustrator" title="Illustrator">illustrator</a>, a descendant of the <a href="/wiki/Frankfurt" title="Frankfurt">Frankfurt</a> branch of the Swiss <a href="/wiki/Merian_family" title="Merian family">Merian family</a>, founders of one of Europe's largest publishing houses in the 17th century.</p>
<p>Merian received her artistic training from her stepfather, <a href="/wiki/Jacob_Marrel" title="Jacob Marrel">Jacob Marrel</a>, a student of the <a href="/wiki/Still_life" title="Still life">still life</a> painter <a href="/wiki/Georg_Flegel" title="Georg Flegel">Georg Flegel</a>. She remained in Frankfurt until 1670, relocating subsequently to <a href="/wiki/Nuremberg" title="Nuremberg">Nuremberg</a>, the small village of <a href="/wiki/Wieuwerd" class="mw-redirect" title="Wieuwerd">Wieuwerd</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Dutch_Republic" title="Dutch Republic">Dutch Republic</a> (1685), where she stayed in a <a href="/wiki/Labadists" title="Labadists">Labadist</a> community till 1691, and <a href="/wiki/Amsterdam" title="Amsterdam">Amsterdam</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Merian published her first book of natural illustrations, titled <i>Neues Blumenbuch</i>, in 1675 at age 28.<sup id="cite_ref-wapo_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wapo-1">[1]</a></sup> In 1699, following eight years of painting and studying, and on the encouragement of <a href="/wiki/Cornelis_van_Aerssen_van_Sommelsdijck" title="Cornelis van Aerssen van Sommelsdijck">Cornelis van Aerssen van Sommelsdijck</a>, the then-governor of the Dutch colony of <a href="/wiki/Surinam_(Dutch_colony)" title="Surinam (Dutch colony)">Surinam</a>, the city of Amsterdam awarded Merian a grant to travel to South America with her daughter Dorothea.<sup id="cite_ref-guardian_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-guardian-2">[2]</a></sup> Her trip, designed as a scientific expedition makes Merian perhaps the first person to "plan a journey rooted solely in science."<sup id="cite_ref-:2_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:2-3">[3]</a></sup> After two years there, <a href="/wiki/Malaria" title="Malaria">malaria</a> forced her to return to Europe.<sup id="cite_ref-wapo_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wapo-1">[1]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-guardian_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-guardian-2">[2]</a></sup> She then proceeded to publish her major work, <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Metamorphosis_insectorum_Surinamensium&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Metamorphosis insectorum Surinamensium (page does not exist)">Metamorphosis insectorum Surinamensium</a><span class="noprint" style="font-size:85%; font-style: normal;">&#160;(<a href="https://de.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metamorphosis_insectorum_Surinamensium" class="extiw" title="de:Metamorphosis insectorum Surinamensium">de</a>)</span></i>, in 1705, for which she became famous. Because of her careful observations and documentation of the <a href="/wiki/Metamorphosis" title="Metamorphosis">metamorphosis</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Butterfly" title="Butterfly">butterfly</a>, she is considered by <a href="/wiki/David_Attenborough" title="David Attenborough">David Attenborough</a><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> to be among the most significant contributors to the field of <a href="/wiki/Entomology" title="Entomology">entomology</a>. She was a leading entomologist of her time and she discovered many new facts about insect life through her studies.<sup id="cite_ref-Handbuch4:35_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Handbuch4:35-5">[5]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life_and_early_career">Early life and early career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Maria_Sibylla_Merian&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life and early career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Maria_Sibylla_Merian_portrait_colors.jpeg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/74/Maria_Sibylla_Merian_portrait_colors.jpeg/170px-Maria_Sibylla_Merian_portrait_colors.jpeg" width="170" height="222" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/7/74/Maria_Sibylla_Merian_portrait_colors.jpeg 1.5x" data-file-width="184" data-file-height="240" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Maria_Sibylla_Merian_portrait_colors.jpeg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Maria Sibylla Merian in 1679</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Maria Sibylla Merian's father, the Swiss engraver and publisher <a href="/wiki/Matth%C3%A4us_Merian" title="Matthäus Merian">Matthäus Merian</a> the Elder, married her mother, his second wife, Johanna Sybilla Heyne, in 1646. Maria was born within the next year in 1647, making her his 9th child. Her father died in 1650, and in 1651 her mother remarried the flower and <a href="/wiki/Still_life" title="Still life">still life</a> painter <a href="/wiki/Jacob_Marrel" title="Jacob Marrel">Jacob Marrel</a>. Marrel encouraged Merian to draw and paint. While he lived mostly in Holland, his pupil <a href="/wiki/Abraham_Mignon" title="Abraham Mignon">Abraham Mignon</a> trained her. At the age of thirteen she painted her first images of insects and plants from specimens she had captured.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-6">[6]</a></sup> Early on, she had access to many books about <a href="/wiki/Natural_history" title="Natural history">natural history</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-:1_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-7">[7]</a></sup> Regarding her youth, in the foreword to <i>Metamorphosis insectorum Surinamensium</i>, Merian wrote: "I spent my time investigating insects. At the beginning, I started with silk worms in my home town of Frankfurt. I realized that other caterpillars produced beautiful butterflies or moths, and that <a href="/wiki/Silkworms" class="mw-redirect" title="Silkworms">silkworms</a> did the same. This led me to collect all the <a href="/wiki/Caterpillars" class="mw-redirect" title="Caterpillars">caterpillars</a> I could find in order to see how they changed".<sup id="cite_ref-foreword_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-foreword-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Merian_Portrait.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b3/Merian_Portrait.jpg/170px-Merian_Portrait.jpg" width="170" height="212" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b3/Merian_Portrait.jpg/255px-Merian_Portrait.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b3/Merian_Portrait.jpg/340px-Merian_Portrait.jpg 2x" data-file-width="784" data-file-height="978" /></a>

Maria Sibylla Merian c. 1700, <a href="/wiki/Etching" title="Etching">Copperplate</a> by <a href="/wiki/Jacobus_Houbraken" title="Jacobus Houbraken">Jacobus Houbraken</a> from a portrait by <a href="/wiki/Georg_Gsell" title="Georg Gsell">Georg Gsell</a></div>

<p>In May 1665, Merian married Marrel's apprentice, Johann Andreas Graff from <a href="/wiki/Nuremberg" title="Nuremberg">Nuremberg</a>; his father was a poet and director of the local high school, one of the leading schools in 17th-century Germany. In January 1668, she had her first child, <a href="/wiki/Johanna_Helena_Herolt" title="Johanna Helena Herolt">Johanna Helena</a>, and the family moved to Nuremberg in 1670, her husband's home town. While living there, Merian continued painting, working on parchment and linen, and creating designs for <a href="/wiki/Embroidery" title="Embroidery">embroidery</a>. She also gave drawing lessons to unmarried daughters of wealthy families (her "<i>Jungferncompaney</i>", i.e. virgin group), which helped her family financially and increased its social standing. This provided her with access to the finest gardens, maintained by the wealthy and elite, where she could continue collecting and documenting insects.<sup id="cite_ref-:1_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-7">[7]</a></sup> In 1678, she gave birth to her second daughter <a href="/wiki/Dorothea_Maria_Graff" title="Dorothea Maria Graff">Dorothea Maria</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> In 1679, she had published her first work on insects which was a two-volume, illustrated book focusing on insect <a href="/wiki/Metamorphosis" title="Metamorphosis">metamorphosis</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1678, the family had moved to <a href="/wiki/Frankfurt" title="Frankfurt">Frankfurt am Main</a>, but her marriage was an unhappy one.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_6-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-6">[6]</a></sup> She moved in with her mother, after her stepfather died in 1681. In 1683 she traveled to <a href="/wiki/Gottorp" class="mw-redirect" title="Gottorp">Gottorp</a> and was attracted to the <a href="/wiki/Labadists" title="Labadists">Labadists</a> community in <a href="/wiki/Holstein" title="Holstein">Holstein</a>. In 1685 Maria traveled with her mother, husband and children to <a href="/wiki/Friesland" title="Friesland">Friesland</a> where her half-brother Caspar Merian had lived since 1677.</p>

<p>After <a href="/wiki/Jean_de_Labadie" title="Jean de Labadie">Jean de Labadie</a> had died, Pierre Yvon moved the community to a stately home – Walt(h)a Castle – at <a href="/wiki/Wieuwerd" class="mw-redirect" title="Wieuwerd">Wieuwerd</a> in Friesland, which belonged to three sisters <a href="/wiki/Van_Aerssen_van_Sommelsdijck" class="mw-redirect" title="Van Aerssen van Sommelsdijck">Van Aerssen van Sommelsdijck</a>, who were his adherents. Here, printing and many other occupations continued, including farming and milling.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>At its peak, the <a href="/wiki/Religious_community" title="Religious community">religious community</a> numbered around 600 with many more adherents further afield. Visitors came from England, Italy, Poland and elsewhere, but not all approved of the strict <a href="/wiki/Discipline" title="Discipline">discipline</a>, <a href="/wiki/Separatism" title="Separatism">separatism</a> and <a href="/wiki/Community_property" title="Community property">community property</a>. Those of arrogant disposition were given the most menial of jobs. Fussiness in matters of food was overcome since all were expected to eat what was put in front of them. It seems the community had problems with Merian's husband; he was refused but came back twice.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>Several noted visitors have left their accounts of visits to the Labadist community. One was <a href="/wiki/Sophia_of_Hanover" title="Sophia of Hanover">Sophie of Hanover</a>, mother of <a href="/wiki/King_George_I_of_Great_Britain" class="mw-redirect" title="King George I of Great Britain">King George I of Great Britain</a>; another was <a href="/wiki/William_Penn" title="William Penn">William Penn</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Quaker" class="mw-redirect" title="Quaker">Quaker</a> pioneer, who gave his name to the US state of <a href="/wiki/Pennsylvania" title="Pennsylvania">Pennsylvania</a>; a third was the English philosopher <a href="/wiki/John_Locke" title="John Locke">John Locke</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> In 1690 her mother died.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Amsterdam">Amsterdam</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Maria_Sibylla_Merian&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Amsterdam">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1691, she moved with her daughters to <a href="/wiki/Amsterdam" title="Amsterdam">Amsterdam</a> and met with <a href="/wiki/Agnes_Block" title="Agnes Block">Agnes Block</a>, <a href="/wiki/Caspar_Commelin" class="mw-redirect" title="Caspar Commelin">Caspar Commelin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Michiel_van_Musscher" title="Michiel van Musscher">Michiel van Musscher</a> and <a href="/wiki/Steven_Blankaart" title="Steven Blankaart">Steven Blankaart</a>. In 1692, her husband divorced from her. In Amsterdam the same year, her daughter Johanna married Jakob Hendrik Herolt, a successful merchant on Surinam, originally from <a href="/wiki/Bacharach" title="Bacharach">Bacharach</a>. The flower painter <a href="/wiki/Rachel_Ruysch" title="Rachel Ruysch">Rachel Ruysch</a> became her pupil.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1699, the city of Amsterdam granted Merian permission to travel to <a href="/wiki/Suriname" title="Suriname">Suriname</a> in South America, along with her younger daughter <a href="/wiki/Dorothea_Maria_Graff" title="Dorothea Maria Graff">Dorothea Maria</a>. On 10 July their ship set sail. The goal of the mission was to spend five years illustrating new species of insects.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_6-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-6">[6]</a></sup> In order to finance the mission, she sold 255 of her own paintings.<sup id="cite_ref-:2_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:2-3">[3]</a></sup> Before departing, she wrote:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Holland" title="Holland">Holland</a>, with much astonishment what beautiful animals came from the East and <a href="/wiki/West_Indies" title="West Indies">West Indies</a>. I was blessed with having been able to look at both the expensive collection of Doctor <a href="/wiki/Nicolaes_Witsen" title="Nicolaes Witsen">Nicolaas Witsen</a>, mayor of Amsterdam and director of the East Indies society, and that of Mr. <a href="/w/index.php?title=Jonas_Witsen&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Jonas Witsen (page does not exist)">Jonas Witsen</a>, secretary of Amsterdam. Moreover, I also saw the collections of Mr. <a href="/wiki/Frederick_Ruysch" class="mw-redirect" title="Frederick Ruysch">Fredericus Ruysch</a>, doctor of medicine and professor of anatomy and botany, Mr. <a href="/wiki/Levinus_Vincent" title="Levinus Vincent">Livinus Vincent</a>, and many other people. In these collections I had found innumerable other insects, but finally if here their origin and their reproduction is unknown, it begs the question[sic] as to how they transform, starting from caterpillars and chrysalises and so on. All this has, at the same time, led me to undertake a long dreamed of journey to Suriname.<sup id="cite_ref-foreword_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-foreword-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<div style="clear:both;"></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Suriname">Suriname</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Maria_Sibylla_Merian&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Suriname">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Esther_Barbara_von_Sandrart.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7c/Esther_Barbara_von_Sandrart.jpg/170px-Esther_Barbara_von_Sandrart.jpg" width="170" height="226" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7c/Esther_Barbara_von_Sandrart.jpg/255px-Esther_Barbara_von_Sandrart.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7c/Esther_Barbara_von_Sandrart.jpg/340px-Esther_Barbara_von_Sandrart.jpg 2x" data-file-width="451" data-file-height="600" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">

This portrait of <a href="/wiki/Joachim_von_Sandrart" title="Joachim von Sandrart">Esther Barbara von Sandrart</a> (Esther Barbara Bloemart (1651–1731 or 1733)<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> is often misidentified as a portrait of Merian</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Merian arrived on 18/19 September in Surinam, and met with the governor <a href="/wiki/Paulus_van_der_Veen" title="Paulus van der Veen">Paulus van der Veen</a>. She worked for two years,<sup id="cite_ref-de_Bray_2001.2C_p._48_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-de_Bray_2001.2C_p._48-16">[16]</a></sup> traveling around the colony and sketching local animals and plants. She criticized Dutch planters' treatment of natives and black slaves.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> She recorded local native names for the plants and described local uses. In June 1701, <a href="/wiki/Malaria" title="Malaria">malaria</a> forced her to return to the <a href="/wiki/Dutch_Republic" title="Dutch Republic">Dutch Republic</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_6-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>Despite her criticism of planters' treatment of slaves, Merian herself owned slaves. Ship lists indicate that she was master to an "Indian woman" who helped to collect specimens while in Suriname. It is speculated that Merian actually owned two "Indian" slaves, but only one returned to Amsterdam with her.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>Back in the Netherlands, Merian lived in Kerkstraat, where she opened a shop. Merian sold specimens she had collected and published a collection of engravings of plant and animal life in Suriname. In 1705, she published a book <i>Metamorphosis Insectorum Surinamensium</i> about the insects of Suriname.<sup id="cite_ref-Handbuch4:35_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Handbuch4:35-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1715, Merian suffered a <a href="/wiki/Stroke" title="Stroke">stroke</a> and was partially paralysed. She continued her work, but her illness probably affected her ability to work. A later registry lists her as a <a href="/wiki/Pauper" class="mw-redirect" title="Pauper">pauper</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Maria Sibylla Merian died in Amsterdam on 13 January 1717 and was buried four days later. Her daughter Dorothea published <i>Erucarum Ortus Alimentum et Paradoxa Metamorphosis</i>, a collection of her mother's work, posthumously.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Modern_appreciation">Modern appreciation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Maria_Sibylla_Merian&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Modern appreciation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the last quarter of the 20th century, the work of Merian was re-evaluated, validated, and reprinted.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> Her portrait was printed on the <a href="/wiki/Deutsche_Mark" title="Deutsche Mark">500 DM note</a> before Germany converted to the <a href="/wiki/Euro" title="Euro">euro</a>. Her portrait has also appeared on a 0.40 DM stamp, released on 17 September 1987, and many schools are named after her. In the late 1980s <a href="/wiki/Archiv" class="mw-redirect" title="Archiv">Archiv</a> imprint of the <a href="/wiki/Polydor" class="mw-redirect" title="Polydor">Polydor</a> label issued a series of new recordings of the piano works of <a href="/wiki/Wolfgang_Amadeus_Mozart" title="Wolfgang Amadeus Mozart">Wolfgang Amadeus Mozart</a> performed on period instruments by <a href="/wiki/Malcolm_Bilson" title="Malcolm Bilson">Malcolm Bilson</a> (fortepiano), with the <a href="/wiki/English_Baroque_Soloists" title="English Baroque Soloists">English Baroque Soloists</a> conducted by <a href="/wiki/John_Eliot_Gardiner" title="John Eliot Gardiner">John Eliot Gardiner</a>; the original cover design of each of the individual discs in the series, and the original boxed set of the complete piano concertos (as well as a disc of Mozart piano quartets) all featured Merian's floral illustrations. In 2005, a modern <a href="/wiki/Research_vessel" title="Research vessel">research vessel</a> named <a href="/wiki/RV_Maria_S._Merian" title="RV Maria S. Merian">Maria S. Merian</a> was launched at <a href="/wiki/Warnem%C3%BCnde" title="Warnemünde">Warnemünde</a>, Germany. She was honored with a <a href="/wiki/Google_Doodle" title="Google Doodle">Google Doodle</a> on 2 April 2013 to mark her 366th birth anniversary.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2016, Merian's <i>Metamorphosis insectorum Surinamensium</i> was re-published with updated scientific descriptions and, in June 2017, a symposium will be held in her honor in Amsterdam.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Work">Work</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Maria_Sibylla_Merian&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Work">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>Merian worked as a <a href="/wiki/Botanical_artist" class="mw-redirect" title="Botanical artist">botanical artist</a>. She published collections of <a href="/wiki/Engravings" class="mw-redirect" title="Engravings">engravings</a> of plants in 1675, 1677, and 1680. She collected and observed live insects and created detailed drawings to illustrate insect metamorphosis. In her time, it was very unusual that someone would be genuinely interested in insects, which had a bad reputation and were considered "vile and disgusting."<sup id="cite_ref-:0_6-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-6">[6]</a></sup> As a consequence of their reputation, the metamorphosis of these animals was largely unknown. Merian described the life cycles of 186 insect species, amassing evidence that contradicted the contemporary notion that insects were "born of mud" by <a href="/wiki/Spontaneous_generation" title="Spontaneous generation">spontaneous generation</a>. Moreover, although certain scholars were aware of the process of metamorphosis from the caterpillar to the butterfly, the majority of people did not understand the process.</p>
<p>Merian's process of creating her art used <a href="/wiki/Vellum" title="Vellum"><i>vellum charta non nata</i></a> which she primed with a white coat.<sup id="cite_ref-:3_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:3-23">[23]</a></sup> Because of the <a href="/wiki/Guild_system" class="mw-redirect" title="Guild system">guild system</a> in Europe, women were not allowed to paint in <a href="/wiki/Oil_paint" title="Oil paint">oil</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-:2_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:2-3">[3]</a></sup> Merian painted with <a href="/wiki/Watercolor_painting" title="Watercolor painting">watercolors</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gouache" title="Gouache">gouache</a>, instead.<sup id="cite_ref-:3_23-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:3-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>The work that Maria Sibylla Merian published, <i>Der Raupen wunderbare Verwandlung und sonderbare Blumennahrung&#160;– The Caterpillars' Marvelous Transformation and Strange Floral Food</i>, was very popular in certain segments of <a href="/wiki/Aristocracy" title="Aristocracy">high society</a> as a result of being published in the <a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">vernacular</a>. However, her work was largely ignored by scientists of the time because the official language of science was still <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a>.</p>
<p>Merian also described many other details of the evolution and life cycle of the insects she observed. For example, she showed that each stage of the change from caterpillar to butterfly depended on a small number of plants for its nourishment. She noted that as a consequence, the eggs were laid near these plants.<sup id="cite_ref-:1_7-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Merian was one of the first naturalists to observe insects directly.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_6-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-6">[6]</a></sup> This approach gave her much more insight into their lives and was contrary to the way that most scientists worked at the time. Today, while Merian has experienced reinvigorated fame in the eyes of the art and science communities, much of her work has finally been accurately reattributed to her daughters, Dorothea and Johanna. <a href="/wiki/Johanna_Helena_Herolt" title="Johanna Helena Herolt">Joanna Helena Herolt</a> often did not receive credit for her artwork because they were created in collaboration with her mother. Today her works are in the process of recovery. Similarly, <a href="/wiki/Dorothea_Maria_Graff" title="Dorothea Maria Graff">Dorothea Maria Graff</a>'s images have only recently been accredited to the German painter: Sam Segal has reattributed 30 or 91 folios in the <a href="/wiki/British_Museum" title="British Museum">British Museum</a>.</p>
<p>The pursuit of her work in <a href="/wiki/Suriname" title="Suriname">Suriname</a> was an unusual endeavor, especially for a woman. In general, only men received royal or government funding to travel in the <a href="/wiki/Colonies" class="mw-redirect" title="Colonies">colonies</a> to find new species of plants and animals, make collections and work there, or settle. Scientific expeditions at this period of time were not common, and Merian's unofficial, self-funded expedition raised many eyebrows. She succeeded, however, in discovering a whole range of previously unknown animals and plants in the interior of Surinam. Merian spent time studying and classifying her findings and described them in great detail. She not only described the insects she found, but also noted their habitat, habits and uses to <a href="/wiki/Indigenous_peoples" title="Indigenous peoples">indigenous people</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-:1_7-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-7">[7]</a></sup> Her classification of butterflies and moths is still relevant today. She used <a href="/wiki/Indigenous_peoples_of_the_Americas" title="Indigenous peoples of the Americas">Native American</a> names to refer to the plants, which became used in Europe:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>I created the first classification for all the insects which had chrysalises, the daytime butterflies and the nighttime moths. The second classification is that of the maggots, worms, flies, and bees. I retained the indigenous names of the plants, because they were still in use in America by both the locals and the Indians.<sup id="cite_ref-foreword_8-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-foreword-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>Merian's drawings of plants, frogs,<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> snakes, spiders, iguanas, and tropical beetles are still collected today by amateurs all over the world. The German word <i>Vogelspinne</i>—(a spider of the infraorder <a href="/wiki/Mygalomorphae" title="Mygalomorphae">Mygalomorphae</a>), translated literally as <i>bird spider</i>—probably has its origins in an engraving by Maria Sibylla Merian. The engraving, created from sketches drawn in Surinam, shows a large spider who had just captured a bird. In the same engraving and accompanying text Merian was the first European to describe both army ants and leaf cutter ants as well as their effect on other organisms.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<p>Shortly before Merian's death, her work was seen in Amsterdam by <a href="/wiki/Peter_the_Great" title="Peter the Great">Peter the Great</a>. After her death, he acquired a significant number of her paintings<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> which to this day are kept in academic collections in St. Petersburg.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Eponyms">Eponyms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Maria_Sibylla_Merian&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Eponyms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A species of South American lizard, <i><a href="/wiki/Salvator_merianae" class="mw-redirect" title="Salvator merianae">Salvator merianae</a></i>, is named in her honor.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
