			<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Black Death</h1>
									<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
									<div id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
								<div id="contentSub"></div>
												<div id="jump-to-nav" class="mw-jump">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>
				</div>

<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Blackdeath2.gif" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Spread of the Black Death in Europe (1346–1353)</div>
</div>

<p>The <b>Black Death</b> was one of the most devastating <a href="/wiki/Pandemic" title="Pandemic">pandemics</a> in <a href="/wiki/History_of_the_world" title="History of the world">human history</a>, resulting in the deaths of an estimated <span class="nowrap">75 to 200 million</span> people in Eurasia and peaking in <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> in the years 1346–1353.<sup id="cite_ref-ABC.2FReuters_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ABC.2FReuters-1">[1]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Health._De-coding_the_Black_Death_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Health._De-coding_the_Black_Death-2">[2]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Black_Death.27s_Gene_Code_Cracked_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Black_Death.27s_Gene_Code_Cracked-3">[3]</a></sup> Although there were several competing theories as to the cause of the Black Death, analyses of <a href="/wiki/DNA" title="DNA">DNA</a> from people in northern and southern Europe published in 2010 and 2011 indicate that the <a href="/wiki/Pathogen" title="Pathogen">pathogen</a> responsible was the <i><a href="/wiki/Yersinia_pestis" title="Yersinia pestis">Yersinia pestis</a></i> <a href="/wiki/Bacterium" class="mw-redirect" title="Bacterium">bacterium</a>, resulting in several forms of <a href="/wiki/Plague_(disease)" title="Plague (disease)">plague</a>, including the <a href="/wiki/Bubonic_plague" title="Bubonic plague">bubonic plague</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-nature_bos_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nature_bos-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Black Death is thought to have originated in the arid plains of <a href="/wiki/Central_Asia" title="Central Asia">Central Asia</a>, where it then travelled along the <a href="/wiki/Silk_Road" title="Silk Road">Silk Road</a>, reaching <a href="/wiki/Crimea" title="Crimea">Crimea</a> by 1343.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> From there, it was most likely carried by <a href="/wiki/Oriental_rat_flea" title="Oriental rat flea">Oriental rat fleas</a> living on the <a href="/wiki/Black_rat" title="Black rat">black rats</a> that were regular passengers on <a href="/wiki/Cargo_ship" title="Cargo ship">merchant ships</a>. Spreading throughout the <a href="/wiki/Mediterranean_Basin" title="Mediterranean Basin">Mediterranean</a> and Europe, the Black Death is estimated to have killed 30–60% of <a href="/wiki/Demographics_of_Europe" title="Demographics of Europe">Europe's total population</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> In total, the plague may have reduced the <a href="/wiki/World_population" title="World population">world population</a> from an estimated 450&#160;million down to 350–375 million in the 14th century.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> The world population as a whole did not recover to pre-plague levels until the 17th century.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> The plague recurred occasionally in Europe until the 19th century.</p>
<p>The plague created a series of religious, social, and economic upheavals, which had profound effects on the course of <a href="/wiki/History_of_Europe" title="History of Europe">European history</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Chronology">Chronology</span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Origins_of_the_disease">Origins of the disease</span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Black_Death_migration" title="Black Death migration">Black Death migration</a></div>
<p>The plague disease, caused by <i><a href="/wiki/Yersinia_pestis" title="Yersinia pestis">Yersinia pestis</a></i>, is <a href="/wiki/Enzootic" title="Enzootic">enzootic</a> (commonly present) in populations of fleas carried by ground <a href="/wiki/Rodent" title="Rodent">rodents</a>, including <a href="/wiki/Marmot" title="Marmot">marmots</a>, in various areas including <a href="/wiki/Central_Asia" title="Central Asia">Central Asia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kurdistan" title="Kurdistan">Kurdistan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Western_Asia" title="Western Asia">Western Asia</a>, <a href="/wiki/North_India" title="North India">Northern India</a> and <a href="/wiki/Uganda" title="Uganda">Uganda</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEZiegler199825_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEZiegler199825-10">[10]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Nestorianism" title="Nestorianism">Nestorian</a> graves dating to 1338–1339 near <a href="/wiki/Issyk_Kul" class="mw-redirect" title="Issyk Kul">Lake Issyk Kul</a> in <a href="/wiki/Kyrgyzstan" title="Kyrgyzstan">Kyrgyzstan</a> have inscriptions referring to plague and are thought by many epidemiologists to mark the outbreak of the epidemic, from which it could easily have spread to China and India.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> In October 2010, medical geneticists suggested that all three of the great waves of the plague originated in China.<sup id="cite_ref-china_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-china-12">[12]</a></sup> In China, the 13th-century <a href="/wiki/Mongol_invasion_of_China" class="mw-redirect" title="Mongol invasion of China">Mongol conquest</a> caused a decline in farming and trading. However, economic recovery had been observed at the beginning of the 14th&#160;century. In the 1330s, a large number of natural disasters and plagues led to widespread famine, starting in 1331, with a deadly plague arriving soon after.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> Epidemics that may have included plague killed an estimated 25&#160;million Chinese and other Asians during the 15&#160;years before it reached <a href="/wiki/Constantinople" title="Constantinople">Constantinople</a> in 1347.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>The disease may have travelled along the <a href="/wiki/Silk_Road" title="Silk Road">Silk Road</a> with <a href="/wiki/Mongols" title="Mongols">Mongol</a> armies and traders or it could have come via ship.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> By the end of 1346, reports of plague had reached the seaports of Europe: "India was depopulated, <a href="/wiki/Tartary" title="Tartary">Tartary</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mesopotamia" title="Mesopotamia">Mesopotamia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Syria" title="Syria">Syria</a>, <a href="/wiki/Armenia" title="Armenia">Armenia</a> were covered with dead bodies".<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>Plague was reportedly first introduced to Europe via <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Genoa" title="Republic of Genoa">Genoese</a> traders at the port city of <a href="/wiki/Feodosia" title="Feodosia">Kaffa</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Crimea" title="Crimea">Crimea</a> in 1347. After a protracted siege, during which the Mongol army under <a href="/wiki/Jani_Beg" title="Jani Beg">Jani Beg</a> was suffering from the disease, the army catapulted the infected corpses over the <a href="/wiki/Defensive_wall" title="Defensive wall">city walls</a> of Kaffa to infect the inhabitants. The Genoese traders fled, taking the plague by ship into <a href="/wiki/Sicily" title="Sicily">Sicily</a> and the south of Europe, whence it spread north.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> Whether or not this hypothesis is accurate, it is clear that several existing conditions such as war, famine, and weather contributed to the severity of the Black Death.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="European_outbreak">European outbreak</span></h3>
<div class="quotebox" style="float: right; clear: right; margin: 0.5em 0 0.8em 1.4em; width:30%; padding: 10px; border: 1px solid #aaa; font-size: 88%; background-color: #F9F9F9;">
<div class="" style="position: relative; text-align: left;">
<div>The seventh year after it began, it came to England and first began in the towns and ports joining on the seacoasts, in Dorsetshire, where, as in other counties, it made the country quite void of inhabitants so that there were almost none left alive.<br />
... But at length it came to Gloucester, yea even to Oxford and to London, and finally it spread over all England and so wasted the people that scarce the tenth person of any sort was left alive.</div>
</div>
<cite style="display: block; text-align: left;"><a href="/wiki/Geoffrey_the_Baker" title="Geoffrey the Baker">Geoffrey the Baker</a>, <i>Chronicon Angliae</i></cite></div>
<p>There appear to have been several introductions into Europe. The plague reached Sicily in October 1347, carried by twelve Genoese galleys,<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> and rapidly spread all over the island. Galleys from <a href="/wiki/Feodosia" title="Feodosia">Kaffa</a> reached Genoa and Venice in January 1348, but it was the outbreak in <a href="/wiki/Pisa" title="Pisa">Pisa</a> a few weeks later that was the entry point to northern Italy. Towards the end of January, one of the galleys expelled from Italy arrived in <a href="/wiki/Marseille" title="Marseille">Marseille</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>From Italy, the disease spread northwest across Europe, striking France, Spain, Portugal and England by June 1348, then turned and spread east through Germany and Scandinavia from 1348 to 1350. It was introduced in Norway in 1349 when a ship landed at <a href="/wiki/Ask%C3%B8y" title="Askøy">Askøy</a>, then spread to Bjørgvin (modern <a href="/wiki/Bergen" title="Bergen">Bergen</a>) and <a href="/wiki/Iceland" title="Iceland">Iceland</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> Finally it spread to northwestern Russia in 1351. The plague was somewhat less common in parts of Europe that had smaller trade relations with their neighbours, including the majority of the <a href="/wiki/Basque_Country_(greater_region)" title="Basque Country (greater region)">Basque Country</a>, isolated parts of Belgium and the Netherlands, and isolated alpine villages throughout the continent.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>Modern researchers do not think that the plague ever became endemic in Europe or its rat population. The disease repeatedly wiped out the rodent carriers so that the fleas died out until a new outbreak from Central Asia repeated the process. The outbreaks have been shown to occur roughly 15 years after a warmer and wetter period in areas where plague is endemic in other species such as <a href="/wiki/Gerbils" class="mw-redirect" title="Gerbils">gerbils</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Middle_Eastern_outbreak">Middle Eastern outbreak</span></h3>
<p>The plague struck various regions in the Middle East during the <a href="/wiki/Pandemic" title="Pandemic">pandemic</a>, leading to serious depopulation and permanent change in both economic and social structures. As it spread to western Europe, the disease entered the region from southern Russia also. By autumn 1347, the plague reached <a href="/wiki/Alexandria" title="Alexandria">Alexandria</a> in Egypt, probably through the port's trade with <a href="/wiki/Constantinople" title="Constantinople">Constantinople</a>, and ports on the <a href="/wiki/Black_Sea" title="Black Sea">Black Sea</a>. During 1347, the disease travelled eastward to <a href="/wiki/Gaza_City" title="Gaza City">Gaza</a>, and north along the eastern coast to cities in <a href="/wiki/Lebanon" title="Lebanon">Lebanon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Syria" title="Syria">Syria</a> and <a href="/wiki/Palestine_(region)" title="Palestine (region)">Palestine</a>, including <a href="/wiki/Ashkelon" title="Ashkelon">Ashkelon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Acre,_Israel" title="Acre, Israel">Acre</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jerusalem" title="Jerusalem">Jerusalem</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sidon" title="Sidon">Sidon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Damascus" title="Damascus">Damascus</a>, <a href="/wiki/Homs" title="Homs">Homs</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Aleppo" title="Aleppo">Aleppo</a>. In 1348–1349, the disease reached <a href="/wiki/Antioch" title="Antioch">Antioch</a>. The city's residents fled to the north, most of them dying during the journey, but the infection had been spread to the people of <a href="/wiki/Asia_Minor" class="mw-redirect" title="Asia Minor">Asia Minor</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Mecca" title="Mecca">Mecca</a> became infected in 1349. During the same year, records show the city of <a href="/wiki/Mosul" title="Mosul">Mawsil</a> (Mosul) suffered a massive epidemic, and the city of <a href="/wiki/Baghdad" title="Baghdad">Baghdad</a> experienced a second round of the disease. In 1351 <a href="/wiki/Yemen" title="Yemen">Yemen</a> experienced an outbreak of the plague, coinciding with the return of <a href="/wiki/Rasulid_dynasty" title="Rasulid dynasty">Sultan al-Mujahid Ali</a> of Yemen from imprisonment in <a href="/wiki/Cairo" title="Cairo">Cairo</a>. His party may have brought the disease with them from Egypt.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Signs_and_symptoms">Signs and symptoms</span></h2>

An inguinal bubo on the upper thigh of a person infected with bubonic plague. Swollen lymph glands (<i>buboes</i>) often occur in the neck, armpit and groin (<i>inguinal</i>) regions of plague victims.</div>

<p>Contemporary accounts of the plague are often varied or imprecise. The most commonly noted symptom was the appearance of <a href="/wiki/Bubo" title="Bubo">buboes</a> (or gavocciolos) in the groin, the neck and armpits, which oozed pus and bled when opened.<sup id="cite_ref-Byrne2004pp21-9_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Byrne2004pp21-9-26">[26]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Boccaccio" class="mw-redirect" title="Boccaccio">Boccaccio</a>'s description is graphic:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>In men and women alike it first betrayed itself by the emergence of certain tumours in the groin or armpits, some of which grew as large as a common apple, others as an egg...From the two said parts of the body this deadly gavocciolo soon began to propagate and spread itself in all directions indifferently; after which the form of the malady began to change, black spots or livid making their appearance in many cases on the arm or the thigh or elsewhere, now few and large, now minute and numerous. As the gavocciolo had been and still was an infallible token of approaching death, such also were these spots on whomsoever they showed themselves.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>The only medical detail that is questionable in Boccaccio's description is that the gavocciolo was an 'infallible token of approaching death', as, if the bubo discharges, recovery is possible.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEZiegler199818.E2.80.9319_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEZiegler199818.E2.80.9319-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<p>This was followed by acute fever and vomiting of blood. Most victims died two to seven days after initial infection. Freckle-like spots and rashes,<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> which could have been caused by flea-bites, were identified as another potential sign of the plague.</p>
<p>Some accounts, like that of <a href="/wiki/Lodewijk_Heyligen" title="Lodewijk Heyligen">Lodewijk Heyligen</a>, whose master the <a href="/wiki/Giovanni_Colonna_(cardinal,_1295%E2%80%931348)" title="Giovanni Colonna (cardinal, 1295–1348)">Cardinal Colonna</a> died of the plague in 1348, noted a distinct form of the disease that infected the lungs and led to respiratory problems<sup id="cite_ref-Byrne2004pp21-9_26-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Byrne2004pp21-9-26">[26]</a></sup> and is identified with <a href="/wiki/Pneumonic_plague" title="Pneumonic plague">pneumonic plague</a>.</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>It is said that the plague takes three forms. In the first people suffer an infection of the lungs, which leads to breathing difficulties. Whoever has this corruption or contamination to any extent cannot escape but will die within two days. Another form...in which boils erupt under the armpits,...a third form in which people of both sexes are attacked in the groin.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>

<p>Medical knowledge had stagnated during the <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">Middle Ages</a>. The most authoritative account at the time came from the medical faculty in Paris in a report to the <a href="/wiki/Philip_VI_of_France" title="Philip VI of France">king of France</a> that blamed the heavens, in the form of a <a href="/wiki/Conjunction_(astronomy)" title="Conjunction (astronomy)">conjunction</a> of three planets in 1345 that caused a "great pestilence in the air".<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHorrox1994159_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHorrox1994159-32">[32]</a></sup> This report became the first and most widely circulated of a series of plague tracts that sought to give advice to sufferers. That the plague was caused by bad air became the most widely accepted theory. Today, this is known as the <a href="/wiki/Miasma_theory" title="Miasma theory">miasma theory</a>. The word <i>plague</i> had no special significance at this time, and only the recurrence of outbreaks during the Middle Ages gave it the name that has become the medical term.</p>
<p>The importance of hygiene was recognised only in the nineteenth century; until then it was common that the streets were filthy, with live animals of all sorts around and human parasites abounding. A transmissible disease will spread easily in such conditions. One development as a result of the Black Death was the establishment of the idea of <a href="/wiki/Quarantine" title="Quarantine">quarantine</a> in <a href="/wiki/Dubrovnik" title="Dubrovnik">Dubrovnik</a> in 1377 after continuing outbreaks.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<p>The dominant explanation for the Black Death is the <a href="/wiki/Plague_(disease)" title="Plague (disease)">plague</a> theory, which attributes the outbreak to <i><a href="/wiki/Yersinia_pestis" title="Yersinia pestis">Yersinia pestis</a></i>, also responsible for an epidemic that began in southern China in 1865, eventually spreading to India. The investigation of the pathogen that caused the 19th-century plague was begun by teams of scientists who visited Hong Kong in 1894, among whom was the French-Swiss bacteriologist <a href="/wiki/Alexandre_Yersin" title="Alexandre Yersin">Alexandre Yersin</a>, after whom the pathogen was named <i>Yersinia pestis</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-Chritakos2005_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Chritakos2005-34">[34]</a></sup> The mechanism by which <i>Y. pestis</i> was usually transmitted was established in 1898 by <a href="/wiki/Paul-Louis_Simond" title="Paul-Louis Simond">Paul-Louis Simond</a> and was found to involve the bites of fleas whose <a href="/wiki/Midgut" title="Midgut">midguts</a> had become obstructed by replicating <i>Y. pestis</i> several days after feeding on an infected host. This blockage results in starvation and aggressive feeding behaviour by the fleas, which repeatedly attempt to clear their blockage by <a href="/wiki/Vomiting" title="Vomiting">regurgitation</a>, resulting in thousands of plague bacteria being flushed into the feeding site, infecting the host. The bubonic plague mechanism was also dependent on two populations of rodents: one resistant to the disease, which act as hosts, keeping the disease <a href="/wiki/Endemic_(epidemiology)" title="Endemic (epidemiology)">endemic</a>, and a second that lack resistance. When the second population dies, the fleas move on to other hosts, including people, thus creating a human <a href="/wiki/Epidemic_typhus" title="Epidemic typhus">epidemic</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Chritakos2005_34-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Chritakos2005-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
<p>The historian <a href="/wiki/Francis_Aidan_Gasquet" title="Francis Aidan Gasquet">Francis Aidan Gasquet</a> wrote about the Great Pestilence in 1893<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGasquet1893_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGasquet1893-35">[35]</a></sup> and suggested that "it would appear to be some form of the ordinary Eastern or bubonic plague". He was able to adopt the epidemiology of the bubonic plague for the Black Death for the second edition in 1908, implicating rats and fleas in the process, and his interpretation was widely accepted for other ancient and medieval epidemics, such as the <a href="/wiki/Justinian_plague" class="mw-redirect" title="Justinian plague">Justinian plague</a> that was prevalent in the <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Roman_Empire" class="mw-redirect" title="Eastern Roman Empire">Eastern Roman Empire</a> from 541 to 700&#160;CE.<sup id="cite_ref-Chritakos2005_34-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Chritakos2005-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
<p>An estimate of the <a href="/wiki/Mortality_rate" title="Mortality rate">mortality rate</a> for the modern <a href="/wiki/Bubonic_plague" title="Bubonic plague">bubonic plague</a>, following the introduction of antibiotics, is 11% (by the US Centers for Disease Control (CDC)), although it may be much higher in economically or medically underdeveloped regions.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> Symptoms of the disease include fever of 38–41&#160;°C (100–106&#160;°F), headaches, painful aching joints, <a href="/wiki/Nausea" title="Nausea">nausea</a> and vomiting, and a general feeling of <a href="/wiki/Malaise" title="Malaise">malaise</a>. Left untreated, of those that contract the bubonic plague, 80 per cent die within eight days.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Pneumonic_plague" title="Pneumonic plague">Pneumonic plague</a> has a mortality rate of 90 to 95 per cent. Symptoms include fever, cough, and blood-tinged <a href="/wiki/Sputum" title="Sputum">sputum</a>. As the disease progresses, sputum becomes free-flowing and bright red. <a href="/wiki/Septicemic_plague" title="Septicemic plague">Septicemic plague</a> is the least common of the three forms, with a mortality rate near 100%. Symptoms are high fevers and purple skin patches (<a href="/wiki/Purpura" title="Purpura">purpura</a> due to <a href="/wiki/Disseminated_intravascular_coagulation" title="Disseminated intravascular coagulation">disseminated intravascular coagulation</a>). In cases of pneumonic and particularly septicemic plague, the progress of the disease is so rapid that there would often be no time for the development of the enlarged lymph nodes that were noted as buboes.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEByrne20048_38-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEByrne20048-38">[38]</a></sup></p>
<p>A number of alternative theories – implicating other diseases in the Black Death pandemic – have also been proposed by some modern scientists (see below – "Alternative Explanations").</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="DNA_evidence">DNA evidence</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Bubonic_plague_victims-mass_grave_in_Martigues,_France_1720-1721.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1b/Bubonic_plague_victims-mass_grave_in_Martigues%2C_France_1720-1721.jpg/220px-Bubonic_plague_victims-mass_grave_in_Martigues%2C_France_1720-1721.jpg" width="220" height="164" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1b/Bubonic_plague_victims-mass_grave_in_Martigues%2C_France_1720-1721.jpg/330px-Bubonic_plague_victims-mass_grave_in_Martigues%2C_France_1720-1721.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1b/Bubonic_plague_victims-mass_grave_in_Martigues%2C_France_1720-1721.jpg/440px-Bubonic_plague_victims-mass_grave_in_Martigues%2C_France_1720-1721.jpg 2x" data-file-width="600" data-file-height="447" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Bubonic_plague_victims-mass_grave_in_Martigues,_France_1720-1721.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Skeletons in a mass grave from 1720–1721 in <a href="/wiki/Martigues" title="Martigues">Martigues</a>, France, yielded molecular evidence of the <i>orientalis</i> strain of <i>Yersinia pestis</i>, the organism responsible for bubonic plague. The second pandemic of bubonic plague was active in Europe from AD&#160;1347, the beginning of the Black Death, until 1750.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>In October 2010, the open-access scientific journal <i><a href="/wiki/PLoS_Pathogens" class="mw-redirect" title="PLoS Pathogens">PLoS Pathogens</a></i> published a paper by a multinational team who undertook a new investigation into the role of <i><a href="/wiki/Yersinia_pestis" title="Yersinia pestis">Yersinia pestis</a></i> in the Black Death following the disputed identification by Drancourt and Raoult in 1998. They assessed the presence of DNA/RNA with <a href="/wiki/Polymerase_chain_reaction" title="Polymerase chain reaction">polymerase chain reaction</a> (PCR) techniques for <i>Y. pestis</i> from the tooth sockets in human skeletons from mass graves in northern, central and southern Europe that were associated archaeologically with the Black Death and subsequent resurgences. The authors concluded that this new research, together with prior analyses from the south of France and Germany,<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> "ends the debate about the cause of the Black Death, and unambiguously demonstrates that <i>Y. pestis</i> was the causative agent of the epidemic plague that devastated Europe during the Middle Ages".<sup id="cite_ref-Haensch2010_40-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Haensch2010-40">[40]</a></sup></p>
<p>The study also found that there were two previously unknown but related <a href="/wiki/Clade" title="Clade">clades</a> (genetic branches) of the <i>Y. pestis</i> <a href="/wiki/Genome" title="Genome">genome</a> associated with medieval mass graves. These clades (which are thought to be extinct) were found to be ancestral to modern isolates of the modern <i>Y. pestis</i> strains <i>Y. p. orientalis</i> and <i>Y. p. medievalis</i>, suggesting the plague may have entered Europe in two waves. Surveys of <a href="/wiki/Plague_pit" title="Plague pit">plague pit</a> remains in France and England indicate the first variant entered Europe through the port of <a href="/wiki/Marseille" title="Marseille">Marseille</a> around November 1347 and spread through France over the next two years, eventually reaching England in the spring of 1349, where it spread through the country in three epidemics. Surveys of plague pit remains from the Dutch town of <a href="/wiki/Bergen_op_Zoom" title="Bergen op Zoom">Bergen op Zoom</a> showed the <i>Y. pestis</i> genotype responsible for the pandemic that spread through the <a href="/wiki/Low_Countries" title="Low Countries">Low Countries</a> from 1350 differed from that found in Britain and France, implying Bergen op Zoom (and possibly other parts of the southern Netherlands) was not directly infected from England or France in 1349 and suggesting a second wave of plague, different from those in Britain and France, may have been carried to the Low Countries from Norway, the <a href="/wiki/Hanseatic_League" title="Hanseatic League">Hanseatic</a> cities or another site.<sup id="cite_ref-Haensch2010_40-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Haensch2010-40">[40]</a></sup></p>
<p>The results of the Haensch study have since been confirmed and amended. Based on genetic evidence derived from Black Death victims in the <a href="/wiki/East_Smithfield" title="East Smithfield">East Smithfield</a> burial site in England, Schuenemann et al. concluded in 2011 "that the Black Death in medieval Europe was caused by a variant of <i>Y. pestis</i> that may no longer exist."<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> A study published in <i><a href="/wiki/Nature_(journal)" title="Nature (journal)">Nature</a></i> in October 2011 sequenced the genome of <i>Y. pestis</i> from plague victims and indicated that the strain that caused the Black Death is ancestral to most modern strains of the disease.<sup id="cite_ref-nature_bos_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nature_bos-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>DNA taken from 25 skeletons from the 14th century found in London have shown the plague is a strain of <i>Y. pestis</i> that is almost identical to that which hit Madagascar in 2013.<sup id="cite_ref-guardian_42-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-guardian-42">[42]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Alternative_explanations">Alternative explanations</span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Theories_of_the_Black_Death" title="Theories of the Black Death">Theories of the Black Death</a></div>
<p>The plague theory was first significantly challenged by the work of British bacteriologist J. F. D. Shrewsbury in 1970, who noted that the reported rates of mortality in rural areas during the 14th-century pandemic were inconsistent with the modern bubonic plague, leading him to conclude that contemporary accounts were exaggerations.<sup id="cite_ref-Chritakos2005_34-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Chritakos2005-34">[34]</a></sup> In 1984 zoologist Graham Twigg produced the first major work to challenge the bubonic plague theory directly, and his doubts about the identity of the Black Death have been taken up by a number of authors, including Samuel K. Cohn, Jr. (2002 and 2013), <a href="/wiki/David_Herlihy" title="David Herlihy">David Herlihy</a> (1997), and Susan Scott and Christopher Duncan (2001).<sup id="cite_ref-Chritakos2005_34-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Chritakos2005-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
<p>It is recognised that an <a href="/wiki/Epidemiological" class="mw-redirect" title="Epidemiological">epidemiological</a> account of the plague is as important as an identification of symptoms, but researchers are hampered by the lack of reliable statistics from this period. Most work has been done on the spread of the plague in England, and even estimates of overall population at the start vary by over 100% as no census was undertaken between the time of publication of the <a href="/wiki/Domesday_Book" title="Domesday Book">Domesday Book</a> and the year 1377.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEZiegler1998233_44-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEZiegler1998233-44">[44]</a></sup> Estimates of plague victims are usually extrapolated from figures from the clergy.</p>
<p>In addition to arguing that the rat population was insufficient to account for a bubonic plague pandemic, sceptics of the bubonic plague theory point out that the symptoms of the Black Death are not unique (and arguably in some accounts may differ from bubonic plague); that transference via fleas in goods was likely to be of marginal significance; and that the DNA results may be flawed and might not have been repeated elsewhere, despite extensive samples from other mass graves.<sup id="cite_ref-Chritakos2005_34-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Chritakos2005-34">[34]</a></sup> Other arguments include the lack of accounts of the death of rats before outbreaks of plague between the 14th and 17th centuries; temperatures that are too cold in northern Europe for the survival of fleas; that, despite primitive transport systems, the spread of the Black Death was much faster than that of modern bubonic plague; that mortality rates of the Black Death appear to be very high; that, while modern bubonic plague is largely endemic as a rural disease, the Black Death indiscriminately struck urban and rural areas; and that the pattern of the Black Death, with major outbreaks in the same areas separated by 5 to 15 years, differs from modern bubonic plague—which often becomes endemic for decades with annual flare-ups.<sup id="cite_ref-Chritakos2005_34-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Chritakos2005-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
<p>McCormick has suggested that earlier archaeologists were simply not interested in the "laborious" processes needed to discover rat remains.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> Walløe complains that all of these authors "take it for granted that Simond's infection model, black rat → rat flea → human, which was developed to explain the spread of plague in India, is the only way an epidemic of <i>Yersinia pestis</i> infection could spread", whilst pointing to several other possibilities.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> Similarly, Green has argued that greater attention is needed to the range of (especially non-<a href="/wiki/Commensalism" title="Commensalism">commensal</a>) animals that might be involved in the transmission of plague.<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Anthrax_PHIL_2033.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5f/Anthrax_PHIL_2033.png/220px-Anthrax_PHIL_2033.png" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5f/Anthrax_PHIL_2033.png/330px-Anthrax_PHIL_2033.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5f/Anthrax_PHIL_2033.png/440px-Anthrax_PHIL_2033.png 2x" data-file-width="3072" data-file-height="2048" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Anthrax_PHIL_2033.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Anthrax" title="Anthrax">Anthrax</a> skin lesion</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>A variety of alternatives to the <i>Y. pestis</i> have been put forward. Twigg suggested that the cause was a form of <a href="/wiki/Anthrax" title="Anthrax">anthrax</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Norman_Cantor" title="Norman Cantor">Norman Cantor</a> (2001) thought it may have been a combination of anthrax and other pandemics. Scott and Duncan have argued that the pandemic was a form of infectious disease that they characterise as <i>hemorrhagic</i> plague similar to <a href="/wiki/Ebola" class="mw-redirect" title="Ebola">Ebola</a>. Archaeologist Barney Sloane has argued that there is insufficient evidence of the extinction of a large number of rats in the archaeological record of the medieval waterfront in London and that the plague spread too quickly to support the thesis that the <i>Y. pestis</i> was spread from fleas on rats; he argues that transmission must have been person to person.<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup> However, no single alternative solution has achieved widespread acceptance.<sup id="cite_ref-Chritakos2005_34-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Chritakos2005-34">[34]</a></sup> Many scholars arguing for the <i>Y. pestis</i> as the major agent of the pandemic suggest that its extent and symptoms can be explained by a combination of bubonic plague with other diseases, including <a href="/wiki/Typhus" title="Typhus">typhus</a>, <a href="/wiki/Smallpox" title="Smallpox">smallpox</a> and respiratory infections. In addition to the bubonic infection, others point to additional septicemic (a type of "blood poisoning") and pneumonic (an airborne plague that attacks the lungs before the rest of the body) forms of the plague, which lengthen the duration of outbreaks throughout the seasons and help account for its high mortality rate and additional recorded symptoms.<sup id="cite_ref-Byrne2004pp21-9_26-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Byrne2004pp21-9-26">[26]</a></sup> In 2014, scientists with <a href="/wiki/Public_Health_England" title="Public Health England">Public Health England</a> announced the results of an examination of 25 bodies exhumed from the Clerkenwell area of London, as well as of wills registered in London during the period, which supported the pneumonic hypothesis.<sup id="cite_ref-guardian_42-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-guardian-42">[42]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Consequences">Consequences</span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Consequences_of_the_Black_Death" title="Consequences of the Black Death">Consequences of the Black Death</a></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Death_toll">Death toll</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Burying_Plague_Victims_of_Tournai.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7d/Burying_Plague_Victims_of_Tournai.jpg/220px-Burying_Plague_Victims_of_Tournai.jpg" width="220" height="230" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7d/Burying_Plague_Victims_of_Tournai.jpg/330px-Burying_Plague_Victims_of_Tournai.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/7/7d/Burying_Plague_Victims_of_Tournai.jpg 2x" data-file-width="400" data-file-height="418" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Burying_Plague_Victims_of_Tournai.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Citizens of <a href="/wiki/Tournai" title="Tournai">Tournai</a> bury plague victims</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>There are no exact figures for the <a href="/wiki/Infectious_disease#Historic_pandemics" class="mw-redirect" title="Infectious disease">death toll</a>; the rate varied widely by locality. In urban centres, the greater the population before the outbreak, the longer the duration of the period of abnormal mortality.<sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup> It killed some <span class="nowrap">75 to 200 million</span> people in <a href="/wiki/Eurasia" title="Eurasia">Eurasia</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-ABC.2FReuters_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ABC.2FReuters-1">[1]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Health._De-coding_the_Black_Death_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Health._De-coding_the_Black_Death-2">[2]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Black_Death.27s_Gene_Code_Cracked_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Black_Death.27s_Gene_Code_Cracked-3">[3]</a></sup> According to medieval historian <a href="/wiki/Philip_Daileader" title="Philip Daileader">Philip Daileader</a> in 2007:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>The trend of recent research is pointing to a figure more like 45–50% of the European population dying during a four-year period. There is a fair amount of geographic variation. In Mediterranean Europe, areas such as Italy, the south of France and Spain, where plague ran for about four years consecutively, it was probably closer to 75–80% of the population. In Germany and England ... it was probably closer to 20%.<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>A death rate as high as 60% in Europe has been suggested by Norwegian historian Ole Benedictow:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>Detailed study of the mortality data available points to two conspicuous features in relation to the mortality caused by the Black Death: namely the extreme level of mortality caused by the Black Death, and the remarkable similarity or consistency of the level of mortality, from Spain in southern Europe to England in north-western Europe. The data is sufficiently widespread and numerous to make it likely that the Black Death swept away around 60 per cent of Europe's population. It is generally assumed that the size of Europe's population at the time was around 80 million. This implies that around 50 million people died in the Black Death.<sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>The most widely accepted estimate for the Middle East, including Iraq, Iran and Syria, during this time, is for a death rate of about a third.<sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup> The Black Death killed about 40% of Egypt's population.<sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup> Half of Paris's population of 100,000 people died. In Italy, the population of <a href="/wiki/Florence" title="Florence">Florence</a> was reduced from 110,000–120,000 inhabitants in 1338 down to 50,000 in 1351. At least 60% of the population of <a href="/wiki/Hamburg" title="Hamburg">Hamburg</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bremen" title="Bremen">Bremen</a> perished,<sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup> and a similar percentage of <a href="/wiki/London" title="London">Londoners</a> may have died from the disease as well.<sup id="cite_ref-guardian_42-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-guardian-42">[42]</a></sup> While contemporary reports account of mass burial pits being created in response to the large numbers of dead, recent scientific investigations of a burial pit in Central London found well-preserved individuals to be buried in isolated, evenly spaced graves, suggesting at least some pre-planning and Christian burials at this time.<sup id="cite_ref-56" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup> Before 1350, there were about 170,000 settlements in Germany, and this was reduced by nearly 40,000 by 1450.<sup id="cite_ref-57" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup> In 1348, the plague spread so rapidly that before any physicians or government authorities had time to reflect upon its origins, about a third of the European population had already perished. In crowded cities, it was not uncommon for as much as 50% of the population to die.<sup id="cite_ref-Chritakos2005_34-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Chritakos2005-34">[34]</a></sup> The disease bypassed some areas, and the most isolated areas were less vulnerable to contagion. Monks and priests were especially hard-hit since they cared for victims of the Black Death.<sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Persecutions">Persecutions</span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Black_Death_Jewish_persecutions" title="Black Death Jewish persecutions">Black Death Jewish persecutions</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Danse_macabre_by_Michael_Wolgemut.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bf/Danse_macabre_by_Michael_Wolgemut.png/220px-Danse_macabre_by_Michael_Wolgemut.png" width="220" height="187" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bf/Danse_macabre_by_Michael_Wolgemut.png/330px-Danse_macabre_by_Michael_Wolgemut.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bf/Danse_macabre_by_Michael_Wolgemut.png/440px-Danse_macabre_by_Michael_Wolgemut.png 2x" data-file-width="705" data-file-height="599" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Danse_macabre_by_Michael_Wolgemut.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Inspired by the Black Death, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Dance_of_Death" class="mw-redirect" title="The Dance of Death">The Dance of Death</a></i> or <i>Danse Macabre</i>, an allegory on the universality of death, was a common painting motif in the late medieval period.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Renewed religious fervour and fanaticism bloomed in the wake of the Black Death. Some Europeans targeted "various groups such as Jews, friars, foreigners, beggars, pilgrims",<sup id="cite_ref-nirenberg_59-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nirenberg-59">[59]</a></sup> lepers,<sup id="cite_ref-nirenberg_59-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nirenberg-59">[59]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-60" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Romani_people" title="Romani people">Romani</a>, thinking that they were to blame for the crisis. <a href="/wiki/Leprosy" title="Leprosy">Lepers</a>, and other individuals with skin diseases such as <a href="/wiki/Acne_vulgaris" class="mw-redirect" title="Acne vulgaris">acne</a> or <a href="/wiki/Psoriasis" title="Psoriasis">psoriasis</a>, were singled out and exterminated throughout Europe.</p>
<p>Because 14th-century healers were at a loss to explain the cause, Europeans turned to astrological forces, earthquakes, and the poisoning of wells by Jews as possible reasons for the plague's emergence.<sup id="cite_ref-Bennet.26Hollister2006_61-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bennet.26Hollister2006-61">[61]</a></sup> The governments of Europe had no apparent response to the crisis because no one knew its cause or how it spread. The mechanism of infection and transmission of diseases was little understood in the 14th century; many people believed only God's anger could produce such horrific displays.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>There were many attacks against <a href="/wiki/Jews_in_the_Middle_Ages" class="mw-redirect" title="Jews in the Middle Ages">Jewish</a> communities.<sup id="cite_ref-JewishEncyclopedia_62-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-JewishEncyclopedia-62">[62]</a></sup> In February 1349, the citizens of <a href="/wiki/Strasbourg" title="Strasbourg">Strasbourg</a> murdered 2,000 Jews.<sup id="cite_ref-JewishEncyclopedia_62-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-JewishEncyclopedia-62">[62]</a></sup> In August 1349, the Jewish communities in <a href="/wiki/Mainz" title="Mainz">Mainz</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cologne" title="Cologne">Cologne</a> were exterminated. By 1351, 60 major and 150 smaller Jewish communities had been destroyed.<sup id="cite_ref-63" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Recurrence">Recurrence</span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Second_plague_pandemic" title="Second plague pandemic">Second plague pandemic</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Great_plague_of_london-1665.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1e/Great_plague_of_london-1665.jpg/170px-Great_plague_of_london-1665.jpg" width="170" height="237" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1e/Great_plague_of_london-1665.jpg/255px-Great_plague_of_london-1665.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1e/Great_plague_of_london-1665.jpg/340px-Great_plague_of_london-1665.jpg 2x" data-file-width="380" data-file-height="529" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Great_plague_of_london-1665.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The Great Plague of London, in 1665, killed up to 100,000 people</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The plague repeatedly returned to haunt Europe and the Mediterranean throughout the 14th to 17th centuries.<sup id="cite_ref-64" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup> According to Biraben, the plague was present somewhere in Europe in every year between 1346 and 1671.<sup id="cite_ref-65" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Plague_(disease)#Second_Pandemic:_Black_Death" title="Plague (disease)">Second Pandemic</a> was particularly widespread in the following years: 1360–1363; 1374; 1400; 1438–1439; 1456–1457; 1464–1466; 1481–1485; 1500–1503; 1518–1531; 1544–1548; 1563–1566; 1573–1588; 1596–1599; 1602–1611; 1623–1640; 1644–1654; and 1664–1667. Subsequent outbreaks, though severe, marked the retreat from most of Europe (18th&#160;century) and northern Africa (19th&#160;century).<sup id="cite_ref-66" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup> According to Geoffrey Parker, "<a href="/wiki/Early_Modern_France" class="mw-redirect" title="Early Modern France">France</a> alone lost almost a million people to the plague in the epidemic of 1628–31."<sup id="cite_ref-67" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup></p>
<p>In England, in the absence of census figures, historians propose a range of preincident population figures from as high as 7&#160;million to as low as 4&#160;million in 1300,<sup id="cite_ref-68" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup> and a postincident population figure as low as 2&#160;million.<sup id="cite_ref-69" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-69">[69]</a></sup> By the end of 1350, the Black Death subsided, but it never really died out in England. Over the next few hundred years, further outbreaks occurred in 1361–1362, 1369, 1379–1383, 1389–1393, and throughout the first half of the 15th&#160;century.<sup id="cite_ref-70" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-70">[70]</a></sup> An outbreak in 1471 took as much as 10–15% of the population, while the death rate of the plague of 1479–1480 could have been as high as 20%.<sup id="cite_ref-71" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-71">[71]</a></sup> The most general outbreaks in <a href="/wiki/Tudor_dynasty" class="mw-redirect" title="Tudor dynasty">Tudor</a> and <a href="/wiki/House_of_Stuart" title="House of Stuart">Stuart</a> England seem to have begun in 1498, 1535, 1543, 1563, 1589, 1603, 1625, and 1636, and ended with the <a href="/wiki/Great_Plague_of_London" title="Great Plague of London">Great Plague of London</a> in 1665.<sup id="cite_ref-72" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-72">[72]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Chumbunt.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/74/Chumbunt.png/220px-Chumbunt.png" width="220" height="167" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/74/Chumbunt.png/330px-Chumbunt.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/7/74/Chumbunt.png 2x" data-file-width="400" data-file-height="303" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Chumbunt.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Plague_Riot" class="mw-redirect" title="Plague Riot">Plague Riot</a> in Moscow in 1771: During the course of the <a href="/wiki/Russian_plague_of_1770%E2%80%931772" class="mw-redirect" title="Russian plague of 1770–1772">city's plague</a>, between 50,000 and 100,000 people died, <span class="nowrap"> </span><span class="frac nowrap"><sup>1</sup>⁄<sub>6</sub></span> to <span class="nowrap"> </span><span class="frac nowrap"><sup>1</sup>⁄<sub>3</sub></span> of its population.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>In 1466, perhaps 40,000&#160;people died of the plague in Paris.<sup id="cite_ref-73" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-73">[73]</a></sup> During the 16th and 17th centuries, the plague was present in Paris around 30 per cent of the time.<sup id="cite_ref-74" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-74">[74]</a></sup> The Black Death ravaged Europe for three years before it continued on into Russia, where the disease was present somewhere in the country 25 times between 1350 and 1490.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEByrne200462_75-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEByrne200462-75">[75]</a></sup> Plague epidemics ravaged London in 1563, 1593, 1603, 1625, 1636, and 1665,<sup id="cite_ref-76" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-76">[76]</a></sup> reducing its population by 10 to 30% during those years.<sup id="cite_ref-77" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-77">[77]</a></sup> Over 10% of <a href="/wiki/Amsterdam" title="Amsterdam">Amsterdam</a>'s population died in 1623–1625, and again in 1635–1636, 1655, and 1664.<sup id="cite_ref-78" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-78">[78]</a></sup> Plague occurred in <a href="/wiki/Venice" title="Venice">Venice</a> 22 times between 1361 and 1528.<sup id="cite_ref-79" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-79">[79]</a></sup> The plague of 1576–1577 killed 50,000 in Venice, almost a third of the population.<sup id="cite_ref-80" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-80">[80]</a></sup> Late outbreaks in central Europe included the <a href="/wiki/Italian_Plague_of_1629%E2%80%931631" class="mw-redirect" title="Italian Plague of 1629–1631">Italian Plague of 1629–1631</a>, which is associated with troop movements during the <a href="/wiki/Thirty_Years%27_War" title="Thirty Years' War">Thirty Years' War</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Great_Plague_of_Vienna" title="Great Plague of Vienna">Great Plague of Vienna</a> in 1679. Over 60% of Norway's population died in 1348–1350.<sup id="cite_ref-forskning_81-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-forskning-81">[81]</a></sup> The last plague outbreak ravaged <a href="/wiki/Oslo" title="Oslo">Oslo</a> in 1654.<sup id="cite_ref-cripkl_82-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cripkl-82">[82]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the first half of the 17th century, a plague claimed some 1.7 million victims in Italy, or about 14% of the population.<sup id="cite_ref-83" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-83">[83]</a></sup> In 1656, the plague killed about half of <a href="/wiki/Naples" title="Naples">Naples</a>' 300,000 inhabitants.<sup id="cite_ref-84" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-84">[84]</a></sup> More than 1.25 million deaths resulted from the extreme incidence of plague in 17th-century <a href="/wiki/Habsburg_Spain" title="Habsburg Spain">Spain</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-85" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-85">[85]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Great_Plague_of_Seville" title="Great Plague of Seville">plague of 1649</a> probably reduced the population of <a href="/wiki/Seville" title="Seville">Seville</a> by half.<sup id="cite_ref-pandemics_86-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pandemics-86">[86]</a></sup> In 1709–1713, a <a href="/wiki/The_plague_during_the_Great_Northern_War" class="mw-redirect" title="The plague during the Great Northern War">plague epidemic that followed the Great Northern War</a> (1700–1721, <a href="/wiki/Swedish_Empire" title="Swedish Empire">Sweden</a> v. Russia and allies)<sup id="cite_ref-87" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-87">[87]</a></sup> killed about 100,000 in Sweden,<sup id="cite_ref-88" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-88">[88]</a></sup> and 300,000 in Prussia.<sup id="cite_ref-pandemics_86-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pandemics-86">[86]</a></sup> The plague killed two-thirds of the inhabitants of <a href="/wiki/Helsinki" title="Helsinki">Helsinki</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-89" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-89">[89]</a></sup> and claimed a third of <a href="/wiki/Stockholm" title="Stockholm">Stockholm</a>'s population.<sup id="cite_ref-90" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-90">[90]</a></sup> Europe's last major epidemic occurred in 1720 in <a href="/wiki/Great_Plague_of_Marseille" title="Great Plague of Marseille">Marseille</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-forskning_81-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-forskning-81">[81]</a></sup></p>

<p>The Black Death ravaged much of the <a href="/wiki/Islamic_world" class="mw-redirect" title="Islamic world">Islamic world</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-91" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-91">[91]</a></sup> Plague was present in at least one location in the Islamic world virtually every year between 1500 and 1850.<sup id="cite_ref-92" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-92">[92]</a></sup> Plague repeatedly struck the cities of North Africa. <a href="/wiki/Algiers" title="Algiers">Algiers</a> lost 30 to 50 thousand inhabitants to it in 1620–1621, and again in 1654–1657, 1665, 1691, and 1740–1742.<sup id="cite_ref-93" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-93">[93]</a></sup> Plague remained a major event in <a href="/wiki/Ottoman_Empire" title="Ottoman Empire">Ottoman</a> society until the second quarter of the 19th century. Between 1701 and 1750, thirty-seven larger and smaller epidemics were recorded in <a href="/wiki/Constantinople" title="Constantinople">Constantinople</a>, and an additional thirty-one between 1751 and 1800.<sup id="cite_ref-94" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-94">[94]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Baghdad" title="Baghdad">Baghdad</a> has suffered severely from visitations of the plague, and sometimes two-thirds of its population has been wiped out.<sup id="cite_ref-95" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-95">[95]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Third_plague_pandemic">Third plague pandemic</span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Third_plague_pandemic" title="Third plague pandemic">Third plague pandemic</a></div>
<p>The third plague pandemic (1855–1859) started in China in the mid-19th century, spreading to all inhabited continents and killing 10 million people in India alone.<sup id="cite_ref-96" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-96">[96]</a></sup> Twelve plague outbreaks in Australia between 1900 and 1925 resulted in well over 1,000 deaths, chiefly in Sydney. This led to the establishment of a Public Health Department there which undertook some leading-edge research on plague transmission from rat fleas to humans via the bacillus <i>Yersinia pestis</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-97" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-97">[97]</a></sup></p>
<p>The first North American plague epidemic was the <a href="/wiki/San_Francisco_plague_of_1900%E2%80%931904" title="San Francisco plague of 1900–1904">San Francisco plague of 1900–1904</a>, followed by another outbreak in 1907–1908.<sup id="cite_ref-98" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-98">[98]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-99" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-99">[99]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-100" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-100">[100]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-101" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-101">[101]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-102" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-102">[102]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-103" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-103">[103]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-104" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-104">[104]</a></sup> From 1944 through 1993, 362 cases of human plague were reported in the United States; approximately 90% occurred in four western states: Arizona, California, Colorado, and New Mexico.<sup id="cite_ref-105" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-105">[105]</a></sup></p>
<p>Modern treatment methods include <a href="/wiki/Insecticide" title="Insecticide">insecticides</a>, the use of <a href="/wiki/Antibiotic" class="mw-redirect" title="Antibiotic">antibiotics</a>, and a <a href="/wiki/Waldemar_Haffkine#Anti-plague_vaccine" title="Waldemar Haffkine">plague vaccine</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Plague_(disease)" title="Plague (disease)">plague</a> bacterium could develop <a href="/wiki/Drug_resistance" title="Drug resistance">drug resistance</a> and again become a major health threat. One case of a drug-resistant form of the bacterium was found in <a href="/wiki/Madagascar" title="Madagascar">Madagascar</a> in 1995.<sup id="cite_ref-106" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-106">[106]</a></sup> A further outbreak in Madagascar was reported in November 2014.<sup id="cite_ref-107" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-107">[107]</a></sup></p>
<p><span id="Etymology"></span><span id="Name"></span><span id="Naming"></span></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Names">Names</span></h2>
<p>The 12th-century French physician <a href="/wiki/Gilles_de_Corbeil" title="Gilles de Corbeil">Gilles de Corbeil</a>'s <i>On the Signs and Symptoms of Diseases</i> (<a href="/wiki/Latin_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Latin language">Latin</a>: <span lang="la"><i>De signis et sinthomatibus egritudinum</i></span>) uses the phrase "black death" (<i><span lang="la">atra mors</span></i>) to refer to a pestilential fever (<i><span lang="la">febris pestilentialis</span></i>).<sup id="cite_ref-108" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-108">[108]</a></sup></p>
<p>Writers contemporary with the plague referred to the event as the <span class="nowrap">"<b>Great Mortality</b>"</span><sup id="cite_ref-109" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-109">[109]</a></sup> or the <span class="nowrap">"<b>Great Plague</b>".</span><sup id="cite_ref-Bennet.26Hollister2006_61-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bennet.26Hollister2006-61">[61]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (May 2015)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>The phrase <span class="nowrap">"black death"</span> (<i><span lang="la">mors nigra</span></i>) was used in 1350 by Simon de Covino or Couvin, a Belgian astronomer, who wrote the poem "On the Judgment of the Sun at a Feast of Saturn" (<i><span lang="la">De judicio Solis in convivio Saturni</span></i>), which attributes the plague to a conjunction of Jupiter and Saturn.<sup id="cite_ref-110" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-110">[110]</a></sup> In 1908, Gasquet claimed that use of the name <i><span lang="la">atra mors</span></i> for the 14th-century epidemic first appeared in a 1631 book on Danish history by <a href="/wiki/Johan_Isaksson_Pontanus" class="mw-redirect" title="Johan Isaksson Pontanus">J.&#160;I. Pontanus</a>: "Commonly and from its effects, they called it the black death" (<i><span lang="la">Vulgo &amp; ab effectu atram mortem vocatibant</span></i>).<sup id="cite_ref-111" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-111">[111]</a></sup> The name spread through Scandinavia and then Germany, gradually becoming attached to the mid 14th-century epidemic as a proper name.<sup id="cite_ref-112" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-112">[112]</a></sup> In England, it was not until 1823, that the medieval epidemic was first called the Black Death.<sup id="cite_ref-113" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-113">[113]</a></sup></p>
