<p>The <b>Rhine–Main–Danube Canal</b> (<a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a>: <i>Rhein-Main-Donau-Kanal</i>; also called <b>Main-Danube Canal</b>, <b>RMD Canal</b> or <b>Europa Canal</b>), in <a href="/wiki/Bavaria" title="Bavaria">Bavaria</a>, <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a>, connects the <a href="/wiki/Main_(river)" title="Main (river)">Main</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Danube" title="Danube">Danube</a> rivers across the <a href="/wiki/European_Watershed" class="mw-redirect" title="European Watershed">European Watershed</a>, running from <a href="/wiki/Bamberg" title="Bamberg">Bamberg</a> via <a href="/wiki/Nuremberg" title="Nuremberg">Nuremberg</a> to <a href="/wiki/Kelheim" title="Kelheim">Kelheim</a>. The canal connects the <a href="/wiki/North_Sea" title="North Sea">North Sea</a> and <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">Atlantic Ocean</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Black_Sea" title="Black Sea">Black Sea</a>, providing a navigable artery between the <a href="/wiki/Rhine" title="Rhine">Rhine</a> delta (at <a href="/wiki/Rotterdam" title="Rotterdam">Rotterdam</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Netherlands</a>), and the <a href="/wiki/Danube_Delta" title="Danube Delta">Danube Delta</a> in south-eastern <a href="/wiki/Romania" title="Romania">Romania</a> and south-western <a href="/wiki/Ukraine" title="Ukraine">Ukraine</a> (or <a href="/wiki/Constan%C8%9Ba" title="Constanța">Constanța</a>, through the <a href="/wiki/Danube%E2%80%93Black_Sea_Canal" title="Danube–Black Sea Canal">Danube–Black Sea Canal</a>). The present canal was completed in 1992 and is 171 kilometres (106&#160;mi) long.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_history">Early history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rhine%E2%80%93Main%E2%80%93Danube_Canal&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Projects for connecting the Danube and Rhine basins by canal have a long history. In 793, the Emperor <a href="/wiki/Charlemagne" title="Charlemagne">Charlemagne</a> ordered the construction of a canal—the <i><a href="/wiki/Fossa_Carolina" title="Fossa Carolina">Fossa Carolina</a></i>, or <i>Karlsgraben</i>—connecting the <a href="/wiki/Schw%C3%A4bische_Rezat" class="mw-redirect" title="Schwäbische Rezat">Schwäbische Rezat</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Tributary" title="Tributary">tributary</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Rednitz" title="Rednitz">Rednitz</a>, to the <a href="/wiki/Altm%C3%BChl" title="Altmühl">Altmühl</a> near <a href="/wiki/Treuchtlingen" title="Treuchtlingen">Treuchtlingen</a>. Between 1836 and 1846 the <i><a href="/wiki/Ludwig_Canal" title="Ludwig Canal">Ludwig Canal</a></i>, or <i>Ludwigskanal</i>, named for King <a href="/wiki/Ludwig_I_of_Bavaria" title="Ludwig I of Bavaria">Ludwig I of Bavaria</a>, was built between Bamberg and Kelheim. This canal had a narrow channel, with many <a href="/wiki/Lock_(water_transport)" class="mw-redirect" title="Lock (water transport)">locks</a>, and a shortage of water in the peak section, so the operation of the waterway soon became uneconomic — especially given the rapidly advancing construction of the <a href="/wiki/Railway" class="mw-redirect" title="Railway">railway</a> network in the southern German countryside. The canal finally was abandoned in 1950, after a decision was made to not repair damage it had suffered during <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>.</p>
<p>In 1917, the <a href="/wiki/Landtag_of_Bavaria" title="Landtag of Bavaria">Landtag of Bavaria</a> passed a law calling for the development of a major shipping route "between <a href="/wiki/Aschaffenburg" title="Aschaffenburg">Aschaffenburg</a> and <a href="/wiki/Passau" title="Passau">Passau</a>", with the capacity to carry the 1,200-ton ships used on the <a href="/wiki/Rhine" title="Rhine">Rhine</a>. On 13 June 1921, Bavaria and the <a href="/wiki/German_Reich" title="German Reich">German Reich</a> concluded an agreement to build the „<i>Main-Donau-Wasserstraße</i>".<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Under this plan, in addition to the expansion of the Main and Danube, a completely new channel linking the rivers was to be created. The Rhein-Main-Donau AG (RMD-AG) was founded on 30 December 1921 to undertake the project. To finance the waterway, the RMD was given control of the water resources of the Main, Danube, <a href="/wiki/Lech_River" class="mw-redirect" title="Lech River">Lech</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Altm%C3%BChl" title="Altmühl">Altmühl</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Regnitz" title="Regnitz">Regnitz</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Construction">Construction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rhine%E2%80%93Main%E2%80%93Danube_Canal&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Construction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The first concrete plans for the new waterway emerged in 1938, for the so-called Mindorfer Linie south of Nurenberg. As early as 1939 the first preparatory work began at <a href="/wiki/Thalm%C3%A4ssing" title="Thalmässing">Thalmässing</a> in <a href="/wiki/Roth_(district)" title="Roth (district)">Landkreis Roth</a>. However, after the war this route was dropped. By 1962, the Main's channel had been expanded as far upstream as Bamberg. In 1966, the Duisburger Vertrag, an agreement between Bavaria and the <a href="/wiki/Federal_Republic_of_Germany" class="mw-redirect" title="Federal Republic of Germany">Federal Republic of Germany</a>, was reached for financing the completion of the project. The contract was signed on 16 September of that year in <a href="/wiki/Duisburg" title="Duisburg">Duisburg</a> by Federal Transport Minister <a href="/wiki/Hans-Christoph_Seebohm" title="Hans-Christoph Seebohm">Hans-Christoph Seebohm</a>, Federal Finance Minister <a href="/wiki/Rolf_Dahlgr%C3%BCn" title="Rolf Dahlgrün">Rolf Dahlgrün</a>, Bavarian Prime Minister <a href="/wiki/Alfons_Goppel" title="Alfons Goppel">Alfons Goppel</a> and the Bavarian Finance Minister Konrad Pöhner.</p>
<p>The last section to be built, between Nuremberg and Kelheim, became politically controversial in the 1970s and 1980s, mainly because of the 34-kilometre (21&#160;mi) long section through the <a href="/wiki/Altm%C3%BChltal" title="Altmühltal">Altmühl valley</a>. On 25 September 1992, the canal was completed. The equivalent of some 2.3&#160;billion <a href="/wiki/Euro" title="Euro">euros</a> were invested in the construction from 1960 to 1992. Almost 20&#160;percent of that went for environmental protection projects.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Route">Route</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rhine%E2%80%93Main%E2%80%93Danube_Canal&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Route">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>From Bamberg to <a href="/wiki/F%C3%BCrth" title="Fürth">Fürth</a> the canal follows the valley of the <a href="/wiki/Regnitz" title="Regnitz">Regnitz</a>, a tributary of the Main. From Fürth to beyond <a href="/wiki/Roth,_Bavaria" title="Roth, Bavaria">Roth</a> it follows the valley of the <a href="/wiki/Rednitz" title="Rednitz">Rednitz</a>, a tributary of the Regnitz. It crosses the <a href="/wiki/Frankish_Alb" class="mw-redirect" title="Frankish Alb">Frankish Alb</a> mountains and joins the river <a href="/wiki/Altm%C3%BChl" title="Altmühl">Altmühl</a> near <a href="/wiki/Dietfurt" title="Dietfurt">Dietfurt</a>. From Dietfurt to <a href="/wiki/Kelheim" title="Kelheim">Kelheim</a> on the Danube the canal follows the Altmühl valley.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Dimensions">Dimensions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rhine%E2%80%93Main%E2%80%93Danube_Canal&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Dimensions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Main-Donau-Kanal-H%C3%B6henprofil.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/30/Main-Donau-Kanal-H%C3%B6henprofil.svg/300px-Main-Donau-Kanal-H%C3%B6henprofil.svg.png" width="300" height="106" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/30/Main-Donau-Kanal-H%C3%B6henprofil.svg/450px-Main-Donau-Kanal-H%C3%B6henprofil.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/30/Main-Donau-Kanal-H%C3%B6henprofil.svg/600px-Main-Donau-Kanal-H%C3%B6henprofil.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="1500" data-file-height="531" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Main-Donau-Kanal-H%C3%B6henprofil.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Profile of the canal showing the locks</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The cross-section of the waterway is normally <a href="/wiki/Trapezoid" title="Trapezoid">trapezoidal</a>, with 31 metres (102&#160;ft) width at the bottom, 55 metres (180&#160;ft) width at the water surface, 4 metres (13&#160;ft) of water depth, and a side grade of 1:3 (i.e., 4x3x2=24 which is just the difference between top and bottom widths). The channel is a <a href="/wiki/Classification_of_European_Inland_Waterways" title="Classification of European Inland Waterways">Waterway Class Vb</a>; the largest authorised vessel are 190 metres (620&#160;ft) long and 11.45 metres (37.6&#160;ft) wide. The channel in the Kelheim-bound Bamberg lock has a depth of 2.70 metres (8.9&#160;ft). In the few sections with a rectangular profile, the width is usually 43 metres (141&#160;ft) (i.e., the mean between top and bottom widths).</p>
<p>The length of the canal is 171 kilometres (106&#160;mi); the summit elevation (between the <a href="/wiki/Hilpoltstein" title="Hilpoltstein">Hilpoltstein</a> and <a href="/wiki/Neumarkt_(district)" title="Neumarkt (district)">Bachhausen</a> locks) is 406 metres (1,332&#160;ft) above <a href="/wiki/Sea_level" title="Sea level">sea level</a>. This is the <a href="/wiki/Extreme_points_of_Earth#Highest_attainable_by_transportation" title="Extreme points of Earth">highest point on Earth</a> that is currently reached by commercial watercraft from the sea.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>The height difference along the north ramp of the canal—from the Main at <a href="/wiki/Bamberg" title="Bamberg">Bamberg</a> to the crest elevation—is 175 metres (574&#160;ft), with 11&#160;locks. From the crest elevation down to the <a href="/wiki/Altm%C3%BChl" title="Altmühl">Altmühl</a> at <a href="/wiki/Dietfurt" title="Dietfurt">Dietfurt</a> is a drop of 51 metres (167&#160;ft) through three locks. The further difference in elevation of 17 metres (56&#160;ft) along the Altmühl, with two more locks, makes a total of 68 metres (223&#160;ft) for the south ramp. This means that the Danube end of the canal is 107.3 metres (352&#160;ft) above the level of the Main end.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Locks">Locks</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rhine%E2%80%93Main%E2%80%93Danube_Canal&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Locks">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Along the course of the canal there are 16&#160;<a href="/wiki/Locks_(water_transport)" class="mw-redirect" title="Locks (water transport)">locks</a> with lifting heights of up to 25 metres (82&#160;ft). The 16 locks are managed from four remote control centres (Neuses since 2007, <a href="/wiki/Erlangen" title="Erlangen">Kriegenbrunn</a>, Hilpoltstein, and Dietfurt from the beginning of 2007). These centres are manned with one worker on the night shift, and two on the day shift. The locks were modernized from 2001 to 2007, replacing the outdated <a href="/wiki/Relay" title="Relay">relay</a> technology with computers and a <a href="/wiki/Programmable_logic_controller" title="Programmable logic controller">PLC</a>. The cost was approximately $&#160;1.3 million per lock.</p>
<p>The summit water level is maintained by pumping water from the canal stretches below; and some water is drained into the summit level from local natural sources.</p>
<p>Thirteen locks are designed to conserve water, which they do by piping first the top third, and then the middle third of the lock water into side tanks during the down cycle. On the up cycle, these tanks replenish first the bottom third and then the middle third of the lock volume.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> The remaining top third is supplied by water from the upper level of the canal.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Commercial_operation">Commercial operation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rhine%E2%80%93Main%E2%80%93Danube_Canal&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Commercial operation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>There were different forecasts of <a href="/wiki/Freight_transport" title="Freight transport">freight transport</a> volumes, from which <a href="/wiki/Benefit-cost_ratio" class="mw-redirect" title="Benefit-cost ratio">benefit-cost ratio</a> could be derived. In 1981, a Federal Minister for Transport cost-benefit account assumed an estimated traffic volume of only 2.7&#160;million tonnes per year for the Main-Danube Canal, and a benefit-cost ratio of 0.52:1. This might have justified the termination of the project. One of the proponents commissioned a study by the Ifo-Institut München, predicting an estimated 5.5&#160;million tonnes per year for the traffic volume on the Main-Danube Canal. In 2004, the freight volume in exchange traffic totalled 5.9&#160;million tonnes and the total transport 6.9&#160;million tonnes.</p>
<p>The course of the planned extension of the Danube <a href="/wiki/Waterway" title="Waterway">waterway</a> between <a href="/wiki/Passau" title="Passau">Passau</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kelheim" title="Kelheim">Kelheim</a> is still controversial. Proponents argue that the cost structure of <a href="/wiki/Inland_navigation" title="Inland navigation">inland navigation</a> will require larger ship sizes, so that larger lock dimensions, deeper channels and secure minimum water depth will be required. Opponents argue the <a href="/wiki/Environmental_degradation" title="Environmental degradation">environmental degradation</a> is too great and that inland navigation is falling.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Transport_volumes">Transport volumes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rhine%E2%80%93Main%E2%80%93Danube_Canal&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Transport volumes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Transport_volumes_for_2006">Transport volumes for 2006</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rhine%E2%80%93Main%E2%80%93Danube_Canal&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Transport volumes for 2006">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The transport volume through the RMD Canal in 2006 was down almost 20% compared to the previous year.</p>
<ul>
<li>Total 6.24 million <a href="/wiki/Tonne" title="Tonne">tonnes</a> (2005: 7.598 million tonnes)</li>
<li>Number of vessels 5,280 (6,467), of which 2,823 travelled in the direction of the Danube and 2,457 toward the Rhine</li>
<li>The channel was closed due to ice at Nuremberg for 25 days for traffic in the direction of the Main, and for 37 days in the direction of the Danube.</li>
</ul>
<p>The most important goods, which were transported on the canal toward the Danube:</p>
<ul>
<li>Food and feed: 836,186 tonnes</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Ore" title="Ore">Ores</a> and scrap: 795,259 tonnes</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Iron" title="Iron">Iron</a>, <a href="/wiki/Steel" title="Steel">steel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nonferrous_metal" class="mw-redirect" title="Nonferrous metal">nonferrous metals</a>: 103,547 tonnes</li>
<li>Stones, soil, building rubble: 510,187 tonnes</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Fertilizer" title="Fertilizer">Fertilizers</a>: 296,340 tonnes</li>
</ul>
<p>Goods transported toward the Rhine</p>
<ul>
<li>Food and feed: 476,422 tonnes</li>
<li>Iron, steel, nonferrous metals: 419,459 tonnes</li>
<li>Fertilizers: 295,701 tonnes.</li>
</ul>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Containerization" title="Containerization">container</a> traffic declined from 3,986 to 2,539 <a href="/wiki/Twenty-foot_equivalent_unit" title="Twenty-foot equivalent unit">TEUs</a>. Of these 85% were in the direction of Danube.</p>
<p>Meanwhile, <a href="/wiki/Tourism" title="Tourism">tourism</a> along the canal is economically important. This has contributed to the extensive creation of habitats to compensate for the canal.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Impact_on_ecology">Impact on ecology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rhine%E2%80%93Main%E2%80%93Danube_Canal&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Impact on ecology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p>The construction of canals involves <a href="/wiki/Ecology" title="Ecology">ecological</a> dangers. The Main-Danube Canal makes it possible for animals to spread from <a href="/wiki/Western_Europe" title="Western Europe">Western</a> to <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Europe" title="Eastern Europe">Eastern Europe</a> or in the opposite direction. These are so-called <a href="/wiki/Invasive_species" title="Invasive species">invasive species</a>, which often cause difficulties in the <a href="/wiki/Ecosystem" title="Ecosystem">ecosystem</a> of their new home: competition with the <a href="/wiki/Species" title="Species">species</a> already living there, lack of <a href="/wiki/Predation" title="Predation">predators</a>, etc. However, there is also the possibility that they will fit into the existing ecosystem and their introduction lead to an enrichment of the resident <a href="/wiki/Wildlife" title="Wildlife">wildlife</a>.</p>
<p>In order to maintain navigable water levels for the waterway in the Main, Regnitz, and Rednitz valleys, water must be diverted via the Altmühlüberleiter canal &amp; tunnel from the upper Altmühl to the Brombachsee reservoirs, across the <a href="/wiki/European_Watershed" class="mw-redirect" title="European Watershed">European Watershed</a> between the <a href="/wiki/Drainage_basin" title="Drainage basin">drainage basins</a> of the Danube and Rhine.</p>
<p>On the other hand, the Canal carries traffic that would otherwise require 250,000 truck trips annually, or as an alternative, 3,000 freight trains on the <a href="/wiki/Deutsche_Bahn" title="Deutsche Bahn">Deutsche Bahn</a> rail network.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Animal_species_which_have_reached_the_Danube_from_the_Rhine">Animal species which have reached the Danube from the Rhine</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rhine%E2%80%93Main%E2%80%93Danube_Canal&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Animal species which have reached the Danube from the Rhine">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>This exchange also works in the opposite direction. Examples are the <a href="/wiki/Asian_clam" class="mw-redirect" title="Asian clam">Asian clam</a> (<i>Corbicula fluminea</i>) and the freshwater shrimp <i><a href="/wiki/Atyidae" title="Atyidae">Atyaephyra</a> desmaresti</i>. In November 2002, the <a href="/wiki/Chinese_mitten_crab" title="Chinese mitten crab">Chinese mitten crab</a> (<i>Eriocheir sinensis</i>) was found in the Austrian Danube for the first time. This <a href="/wiki/Crab" title="Crab">crab</a> has already caused significant problems, particularly in the <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Netherlands</a> and the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="1979_damburst_at_Katzwang">1979 damburst at Katzwang</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rhine%E2%80%93Main%E2%80%93Danube_Canal&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: 1979 damburst at Katzwang">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Gedenktafel_Main-Donau-Kanal.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e7/Gedenktafel_Main-Donau-Kanal.jpg/250px-Gedenktafel_Main-Donau-Kanal.jpg" width="250" height="188" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e7/Gedenktafel_Main-Donau-Kanal.jpg/375px-Gedenktafel_Main-Donau-Kanal.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e7/Gedenktafel_Main-Donau-Kanal.jpg/500px-Gedenktafel_Main-Donau-Kanal.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1280" data-file-height="960" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Gedenktafel_Main-Donau-Kanal.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Memorial to the dam break in the Main-Danube Canal at Katzwang</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The construction work was interrupted on 26 March 1979 by a serious accident in the Nuremberg district of <a href="/wiki/Katzwang" title="Katzwang">Katzwang</a>. A dam broke on the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Eibach_(N%C3%BCrnberg)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Eibach (Nürnberg) (page does not exist)">Eibach</a>–<a href="/wiki/Schwanstetten" title="Schwanstetten">Schwanstetten</a> section, which was still under construction but already flooded. About 350,000 cubic metres (12,000,000&#160;cu&#160;ft) of water poured through the 15-metre (49&#160;ft) wide hole and flooded large parts of old Katzwang.</p>
<p>The force of the water was so great that it dug a 10-metre (33&#160;ft) wide crater and swept away cars, people and houses. During the rescue operations, a 12-year-old girl died. The damage was around 12&#160;million <a href="/wiki/Euro" title="Euro">euros</a><sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>.</p>
<p>After the disaster, the entire canal line was checked for weaknesses and retrofitted at critical points.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Triathlon">Triathlon</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rhine%E2%80%93Main%E2%80%93Danube_Canal&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Triathlon">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The long-distance <a href="/wiki/Challenge_Roth" title="Challenge Roth">Challenge Roth</a> <a href="/wiki/Triathlon" title="Triathlon">triathlon</a>, which is held annually in July, includes a 3.8-kilometre (2.4&#160;mi) swim in the Main-Danube Canal at <a href="/wiki/Hilpoltstein" title="Hilpoltstein">Hilpoltstein</a>. The canal is closed to vessel traffic during the race. An approximately 14-kilometre (8.7&#160;mi)-long section of the <a href="/wiki/Marathon" title="Marathon">marathon</a> route runs along the canal from the Hilpoltstein lock to the Leerstetten lock.</p>
