Construction of the route had a large environmental impact; shown: <i>Kösching Forest</i> near Ingolstadt in 2001.</div>

<p>The northern section, between Nuremberg and <a href="/wiki/Ingolstadt" title="Ingolstadt">Ingolstadt</a>, is a new 300&#160;km/h (186&#160;mph) track built from scratch between 1998 and 2006. It is 90.1&#160;km (56.0&#160;mi) in length with nine tunnels (total length: 27&#160;km or 17&#160;mi). In order to minimize damage to the environment, it runs for the most part right next to <a href="/wiki/Bundesautobahn_9" title="Bundesautobahn 9">Bundesautobahn 9</a>.</p>
<p>The southern section, between <a href="/wiki/Ingolstadt" title="Ingolstadt">Ingolstadt</a> and Munich, is 19th-century track. Its southern section has been upgraded for up to 200&#160;km/h (124&#160;mph). Between 2010 and 2013, further upgrades to the midsection of the track will be done. The minimum speed on the Munich-Ingolstadt section should then be 160&#160;km/h (99&#160;mph), with 190&#160;km/h (118&#160;mph) in the middle and 200&#160;km/h in the southern section.</p>
<p>Both long-distance and regional services operate on the line. <a href="/wiki/InterCityExpress" class="mw-redirect" title="InterCityExpress">InterCityExpress</a> trains reach the tracks' 300&#160;km/h speed-limit. <a href="/wiki/Intercity_(Deutsche_Bahn)" title="Intercity (Deutsche Bahn)">InterCity</a> and <a href="/wiki/RegionalExpress" class="mw-redirect" title="RegionalExpress">RegionalExpress</a> trains travel at a maximum speed of 200&#160;km/h. The <a href="/wiki/Allersberg-Express" title="Allersberg-Express">Allersberg-Express</a>, a <a href="/wiki/RegionalBahn" class="mw-redirect" title="RegionalBahn">RegionalBahn</a> shuttle service, is operated between <a href="/wiki/Allersberg" title="Allersberg">Allersberg</a> and Nuremberg.</p>
<p>The line was officially inaugurated on May 13, 2006. Limited operation with a twice-hourly long-distance service started on May 28, 2006. The line has been in full operation since December 2006. Compared to the former track via <a href="/wiki/Augsburg" title="Augsburg">Augsburg</a>, it cut off 29&#160;km (18&#160;mi), or about 30 minutes journey time on long-distance and an hour on regional trains.</p>
<p>Most of the track is equipped with <a href="/wiki/Linienzugbeeinflussung" title="Linienzugbeeinflussung">Linienzugbeeinflussung</a> and <a href="/wiki/GSM-R" title="GSM-R">GSM-R</a>. <a href="/wiki/European_Train_Control_System" title="European Train Control System">ETCS</a> was planned to be introduced in 2009, although this seems to be delayed until at least 2017. The total costs (as of January 2006) were about <a href="/wiki/Euro" title="Euro">€</a> 3.6 billion.</p>
<p>The line is part of the <a href="/wiki/Berlin%E2%80%93Palermo_railway_axis" title="Berlin–Palermo railway axis">Line 1</a> of <a href="/wiki/Trans-European_Transport_Networks" title="Trans-European Transport Networks">Trans-European Transport Networks</a> (TEN-T).</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nuremberg%E2%80%93Munich_high-speed_railway&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Munich–Ingolstadt line was opened in 1867 and was extended to <a href="/wiki/Treuchtlingen" title="Treuchtlingen">Treuchtlingen</a> as the <a href="/wiki/Ingolstadt%E2%80%93Treuchtlingen_railway" title="Ingolstadt–Treuchtlingen railway">Ingolstadt–Treuchtlingen line</a> in 1870.</p>
<p>The first proposal for a high-speed line dates back to 1983, when the Nuremberg section of <a href="/wiki/Deutsche_Bundesbahn" title="Deutsche Bundesbahn">Deutsche Bundesbahn</a> proposed a more direct line between Nuremberg and Munich. The project was added to the 1985 federal traffic infrastructure plan. The following years were marked by heated debate on the route of the line, in particular if it should run via <a href="/wiki/Ingolstadt" title="Ingolstadt">Ingolstadt</a> or <a href="/wiki/Augsburg" title="Augsburg">Augsburg</a>. While the Ingolstadt line is much more direct (171&#160;km or 106&#160;mi) than the existing Augsburg route (199&#160;km or 124&#160;mi), the metropolitan area of Augsburg is considered much larger than Ingolstadt's. Apart from concerns that fewer long-distance trains would run via (and stop at) Augsburg, there were also concerns about the environmental effects of the 75&#160;km (47&#160;mi) of track that had to be built from scratch. Large-scale construction began in 1998, when numerous disputes had finally been settled and the total cost was estimated to be <a href="/wiki/Euro" title="Euro">€</a> 2.3 billion. The <a href="/wiki/Euro" title="Euro">€</a> 1.3 billion cost increase arose from numerous geological problems found during construction and additional works required to meet environmental and security concerns.</p>
<p>On September 2, 2006, <a href="/wiki/%C3%96sterreichische_Bundesbahnen" class="mw-redirect" title="Österreichische Bundesbahnen">ÖBB</a> locomotive 1216 050 (a <a href="/wiki/Siemens_Eurosprinter" class="mw-redirect" title="Siemens Eurosprinter">Siemens Eurosprinter</a>) set a new world record for locomotives with a top speed of 357&#160;km/h (222&#160;mph); reached near <a href="/wiki/Hilpoltstein" title="Hilpoltstein">Hilpoltstein</a>.</p>
