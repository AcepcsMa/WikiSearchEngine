<p><b><a href="/wiki/Geography" title="Geography">Geography</a></b> is the <a href="/wiki/Science" title="Science">science</a> that studies the lands, the features, the inhabitants, and the phenomena of the <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a>. A literal translation would be "to describe or write about the Earth". The first person to use the word "geography" was <a href="/wiki/Eratosthenes" title="Eratosthenes">Eratosthenes</a> (276–194 BC). Four historical traditions in geographical research are the <a href="/wiki/Spatial_analysis" title="Spatial analysis">spatial analysis</a> of the natural and the <a href="/wiki/Human" title="Human">human</a> phenomena (geography as the study of distribution), the <a href="/wiki/Area_studies" title="Area studies">area studies</a> (places and regions), the study of the human-land relationship, and research in the <a href="/wiki/Earth_science" title="Earth science">Earth sciences</a>. Modern geography is an all-encompassing discipline that foremost seeks to understand the Earth and all of its human and natural complexities—not merely where objects are, but how they have changed and come to be. Geography has been called "the world discipline" and "the bridge between the human and the <a href="/wiki/Physical_science" class="mw-redirect" title="Physical science">physical science</a>". Geography is divided into two main branches: <a href="/wiki/Human_geography" title="Human geography">human geography</a> and <a href="/wiki/Physical_geography" title="Physical geography">physical geography</a>.</p>

<h2 style="font-family:sans-serif; font-size:100%; font-weight:bold; border: none; margin: 0; padding:0; padding-bottom:.1em; color:green;"><span class="mw-headline" id="Featured_article">Featured article</span></h2>

<div style="border: 1px solid #FABD23; border-top-width: 1px; vertical-align: top;background: white;opacity: 1; color: black; text-align: left; margin: 0 0 10px; padding: 1em;padding-top: .3em;">
<div style="float:left;margin-right:1em"><a href="/wiki/File:Flag_of_Cameroon.svg" class="image" title="Cameroon"><img alt="Cameroon" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4f/Flag_of_Cameroon.svg/120px-Flag_of_Cameroon.svg.png" width="120" height="80" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4f/Flag_of_Cameroon.svg/180px-Flag_of_Cameroon.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4f/Flag_of_Cameroon.svg/240px-Flag_of_Cameroon.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="600" data-file-height="400" /></a></div>
The <b><a href="/wiki/Cameroon" title="Cameroon">Republic of Cameroon</a></b> is a <a href="/wiki/Unitary_state" title="Unitary state">unitary republic</a> of central and western <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a>. Cameroon's coastline lies on the <a href="/wiki/Bight_of_Bonny" class="mw-redirect" title="Bight of Bonny">Bight of Bonny</a>, part of the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Guinea" title="Gulf of Guinea">Gulf of Guinea</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">Atlantic Ocean</a>. The country is called "<a href="/wiki/Tourism_in_Cameroon" title="Tourism in Cameroon">Africa in miniature</a>" for its geological and cultural diversity. Natural features include beaches, deserts, mountains, rainforests, and savannas. The highest point is <a href="/wiki/Mount_Cameroon" title="Mount Cameroon">Mount Cameroon</a> in the southwest, and the largest cities are <a href="/wiki/Douala" title="Douala">Douala</a>, <a href="/wiki/Yaound%C3%A9" title="Yaoundé">Yaoundé</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Garoua" title="Garoua">Garoua</a>. Cameroon is home to over 200 different ethnic and linguistic groups. The country is well known for its native styles of music, particularly <a href="/wiki/Makossa" title="Makossa">makossa</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bikutsi" title="Bikutsi">bikutsi</a>, and for its successful <a href="/wiki/Cameroon_national_football_team" title="Cameroon national football team">national football team</a>. English and French are the official languages. In 1960, French <a href="/wiki/Cameroun" class="mw-redirect" title="Cameroun">Cameroun</a> became independent as the Republic of Cameroun under President <a href="/wiki/Ahmadou_Ahidjo" title="Ahmadou Ahidjo">Ahmadou Ahidjo</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Southern_Cameroons" title="Southern Cameroons">southern part</a> of British <a href="/wiki/Cameroons" class="mw-redirect" title="Cameroons">Cameroons</a> merged with it in 1961 to form the Federal Republic of Cameroon. The country was renamed the United Republic of Cameroon in 1972 and the Republic of Cameroon in 1984. Compared with other African countries, Cameroon enjoys political and social stability. This has permitted the development of agriculture, roads, railways, and large petroleum and timber industries. Nevertheless, large numbers of Cameroonians live in poverty as subsistence farmers. Power lies firmly in the hands of the president, <a href="/wiki/Paul_Biya" title="Paul Biya">Paul Biya</a>, and his <a href="/wiki/Cameroon_People%27s_Democratic_Movement" title="Cameroon People's Democratic Movement">Cameroon People's Democratic Movement</a> party, and <a href="/wiki/Political_corruption" title="Political corruption">corruption</a> is widespread.

<h2 style="font-family:sans-serif; font-size:100%; font-weight:bold; border: none; margin: 0; padding:0; padding-bottom:.1em; color:green;"><span class="mw-headline" id="Featured_biography">Featured biography</span></h2>
</div>
<div style="border: 1px solid #FABD23; border-top-width: 1px; vertical-align: top;background: white;opacity: 1; color: black; text-align: left; margin: 0 0 10px; padding: 1em;padding-top: .3em;">
<div style="float:right;margin-left:0.2em"><a href="/wiki/File:Gerhard_von_K%C3%BCgelgen_portrait_of_Friedrich.jpg" class="image" title="Caspar David Friedrich"><img alt="Caspar David Friedrich" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ac/Gerhard_von_K%C3%BCgelgen_portrait_of_Friedrich.jpg/120px-Gerhard_von_K%C3%BCgelgen_portrait_of_Friedrich.jpg" width="120" height="156" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ac/Gerhard_von_K%C3%BCgelgen_portrait_of_Friedrich.jpg/180px-Gerhard_von_K%C3%BCgelgen_portrait_of_Friedrich.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ac/Gerhard_von_K%C3%BCgelgen_portrait_of_Friedrich.jpg/240px-Gerhard_von_K%C3%BCgelgen_portrait_of_Friedrich.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1443" data-file-height="1871" /></a></div>
<b><a href="/wiki/Caspar_David_Friedrich" title="Caspar David Friedrich">Caspar David Friedrich</a></b> was a 19th-century <a href="/wiki/German_Romanticism" title="German Romanticism">German Romantic</a> <a href="/wiki/Landscape_art" class="mw-redirect" title="Landscape art">landscape</a> painter, generally considered the most important of the movement. He is best known for his mid-period <a href="/wiki/Allegorical" class="mw-redirect" title="Allegorical">allegorical</a> landscapes, which typically feature contemplative figures silhouetted against night skies, morning mists, barren trees or <a href="/wiki/Gothic_Architecture" class="mw-redirect" title="Gothic Architecture">Gothic</a> ruins. His primary interest as an artist was the contemplation of nature, and his often symbolic and anti-classical work seeks to convey a subjective, emotional response to the natural world. Friedrich's work characteristically sets the human element in diminished perspective amid expansive landscapes, reducing the figures to a scale that. Friedrich was born in the <a href="/wiki/Swedish_Pomerania" title="Swedish Pomerania">Swedish Pomeranian</a> town of <a href="/wiki/Greifswald" title="Greifswald">Greifswald</a>, where he began his studies in art as a youth. Later, he studied in <a href="/wiki/Copenhagen" title="Copenhagen">Copenhagen</a> until 1798, before settling in <a href="/wiki/Dresden" title="Dresden">Dresden</a>. Friedrich's work brought him renown early in his career, and contemporaries such as the French sculptor <a href="/wiki/Pierre_Jean_David" class="mw-redirect" title="Pierre Jean David">David d'Angers</a> spoke of him as a man who had discovered "the tragedy of landscape". However, his work fell from favour during his later years, and he died in obscurity. By the 1920s his paintings had been discovered by the <a href="/wiki/Expressionist" class="mw-redirect" title="Expressionist">Expressionists</a>, and in the 1930s and early 1940s <a href="/wiki/Surrealist" class="mw-redirect" title="Surrealist">Surrealists</a> and <a href="/wiki/Existentialist" class="mw-redirect" title="Existentialist">Existentialists</a> frequently drew ideas from his work.
