<p>The <b>nave</b> <span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'n' in 'nigh'">n</span><span title="/eɪ/: 'a' in 'face'">eɪ</span><span title="'v' in 'vie'">v</span></span>/</a></span></span> is the central <a href="/wiki/Aisle" title="Aisle">aisle</a> of a <a href="/wiki/Basilica" title="Basilica">basilica</a> <a href="/wiki/Church_architecture" title="Church architecture">church</a>, or the main body of a church (whether aisled or not) between its rear wall and the far end of its intersection with the <a href="/wiki/Transcept" class="mw-redirect" title="Transcept">transcept</a> at the <a href="/wiki/Chancel" title="Chancel">chancel</a>. It is the zone of a church accessible by the <a href="/wiki/Laity" title="Laity">laity</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nave&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The nave extends from the entry — which may have a separate vestibule (the <a href="/wiki/Narthex" title="Narthex">narthex</a>) — to the chancel and may be flanked by lower <a href="/wiki/Aisle#Church_architecture" title="Aisle">side-aisles</a><sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> separated from the nave by an <a href="/wiki/Arcade_(architecture)" title="Arcade (architecture)">arcade</a>. If the aisles are high and of a width comparable to the central nave, the structure is sometimes said to have three naves. It provides the central approach to the high <a href="/wiki/Altar" title="Altar">altar</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nave&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The term <i>nave</i> is from <i>navis</i>, the <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> word for <i>ship</i>, an early Christian symbol.<sup id="cite_ref-newadvent_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-newadvent-3">[3]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> The term may also have been suggested by the keel shape of the <a href="/wiki/Vault_(architecture)" title="Vault (architecture)">vaulting</a> of a church. In many Scandinavian and Baltic countries a model ship is commonly found hanging in the nave of a church,<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> and in some languages the same word means both 'nave' and 'ship', as for instance Danish <i><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/skib" class="extiw" title="wikt:skib">skib</a></i> or Swedish <i><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/skepp" class="extiw" title="wikt:skepp">skepp</a></i>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nave&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Affresco_dell%27aspetto_antico_della_basilica_costantiniana_di_san_pietro_nel_IV_secolo.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3d/Affresco_dell%27aspetto_antico_della_basilica_costantiniana_di_san_pietro_nel_IV_secolo.jpg/220px-Affresco_dell%27aspetto_antico_della_basilica_costantiniana_di_san_pietro_nel_IV_secolo.jpg" width="220" height="203" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3d/Affresco_dell%27aspetto_antico_della_basilica_costantiniana_di_san_pietro_nel_IV_secolo.jpg/330px-Affresco_dell%27aspetto_antico_della_basilica_costantiniana_di_san_pietro_nel_IV_secolo.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3d/Affresco_dell%27aspetto_antico_della_basilica_costantiniana_di_san_pietro_nel_IV_secolo.jpg/440px-Affresco_dell%27aspetto_antico_della_basilica_costantiniana_di_san_pietro_nel_IV_secolo.jpg 2x" data-file-width="942" data-file-height="870" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Affresco_dell%27aspetto_antico_della_basilica_costantiniana_di_san_pietro_nel_IV_secolo.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A fresco showing Old St Peter's Basilica, built in the 4th century: the central area, illuminated by high windows, is flanked by aisles.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The earliest churches were built when builders were familiar with the form of the Roman <a href="/wiki/Basilica" title="Basilica">basilica</a>, a public building for business transactions. It had a wide central area, with aisles separated by columns, and with windows near the ceiling. <a href="/wiki/Old_St._Peter%27s_Basilica" title="Old St. Peter's Basilica">Old St. Peter's Basilica</a> in Rome is an early church which had this form. It was built in the 4th century on the orders of Roman emperor <a href="/wiki/Constantine_I" class="mw-redirect" title="Constantine I">Constantine I</a>, and replaced in the 16th century.<sup id="cite_ref-newadvent_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-newadvent-3">[3]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-britannica_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-britannica-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>The nave, the main body of the building, is the section set apart for the laity, while the <a href="/wiki/Chancel" title="Chancel">chancel</a> is reserved for the clergy. In medieval churches the nave was separated from the chancel by the <a href="/wiki/Rood_screen" title="Rood screen">rood screen</a>; these, being elaborately decorated, were notable features in European churches from the 14th to the mid-16th century.<sup id="cite_ref-newadvent_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-newadvent-3">[3]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-britannica_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-britannica-6">[6]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Medieval naves were divided into bays, the repetition of form giving an effect of great length; and the vertical element of the nave was emphasized. During the Renaissance, in place of dramatic effects there were more balanced proportions.<sup id="cite_ref-britannica_6-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-britannica-6">[6]</a></sup></p>

<li>Longest nave in the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>: <a href="/wiki/Cathedral_of_Saint_John_the_Divine" title="Cathedral of Saint John the Divine">Cathedral of Saint John the Divine</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a>, United States (Episcopal), 70&#160;m (230&#160;ft)</li>
<li>Highest vaulted nave: <a href="/wiki/Beauvais_Cathedral" title="Beauvais Cathedral">Beauvais Cathedral</a>, France, 48&#160;m (157&#160;ft), but only one bay of the nave was actually built; however, choir and transepts were completed to the same height.</li>
<li>Highest completed nave: <a href="/wiki/St._Peter%27s_Basilica" title="St. Peter's Basilica">Rome, St. Peter's</a>, Italy, 46&#160;m (151&#160;ft)</li>
</ul>
