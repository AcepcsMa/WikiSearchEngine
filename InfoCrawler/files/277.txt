<p>The primary administrative subdivision of German states is called a <span lang="de" xml:lang="de"><i><b>Landkreis</b></i></span> ("rural <a href="/wiki/District" title="District">district</a>"), except in the states of <a href="/wiki/North_Rhine-Westphalia" title="North Rhine-Westphalia">North Rhine-Westphalia</a> and <span lang="de" xml:lang="de"><a href="/wiki/Schleswig-Holstein" title="Schleswig-Holstein">Schleswig-Holstein</a></span> where it is called simply a <i><b><span lang="de" xml:lang="de">Kreis</span></b></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Most major cities in Germany are not part of a rural district, but perform district-like functions on their own. In this context, those cities are referred to as <i><b><span lang="de" xml:lang="de">Kreisfreie Stadt</span></b></i> (literally "district-free town") or <i><b><span lang="de" xml:lang="de">Stadtkreis</span></b></i> ("urban district").</p>
<p>Rural districts are at an intermediate level of administration between the <a href="/wiki/States_of_Germany" title="States of Germany">German states</a> (<i><span lang="de" xml:lang="de">Länder</span></i>) and the municipal governments (<i><span lang="de" xml:lang="de"><a href="/wiki/States_of_Germany#Municipalities_.28Gemeinden.29" title="States of Germany">Gemeinden</a></span></i>).<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> They correspond to level 3 administrative units of the <a href="/wiki/Nomenclature_of_Territorial_Units_for_Statistics" title="Nomenclature of Territorial Units for Statistics">Nomenclature of Territorial Units for Statistics</a> (NUTS 3), and are roughly equivalent to <a href="/wiki/County" title="County">counties</a> in the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>.</p>
<p>Previously, the similar title <i><span lang="de" xml:lang="de">Reichskreis</span></i> (<a href="/wiki/Imperial_Circle" title="Imperial Circle">Imperial Circle</a>) was given to groups of states in the <a href="/wiki/Holy_Roman_Empire" title="Holy Roman Empire">Holy Roman Empire</a>. The related term <i><span lang="de" xml:lang="de">Landeskommissariat</span></i> was used for similar administrative divisions in some German territories until the 19th century.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Types_of_districts">Types of districts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Districts_of_Germany&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Types of districts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:352px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Administrative_divisions_of_Germany.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3b/Administrative_divisions_of_Germany.svg/350px-Administrative_divisions_of_Germany.svg.png" width="350" height="175" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3b/Administrative_divisions_of_Germany.svg/525px-Administrative_divisions_of_Germany.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3b/Administrative_divisions_of_Germany.svg/700px-Administrative_divisions_of_Germany.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="1264" data-file-height="632" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Administrative_divisions_of_Germany.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Administrative divisions of Germany</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The majority of <a href="/wiki/List_of_districts_of_Germany" title="List of districts of Germany">German districts</a> are "rural districts"<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> (German: <span lang="de" xml:lang="de"><i>Landkreise</i></span>) of which there are currently (2017) 294. Cities with more than 100,000 inhabitants (and smaller towns in some states) do not usually belong to a district, but take over district responsibilities themselves, similar to the concept of <a href="/wiki/Independent_city" title="Independent city">independent cities</a>. These are known as "urban districts" (German: <span lang="de" xml:lang="de"><i>Kreisfreie Städte</i> or <i>Stadtkreise</i></span>)—cities which constitute a district in their own right—and there are 107 of them,<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> bringing the total number of districts to 401. As of 2016, approximately 26 million people live in these 107 urban districts.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/North_Rhine-Westphalia" title="North Rhine-Westphalia">North Rhine-Westphalia</a>, there are some cities with more than 100,000 inhabitants which are not urban districts, for example <a href="/wiki/Recklinghausen" title="Recklinghausen">Recklinghausen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Siegen" title="Siegen">Siegen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Paderborn" title="Paderborn">Paderborn</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bergisch_Gladbach" title="Bergisch Gladbach">Bergisch Gladbach</a>, <a href="/wiki/Neuss" title="Neuss">Neuss</a> and <a href="/wiki/Moers" title="Moers">Moers</a>. Nevertheless, these cities take over many district responsibilities themselves, although they are still part of a larger rural district. Midsize towns can perform particular administrative functions of the district as well, especially to provide common services to the local citizens. The classification as "midsize" town is usually based on a town's registered population, but varies from state to state.</p>
<p>A special type of rural districts are the three so-called <span lang="de" xml:lang="de"><i>Kommunalverbände besonderer Art</i></span> (<i>Municipal unions of special kind</i>), a fusion of a district-free town with its adjacent rural district: Besides the <a href="/wiki/Saarbr%C3%BCcken_(district)" title="Saarbrücken (district)">Regionalverband Saarbrücken</a> (<i>Saarbrücken regional association</i>), from 1974 until 2007 called "Stadtverband Saarbrücken" (<i>Saarbrücken town association</i>), there is the <a href="/wiki/Hanover_Region" title="Hanover Region">Hanover Region</a> since 2001 and the <a href="/wiki/Aachen_(district)#History" title="Aachen (district)">Städteregion Aachen</a> (<i>Aachen region of towns</i>) since 2009. Aachen, Hanover and <a href="/wiki/G%C3%B6ttingen" title="Göttingen">Göttingen</a> retain certain rights of an urban district (<i>Kreisfreie Stadt</i>); Saarbrücken has not explicitly determined a similar provision in its legislation.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Responsibilities">Responsibilities</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Districts_of_Germany&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Responsibilities">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Landkreise,_Kreise_und_kreisfreie_St%C3%A4dte_in_Deutschland_2011-09-04.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7e/Landkreise%2C_Kreise_und_kreisfreie_St%C3%A4dte_in_Deutschland_2011-09-04.svg/220px-Landkreise%2C_Kreise_und_kreisfreie_St%C3%A4dte_in_Deutschland_2011-09-04.svg.png" width="220" height="291" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7e/Landkreise%2C_Kreise_und_kreisfreie_St%C3%A4dte_in_Deutschland_2011-09-04.svg/330px-Landkreise%2C_Kreise_und_kreisfreie_St%C3%A4dte_in_Deutschland_2011-09-04.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7e/Landkreise%2C_Kreise_und_kreisfreie_St%C3%A4dte_in_Deutschland_2011-09-04.svg/440px-Landkreise%2C_Kreise_und_kreisfreie_St%C3%A4dte_in_Deutschland_2011-09-04.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="9463" data-file-height="12526" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Landkreise,_Kreise_und_kreisfreie_St%C3%A4dte_in_Deutschland_2011-09-04.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Map of German districts. Yellow districts are urban, white are rural.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>According to common federal and state laws, the districts are responsible for the following tasks:</p>
<ul>
<li>The building and upkeep of "district roads" (German: <a href="/wiki/Kreisstra%C3%9Fe" title="Kreisstraße">Kreisstraßen</a>)</li>
<li>Other building plans which cover more than one local authority's area</li>
<li>Caring for national parks</li>
<li>Social welfare</li>
<li>Youth welfare</li>
<li>The building and upkeep of hospitals</li>
<li>The building and upkeep of state schools of secondary education</li>
<li>Household waste collection and disposal</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Vehicle_registration_plates_of_Germany" title="Vehicle registration plates of Germany">Car registration</a></li>
<li>Accommodation of foreign <a href="/wiki/Refugees" class="mw-redirect" title="Refugees">refugees</a></li>
<li>Electing the <span lang="de" xml:lang="de"><i>Landrat</i></span> or <span lang="de" xml:lang="de"><i>Landrätin</i></span>, the chief executive and representative of the district</li>
</ul>
<p>Districts can perform additional functions, based on varying local laws in each region:</p>
<ul>
<li>Financial support for culture</li>
<li>The building of pedestrian zones and <a href="/wiki/Bicycle_lane" class="mw-redirect" title="Bicycle lane">bicycle lanes</a></li>
<li>Financial support for school exchanges</li>
<li>The building and upkeep of public libraries</li>
<li>Revitalisation of the economy</li>
<li>Encouraging tourism</li>
<li>The management of state-run adult education colleges (German: <span lang="de" xml:lang="de"><i><a href="/wiki/Volkshochschule" class="mw-redirect" title="Volkshochschule">Volkshochschulen</a></i></span>)</li>
</ul>
<p>All these tasks are carried out by local (municipal) authorities operating together. Urban districts have these responsibilities and also those of the municipalities.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="District_council">District council</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Districts_of_Germany&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: District council">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The district council (German: <span lang="de" xml:lang="de"><i>Kreistag</i></span>) is the highest institution of a rural district and is responsible for all fundamental guidelines of regional self-administration. This council is elected directly every five years, except in <a href="/wiki/Bavaria" title="Bavaria">Bavaria</a> where it is elected every six years. Usually the administrative seat of a rural district is located in one of its largest towns. However, district council and administrative seat of some rural districts are not situated within the district proper, but in an adjacent district-free city. Most of those rural districts are named after this central city as well (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Bamberg" title="Bamberg">Bamberg</a> and <a href="/wiki/Karlsruhe" title="Karlsruhe">Karlsruhe</a>). <a href="/wiki/Moers" title="Moers">Moers</a> is the biggest city in Germany (and at present time the only one with more than 100,000 inhabitants) that is neither an urban district, nor the district seat of an adjacent rural district.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="District_administration">District administration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Districts_of_Germany&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: District administration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The highest administrative position of a rural district is an officer known as <span lang="de" xml:lang="de"><i>Landrat</i></span> or <span lang="de" xml:lang="de"><i>Landrätin</i></span>, who is responsible for the district's day-to-day administration and acts as its representative for official purposes. In parts of northern Germany, <i>Landrat</i> is also the name of the entire district administration, which in southern Germany is known as <span lang="de" xml:lang="de"><i>Kreisverwaltung</i></span> or <span lang="de" xml:lang="de"><i>Landratsamt</i></span>.</p>
<p>In urban districts similar administrative functions are performed by a mayor, in most greater cities usually by the <a href="/wiki/Lord_Mayor" class="mw-redirect" title="Lord Mayor">Lord Mayor</a>.</p>
<p>Rural districts in some German states have an additional administrative commission called <i>Kreisausschuss</i>. This commission is generally led by the Landrat and includes a number of additional voluntary members. It takes over certain administrative functions for the district, following decisions of the district council. However, the exact role and regulations of this panel vary greatly between different states.</p>
