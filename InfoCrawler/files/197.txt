<p><b>Richard David Shepherd</b> <a href="/wiki/Order_of_the_British_Empire" title="Order of the British Empire">CBE</a> <a href="/wiki/Royal_Society_of_Arts" title="Royal Society of Arts">FRSA</a> <a href="/wiki/Guild_of_Railway_Artists" title="Guild of Railway Artists">FGRA</a> (25 April 1931 – 19 September 2017)<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> was a British artist and one of the world's most outspoken <a href="/wiki/Conservation_movement" title="Conservation movement">conservationists</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-gower_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gower-2">[2]</a></sup> He was most famous for his paintings of <a href="/wiki/Steam_locomotives" class="mw-redirect" title="Steam locomotives">steam locomotives</a> (he owned a number of them) and <a href="/wiki/Wildlife" title="Wildlife">wildlife</a>, although he also often painted aircraft, portraits (notably <a href="/wiki/Queen_Elizabeth_The_Queen_Mother" title="Queen Elizabeth The Queen Mother">The Queen Mother</a>) and landscapes. His work has been extremely popular since the 1960s in <a href="/wiki/Limited_edition" class="mw-redirect" title="Limited edition">limited edition</a> print reproduction and <a href="/wiki/Poster" title="Poster">poster</a> form, as well as other media such as <a href="/wiki/Wedgwood" title="Wedgwood">Wedgwood</a> limited edition plates. He published five books about his art,<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> including an autobiography.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Life_and_work">Life and work</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=David_Shepherd_(artist)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Life and work">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:282px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Shepherd-Elephants.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/2/2f/Shepherd-Elephants.jpg/280px-Shepherd-Elephants.jpg" width="280" height="159" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/2/2f/Shepherd-Elephants.jpg 1.5x" data-file-width="419" data-file-height="238" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Shepherd-Elephants.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
David Shepherd. <i>Three Tuskers</i></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>David Shepherd was born in <a href="/wiki/Hendon" title="Hendon">Hendon</a>, London, England.<sup id="cite_ref-davidshepherd_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-davidshepherd-5">[5]</a></sup> As a child he lived in <a href="/wiki/Totteridge" title="Totteridge">Totteridge</a>, <a href="/wiki/North_London" title="North London">North London</a> and he won a children's painting competition in a magazine called <i>Nursery World</i> when he was eight years old. He then attended <a href="/wiki/Stowe_School" title="Stowe School">Stowe School</a> in Buckinghamshire. Upon leaving school he travelled to <a href="/wiki/Kenya" title="Kenya">Kenya</a> with the hope of becoming a <a href="/wiki/Game_warden" title="Game warden">game warden</a>, but was rejected as having "no talent whatsoever".<sup id="cite_ref-davidshepherd_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-davidshepherd-5">[5]</a></sup> He returned to the UK but was rejected by the <a href="/wiki/Slade_School_of_Fine_Art" title="Slade School of Fine Art">Slade School of Fine Art</a> in London.<sup id="cite_ref-org_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-org-6">[6]</a></sup> However, he was taken in by the artist <a href="/w/index.php?title=Robin_Goodwin&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Robin Goodwin (page does not exist)">Robin Goodwin</a> who trained him for three years.<sup id="cite_ref-org_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-org-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>Neal Brown said in <i><a href="/wiki/Frieze_(magazine)" title="Frieze (magazine)">frieze</a></i> magazine: "David Shepherd is one of the most financially rewarded painters in the UK... Shepherd has brought pleasure to millions, as seen on the many table mats, posters and commemorative plates that bear his work."<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/David_Gower" title="David Gower">David Gower</a> said, "There is a sense of the atmosphere of the African bush that emanates from all his work."<sup id="cite_ref-gower_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gower-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Conservationist">Conservationist</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=David_Shepherd_(artist)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Conservationist">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Shepherd became interested in <a href="/wiki/Conservation_ethic" class="mw-redirect" title="Conservation ethic">conservation</a> during an early expedition into the African bush, where he discovered a poisoned water hole with a large number of dead <a href="/wiki/Zebra" title="Zebra">zebra</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-gower_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gower-2">[2]</a></sup> He had become an outspoken world-known campaigner,<sup id="cite_ref-gower_2-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gower-2">[2]</a></sup> and devoted much of his time to this. He was also a steam railway enthusiast, but said in a letter to the UK's <i><a href="/wiki/The_Railway_Magazine" title="The Railway Magazine">The Railway Magazine</a></i>, "you can always build another steam loco but you can't build another tiger." One of his best known paintings, <i>Tiger in The Sun</i>, was painted in 1977. His paintings are not only prized by art collectors but have also raised huge sums for conservation. His first major fund-raising success was a work titled <i>Tiger Fire</i> which raised £127,000 for <a href="/wiki/Indira_Gandhi" title="Indira Gandhi">Indira Gandhi</a>’s Operation Tiger in 1973. Shephard was also known for his paintings of elephants and among his most well known works were <i>The Ivory is Theirs</i> and <i>Wise Old Elephant</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-express_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-express-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>He was the founder of the David Shepherd Wildlife Foundation<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> and was made an <a href="/wiki/Officer_of_the_Order_of_the_British_Empire" class="mw-redirect" title="Officer of the Order of the British Empire">Officer of the Order of the British Empire</a> (OBE) in the 1980 <a href="/wiki/New_Year_Honours" title="New Year Honours">New Year Honours</a> list "for services to the conservation of wildlife."<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> He was appointed a <a href="/wiki/Commander_of_the_Order_of_the_British_Empire" class="mw-redirect" title="Commander of the Order of the British Empire">Commander of the Order of the British Empire</a> (CBE) in the 2008 <a href="/wiki/Birthday_Honours" title="Birthday Honours">Birthday Honours</a> list for services to charity and wildlife conservation.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Steam_enthusiast">Steam enthusiast</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=David_Shepherd_(artist)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Steam enthusiast">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:HGG-DavidAvrilShepherd15FKimberley.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/11/HGG-DavidAvrilShepherd15FKimberley.jpg/220px-HGG-DavidAvrilShepherd15FKimberley.jpg" width="220" height="330" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/11/HGG-DavidAvrilShepherd15FKimberley.jpg/330px-HGG-DavidAvrilShepherd15FKimberley.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/11/HGG-DavidAvrilShepherd15FKimberley.jpg/440px-HGG-DavidAvrilShepherd15FKimberley.jpg 2x" data-file-width="800" data-file-height="1200" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:HGG-DavidAvrilShepherd15FKimberley.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Shepherd (left) and his wife Avril receiving <a href="/wiki/South_African_Class_15F_4-8-2" title="South African Class 15F 4-8-2">15F</a> 3052 named <i>Avril</i>, 1991</div>
</div>

<p>Shepherd owned a number of <a href="/wiki/Steam_locomotive" title="Steam locomotive">steam locomotives</a>. He bought both <a href="/wiki/BR_Standard_Class_9F" title="BR Standard Class 9F">9F</a> <i>Black Prince</i> <a href="/wiki/BR_standard_class_9F_92203" class="mw-redirect" title="BR standard class 9F 92203">92203</a> and <a href="/wiki/BR_standard_class_4_4-6-0" class="mw-redirect" title="BR standard class 4 4-6-0">Class 4MT</a> No.75029 <i>The Green Knight</i> direct from <a href="/wiki/British_Railways" class="mw-redirect" title="British Railways">British Railways</a>, where he had connections from painting various scenes for them.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> No.75029 <i>The Green Knight</i> was sold to the <a href="/wiki/North_Yorkshire_Moors_Railway" title="North Yorkshire Moors Railway">North Yorkshire Moors Railway</a> to finance <i>Black Prince</i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span>s overhaul, then in 2015 <i>Black Prince</i> was sold to the <a href="/wiki/North_Norfolk_Railway" title="North Norfolk Railway">North Norfolk Railway</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>All the African locomotives he acquired were British-built. In South Africa the <a href="/wiki/South_African_Class_15F_4-8-2" title="South African Class 15F 4-8-2">15F</a> (Nº 3052), presented to him by <a href="/wiki/Spoornet" class="mw-redirect" title="Spoornet">Spoornet</a> in 1991, is stored at <a href="/wiki/Sandstone_Estates" title="Sandstone Estates">Sandstone Estates</a> in <a href="/wiki/Ficksburg" title="Ficksburg">Ficksburg</a>. It has carried various names, including <i>City of Germiston</i> and, more recently, his wife's name, <i>Avril</i>. It was moved to Ficksburg in light steam from <a href="/wiki/Pietermaritzburg" title="Pietermaritzburg">Pietermaritzburg</a> by Friends of the Rail (a <a href="/wiki/Pretoria" title="Pretoria">Pretoria</a>-based heritage steam association) in April 2003 and it steamed again in April 2006, when Friends of the Rail operated it for several trips between <a href="/wiki/Ficksburg" title="Ficksburg">Ficksburg</a> and <a href="/wiki/Magaliesberg" title="Magaliesberg">Kommandonek</a> with Shepherd on board. He also owned two Zambian locomotives from the <a href="/wiki/Mulobezi_Railway" title="Mulobezi Railway">Mulobezi Railway</a>, given to him by then President <a href="/wiki/Kenneth_Kaunda" title="Kenneth Kaunda">Kenneth Kaunda</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Gift_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gift-15">[15]</a></sup> One is still in the railway museum in <a href="/wiki/Livingstone,_Zambia" title="Livingstone, Zambia">Livingstone, Zambia</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-Livingstone_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Livingstone-16">[16]</a></sup> the other was located on the <a href="/wiki/Sandstone_Estates" title="Sandstone Estates">Sandstone Estates</a> complex in South Africa. Shepherd donated it, along with a coach, to the <a href="/wiki/National_Railway_Museum" title="National Railway Museum">National Railway Museum</a> in <a href="/wiki/York" title="York">York</a> in the UK, where it is undergoing restoration.<sup id="cite_ref-Yorkloco_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Yorkloco-17">[17]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Yorkcoach_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Yorkcoach-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>Shepherd was involved in founding a heritage steam railway in the UK, the <a href="/wiki/East_Somerset_Railway" title="East Somerset Railway">East Somerset Railway</a>, where the signal box at <a href="/wiki/Cranmore_railway_station" title="Cranmore railway station">Cranmore Station</a> has become a small gallery displaying his work. He was also president of the "Railway Ramblers".<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life_and_death">Personal life and death</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=David_Shepherd_(artist)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Personal life and death">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Shepherd's parents gave him a <a href="/wiki/Victorian_era" title="Victorian era">Victorian</a> cottage, with more than an acre of land, in <a href="/wiki/Frensham" title="Frensham">Frensham</a>, Surrey, next to their own house, to encourage him to get engaged to his future wife Avril. The couple had four daughters and lived in <a href="/wiki/West_Sussex" title="West Sussex">West Sussex</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> Their daughter Mandy is also a wildlife and military artist.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>On 20 September 2017 the Surrey-based David Shepherd Wildlife Foundation (DSWF) announced that Shepherd had died, on 19 September, after a 10-week fight in hospital with <a href="/wiki/Parkinson%27s_disease" title="Parkinson's disease">Parkinson's disease</a>. The charity paid tribute to his devotion to the animals he painted, with chief executive Karen Botha saying, "David’s passion for wildlife and the role of man in its demise infuriated and inspired him. He was dedicated, tenacious and outspoken, a champion of animals and the people who worked to protect them. He will be greatly missed."<sup id="cite_ref-express_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-express-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>Julian Birley, chairman of the <a href="/wiki/North_Norfolk_Railway" title="North Norfolk Railway">North Norfolk Railway</a>, said: "It is the end of an era. A great man who will forever be credited as one of this country’s greatest pioneers of railway preservation. And in so doing brought pleasure to hundreds of thousands of people."<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
