<p><b>Hartmann Schedel</b> (13 February 1440 – 28 November 1514) was a German physician, <a href="/wiki/Humanism" title="Humanism">humanist</a>, <a href="/wiki/Historian" title="Historian">historian</a>, and one of the first <a href="/wiki/Cartographer" class="mw-redirect" title="Cartographer">cartographers</a> to use the <a href="/wiki/Printing_press" title="Printing press">printing press</a>. He was born and died in <a href="/wiki/Nuremberg" title="Nuremberg">Nuremberg</a>. <a href="/wiki/Matheolus_Perusinus" title="Matheolus Perusinus">Matheolus Perusinus</a> served as his <a href="/wiki/Tutor" title="Tutor">tutor</a>.</p>
<p>Schedel is best known for his writing the text for the <i><a href="/wiki/Nuremberg_Chronicle" title="Nuremberg Chronicle">Nuremberg Chronicle</a></i>, known as <i>Schedelsche Weltchronik</i> (English: <i>Schedel's World Chronicle</i>), published in 1493 in Nuremberg. It was commissioned by Sebald Schreyer (1446 – 1520) and Sebastian Kammermeister (1446 – 1503).<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Maps in the <i>Chronicle</i> were the first ever illustrations of many cities and countries.</p>
<p>With the <a href="/wiki/Movable_type" title="Movable type">invention of the printing press</a> by <a href="/wiki/Johannes_Gutenberg" title="Johannes Gutenberg">Johannes Gutenberg</a> in 1447, it became feasible to print books and maps for a larger customer basis. Because they had to be handwritten, books were previously rare and very expensive.</p>
<p>Schedel was also a notable collector of books, art and <a href="/wiki/Old_master_print" title="Old master print">old master prints</a>. An album he had bound in 1504, which once contained five <a href="/wiki/Engraving" title="Engraving">engravings</a> by <a href="/wiki/Jacopo_de%27_Barbari" title="Jacopo de' Barbari">Jacopo de' Barbari</a>, provides important evidence for dating de' Barbari's work.</p>
