			<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Hans Folz</h1>
									<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
									<div id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
								<div id="contentSub"></div>

<p><b>Hans Folz</b> (<abbr title="circa">c.</abbr> 1437 – January 1513) was a German author of the late medieval or <a href="/wiki/German_Renaissance" title="German Renaissance">early Renaissance</a> period.</p>
<p>Folz was born in <a href="/wiki/Worms,_Germany" title="Worms, Germany">Worms</a>. He was made a citizen of the city of <a href="/wiki/Nuremberg" title="Nuremberg">Nuremberg</a>, Germany in 1459 and master barber of the city in 1486. Folz was a reformer of the meistersangs, adding 27 new tones to those that had been allowed by the twelve "Alten Meister" (old masters) up to that point. His <i>Meisterlieder</i> (a type of song), of which he wrote about a thousand, were mostly devoted to religious questions. He also wrote twelve <i>Fastnachtsspiele</i> (short plays that made light of people in medieval society, for instance farmers, priests, and the <a href="/wiki/Bourgeoisie" title="Bourgeoisie">bourgeoisie</a>) in the same style as <a href="/w/index.php?title=Hans_Rosenpl%C3%BCt&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Hans Rosenplüt (page does not exist)">Hans Rosenplüt</a>, but with more subtle language.</p>
<p>Folz has been labelled as an <a href="/wiki/Anti-semitism" class="mw-redirect" title="Anti-semitism">anti-semite</a> by <a href="/wiki/Andrew_Gow" title="Andrew Gow">Andrew Gow</a>, who called him "a thorough-going Jew-hater" for his work <i>Ein Spil von dem Herzogen von Burgund</i> (<i>A Story of the Dukes of Burgund</i>).<sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_1"><a class="external autonumber" href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hans_Folz#endnote_1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>According to <a href="/w/index.php?title=Albert_Wimmer&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Albert Wimmer (page does not exist)">Albert Wimmer</a>'s <i>Anthology of Medieval German Literature</i>, "Folz's plays were trendsetters in the development of moderately dramatic plays (so-called «Handlungsspiele»)".<sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_2"><a class="external autonumber" href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hans_Folz#endnote_2">[2]</a></sup>.</p>
<p>His name was adapted by <a href="/wiki/Richard_Wagner" title="Richard Wagner">Richard Wagner</a> for the coppersmith "Hans Foltz," one of the Mastersingers portrayed in the opera <i><a href="/wiki/Die_Meistersinger_von_N%C3%BCrnberg" title="Die Meistersinger von Nürnberg">Die Meistersinger von Nürnberg</a></i>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Works">Works</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hans_Folz&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Works">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul>
<li><i>Bäderbüchlein</i> (<i>Bath Booklet</i>, in 1480) <i>Dises puchlein saget vns von allen paden, die von natur heiß sein.</i> <sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_3"><a class="external autonumber" href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hans_Folz#endnote_3">[3]</a></sup>. <a rel="nofollow" class="external autonumber" href="http://www.rcs-krueger.de/BadBuch.htm">[4]</a></li>
<li><i>Hausratbüchlein</i> (<i>Furniture Booklet</i>, in 1490)</li>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/Solomon_and_Marcolf" title="Solomon and Marcolf">Das Spiel von dem König Salomon und dem Bauern Markolf <i>(</i>The Story of King Salomon and the Farmer Markolf<i>)</i></a></i></li>
<li><i>Von einem Kaiser und einem Abt</i> (<i>Of an Emperor and an Abbott</i>)</li>
<li><i>Die Bauernheirat</i> (<i>The Farmer's Marriage</i>)</li>
<li><i>Ein Spiel von dem Herzogen von Burgund</i> (<i>A Story of the Dukes of Burgund</i>)</li>
</ul>
<p>Folz is also the author of a <a href="/wiki/Fechtbuch" class="mw-redirect" title="Fechtbuch">fechtbuch</a> of c. 1480, preserved in <a href="/wiki/Weimar" title="Weimar">Weimar</a> as MS Q566.</p>
