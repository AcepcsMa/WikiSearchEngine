<p><b>Sacramental bread</b> (Latin: <i>hostia</i>), sometimes called <b>altar bread</b>, <b>Communion bread</b>, the <b><a href="/wiki/Lamb_(liturgy)" title="Lamb (liturgy)">Lamb</a></b> or simply the <b>host</b>, is the bread which is used in the Christian ritual of the <a href="/wiki/Eucharist" title="Eucharist">Eucharist</a>. Both Eastern and Western traditions insist that the bread must be made from wheat.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic" class="mw-redirect" title="Roman Catholic">Roman Catholic</a> theology generally teaches that at the <a href="/wiki/Words_of_Institution" title="Words of Institution">Words of Institution</a> the bread is changed into the <a href="/wiki/Body_of_Christ" title="Body of Christ">Body of Christ</a> (see <a href="/wiki/Transubstantiation" title="Transubstantiation">transubstantiation</a>), whereas Eastern Christian theology generally views the <a href="/wiki/Epiclesis" title="Epiclesis">epiclesis</a> as the point at which the change occurs. Some <a href="/wiki/Protestants" class="mw-redirect" title="Protestants">Protestants</a> believe <a href="/wiki/Transignification" title="Transignification">transignification</a> occurs at the <a href="/wiki/Words_of_Institution" title="Words of Institution">Words of Institution</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology_of_host">Etymology of <i>host</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sacramental_bread&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology of host">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The word "host" is derived from the <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Glossary_of_ancient_Roman_religion#hostia" title="Glossary of ancient Roman religion">hostia</a></i>, which means "<a href="/wiki/Sacrifice" title="Sacrifice">sacrificial victim</a>". The term can be used to describe the bread both before and after <a href="/wiki/Consecration" title="Consecration">consecration</a>, though it is more correct to use it after consecration. Prior to consecration, the term "altar bread" is preferred. The sacrifice of Jesus according to Christian theology puts an end to the need for animal sacrifice as had been practiced in the Jerusalem Temple and all blood sacrifice once-for-all. However the word was retained to describe the bread of Eucharist as a liturgical representation of the Christ's sacrifice.</p>

<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Prosphora" title="Prosphora">Prosphora</a> and <a href="/wiki/Azymes" title="Azymes">Azymes</a></div>
<p>With the exception of Churches of the <a href="/wiki/Armenian_Rite" title="Armenian Rite">Armenian Rite</a>, <a href="/wiki/Orthodox_Church" class="mw-redirect" title="Orthodox Church">Orthodox Churches</a> and <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Catholic_Churches" title="Eastern Catholic Churches">Eastern Catholic Churches</a> use <a href="/wiki/Leaven" class="mw-redirect" title="Leaven">leavened</a> bread for the Eucharist. Thus, the sacramental bread symbolizes the <a href="/wiki/Resurrection_of_Jesus" title="Resurrection of Jesus">Resurrected Christ</a>. The <i>hostia</i> or sacramental bread, known as <i><a href="/wiki/Prosphora" title="Prosphora">prosphorá</a></i> or a πρόσφορον (<i>prósphoron</i>, "offering") may be made out of only four ingredients: fine (white) <a href="/wiki/Wheat_flour" title="Wheat flour">wheat flour</a>, pure water, yeast, and salt. Sometimes <a href="/wiki/Holy_water" title="Holy water">holy water</a> will be either sprinkled into the dough or on the kneading trough at the beginning of the process.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Armenian_Rite">Armenian Rite</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sacramental_bread&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Armenian Rite">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Because leaven is symbolic of sin, the <a href="/wiki/Armenian_Catholic_Church" title="Armenian Catholic Church">Armenian Catholic Church</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Armenian_Apostolic_Orthodox_Church" class="mw-redirect" title="Armenian Apostolic Orthodox Church">Armenian Apostolic Orthodox Church</a> traditionally offer unleavened bread (although it is distinctively different from the kind used by the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic_Church" class="mw-redirect" title="Roman Catholic Church">Roman Catholic Church</a>) to symbolize the sinlessness of <a href="/wiki/Christ" class="mw-redirect" title="Christ">Christ</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Eastern_Orthodox_Churches">Eastern Orthodox Churches</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sacramental_bread&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Eastern Orthodox Churches">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The baking may only be performed by a believing Orthodox <a href="/wiki/Christian" title="Christian">Christian</a> in good standing—having preferably been recently to <a href="/wiki/Confession_(religion)" title="Confession (religion)">Confession</a>, and is accompanied by prayer and fasting. Before baking, each loaf is formed by placing two disks of dough, one on top of the other, and stamping it with a special liturgical seal. The prosphora should be fresh and not stale or moldy when presented at the <a href="/wiki/Altar" title="Altar">altar</a> for use in the <a href="/wiki/Divine_Liturgy" title="Divine Liturgy">Divine Liturgy</a>. Often several prosphora will be baked and offered by the faithful, and the priest chooses the best one for the <a href="/wiki/Lamb_(liturgy)" title="Lamb (liturgy)">Lamb</a> (Host) that will be <a href="/wiki/Consecrated" class="mw-redirect" title="Consecrated">consecrated</a>. The remaining loaves are blessed and offered back to the congregation after the end of the <a href="/wiki/Divine_Liturgy" title="Divine Liturgy">Divine Liturgy</a> (Eucharist); this bread is called the <i><a href="/wiki/Antidoron" title="Antidoron">antidoron</a></i> (<a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a>: αντίδωρον, <i>antídōron</i>), i.e. a "gift returned", or "in place of the gifts".</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Eastern_Catholic_Churches">Eastern Catholic Churches</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sacramental_bread&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Eastern Catholic Churches">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Byzantine_Rite" title="Byzantine Rite">Byzantine Rite</a> <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Catholic_Churches" title="Eastern Catholic Churches">Eastern Catholic Churches</a> (like the <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Orthodox_Church" title="Eastern Orthodox Church">Eastern Orthodox Church</a>) use leavened bread for <a href="/wiki/Prosphora" title="Prosphora">Prosphora</a> (the Greek word for Eucharistic altar bread).</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Syro-Malabar_Church" class="mw-redirect" title="Syro-Malabar Church">Syro-Malabar Church</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Maronite_Church" class="mw-redirect" title="Maronite Church">Maronite Church</a> have adopted the use of unleavened bread due to <a href="/wiki/Liturgical_Latinisation" title="Liturgical Latinisation">Liturgical Latinisation</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Western_Traditions">Western Traditions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sacramental_bread&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Western Traditions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Roman_Catholic_Church">Roman Catholic Church</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sacramental_bread&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Roman Catholic Church">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>A <b>Host</b> is a portion of bread used for <a href="/wiki/Holy_Communion" class="mw-redirect" title="Holy Communion">Holy Communion</a> in many <a href="/wiki/Christian" title="Christian">Christian</a> churches. In <a href="/wiki/Western_Christianity" title="Western Christianity">Western Christianity</a> the host is often a thin, round <a href="/wiki/Azymes" title="Azymes">unleavened</a> <a href="/wiki/Wafer" title="Wafer">wafer</a>.</p>

<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Latin_Rite" class="mw-redirect" title="Latin Rite">Latin Rite</a>, <a href="/wiki/Unleavened_bread" title="Unleavened bread">unleavened bread</a> is used as in the Jewish <a href="/wiki/Passover" title="Passover">Passover</a> or Feast of Unleavened Bread. The <a href="/wiki/Canon_law_(Catholic_Church)" class="mw-redirect" title="Canon law (Catholic Church)">Code of Canon Law</a>, <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.vatican.va/archive/ENG1104/__P3A.HTM">Canon 924</a> requires that the hosts be made from wheat flour and water only and recently made so that there is no danger of spoiling.</p>
<p>Hosts are often made by <a href="/wiki/Nun" title="Nun">nuns</a> as a means of supporting their religious communities. However in New Zealand, the St Vincent de Paul Society hires individuals with intellectual disabilities to bake, cut out and sort the bread thereby offering paid employment to those who wouldn't have that option otherwise.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/General_Instruction_of_the_Roman_Missal" title="General Instruction of the Roman Missal">General Instruction of the Roman Missal</a> §321 recommends that "the eucharistic bread ... be made in such a way that the priest at Mass with a congregation is able in practice to break it into parts for distribution to at least some of the faithful. ... The action of the <a href="/wiki/Fraction_(religion)" title="Fraction (religion)">fraction</a> or breaking of bread, which gave its name to the <a href="/wiki/Eucharist" title="Eucharist">Eucharist</a> in apostolic times, will bring out more clearly the force and importance of the sign of unity of all in the one bread, and of the sign of charity by the fact that the one bread is distributed among the brothers and sisters".</p>
<p>In 1995 <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Cardinal_Ratzinger" class="mw-redirect" title="Joseph Cardinal Ratzinger">Joseph Cardinal Ratzinger</a>, then Prefect of the Congregation for Divine Worship and the Discipline of the Sacraments, wrote a letter to the Episcopal Conferences in which he expanded the Code of Canon Law, stating that low-<a href="/wiki/Gluten" title="Gluten">gluten</a> bread would be considered “valid matter” for hosts as long as no additional substances “alter[ed] the nature of the substance of the bread.”<sup id="cite_ref-thinkprogress.org_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-thinkprogress.org-2">[2]</a></sup> Since the 2000s, hosts with low gluten content have been manufactured in the United States, especially in parts of Missouri and New York.<sup id="cite_ref-thinkprogress.org_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-thinkprogress.org-2">[2]</a></sup> Hosts are one of the causes of lack of recovery for people with <a href="/wiki/Celiac_disease" class="mw-redirect" title="Celiac disease">celiac disease</a> who follow a <a href="/wiki/Gluten-free_diet" title="Gluten-free diet">gluten-free diet</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-CiacciCiclitira2015_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CiacciCiclitira2015-3">[3]</a></sup> which must be strict and maintained for life to allow the recovery of the intestinal mucosa and reduce the risk of developing severe health complications.<sup id="cite_ref-SeeKaukinen2015_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SeeKaukinen2015-4">[4]</a></sup>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Protestantism">Protestantism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sacramental_bread&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Protestantism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:201px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Methodistcommunion6.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/a/a4/Methodistcommunion6.jpg" width="199" height="232" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="199" data-file-height="232" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Methodistcommunion6.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/United_Methodist" class="mw-redirect" title="United Methodist">United Methodist</a> <a href="/wiki/Elder_(Methodism)" class="mw-redirect" title="Elder (Methodism)">Elder</a> presiding at the Eucharist, using a leavened loaf of bread</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>In the varying <a href="/wiki/Protestant" class="mw-redirect" title="Protestant">Protestant</a> denominations, there is a wide variety of practices concerning the sacramental bread used. Some, such as the <a href="/wiki/Christian_Congregation_of_Brazil" class="mw-redirect" title="Christian Congregation of Brazil">Christian Congregation</a> use leavened loaves of bread, others, such as <a href="/wiki/Lutheranism" title="Lutheranism">Lutherans</a>, continue to use unleavened <a href="/wiki/Wafer" title="Wafer">wafers</a> like the Roman Catholics, and some use <a href="/wiki/Matzo" title="Matzo">matzo</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Reformed" class="mw-redirect" title="Reformed">Reformed</a> Christians use rolls which are <a href="/wiki/Fractio_panis_(practice)" class="mw-redirect" title="Fractio panis (practice)">broken</a> and distributed to symbolize their belief that Christ is <a href="/wiki/Real_presence_of_Christ_in_the_Eucharist#Reformed:_.22pneumatic_presence.22" title="Real presence of Christ in the Eucharist">not physically present in the bread</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Among those who use the unleavened wafers, there is a great deal of variation: some are square or triangular rather than round, and may even be made out of <a href="/wiki/Whole_wheat_flour" class="mw-redirect" title="Whole wheat flour">whole wheat flour</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Latter-day_Saint">Latter-day Saint</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sacramental_bread&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Latter-day Saint">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/The_Church_of_Jesus_Christ_of_Latter-day_Saints" title="The Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints">The Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints</a> (Mormons) has no strict rules on the type of bread used for sacramental purposes. Latter-day Saint scriptures state: "For, behold, I say unto you, that it mattereth not what ye shall eat or what ye shall drink when ye partake of the sacrament, if it so be that ye do it with an eye single to my glory—remembering unto the Father my body which was laid down for you, and my blood which was shed for the remission of your sins." (<a href="/wiki/Doctrine_and_Covenants" title="Doctrine and Covenants">Doctrine and Covenants</a> 27:2) Different congregations may use either commercial bread or homemade bread prepared by members of the congregation. It is permissible to substitute rice cakes or other gluten-free breads for members who suffer from food allergies.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> The bread is broken into fragments just prior to being blessed by one of the officiating <a href="/wiki/Priest_(Latter_Day_Saints)" title="Priest (Latter Day Saints)">priests</a>.</p>
