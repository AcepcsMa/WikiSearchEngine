			<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Meistersinger</h1>

<p>A <b><span lang="de" xml:lang="de">Meistersinger</span></b> (German for "master singer") was a member of a German <a href="/wiki/Guild" title="Guild">guild</a> for <a href="/wiki/Lyric_poetry" title="Lyric poetry">lyric poetry</a>, <a href="/wiki/Composer" title="Composer">composition</a> and <a href="/wiki/A_cappella" title="A cappella">unaccompanied</a> <a href="/wiki/Art_song" title="Art song">art song</a> of the 14th, 15th and 16th centuries. The Meistersingers were drawn from middle class males for the most part.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Guilds">Guilds</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Meistersinger&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Guilds">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Meistersingers carried on and developed the traditions of the medieval <a href="/wiki/Minnesinger" class="mw-redirect" title="Minnesinger">Minnesingers</a>. They belonged to the artisan and trading classes of the German towns, and regarded as their masters and the founders of their <a href="/wiki/Guild" title="Guild">guild</a> twelve poets of the <a href="/wiki/Middle_High_German" title="Middle High German">Middle High German</a> period, including <a href="/wiki/Wolfram_von_Eschenbach" title="Wolfram von Eschenbach">Wolfram von Eschenbach</a>, <a href="/wiki/Konrad_von_W%C3%BCrzburg" title="Konrad von Würzburg">Konrad von Würzburg</a>, <a href="/wiki/Reinmar_von_Zweter" title="Reinmar von Zweter">Reinmar von Zweter</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Heinrich_Frauenlob" title="Heinrich Frauenlob">Heinrich Frauenlob</a>. Frauenlob is said to have established the earliest Meistersinger school at <a href="/wiki/Mainz" title="Mainz">Mainz</a>, early in the 14th century. The schools were established first in the upper <a href="/wiki/Rhine" title="Rhine">Rhine</a> district, then elsewhere. In the 14th century there were schools at Mainz, <a href="/wiki/Strasbourg" title="Strasbourg">Strasbourg</a>, <a href="/wiki/Frankfurt" title="Frankfurt">Frankfurt</a>, <a href="/wiki/W%C3%BCrzburg" title="Würzburg">Würzburg</a>, <a href="/wiki/Zurich" class="mw-redirect" title="Zurich">Zurich</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Prague" title="Prague">Prague</a>; in the 15th at <a href="/wiki/Augsburg" title="Augsburg">Augsburg</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nuremberg" title="Nuremberg">Nuremberg</a>. Nuremberg, under the leadership of <a href="/wiki/Hans_Sachs" title="Hans Sachs">Hans Sachs</a>, became the most famous school in the 16th century, by which time Meistersinger schools had spread all over Germany and farther north, to <a href="/wiki/Magdeburg" title="Magdeburg">Magdeburg</a>, <a href="/wiki/Breslau" class="mw-redirect" title="Breslau">Breslau</a> <a href="/wiki/G%C3%B6rlitz" title="Görlitz">Görlitz</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Danzig" class="mw-redirect" title="Danzig">Danzig</a>.</p>
<p>Each guild had various classes of members, ranging from beginners, or <i>Schüler</i> (corresponding to trade <a href="/wiki/Apprentice" class="mw-redirect" title="Apprentice">apprentices</a>), and <i>Schulfreunde</i> (who were equivalent to <i>Gesellen</i> or <a href="/wiki/Journeyman" title="Journeyman">journeymen</a>), to <i>Meister</i>. Meisters were poets who could both write new verses to existing melodies and invent new melodies. The poem was technically known as a <i>Bar</i> or <i>Gesetz</i>, the melody as a <i>Ton</i> or <i>Weis</i>. The songs were all sung without accompaniment.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Rules">Rules</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Meistersinger&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Rules">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The rules of the art were set down in the so-called <i>Tabulatur</i> or law-book of the guild. The <i>Tabulatur</i> dealt with three matters: (1) The kinds of poems and the parts of a meistergesang; (2) permissible rhymes; (3) mistakes to be avoided, including errors of delivery, of melody, of structure and of opinion and, especially, errors of rhyme, word choice, or meter. Poetry was to them a mechanical art that could be learned through diligent study, not something dependent on divine inspiration.</p>
<p>Their songs cover a variety of strophic forms corresponding to the many new tunes which the Meistersingers invented and gave complicated names such as <i><span lang="de" xml:lang="de">Gestreiftsafranblumleinweis</span></i> (Little striped saffron flower melody), <i><span lang="de" xml:lang="de">Fettdachsweis</span></i>, <i><span lang="de" xml:lang="de">Vielfrassweis</span></i> (Melody of eating much), <i><span lang="de" xml:lang="de">geblümte Paradiesweis</span></i> (Flowery paradise melody), etc. More attention was paid to fitting the syllables to the melody than to the text's meaning, sentiment, or message. The various songs were divided into three <a href="/wiki/Strophe" title="Strophe">strophes</a>, and each strophe was divided into two <i><span lang="de" xml:lang="de">Stollen</span></i> and a <a href="/wiki/Discant" title="Discant">discant</a> or <i><span lang="de" xml:lang="de">Abgesang</span></i>. Plate, in "<span lang="de" xml:lang="de">Die Kunstausdrücke der Meistersinger</span>",<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> gives a long list of the various features of rhythm and rhyme in this complicated poetry, in all of which can be observed a singular likeness to the technicalities invented by the lesser, and even by the better, poets two centuries earlier in Southern France.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Meetings">Meetings</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Meistersinger&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Meetings">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The meetings took place either in the town hall (<i><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Rathaus" class="extiw" title="wikt:Rathaus">Rathaus</a></i>) or, more frequently, on Sundays in the church. Three times a year, at <a href="/wiki/Easter" title="Easter">Easter</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pentecost" title="Pentecost">Pentecost</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Christmas" title="Christmas">Christmas</a>, special festivals and singing competitions were instituted. At such competitions or <i>Schulsingen</i>, judges (<i>Merker</i>) were appointed to criticize the competitors and note their offences against the rules of the <i>Tabulatur</i>. Prizes were awarded, and those who sang ill were fined. The prize was sometimes money, sometimes a crown, as at Nuremberg in the time of Hans Sachs. Flowers had also an important part in these competitions. Often in the older days one singer would hang up a wreath as a challenge and as a reward for victory. The meistersinger often wore a costume which was not seldom motley and which was often sumptuous.</p>
<p>In Nuremberg, where the master singers flourished particularly, the special festivals were opened with free singing, in which anybody might sing, though not belonging to the corporation. In this the choice of the subjects was left comparatively uncontrolled. Then followed the chief singing, when only those who belonged to the corporation were allowed to sing, and only on scriptural subjects. The judges sat behind a curtain. There were four: one watched whether the song was according to the text of the <a href="/wiki/Bible" title="Bible">Bible</a>, which lay open before him; the second whether the <a href="/wiki/Prosody_(linguistics)" title="Prosody (linguistics)">prosody</a> was correct; the third criticized the rhymes; the fourth the tunes. Every fault was marked, and he who had fewest received the prize.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cultural_role">Cultural role</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Meistersinger&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Cultural role">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Meistersinger poetry played a large part in German town life of the 15th and 16th century. The tradition often reinforced German burgher values; as such, it was middle-class popular art rather than high art. The "<span lang="de" xml:lang="de">Meistergesang</span>" culminated in the 16th century and declined shortly thereafter. Meistersinger traditions lingered in southern Germany as late as the 19th century: a society in <a href="/wiki/Ulm" title="Ulm">Ulm</a> dissolved in 1839; the last school died out at <a href="/wiki/Memmingen" title="Memmingen">Memmingen</a> in 1875.</p>

<p>The plot of <a href="/wiki/Richard_Wagner" title="Richard Wagner">Richard Wagner</a>'s 1868 opera <i><a href="/wiki/Die_Meistersinger_von_N%C3%BCrnberg" title="Die Meistersinger von Nürnberg">Die Meistersinger von Nürnberg</a></i> revolves around the guild of Meistersingers and their singing contest.</p>
