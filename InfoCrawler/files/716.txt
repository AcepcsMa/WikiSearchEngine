<p><b>Johann Kaspar Hechtel</b> (1 May 1771 – 20 December 1799) was a <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">German</a> businessman, owner of a brass factory in <a href="/wiki/Nuremberg" title="Nuremberg">Nuremberg</a>, non-fiction writer and designer of parlour games including the prototype for the Petit Lenormand <a href="/wiki/Cartomancy" title="Cartomancy">cartomancy</a> deck. According to published biographies, Hechtel also contributed anonymously to some treatises on physics.<sup id="cite_ref-Will_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Will-1">[1]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Meusel_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Meusel-2">[2]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Kayser_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kayser-3">[3]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Bieling_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bieling-4">[4]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Johann_Kaspar_Hechtel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Hechtel was born on 1 May 1771 in Nuremberg.<sup id="cite_ref-Will_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Will-1">[1]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Meusel_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Meusel-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Death">Death</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Johann_Kaspar_Hechtel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Death">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Hechtel died on 20 December 1799 in Nuremberg during a <a href="/wiki/Smallpox" title="Smallpox">smallpox</a> epidemic and was survived by a wife.<sup id="cite_ref-Will_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Will-1">[1]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Meusel_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Meusel-2">[2]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Lenormand_cards">Lenormand cards</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Johann_Kaspar_Hechtel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Lenormand cards">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In the mid 19th century after the death of the famous French fortune-teller <a href="/wiki/Marie_Anne_Lenormand" title="Marie Anne Lenormand">Marie Anne Lenormand</a>, Lenormand's name was used on several cartomancy decks including a deck of 36 illustrated cards known as the Petit Lenormand or simply Lenormand cards still used extensively today. The 36 card Lenormand deck is modelled on a deck of cards published c1799 as part of <i>Das Spiel der Hoffnung</i> (<i>The Game of Hope</i>), a game of chance designed by Hechtel which was still being advertised in 1820. So-called Lenormand decks have the same card numbering, primary symbols and playing card associations as the cards in Hechtel's <i>Das Spiel der Hoffnung</i> game. Some examples of the game are included in an extensive collection of playing cards bequeathed to the <a href="/wiki/British_Museum" title="British Museum">British Museum</a> by <a href="/wiki/Lady_Charlotte_Guest" title="Lady Charlotte Guest">Lady Charlotte Schreiber</a>. <i>Das Spiel der Hoffnung</i> is listed among Hechtel's works in an advertisement by publisher Gustav Philipp Jakob Bieling of Nuremberg dated 1799.<sup id="cite_ref-Bieling_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bieling-4">[4]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
