<p><b>Charles Theodore</b> (German: <span lang="de" xml:lang="de"><i>Karl Theodor</i></span>; 11 December 1724 – 16 February 1799) reigned as <a href="/wiki/Prince-elector" title="Prince-elector">Prince-elector</a> and <a href="/wiki/Electoral_Palatinate" title="Electoral Palatinate">Count Palatine</a> from 1742, as <a href="/wiki/Duchy_of_J%C3%BClich" title="Duchy of Jülich">Duke of Jülich</a> and <a href="/wiki/Berg_(state)" class="mw-redirect" title="Berg (state)">Berg</a> from 1742 and also as prince-elector and Duke of <a href="/wiki/Electorate_of_Bavaria" title="Electorate of Bavaria">Bavaria</a> from 1777 to his death. He was a member of the House of <a href="/wiki/Palatinate-Sulzbach" title="Palatinate-Sulzbach">Palatinate-Sulzbach</a>, a branch of the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Wittelsbach" title="House of Wittelsbach">House of Wittelsbach</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Family_and_ascent">Family and ascent</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Charles_Theodore,_Elector_of_Bavaria&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Family and ascent">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Charles Theodore was of the <a href="/wiki/Wittelsbach" class="mw-redirect" title="Wittelsbach">Wittelsbach</a> house <a href="/wiki/Palatinate-Sulzbach" title="Palatinate-Sulzbach">Palatinate-Sulzbach</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Brockhaus_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Brockhaus-1">[1]</a></sup> His father was <a href="/wiki/John_Christian,_Count_of_Palatinate-Sulzbach" class="mw-redirect" title="John Christian, Count of Palatinate-Sulzbach">Johann Christian</a>, who later became Count Palatine of Sulzbach. His mother was <a href="/wiki/Maria_Henriette_de_La_Tour_d%27Auvergne" title="Maria Henriette de La Tour d'Auvergne">Marie-Anne-Henriette-Leopoldine de La Tour d'Auvergne, Margravine of Bergen op Zoom</a>, a grandniece of <a href="/wiki/Henri_de_la_Tour_d%27Auvergne,_Vicomte_de_Turenne" title="Henri de la Tour d'Auvergne, Vicomte de Turenne">Henri de la Tour d'Auvergne, Vicomte de Turenne</a>. Charles Theodore was born in <a href="/wiki/Drogenbos" title="Drogenbos">Drogenbos</a> near <a href="/wiki/Brussels" title="Brussels">Brussels</a> and educated in <a href="/wiki/Mannheim" title="Mannheim">Mannheim</a>.</p>
<p>Charles Theodore was the <a href="/wiki/Lords_and_margraves_of_Bergen_op_Zoom" title="Lords and margraves of Bergen op Zoom">Margrave of Bergen op Zoom</a> from 1728 onwards. He then succeeded his father as Count Palatine of Sulzbach in 1733 and inherited the <a href="/wiki/Electoral_Palatinate" title="Electoral Palatinate">Electoral Palatinate</a> and the duchies of Jülich and Berg in 1742, with the death of <a href="/wiki/Charles_III_Philip,_Elector_Palatine" title="Charles III Philip, Elector Palatine">Charles III Philip, Elector Palatine</a>. To strengthen the union of all lines of the <a href="/wiki/Wittelsbach" class="mw-redirect" title="Wittelsbach">Wittelsbach</a> dynasty Charles III Philip had organised a wedding on 17 January 1742 when his granddaughter <a href="/wiki/Countess_Elizabeth_Augusta_of_Sulzbach" class="mw-redirect" title="Countess Elizabeth Augusta of Sulzbach">Elizabeth Augusta</a> was married to Charles Theodore and her sister Maria Anna to the Bavarian prince <a href="/wiki/Duke_Clement_Francis_of_Bavaria" title="Duke Clement Francis of Bavaria">Clement</a>.</p>
<p>As reigning Prince Elector <a href="/wiki/Electoral_Palatinate" title="Electoral Palatinate">Palatine</a>, Charles Theodore won the hearts of his subjects by founding an academy of science, stocking up the museums' collections and supporting the arts. When <a href="/wiki/Maximilian_III,_Elector_of_Bavaria" class="mw-redirect" title="Maximilian III, Elector of Bavaria">Maximilian III Joseph</a> of Bavaria died in 1777, Charles Theodore became also Elector and Duke of Bavaria and moved to <a href="/wiki/Munich" title="Munich">Munich</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Bavarian_succession">Bavarian succession</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Charles_Theodore,_Elector_of_Bavaria&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Bavarian succession">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Charles Theodore did not immediately take up his new title. He had several mistresses and many illegitimate children. However, these people could inherit neither the Electorate of Bavaria nor that of the Palatine; Charles Theodore needed territory that he could bequeath to his illegitimate children. Charles Theodore also dreamed of resurrecting the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Burgundy" title="Kingdom of Burgundy">Burgundian Empire</a> of the Middle Ages.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="Kingdom of Burgundy or Valois Duchy of Burgundy? (October 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>On 3 January 1778, shortly after the death of Max Joseph, Charles Theodore signed an agreement with <a href="/wiki/Joseph_II,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Joseph II, Holy Roman Emperor">Emperor Joseph II</a> to exchange southern Bavaria for part of the <a href="/wiki/Austrian_Netherlands" title="Austrian Netherlands">Austrian Netherlands</a>.</p>
<p>The plan was strongly opposed by <a href="/wiki/Maria_Anna_Sophia_of_Saxony" title="Maria Anna Sophia of Saxony">Maria Anna Sophia of Saxony</a>, the widow of Max Joseph, and Charles Theodore's cousin <a href="/wiki/Charles_II_August,_Duke_of_Zweibr%C3%BCcken" title="Charles II August, Duke of Zweibrücken">Charles II August, Duke of Zweibrücken</a>, the head of the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Palatinate-Birkenfeld" title="House of Palatinate-Birkenfeld">House of Palatinate-Birkenfeld</a> and next heir of Bavaria and the Palatinate. They were supported by <a href="/wiki/Frederick_II_of_Prussia" class="mw-redirect" title="Frederick II of Prussia">Frederick II of Prussia</a>, and most of the German minor states.</p>
<p>The ensuing diplomatic crisis led to the <a href="/wiki/War_of_the_Bavarian_Succession" title="War of the Bavarian Succession">War of the Bavarian Succession</a>, which was ended by the <a href="/wiki/Peace_of_Teschen" class="mw-redirect" title="Peace of Teschen">Peace of Teschen</a> (1779). Charles Theodore accepted the Bavarian succession, but agreed that his illegitimate descendants could not inherit Bavaria.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Austria acquired the <a href="/wiki/Innviertel" title="Innviertel">Innviertel</a>, a part of Bavaria in the basin of the Inn river.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Charles Theodore had only one son with his wife, <a href="/wiki/Countess_Elizabeth_Augusta_of_Sulzbach" class="mw-redirect" title="Countess Elizabeth Augusta of Sulzbach">Countess Elizabeth Augusta of Sulzbach</a>, who died a day after birth. His wife died in 1794. In 1795, he married <a href="/wiki/Maria_Leopoldine_of_Austria-Este" class="mw-redirect" title="Maria Leopoldine of Austria-Este">Maria Leopoldine of Austria-Este</a>, Joseph's niece, but they had no children. A second proposal to exchange Bavaria for the Austrian Netherlands in 1784 also failed as Frederick II of Prussia initiated the <a href="/wiki/F%C3%BCrstenbund" title="Fürstenbund">Fürstenbund</a>.</p>
<p>When Charles Theodore died, Bavaria and the Electorate passed to his cousin, <a href="/wiki/Maximilian_I_Joseph_of_Bavaria" title="Maximilian I Joseph of Bavaria">Max Joseph, Duke of Zweibrücken</a>, the younger brother of Charles August, who had died in 1795.</p>
<p>In 1989, Marvin E. Thomas in <i>Karl Theodor and the Bavarian Succession, 1777–1778</i> argued that in fact Charles Theodore wanted to maintain possession of his new territory, as is shown in his diplomatic correspondence. Thomas is the only scholar to produce such an analysis.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> It is more widely understood that Charles Theodore continued the despotic and expensive habits he had developed as Elector Palatine.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Rule_as_elector_of_Bavaria">Rule as elector of Bavaria</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Charles_Theodore,_Elector_of_Bavaria&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Rule as elector of Bavaria">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Charles Theodore never became popular as a ruler in Bavaria according to his critic <a href="/wiki/Lorenz_von_Westenrieder" title="Lorenz von Westenrieder">Lorenz von Westenrieder</a>. He attempted, without success to exchange the ducal lands of Bavaria, for the <a href="/wiki/Austrian_Netherlands" title="Austrian Netherlands">Austrian Netherlands</a> and a royal crown, and he never managed to control the mounting social tensions in Bavaria. After a dispute with Munich's city council, he even moved the electoral residence in 1788 to Mannheim but returned only one year later.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1785, he appointed the <a href="/wiki/Loyalist_(American_Revolution)" title="Loyalist (American Revolution)">American Loyalist</a> exile <a href="/wiki/Benjamin_Thompson" title="Benjamin Thompson">Benjamin Thompson</a> as his aide-de-camp and chamberlain. Over the next 11 years, Thompson reformed the army and many aspects of the state, rising to high ministerial rank with Charles Theodore's backing, and becoming Count von Rumford.</p>
<p>Charles Theodore is also known for disbanding <a href="/wiki/Adam_Weishaupt" title="Adam Weishaupt">Adam Weishaupt</a>'s order of the <i><a href="/wiki/Illuminati" title="Illuminati">Illuminati</a></i> in 1785.</p>
<p>In 1794, the armies of revolutionary France occupied the <a href="/wiki/Duchy_of_J%C3%BClich" title="Duchy of Jülich">Duchy of Jülich</a>, in 1795 they invaded the Palatinate, and in 1796 marched towards Bavaria. Charles Theodore begged <a href="/wiki/Francis_II,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Francis II, Holy Roman Emperor">Francis II</a> for help that would have made Bavaria a puppet state of Austria. When he died of a stroke in Munich in 1799, the population in Munich celebrated for several days. He is buried in the crypt of the <a href="/wiki/Theatinerkirche_(M%C3%BCnchen)" class="mw-redirect" title="Theatinerkirche (München)">Theatinerkirche</a> in Munich.</p>
<p>Despite the mutual dislike and distrust between the Duke and his Bavarian subjects, Charles Theodore left a distinctive mark on the city of Munich: it was during his reign that the <i><a href="/wiki/Englischer_Garten_(Munich)" class="mw-redirect" title="Englischer Garten (Munich)">English Garden</a></i>, Munich's largest park, was created, and the city's old fortifications were dismantled to make place for a modern, expanding city. One of Munich's major squares, <i>Karlsplatz</i>, is named after Charles Theodore. Munich natives, however, seldom use that name, calling the square instead <i><a href="/wiki/Stachus" class="mw-redirect" title="Stachus">Stachus</a></i>, after the pub "<i>Beim Stachus</i>" that was located there until construction work for Karlsplatz began. One of the main reasons for is that Charles Theodore, as noted above, never enjoyed the popularity in Bavaria that he enjoyed in the Palatinate.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Character">Character</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Charles_Theodore,_Elector_of_Bavaria&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Character">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Charles Theodore was more interested in arts and philosophy than in politics. Historian <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Carlyle" title="Thomas Carlyle">Thomas Carlyle</a> referred to him as a "poor idle creature, of purely egoistical, ornamental, dilettante nature; sunk in theatricals, bastard children and the like; much praised by Voltaire, who sometimes used to visit him; and <a href="/wiki/Cosimo_Alessandro_Collini" title="Cosimo Alessandro Collini">Collini</a>, to whom he [Charles Theodore] is a kind master."<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>The French foreign minister, <a href="/wiki/Charles_Gravier,_comte_de_Vergennes" title="Charles Gravier, comte de Vergennes">Charles Gravier, comte de Vergennes</a>, knew him and described his foibles even more succinctly:</p>
<dl>
<dd>
<blockquote>
<p>Although by nature intelligent, he has never succeeded in ruling by himself; he has always been governed by his ministers or by his father-confessor or (for a time) by the [E]lectress [his wife]. This conduct has increased his natural weakness and apathy to such a degree that for a long time he has had no opinions save those inspired in him by his entourage. The void which this indolence has left in his soul is filled with the amusements of the hunt and of music and by secret liaisons, for which <i>His Electoral Majesty</i> has at all times had a particular penchant.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
</dd>
</dl>
<p>In the course of his career, Charles Theodore acquired a celebrated secretary when the <a href="/wiki/Florence" title="Florence">Florentine</a> noble, <a href="/wiki/Cosimo_Alessandro_Collini" title="Cosimo Alessandro Collini">Cosimo Alessandro Collini</a> (1727–1806), who had been <a href="/wiki/Voltaire" title="Voltaire">Voltaire</a>'s secretary, accepted his patronage and remained in the Palatine.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Although Charles Theodore certainly wished to acquire more territory, he had only <a href="/wiki/Legitimacy_(family_law)" title="Legitimacy (family law)">natural</a> sons, thus he preferred territory that he could dispose of through his <a href="/wiki/Will_and_testament" title="Will and testament">testament</a>, rather than territory encumbered by a legal <a href="/wiki/Fee_tail" title="Fee tail">entailment</a> that could only pass to a legitimate son.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cultural_legacy">Cultural legacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Charles_Theodore,_Elector_of_Bavaria&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Cultural legacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Charles Theodore was a great lover of the arts, including drama and especially music. His Mannheim court orchestra was considered one of the finest in its time. The <a href="/wiki/Mannheim_School" class="mw-redirect" title="Mannheim School">Mannheim School</a> (including composer <a href="/wiki/Christian_Cannabich" title="Christian Cannabich">Christian Cannabich</a> and conductor <a href="/wiki/Johann_Stamitz" title="Johann Stamitz">Johann Stamitz</a>) did groundbreaking work that the celebrated <a href="/wiki/Wiener_Klassik" class="mw-redirect" title="Wiener Klassik">Wiener Klassik</a> would later draw upon. <a href="/wiki/Wolfgang_Amadeus_Mozart" title="Wolfgang Amadeus Mozart">Mozart</a> applied for a position with the Mannheim orchestra in 1777, but was turned down, as the court was about to move to Munich. In 1780, Charles Theodore commissioned <i><a href="/wiki/Idomeneo" title="Idomeneo">Idomeneo</a></i> from the composer. Mozart quotes him as saying "No music has ever made such an impression on me. It is magnificent."<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>In keeping with the customs of the time, an Italian opera company as well as a troupe of French actors were employed at Mannheim, each performing in their respective tongues. Later, the <i>Nationaltheater</i> (national theatre) was established, one of the first theatres in Germany to exclusively showcase plays in the native tongue (most notably, the first staging of <a href="/wiki/Friedrich_Schiller" title="Friedrich Schiller">Schiller's</a> <a href="/wiki/The_Robbers" title="The Robbers">"Die Räuber"</a> in 1782).</p>
<p>In the visual arts, a massive collection of plaster casts taken from celebrated antique works was assembled at Mannheim. The preexisting <a href="/wiki/D%C3%BCsseldorf" title="Düsseldorf">Düsseldorf</a> gallery, including many works by <a href="/wiki/Rubens" class="mw-redirect" title="Rubens">Rubens</a>, was first transferred to Mannheim, then to Munich, where it was later incorporated in the <a href="/wiki/Alte_Pinakothek" title="Alte Pinakothek">Alte Pinakothek</a>. While none of the Mannheim painters are particularly notable today (with the possible exception of Kobell, primarily a master of landscapes), the elector had several highly talented sculptors at his disposal, among them <a href="/wiki/Peter_Anton_von_Verschaffelt" title="Peter Anton von Verschaffelt">Verschaffelt</a>, Simon Peter Lamine and Konrad Linck. Linck also distinguished himself as a designer of porcelain figurines at <a href="/wiki/Frankenthal" title="Frankenthal">Frankenthal</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>Charles Theodore’s chief architect Nicolas de Pigage was charged to complete the <a href="/wiki/Mannheim_Palace" title="Mannheim Palace">Mannheim Palace</a>, design the <a href="/wiki/Schlosstheater_Schwetzingen" title="Schlosstheater Schwetzingen">Schlosstheater</a> and the gardens at <a href="/wiki/Schwetzingen_Castle" class="mw-redirect" title="Schwetzingen Castle">Schwetzingen Palace</a> ( including numerous pavilions, among them several artificial "Roman" ruins and a "mosque") as well as <a href="/wiki/Schloss_Benrath" title="Schloss Benrath">Benrath palace</a>. While these works are in a pure contemporary French style (marked by the transition from late Baroque to early classicism), some of the other architects employed by Charles Theodore were proponents of a more Italianate style. This mixture of influences is, in fact, typical of many German courts of the period. Charles Theodore was also responsible for the construction of the <a href="/wiki/Old_Bridge_(Heidelberg)" title="Old Bridge (Heidelberg)">Old Bridge</a> in Heidelberg, which bears his name as the <i>Karl-Theodor-Brücke</i>.</p>

<p>Charles Theodore liked to style himself as a prince of peace, in contrast to other princes like his chief political opponent, the great warrior <a href="/wiki/Frederick_the_Great" title="Frederick the Great">Frederick II</a> of <a href="/wiki/Prussia" title="Prussia">Prussia</a>. Allegorically, <a href="/wiki/Minerva" title="Minerva">Minerva</a>, the Roman goddess of wisdom and protectress of the arts, is often depicted as a stand-in for the elector himself. This self-view is best summed up in the inscription of a small monument at Schwetzingen:</p>
<p><i>“A field of war and death of Romans and Germans has been discovered, through the unearthing of weapons, urns and bones, in the year 1765. – To the arts of peace, which are the sole joys of his life, the elector Charles Theodore has dedicated this spot, excavated to the height of seven feet, and had this monument erected in 1768.”</i></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Marriages_and_children">Marriages and children</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Charles_Theodore,_Elector_of_Bavaria&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Marriages and children">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On 17 January 1742, in Mannheim, he married <a href="/wiki/Elizabeth_Augusta_of_Sulzbach" class="mw-redirect" title="Elizabeth Augusta of Sulzbach">Elisabeth Auguste</a>, daughter of <a href="/wiki/Count_Palatine_Joseph_Charles_of_Sulzbach" title="Count Palatine Joseph Charles of Sulzbach">Count Palatine Joseph Charles of Sulzbach</a> and his consort <a href="/wiki/Countess_Palatine_Elizabeth_Augusta_Sophie_of_Neuburg" class="mw-redirect" title="Countess Palatine Elizabeth Augusta Sophie of Neuburg">Countess Palatine Elizabeth Augusta Sophie of Neuburg</a>. There was one child of this marriage who died in infancy, Francis Louis Joseph (28 June 1762 – 29 June 1762).</p>
<p>On 15 February 1795, in <a href="/wiki/Innsbruck" title="Innsbruck">Innsbruck</a>, he married Archduchess <a href="/wiki/Maria_Leopoldine_of_Austria-Este" class="mw-redirect" title="Maria Leopoldine of Austria-Este">Maria Leopoldine of Austria-Este</a>. There were no children of this marriage.</p>

<li>Karoline Josepha von Bretzenheim (27 January 1768 – 27 April 1786); married Count Maximilian Josef von Holnstein (20 May 1760 – 1838) in 1784</li>
<li>Karl August, Count of Heydeck and Reichsfürst von Bretzenheim, (24 December 1769 – 27 February 1823) married Maria Walburga von <a href="/wiki/House_of_Oettingen-Spielberg" title="House of Oettingen-Spielberg">Oettingen-Spielberg</a> (29 August 1766 – 8 May 1833) on 27 April 1788, Oettingen</li>
<li>Eleonore Karoline von Bretzenheim (9 December 1771 – 23 December 1832); married Prince Wilhelm Karl von <a href="/wiki/House_of_Leiningen" class="mw-redirect" title="House of Leiningen">Leiningen</a> (5 July 1737 – 26 January 1809) on 21 November 1787 (divorced 1801)</li>
<li>Friederike Karoline von Bretzenheim (9 December 1771 – 2 March 1816), twin with Eleonore; married Count Maximilian von Westerholt-Gysenberg (1772 – 19 April 1854) in 1796</li>
</ol>
<p>From his liaison with Maria Anna, Gräfin zu Leiningen, widow of Franz Friedrich, Graf von Sayn-Wittgenstein-Hohenstein-Vallendar:</p>
<ol>
<li>Karoline von Ezenried (31 August 1771 Münstereifel – 24 September 1828, Schloss Laim bei München, married in second marriage with Dr. Joseph Reubel, Professor of Medicine, University of München)</li>
</ol>
