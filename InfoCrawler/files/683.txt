<p><b>Antalya</b> (<small>Turkish pronunciation:&#160;</small><span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Turkic_dialects" title="Help:IPA/Turkic dialects">[anˈtalja]</a></span>) is the fifth-most populous city in <a href="/wiki/Turkey" title="Turkey">Turkey</a> and the capital of its <a href="/wiki/Antalya_Province" title="Antalya Province">eponymous province</a>. Located on <a href="/wiki/Anatolia" title="Anatolia">Anatolia</a>'s flourishing southwest coast bordered by the <a href="/wiki/Taurus_Mountains" title="Taurus Mountains">Taurus Mountains</a>, Antalya is the largest Turkish city on the Mediterranean coast with over one million people in its metropolitan area.<sup id="cite_ref-rapor.tuik.gov.tr_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rapor.tuik.gov.tr-2">[2]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>The city that is now Antalya was first settled around 200 BC by the <a href="/wiki/Attalid_dynasty" title="Attalid dynasty">Attalid dynasty</a> of Pergamon, which was soon subdued by the Romans. Roman rule saw Antalya thrive, including the construction of several new monuments, such as <a href="/wiki/Hadrian%27s_Gate" title="Hadrian's Gate">Hadrian's Gate</a>, and the proliferation of neighboring cities. The city has changed hands several times, including to the <a href="/wiki/Byzantine_Empire" title="Byzantine Empire">Byzantine Empire</a> in 1207 and an expanding <a href="/wiki/Ottoman_Empire" title="Ottoman Empire">Ottoman Empire</a> in 1391.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Ottoman rule brought relative peace and stability for the next five hundred years. The city was transferred to Italian suzerainty in the aftermath of <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a>, but was recaptured by a newly independent Turkey in the <a href="/wiki/Turkish_War_of_Independence" title="Turkish War of Independence">War of Independence</a>.</p>
<p>Antalya is Turkey's biggest international sea resort, located on the <a href="/wiki/Turkish_Riviera" title="Turkish Riviera">Turkish Riviera</a>. Large-scale development and governmental funding has promoted tourism. A record 12.5 million tourists passed through the city in 2014.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Antalya was the host city for the <a href="/wiki/2015_G-20_Antalya_summit" class="mw-redirect" title="2015 G-20 Antalya summit">2015 G-20 summit</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Expo_2016" title="Expo 2016">EXPO 2016</a>.</p>
<p>In 2015, the population was 2.288 million.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Antalya&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The city was founded as "Attaleia" (<a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a>: <span lang="el">Αττάλεια</span>), named after its founder <a href="/wiki/Attalus_II_Philadelphus" title="Attalus II Philadelphus">Attalos II</a>, king of <a href="/wiki/Pergamon" title="Pergamon">Pergamon</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> This name, still in use in Greek, was later evolved in Turkish as Adalia and then Antalya.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Attaleia was also the name of a festival at <a href="/wiki/Delphi" title="Delphi">Delphi</a> and Attalis (Greek: Ἀτταλίς) was the name of an old Greek tribe at Athens.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Despite the close similarity, there is no relationship to the name <a href="/wiki/Anatolia" title="Anatolia">Anatolia</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Antalya&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">

<p>King <a href="/wiki/Attalus_II_Philadelphus" title="Attalus II Philadelphus">Attalus II</a> of <a href="/wiki/Pergamon" title="Pergamon">Pergamon</a> is looked on as founder of the city in about 150 BC, during the <a href="/wiki/Hellenistic_period" title="Hellenistic period">Hellenistic period</a>. It was named <b>Attaleia</b> or <b>Attalia</b> in his honour. The city served as a naval base for Attalus's powerful fleet. Excavations in 2008, in the <a href="/wiki/Do%C4%9Fu_Garaj%C4%B1" title="Doğu Garajı">Doğu Garajı</a> plot, uncovered remains dating to the 3rd century BC, suggesting that Attalea was a rebuilding and expansion of an earlier town.</p>

Statue of Attalus II in the city</div>

<p>Attalea became part of the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Republic" title="Roman Republic">Roman Republic</a> in 133 BC when <a href="/wiki/Attalus_III" title="Attalus III">Attalus III</a>, a nephew of Attalus II bequeathed his kingdom to Rome at his death in 133 BC. The city grew and prospered during the <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Roman" class="mw-redirect" title="Ancient Roman">Ancient Roman</a> period and was part of the <a href="/wiki/Roman_province" title="Roman province">Roman province</a> of <a href="/wiki/Pamphylia_Secunda" class="mw-redirect" title="Pamphylia Secunda">Pamphylia Secunda</a>, whose capital was <a href="/wiki/Perga" title="Perga">Perga</a>.</p>
<p>Christianity started to spread to the region even in the 1st century: Antalya was visited by <a href="/wiki/Paul_of_Tarsus" class="mw-redirect" title="Paul of Tarsus">Paul of Tarsus</a> and <a href="/wiki/Barnabas" title="Barnabas">Barnabas</a>, as recorded in the <a href="/wiki/Acts_of_the_Apostles" title="Acts of the Apostles">Acts of the Apostles</a>: "Then they passed through <a href="/wiki/Pisidia" title="Pisidia">Pisidia</a> and came to <a href="/wiki/Pamphylia" title="Pamphylia">Pamphylia</a>. And when they had spoken the word in <a href="/wiki/Perga" title="Perga">Perga</a>, they went down to Attalia, and from there they sailed to <a href="/wiki/Antioch" title="Antioch">Antioch</a>".<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Some of the bishops attributed to the <a href="/wiki/Episcopal_see" title="Episcopal see">episcopal see</a> of Attalea in Pamphylia may instead have been bishops of <a href="/wiki/Lydia#Episcopal_sees" title="Lydia">Attalea in Lydia</a> (Yanantepe), since <a href="/wiki/Lequien" class="mw-redirect" title="Lequien">Lequien</a> lists them under both sees.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> No longer a residential bishopric, Attalea in Pamphylia is today listed by the <a href="/wiki/Catholic_Church" title="Catholic Church">Catholic Church</a> as a <a href="/wiki/Titular_see" title="Titular see">titular see</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>The 13th-century <a href="/wiki/Seljuk" title="Seljuk">Seljuk</a> mosque at Attalea, now in ruins, had been a Christian Byzantine basilica from the 7th century. The Great Mosque had also been a Christian basilica and the Kesik Minare Mosque had been the 5th-century Christian Church of the Panaghia or Virgin and was decorated with finely carved marble. The archaeological museum at Attalia houses some sarcophagi and mosaics from nearby Perga and a casket of bones reputed to be those of <a href="/wiki/St._Nicholas" class="mw-redirect" title="St. Nicholas">St. Nicholas</a>, the bishop of <a href="/wiki/Myra" title="Myra">Myra</a>, further down the Turquoise coast.</p>
<p>Antalya was a major city in the <a href="/wiki/Byzantine_Empire" title="Byzantine Empire">Byzantine Empire</a>. It was the capital of the <a href="/wiki/Theme_(country_subdivision)#Establishment" class="mw-redirect" title="Theme (country subdivision)">Byzantine Theme</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Cibyrrhaeots" class="mw-redirect" title="Cibyrrhaeots">Cibyrrhaeots</a>, which occupied the southern coasts of <a href="/wiki/Anatolia" title="Anatolia">Anatolia</a>. According to the research of Speros Vryonis, it was the major naval station on the southern Anatolian coast, a major commercial center, and the most convenient harbor between the <a href="/wiki/Aegean_Sea" title="Aegean Sea">Aegean Sea</a> and Cyprus and points further east. Besides the local merchants, "one could expect to see Armenians, Saracens, Jews, and Italians."<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>At the time of the accession of <a href="/wiki/John_II_Comnenus" class="mw-redirect" title="John II Comnenus">John II Comnenus</a> in 1118 Antalya was an isolated outpost surrounded by Turkish beyliks, accessible only by sea.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> Following the <a href="/wiki/Siege_of_Constantinople_(1204)" class="mw-redirect" title="Siege of Constantinople (1204)">fall of Constantinople</a> in 1204, <a href="/wiki/Niketas_Choniates" title="Niketas Choniates">Niketas Choniates</a> records that one Aldebrandus, "an Italian by birth who was strictly raised according to Roman tradition" controlled Antalya as his own fief. When Kaykhusraw, sultan of the <a href="/wiki/Seljuk_Turks" class="mw-redirect" title="Seljuk Turks">Seljuk Turks</a> attempted to capture the city, Aldebrandus sent to Cyprus for help and received 200 Latin infantry who defeated the attackers after a siege of less than 16 days.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>

<p>The city and the surrounding region were conquered by the <a href="/wiki/Seljuk_Turks" class="mw-redirect" title="Seljuk Turks">Seljuk Turks</a> in the early 13th century. Antalya was the capital of the Turkish <a href="/wiki/Beylik_of_Teke" title="Beylik of Teke">beylik of Teke</a> (1321–1423) until its conquest by the Ottomans, except for a period of <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Cyprus" title="Kingdom of Cyprus">Cypriot</a> rule between 1361 and 1373. The Arabic traveler <a href="/wiki/Ibn_Battuta" title="Ibn Battuta">Ibn Battuta</a>, who visited the city in 1335–1340, noted:<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>From Alanya I went to Antaliya [Adalia], a most beautiful city. It covers an immense area, and though of vast bulk is one of the most attractive towns to be seen anywhere, besides being exceedingly populous and well laid out. Each section of the inhabitants lives in a separate quarter. The Christian merchants live in a quarter of the town known as the Mina [the Port], and are surrounded by a wall, the gates of which are shut upon them from without at night and during the Friday service. The Greeks, who were its former inhabitants, live by themselves in another quarter, the Jews in another, and the king and his court and Mamluks in another, each of these quarters being walled off likewise. The rest of the Muslims live in the main city. Round the whole town and all the quarters mentioned there is another great wall. The town contains orchards and produces fine fruits, including an admirable kind of apricot, called by them Qamar ad-Din, which has a sweet almond in its kernel. This fruit is dried and exported to Egypt, where it is regarded as a great luxury.</p>
</blockquote>
<p>In the second half of the 17th century <a href="/wiki/Evliya_%C3%87elebi" title="Evliya Çelebi">Evliya Çelebi</a> wrote of a city of narrow streets containing 3,000 houses in 20 Turkish and four Greek neighborhoods. The town had grown beyond the city walls and the port was reported to hold up to 200 boats.</p>

<p>In the 19th century, in common with most of Anatolia, its sovereign was a "dere <a href="/wiki/Bey" title="Bey">bey</a>" (land lord or landowner). The family of Tekke Oğlu, domiciled near <a href="/wiki/Perge" class="mw-redirect" title="Perge">Perge</a> had been reduced to submission in 1812 by <a href="/wiki/Mahmud_II" title="Mahmud II">Mahmud II</a>, but continued to be a rival power to the Ottoman governor until the early 20th century, surviving by many years the fall of the other great beys of Anatolia. The records of the Levant (Turkey) Company, which maintained an agency in Antalya until 1825, documented the local dere beys.<sup id="cite_ref-EB1911_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EB1911-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the early 20th century, Antalya had two factories spinning and weaving <a href="/wiki/Cotton" title="Cotton">cotton</a>. As of 1920, the factories had 15,000 <a href="/wiki/Spindle_(textiles)" title="Spindle (textiles)">spindles</a> and over 200 <a href="/wiki/Loom" title="Loom">looms</a>. A <a href="/wiki/German_people" class="mw-redirect" title="German people">German</a>-owned <a href="/wiki/Cotton_mill" title="Cotton mill">mill</a> baled cotton. There were <a href="/wiki/Gin_mill" class="mw-redirect" title="Gin mill">gin mills</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Prothero113_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Prothero113-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the 20th century the population of Antalya increased as Turks from the Caucasus and the Balkans moved into Anatolia. The economy was centered on its port that served the inland areas, particularly Konya. Antalya (then Adalia) was picturesque rather than modern. The chief attraction for visitors was the city wall, and outside a promenade, a portion of which survives. The government offices and the houses of the higher classes were outside the walls.<sup id="cite_ref-EB1911_18-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EB1911-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>As of 1920, Antalya was reported as having a population of approximately 30,000. The harbor was described as small, and unsafe for vessels to visit in the winter. Antalya was exporting wheat, flour, <a href="/wiki/Sesame_seed" class="mw-redirect" title="Sesame seed">sesame seeds</a>, livestock, timber and charcoal. The latter two were often exported to <a href="/wiki/Egypt" title="Egypt">Egypt</a> and other goods to Italy or other Greek islands, who received mainly flour. In 1920, the city had seven <a href="/wiki/Flour_mill" class="mw-redirect" title="Flour mill">flour mills</a>. Wheat was imported, and then processed in town before exportation. Antalya imported manufactured items, mainly from the United Kingdom.<sup id="cite_ref-Prothero81_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Prothero81-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>The city was occupied by the Italians from the end of the First World War until the founding of the Turkish Republic in 1923. Large-scale development beginning in the 1970s transformed Antalya from a pastoral town into one of Turkey's largest metropolitan areas. Much of this has been due to tourism, which expanded in the 21st century. In the 1985 singing diva <a href="/wiki/Dalida" title="Dalida">Dalida</a> held her last concert in Antalya.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Menderes_T%C3%BCrel" title="Menderes Türel">Menderes Türel</a> of the AKP served as Mayor between 2004 and 2009. The CHP candidate <a href="/wiki/Mustafa_Akayd%C4%B1n" title="Mustafa Akaydın">Mustafa Akaydın</a> replaced him in 2009.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>

<p>The area is shielded from the northerly winds by the <a href="/wiki/Taurus_Mountains" title="Taurus Mountains">Taurus Mountains</a>. Antalya has a <a href="/wiki/Hot-summer_Mediterranean_climate" class="mw-redirect" title="Hot-summer Mediterranean climate">hot-summer Mediterranean climate</a> (<a href="/wiki/K%C3%B6ppen-Geiger_climate_classification_system" class="mw-redirect" title="Köppen-Geiger climate classification system">Köppen</a>: Csa) with hot and dry summers and mild and rainy winters. Around 300 days of the year are sunny, with nearly 3,000 hours of sunlight per year. The mean sea temperature ranges between 16&#160;°C (61&#160;°F) in winter and 27&#160;°C (81&#160;°F) in summer.<sup id="cite_ref-Weather2Travel_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Weather2Travel-21">[21]</a></sup> The highest record air temperature reached 45.4&#160;°C (113.7&#160;°F) on 1 July 2017 which normally averages as high as 34.4&#160;°C (93.9&#160;°F) and the lowest record dropped to −4.6&#160;°C (23.7&#160;°F) in February, when the low average is as low as 6.1&#160;°C (43&#160;°F).</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Economy">Economy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Antalya&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Economy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Tourism_in_Antalya" title="Tourism in Antalya">Tourism in Antalya</a></div>
<p>Agricultural production includes <a href="/wiki/Citrus" title="Citrus">citrus fruits</a>, cotton, cut flowers, olives, <a href="/wiki/Olive_oil" title="Olive oil">olive oil</a> and bananas. Antalya Metropolitan Municipality’s covered wholesale food market complex meets 65% of the fresh fruit and vegetable demand of the province.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p>Since 2000, shipyards have been opened in Antalya Free Zone,<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> specialized in building pleasure yachts. Some of these yards have advanced in composites boat building technology.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Corendon_Airlines" title="Corendon Airlines">Corendon Airlines</a> and <a href="/wiki/SunExpress" title="SunExpress">SunExpress</a> are headquartered in Antalya.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cityscape">Cityscape</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Antalya&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Cityscape">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Antalyabeach.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/97/Antalyabeach.JPG/220px-Antalyabeach.JPG" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/97/Antalyabeach.JPG/330px-Antalyabeach.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/97/Antalyabeach.JPG/440px-Antalyabeach.JPG 2x" data-file-width="3456" data-file-height="2592" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Antalyabeach.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Konyaalt%C4%B1_Beach" title="Konyaaltı Beach">Konyaaltı Beach</a> as seen from the nearby cliffs. The Beydağları mountains can be seen in the background.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Despite having architectural heritage dating back up to <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greece" title="Ancient Greece">Hellenistic</a> times, most historical architecture in Antalya date to the medieval Ottoman period, with a number of <a href="/wiki/Mosque" title="Mosque">mosques</a>, <a href="/wiki/Madrasahs" class="mw-redirect" title="Madrasahs">madrasahs</a>, <a href="/wiki/Masjid" class="mw-redirect" title="Masjid">masjids</a>, <a href="/wiki/Caravanserai" title="Caravanserai">caravanserais</a>, <a href="/wiki/Turkish_bath" title="Turkish bath">Turkish baths</a> and tombs giving the city a Turkish-Islamic character.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-konutdisi_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-konutdisi-32">[32]</a></sup> Historical architecture is concentrated in the walled city, <a href="/wiki/Kalei%C3%A7i" title="Kaleiçi">Kaleiçi</a>; ancient structures are not well-preserved in the rest of the city of Antalya as the modern city was built on the ancient city.<sup id="cite_ref-kulturgenel_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kulturgenel-33">[33]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Kalei%C3%A7i" title="Kaleiçi">Kaleiçi</a>, with its narrow cobbled streets of historic Ottoman era houses, is the old center of Antalya. With its hotels, bars, clubs, restaurants, and shopping, it has been restored to retain much of its historical character.<sup id="cite_ref-kulturgenel_33-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kulturgenel-33">[33]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-orbasli_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-orbasli-34">[34]</a></sup> It is surrounded by two walls in the shape of a horsenail, one of which is along the seafront, built in a continuous process from Hellenistic to Ottoman times. The historical harbour is located in this part of the city; narrow streets extend from the harbour and branch off into the old city, surrounded by wooden historical houses.<sup id="cite_ref-konutdisi_32-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-konutdisi-32">[32]</a></sup> Cumhuriyet Square, the main square of the city and a spot very popular for tourists and locals, is surrounded by shopping and business centres and public buildings.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> There are sites with traces of Hellenistic, <a href="/wiki/Roman_Empire" title="Roman Empire">Roman</a>, <a href="/wiki/Byzantine_Empire" title="Byzantine Empire">Byzantine</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Seljuk_Turks" class="mw-redirect" title="Seljuk Turks">Seljuk</a> architecture and cultures.<sup id="cite_ref-kulturgenel_33-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kulturgenel-33">[33]</a></sup> There are also examples of the local Greek architecture in the city, with five Greek Orthodox churches in the old city.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
<p>The walled city is surrounded by a large metropolitan area. With high rates of immigration since the 1970s, this area contains large <a href="/wiki/Gecekondu" title="Gecekondu">gecekondu</a> neighborhoods that are not well-integrated into the fabric of the city and suffer from poor economic conditions and insufficient education. Gecekondu areas are concentrated in the <a href="/wiki/Kepez,_Antalya" title="Kepez, Antalya">Kepez</a> district, where an estimated 70% of the houses were gecekondus in 2008.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> In 2011, it was estimated that there were 50-60,000 gecekondus in Antalya, housing around 250,000 people.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup></p>
<p>Antalya has beaches including <a href="/wiki/Konyaalt%C4%B1" title="Konyaaltı">Konyaaltı</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lara,_Antalya" title="Lara, Antalya">Lara</a> and Karpuzkaldıran. Beydağları and <a href="/wiki/Saklikent" class="mw-redirect" title="Saklikent">Saklikent</a> are used for winter sports.</p>

<li><a href="/wiki/Ancient_monument" title="Ancient monument">Ancient monuments</a> include the City Walls, <a href="/wiki/H%C4%B1d%C4%B1rl%C4%B1k_Tower" title="Hıdırlık Tower">Hıdırlık Tower</a>, Hadrian's Gate (also known as Triple Gate), and the Clock Tower.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Hadrian%27s_Gate" title="Hadrian's Gate">Hadrian's Gate</a>: constructed in the 2nd century by the Romans in honor of the <a href="/wiki/Hadrian" title="Hadrian">Emperor Hadrian</a>.</li>
<li>İskele Mosque: A 19th-century Mosque near the Marina.</li>
<li>Karatay Medrese: A Medrese (Islamic theological seminary) built in 1250 by Emir Celaleddin Karatay.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Kesik_Minare" title="Kesik Minare">Kesik Minare</a> (Broken Minaret) Mosque: Once a Roman temple then converted to a Byzantine Panaglia church and finally into a mosque.</li>

This minaret eventually became the symbol of the city.</li>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Elections">Elections</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Antalya&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Elections">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Antalya has traditionally been seen as a stronghold for the <a href="/wiki/Kemalist" class="mw-redirect" title="Kemalist">Kemalist</a> centre-left party <a href="/wiki/Republican_People%27s_Party_(Turkey)" title="Republican People's Party (Turkey)">Republican People's Party</a> (CHP). Being the capital of the fifth most populous province in Turkey, Antalya is politically strategic and has been a target for the governing center-right <a href="/wiki/Justice_and_Development_Party_(Turkey)" title="Justice and Development Party (Turkey)">Justice and Development Party</a> (AKP). The AKP unexpectedly won control of the Antalya Metropolitan Municipality in the <a href="/wiki/Turkish_local_elections,_2004" title="Turkish local elections, 2004">2004 local election</a>. The AKP won a plurality in Antalya in the <a href="/wiki/Turkish_general_election,_2007" title="Turkish general election, 2007">2007 general election</a>, symbolising the city's political transformation from a CHP stronghold to a CHP-AKP marginal battleground in the 21st century. The loss of Antalya was a major political setback for the CHP not only because of its significance as a centre for tourism, but also because the CHP's former leader <a href="/wiki/Deniz_Baykal" title="Deniz Baykal">Deniz Baykal</a> is a <a href="/wiki/Member_of_Parliament" class="mw-redirect" title="Member of Parliament">Member of Parliament</a> for the province. The province is divided into 19 districts.</p>
<p>The CHP regained control of the Metropolitan Municipality in the <a href="/wiki/Turkish_local_elections,_2009" title="Turkish local elections, 2009">2009 local elections</a>, though the AKP won a plurality in the <a href="/wiki/Turkish_general_election,_2011" title="Turkish general election, 2011">2011 general election</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Nationalist_Movement_Party" title="Nationalist Movement Party">Nationalist Movement Party</a> (MHP) also have a strong political base in Antalya, winning approximately between 15 and 25% of the vote in elections since 2007. The city voted in favour of the AKP government's proposed constitutional reforms in the <a href="/wiki/Turkish_constitutional_referendum,_2007" title="Turkish constitutional referendum, 2007">2007 referendum</a>, but <a href="/wiki/Turkish_constitutional_referendum,_2010" title="Turkish constitutional referendum, 2010">voted against</a> the reforms proposed in 2010.</p>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Turkish_local_elections,_2014" title="Turkish local elections, 2014">2014 local elections</a>, Antalya once again voted for the AKP, with the MHP taking support away from the CHP by winning a record 24.3% of the vote. The CHP subsequently accused the AKP of systematic electoral fraud, and the presence of government minister <a href="/wiki/Mevl%C3%BCt_%C3%87avu%C5%9Fo%C4%9Flu" title="Mevlüt Çavuşoğlu">Mevlüt Çavuşoğlu</a> at one of the vote counting centres drew strong condemnation.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> In the <a href="/wiki/Turkish_presidential_election,_2014" title="Turkish presidential election, 2014">2014 presidential election</a>, the CHP and MHP's joint candidate <a href="/wiki/Ekmeleddin_%C4%B0hsano%C4%9Flu" title="Ekmeleddin İhsanoğlu">Ekmeleddin İhsanoğlu</a> won 53.08% of the vote. The AKP's candidate <a href="/wiki/Recep_Tayyip_Erdo%C4%9Fan" title="Recep Tayyip Erdoğan">Recep Tayyip Erdoğan</a> won 41.63% of the vote, and the <a href="/wiki/Peoples%27_Democratic_Party_(Turkey)" title="Peoples' Democratic Party (Turkey)">HDP</a> democratic socialist candidate <a href="/wiki/Selahattin_Demirta%C5%9F" title="Selahattin Demirtaş">Selahattin Demirtaş</a> won 5.30% of the vote. Antalya was a hotspot for the <a href="/wiki/2013%E2%80%9314_protests_in_Turkey" class="mw-redirect" title="2013–14 protests in Turkey">2013–14 anti-government protests</a> against the AKP.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sports">Sports</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Antalya&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Sports">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The football club of Antalya, <a href="/wiki/Antalyaspor" title="Antalyaspor">Antalyaspor</a> plays in the <a href="/wiki/S%C3%BCper_Lig" title="Süper Lig">Süper Lig</a>. The team's home venue is <a href="/wiki/New_Antalya_Stadium" title="New Antalya Stadium">Antalya Arena</a>, with a capacity of 33,000, which was opened in 2015.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> Another football venue in the city is <a href="/wiki/Antalya_Atat%C3%BCrk_Stadium" title="Antalya Atatürk Stadium">Antalya Atatürk Stadium</a>.</p>
<p>The city hosts a number of international sports competitions due to its longer lasting warm weather condition. Since 2006, one of the four stages of <a href="/wiki/Archery_World_Cup" title="Archery World Cup">Archery World Cup</a> events are held at the <a href="/wiki/Antalya_Centennial_Archery_Field" title="Antalya Centennial Archery Field">Antalya Centennial Archery Field</a>. It also hosted European Weightlifting Championship in 2012, European Beach Volleyball Championship in 2003, European Triathlon Championship in 2013 and World Kickboxing Championships in 2013.</p>
<p>Since 2010, an international <a href="/wiki/Multiday_race" class="mw-redirect" title="Multiday race">multiday</a> <a href="/wiki/Trail_running" title="Trail running">trail running</a> <a href="/wiki/Ultramarathon" title="Ultramarathon">ultramarathon</a>, called <a href="/wiki/Lycian_Way_Ultramarathon" title="Lycian Way Ultramarathon">Lycian Way Ultramarathon</a>, is held on the historical <a href="/wiki/Lycian_Way" title="Lycian Way">Lycian Way</a>. The event runs eastward on a route of around 220–240&#160;km (140–150&#160;mi) from <a href="/wiki/%C3%96l%C3%BCdeniz" title="Ölüdeniz">Ölüdeniz</a> in <a href="/wiki/Fethiye" title="Fethiye">Fethiye</a> district of <a href="/wiki/Mu%C4%9Fla_Province" title="Muğla Province">Muğla Province</a> to Antalya in six days.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup></p>

<p>Antalya’s signature cuisine includes <a href="/wiki/Piyaz" title="Piyaz">Piyaz</a> (made with <a href="/wiki/Tahini" title="Tahini">tahini</a>, garlic, walnuts, and boiled beans), <a href="/wiki/%C5%9Ei%C5%9F_k%C3%B6fte" title="Şiş köfte">şiş köfte</a> (spicy meatball which is cooked around a stick) spicy hibeş with mixed cumin and tahini, tandır kebap, domates civesi, şakşuka, and various cold Mediterranean dishes with <a href="/wiki/Olive_oil" title="Olive oil">olive oil</a>. One local speciality is <i>tirmis</i>, boiled seeds of the lupin, eaten as a snack. "Grida" (also known as Lagos or Mediterranean white grouper) is a fish common in local dishes.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. <p>The city has a main port at the south of the Konyaaltı.</p>

<p>There is a network of look-alike <a href="/wiki/Dolmu%C5%9F" title="Dolmuş">Dolmuşes</a> that are privately owned and operated <a href="/wiki/Minibus" title="Minibus">minibuses</a>, under municipal government control.</p>
<p>Antalya Ulaşım, a municipally-owned corporation, runs the public bus system. The corporation owns Antobus and Antray. Antobus was started in September 2010. In 2010, the city planned to increase from 40 to 140 more buses.<sup id="cite_ref-57" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup></p>
<p>Payment for public transportation was made in cash until the launch of a public transportation card, <i>Antkart</i>, in late 2007. The card system met with criticism and was subsequently canceled in June 2009, returning to a cash system. <i>Halkkart</i> has been used for the transportation system since the summer of 2010. <i>Halkkart</i> is managed by A-Kent Smart City Technologies under the control of Antalya Metropolitan Municipal government. Passengers can use identified cards to take buses or trams.<sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <i>Halkkart</i> system replaced by <i>Antalyakart</i>, jointly owned by Metropolitan Municipality and Kentkart in January 2016.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Light_Rail">Light Rail</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Antalya&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Light Rail">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Antalya_Trams" class="mw-redirect" title="Antalya Trams">Antalya Trams</a></div>
<p>A <a href="/wiki/Tram" title="Tram">tram</a> system, opened in 1999, runs from Antalya Museum, and the Sheraton Voyager and Falez hotels, along the main boulevard through the city center at Kalekapisi, <a href="/wiki/Hadrian%27s_Gate" title="Hadrian's Gate">Hadrian's Gate</a>, Karaalioglu Park, and ending at Talya Oteli. Trams depart on the hour and half-hour from the terminal (east and west), and reach Kalekapisi between 10 and 15 minutes later.</p>
<p>In December 2009, an 11.1-kilometre (6.9&#160;mi) <a href="/wiki/Light_rail" title="Light rail">light rail</a> line Antray was opened from one of the main city public bus hubs northwest to beyond <a href="/wiki/Kepez,_Antalya" title="Kepez, Antalya">suburban areas</a> and the zoo. An extension to Airport, <a href="/wiki/Aksu,_Antalya" title="Aksu, Antalya">Aksu</a> and <a href="/wiki/Expo_2016" title="Expo 2016">Expo 2016</a> site was completed in 2016.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Major_routes">Major routes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Antalya&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Major routes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Antalya is the southern terminus of <a href="/wiki/European_route_E87" title="European route E87">European route E87</a>, which connects to <a href="/wiki/Korkuteli" title="Korkuteli">Korkuteli</a>, <a href="/wiki/Denizli" title="Denizli">Denizli</a>, <a href="/wiki/%C4%B0zmir" title="İzmir">İzmir</a>, <a href="/wiki/%C3%87anakkale" title="Çanakkale">Çanakkale</a>, <a href="/wiki/Edirne" title="Edirne">Edirne</a> in Turkey, alongside with Varna in <a href="/wiki/Bulgaria" title="Bulgaria">Bulgaria</a>, <a href="/wiki/Constanta" class="mw-redirect" title="Constanta">Constanta</a> in <a href="/wiki/Romania" title="Romania">Romania</a> and <a href="/wiki/Odessa" title="Odessa">Odessa</a> in <a href="/wiki/Ukraine" title="Ukraine">Ukraine</a>. Antalya is also the terminus of Turkish Highway <a href="/wiki/State_road_D650_(Turkey)" class="mw-redirect" title="State road D650 (Turkey)">D650</a>, which connects <a href="/wiki/Burdur" title="Burdur">Burdur</a>, <a href="/wiki/Afyon" class="mw-redirect" title="Afyon">Afyon</a>, <a href="/wiki/K%C3%BCtahya" title="Kütahya">Kütahya</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Adapazar%C4%B1" title="Adapazarı">Sakarya</a>.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/State_road_D400_(Turkey)" class="mw-redirect" title="State road D400 (Turkey)">D400</a> connects with D650 in Antalya, while D650 alternative <a href="/w/index.php?title=State_road_D685_(Turkey)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="State road D685 (Turkey) (page does not exist)">D685</a> connects to <a href="/wiki/Isparta" title="Isparta">Isparta</a> and provincial road 07-50 connects to <a href="/wiki/Kumluca" title="Kumluca">Kumluca</a> by <a href="/wiki/Alt%C4%B1nyaka,_Kumluca" title="Altınyaka, Kumluca">Altınyaka</a>, an alternative to D400.<sup id="cite_ref-59" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Airports">Airports</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Antalya&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Airports">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Antalya_Airport" title="Antalya Airport">Antalya Airport</a> has two international terminals and one domestic terminal. In 2007, its number of passengers on international flights surpassed the total number at <a href="/wiki/Istanbul_Ataturk_Airport" class="mw-redirect" title="Istanbul Ataturk Airport">Istanbul Ataturk Airport</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sabiha_G%C3%B6k%C3%A7en_International_Airport" title="Sabiha Gökçen International Airport">Sabiha Gökçen International Airport</a> for the first time, officially earning the title of "the capital of Turkish tourism".<sup id="cite_ref-60" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-61" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Antalya_Public_Transportation_Statistics">Antalya Public Transportation Statistics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Antalya&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Antalya Public Transportation Statistics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The average amount of time people spend commuting with public transit in Antalya, for example to and from work, on a weekday is 63 min. 14.% of public transit riders, ride for more than 2 hours every day. The average amount of time people wait at a stop or station for public transit is 23 min, while 50% of riders wait for over 20 minutes on average every day. The average distance people usually ride in a single trip with public transit is 7.6 km, while 13% travel for over 12 km in a single direction. <sup id="cite_ref-62" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup></p>
