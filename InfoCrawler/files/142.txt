<p><b>Ramsey Abbey</b> was a <a href="/wiki/Order_of_Saint_Benedict" title="Order of Saint Benedict">Benedictine</a> <a href="/wiki/Abbey" title="Abbey">abbey</a> in <a href="/wiki/Ramsey,_Cambridgeshire" title="Ramsey, Cambridgeshire">Ramsey</a>, <a href="/wiki/Huntingdonshire" title="Huntingdonshire">Huntingdonshire</a> (now part of <a href="/wiki/Cambridgeshire" title="Cambridgeshire">Cambridgeshire</a>), England. It was founded in AD 969 and <a href="/wiki/Dissolution_of_the_Monasteries" title="Dissolution of the Monasteries">dissolved</a> in 1537.</p>
<p>The site of the abbey in Ramsey is now a <a href="/wiki/Scheduled_monument" title="Scheduled monument">Scheduled Ancient Monument</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Most of the abbey's buildings were demolished after the dissolution. Parts of a few buildings survive, and are now <a href="/wiki/Listed_building#Categories_of_listed_building" title="Listed building">Grade I and Grade II* listed</a> buildings.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Abbey">The Abbey</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ramsey_Abbey&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: The Abbey">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Ramsey Abbey was founded in 969 by Saint <a href="/wiki/Oswald_of_Worcester" title="Oswald of Worcester">Oswald</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bishop_of_Worcester" title="Bishop of Worcester">Bishop of Worcester</a> through the gift of a local Earl, Ailwyn or Æthelwine. The foundation was part of the mid-10th century <a href="/wiki/English_Benedictine_reform" class="mw-redirect" title="English Benedictine reform">English Benedictine reform</a>, in which <a href="/wiki/Ely_Cathedral" title="Ely Cathedral">Ely</a> and <a href="/wiki/Peterborough_Cathedral" title="Peterborough Cathedral">Peterborough</a> were also refounded. It paid 4,000 eels yearly in <a href="/wiki/Lent" title="Lent">Lent</a> to <a href="/wiki/Peterborough_Abbey" class="mw-redirect" title="Peterborough Abbey">Peterborough Abbey</a> for access to its quarries of <a href="/wiki/Barnack" title="Barnack">Barnack</a> limestone.</p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Ramsey.Psalter.1310.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a9/Ramsey.Psalter.1310.jpg/220px-Ramsey.Psalter.1310.jpg" width="220" height="154" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a9/Ramsey.Psalter.1310.jpg/330px-Ramsey.Psalter.1310.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a9/Ramsey.Psalter.1310.jpg/440px-Ramsey.Psalter.1310.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2296" data-file-height="1605" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Ramsey.Psalter.1310.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
This Ramsey Psalter of c. 1310 was a gift to the <a href="/wiki/Abbot" title="Abbot">abbot</a> from the <a href="/wiki/Cellarer" class="mw-redirect" title="Cellarer">cellarer</a> (facsimile)</div>
</div>

<p>The important <a href="/wiki/Ramsey_Psalter" title="Ramsey Psalter">Ramsey Psalter</a> or Psalter of Oswald (<a href="/wiki/British_Library" title="British Library">British Library</a>) appears to have been made for Ramsey Abbey around 980. This is not to be confused with another Ramsey Psalter in the <a href="/wiki/Pierpont_Morgan_Library" class="mw-redirect" title="Pierpont Morgan Library">Pierpont Morgan Library</a>, New York (MS M. 302), made between 1286 and 1316.</p>
<p>Æthelwine at the suggestion of Saint <a href="/wiki/Oswald_of_Worcester" title="Oswald of Worcester">Oswald</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bishop_of_Worcester" title="Bishop of Worcester">Bishop of Worcester</a> founded a small hermitage for three hermits with a wooden chapel at a location indicated by the actions of a bull, on a narrow peninsula of gravel called Bodsey with impassible fen on three sides. Impressed by the story Oswald sent a Prior from Westbury (Germanus) and 12 monks to form the Abbey, on the Island of Ramsey. Starting in 969, a large stone-built church was built over the next five years. Two towers stood up at the topmost points of the roofs, the smaller one at the front of the Church towards the west, offered a beautiful sight from afar to people coming to the island. The larger one, in the middle of a four-armed structure rested on four columns stabilzed by connecting arches. This abbey building remained until a Norman abbot had a grander church built in the 12th century.</p>
<p>In 1143 <a href="/wiki/Geoffrey_de_Mandeville,_1st_Earl_of_Essex" title="Geoffrey de Mandeville, 1st Earl of Essex">Geoffrey de Mandeville</a> expelled the monks, used the abbey as a fortress and considerably damaged the buildings.</p>
<p>An effigy of Ailwyn/Æthelwine dating from 1230 is thought to be within the Abbey.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="Is the effigy only &quot;thought to&quot; exist? Has anyone seen it recently? What is this sentence really intended to mean? (June 2017)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>In the order of precedence for abbots in Parliament, Ramsey was third after <a href="/wiki/Glastonbury_Abbey" title="Glastonbury Abbey">Glastonbury</a> and <a href="/wiki/St_Alban%27s_Abbey" class="mw-redirect" title="St Alban's Abbey">St Alban's</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-CE_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CE-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>The abbey was an international centre of Hebrew scholarship in the late Middle Ages. It prospered until the <a href="/wiki/Dissolution_of_the_Monasteries" title="Dissolution of the Monasteries">Dissolution of the Monasteries</a> in 1537. At the time of the Dissolution there were 34 monks.</p>
<p>In 1787 Mark Noble noted:<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTENoble178718.2C_19_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTENoble178718.2C_19-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>The abbey of Ramsey, i.e. the Ram's isle, was one of the richest foundations in the kingdom: the abbot was mitred, and sat in the house of lords as baron of Broughton; the abbey had 387 <a href="/wiki/Hide_(unit)" title="Hide (unit)">hides</a> of land, 200 of which were in Huntingdonshire: the monks were not famed for their liberality, if we believe the following ancient lines:</p>
<dl>
<dd>Crowland as courteous, as courteous as may bee,</dd>
<dd>Thorney the bane of many a good Tree,</dd>
<dd>Ramsey the rich, and Peterborough the proud,</dd>
<dd>Sawtry by the way that poor abbay,</dd>
<dd>Gave more almes than all they.</dd>
</dl>
</blockquote>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="After_the_Dissolution">After the Dissolution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ramsey_Abbey&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: After the Dissolution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1540 <a href="/wiki/The_Crown" title="The Crown">the Crown</a> sold the abbey lands to Sir <a href="/wiki/Richard_Williams_(alias_Cromwell)" title="Richard Williams (alias Cromwell)">Richard Williams (alias Cromwell)</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-CE_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CE-2">[2]</a></sup> He used most of the abbey buildings as a source of stone for walls and cottages at hand, and to provide good Barnack stone for new buildings. He had part of the <a href="/wiki/Ramsey_Abbey_Gatehouse" title="Ramsey Abbey Gatehouse">abbey gatehouse</a> dismantled and re-erected at <a href="/wiki/Hinchingbrooke_House" title="Hinchingbrooke House">Hinchingbrooke House</a>. Much stone was taken to Cambridge to build <a href="/wiki/Gonville_and_Caius_College,_Cambridge" title="Gonville and Caius College, Cambridge">Gonville and Caius</a>, <a href="/wiki/King%27s_College,_Cambridge" title="King's College, Cambridge">King's</a> and <a href="/wiki/Trinity_College,_Cambridge" title="Trinity College, Cambridge">Trinity</a> colleges. Stone was taken for the tower for the parish church of St Mary the Virgin in <a href="/wiki/Godmanchester" title="Godmanchester">Godmanchester</a>. This included a doorway from the abbey that was dismantled and re-erected as the west doorway of St Mary's. As late as 1672 stone for a new tower for Ramsey's own <a href="/wiki/Church_of_St_Thomas_a_Becket,_Ramsey" title="Church of St Thomas a Becket, Ramsey">parish church of St Thomas a Becket</a> was also taken from the Abbey.</p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Ramsey_Abbey_1.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9b/Ramsey_Abbey_1.jpg/220px-Ramsey_Abbey_1.jpg" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9b/Ramsey_Abbey_1.jpg/330px-Ramsey_Abbey_1.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9b/Ramsey_Abbey_1.jpg/440px-Ramsey_Abbey_1.jpg 2x" data-file-width="640" data-file-height="480" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Ramsey_Abbey_1.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Ramsey Abbey House, remodelled in 1804–06 by Sir <a href="/wiki/John_Soane" title="John Soane">John Soane</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Around 1600 Sir <a href="/wiki/Henry_Williams_(alias_Cromwell)" title="Henry Williams (alias Cromwell)">Henry Williams (alias Cromwell)</a> had a house built on the site of the abbey church. Six bays of the 13th-century Lady Chapel survive as the basement of the house.<sup id="cite_ref-NHLE-School_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NHLE-School-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1737 <a href="/w/index.php?title=Coulson_Fellowes&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Coulson Fellowes (page does not exist)">Coulson Fellowes</a>, later MP for <a href="/wiki/Huntingdonshire_(UK_Parliament_constituency)" title="Huntingdonshire (UK Parliament constituency)">Huntingdonshire</a>, bought the house. It passed down through several generations of the family. In 1804–06 <a href="/wiki/William_Henry_Fellowes" title="William Henry Fellowes">William Henry Fellowes</a> had the abbey house enlarged to designs by Sir <a href="/wiki/John_Soane" title="John Soane">John Soane</a>. In 1889 his sone <a href="/wiki/Edward_Fellowes,_1st_Baron_de_Ramsey" title="Edward Fellowes, 1st Baron de Ramsey">Edward Fellowes</a> was created 1st <a href="/wiki/Baron_de_Ramsey" title="Baron de Ramsey">Baron de Ramsey</a>. In 1931 at the coming of age of <a href="/wiki/John_Ailwyn_Fellowes,_4th_Baron_de_Ramsey" title="John Ailwyn Fellowes, 4th Baron de Ramsey">John Ailwyn Fellowes, 4th Baron de Ramsey</a> the family moved its seat to <a href="/wiki/Abbots_Ripton" title="Abbots Ripton">Abbots Ripton Hall</a>. In 1937 the Fellowes leased the building for 99 years to Ramsey Abbey School. In 1952 <a href="/wiki/Ailwyn_Fellowes,_3rd_Baron_de_Ramsey" title="Ailwyn Fellowes, 3rd Baron de Ramsey">Ailwyn Fellowes, 3rd Baron de Ramsey</a> gave the gatehouse to the <a href="/wiki/National_Trust_for_Places_of_Historic_Interest_or_Natural_Beauty" title="National Trust for Places of Historic Interest or Natural Beauty">National Trust</a> in memory of his sister Diana Broughton.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Surviving_buildings_and_artefacts">Surviving buildings and artefacts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ramsey_Abbey&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Surviving buildings and artefacts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Ramsey_Censer_and_Incense_Boat.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a9/Ramsey_Censer_and_Incense_Boat.JPG/170px-Ramsey_Censer_and_Incense_Boat.JPG" width="170" height="227" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a9/Ramsey_Censer_and_Incense_Boat.JPG/255px-Ramsey_Censer_and_Incense_Boat.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a9/Ramsey_Censer_and_Incense_Boat.JPG/340px-Ramsey_Censer_and_Incense_Boat.JPG 2x" data-file-width="3000" data-file-height="4000" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Ramsey_Censer_and_Incense_Boat.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Ramsey Abbey Censer and Incense Boat, early to mid 14th-century, in the <a href="/wiki/Victoria_and_Albert_Museum" title="Victoria and Albert Museum">V&amp;A Museum</a>, London</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Ramsey Abbey House, the Gatehouse, and the <a href="/wiki/Church_of_St_Thomas_a_Becket,_Ramsey" title="Church of St Thomas a Becket, Ramsey">parish church of St Thomas a Becket</a> all survive,<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> along with part of the abbey's Medieval precinct wall.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Ramsey Abbey House, the former 17th century home of Sir Henry Cromwell and latterly the seat of the Fellowes family, is currently part of <a href="/wiki/Abbey_College,_Ramsey" title="Abbey College, Ramsey">Abbey College</a>.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Ramsey_Abbey_Gatehouse" title="Ramsey Abbey Gatehouse">Abbey Gatehouse</a> is a <a href="/wiki/National_Trust_for_Places_of_Historic_Interest_or_Natural_Beauty" title="National Trust for Places of Historic Interest or Natural Beauty">National Trust</a> property.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> This is believed to be an inner gatehouse, the main outer gatehouse was removed by the son and heir of Sir Richard (Sir Henry Williams (alias Cromwell)) to form the main gateway to <a href="/wiki/Hinchingbrooke_House" title="Hinchingbrooke House">Hinchingbrooke House</a> in Huntingdon, his newly built winter residence.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Today what remains of the gatehouse also forms a part of the Abbey College.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[a]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Church_of_St_Thomas_a_Becket,_Ramsey" title="Church of St Thomas a Becket, Ramsey">Church of St Thomas a Becket, Ramsey</a> was built in about 1180 or 1190 as either the <i><a href="/wiki/Hospitium#Medieval_Hospitia" title="Hospitium">hospitium</a></i> or the infirmary of the abbey. It was originally an aisled hall with a chapel at the east end with a vestry on the north side and the warden's lodgings on the south, but both these have been demolished. The building was converted into the parish church in about 1222.</p>
<p>When <a href="/wiki/Whittlesey_Mere" title="Whittlesey Mere">Whittlesey Mere</a> was drained, a <a href="/wiki/Thurible" title="Thurible">thurible</a> and other silver items were found in the bed of the mere and from the ram's head on one of these pieces were believed to have come from the Abbey.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[10]</a></sup> The thurible (or <a href="/wiki/Censer" title="Censer">censer</a>),<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[11]</a></sup> and an incense boat<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[12]</a></sup> are now in the <a href="/wiki/Victoria_and_Albert_Museum" title="Victoria and Albert Museum">Victoria and Albert Museum</a>. Also found in the bed were blocks of quarried stone,<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[13]</a></sup> that are conjectured to have fallen from a barge on the way to the Abbey.</p>

<li><a href="/wiki/Ivo_of_Ramsey" title="Ivo of Ramsey">Ivo of Ramsey</a> who gave his name to <a href="/wiki/St_Ives,_Huntingdonshire" class="mw-redirect" title="St Ives, Huntingdonshire">St Ives, Huntingdonshire</a></li>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Abbots">Abbots</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ramsey_Abbey&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Abbots">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The names of abbots from AD 993 onwards are known.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[14]</a></sup> Notable among them are:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Eadnoth_the_Younger" title="Eadnoth the Younger">Eadnoth the Younger</a>, who was also Bishop of <a href="/wiki/Dorchester_Abbey" title="Dorchester Abbey">Dorchester</a>, and was killed at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Assandun" title="Battle of Assandun">Battle of Assandun</a> in 1016</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Herbert_de_Losinga" title="Herbert de Losinga">Herbert de Losinga</a>, the first <a href="/wiki/Bishop_of_Norwich" title="Bishop of Norwich">Bishop of Norwich</a>, who died in 1119</li>
<li>Walter, abbot from 1133-1161, during the reign of <a href="/wiki/Stephen,_King_of_England" title="Stephen, King of England">Stephen</a> and the invasion of Ramsey by <a href="/wiki/Geoffrey_de_Mandeville,_1st_Earl_of_Essex" title="Geoffrey de Mandeville, 1st Earl of Essex">Geoffrey de Mandeville</a>, first Earl of Essex.</li>
</ul>
