<p><b>Muhammad Afzal Ahsan Randhawa</b> (<a href="/wiki/Urdu_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Urdu language">Urdu</a>: <span lang="ur" dir="rtl" xml:lang="ur"><span class="Nastaliq" title="Nastaliq" style="font-size: 125%; font-family: 'Jameel Noori Nastaleeq', 'Urdu Typesetting', 'Noto Nastaliq Urdu', 'Noto Nastaliq Urdu Draft', 'Hussaini Nastaleeq', 'AlQalam Taj Nastaleeq', IranNastaliq, 'Awami Nastaliq', 'Awami Nastaliq Beta3', 'Awami Nastaliq Beta2', 'Awami Nastaliq Beta1', 'Nafees Nastaleeq', 'Nafees Nastaleeq v1.01', 'Pak Nastaleeq', 'PDMS_Jauhar', 'Alvi Lahori Nastaleeq';">محمد افضل احسن رندھاوا</span>‎</span>‎, 1 September 1937 – 18 September 2017) was a Pakistani <a href="/wiki/Punjabi_language" title="Punjabi language">Punjabi language</a> writer, poet, translator, playwright and a politician.<sup id="cite_ref-DAWN_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DAWN-2">[2]</a></sup> He authored several short stories and novels in the <a href="/wiki/Punjabi_language" title="Punjabi language">Punjabi language</a> including <i>Sooraj Grehan</i> and <i>Doaba</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> He received the <a href="/wiki/Pride_of_Performance" title="Pride of Performance">Pride of Performance</a> and <i>Kamal-e-Fun</i> awards for his literary work.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_and_personal_life">Early and personal life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Afzal_Ahsan_Randhawa&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early and personal life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Muhammad Afzal Ahsan Randhawa was born in <a href="/wiki/Amritsar" title="Amritsar">Amritsar</a>, <a href="/wiki/Punjab_Province_(British_India)" title="Punjab Province (British India)">Punjab</a>, <a href="/wiki/British_India" class="mw-redirect" title="British India">British India</a> (now <a href="/wiki/Punjab,_India" title="Punjab, India">Punjab, India</a>) on 1 September 1937.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> He belonged to the <a href="/wiki/Randhawa" title="Randhawa">Randhawa</a> clan of the <a href="/wiki/Bhatti_Rajput" title="Bhatti Rajput">Bhatti Rajput</a> tribe.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-DAWN_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DAWN-2">[2]</a></sup> Randhawa grew up in a rural part of the <a href="/wiki/Narowal_District" title="Narowal District">Sialkot district</a> in <a href="/wiki/Pakistan" title="Pakistan">Pakistan</a>, where he was the editor of the magazine of the Mission High School.<sup id="cite_ref-DAWN2_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DAWN2-6">[6]</a></sup> He graduated from <a href="/wiki/Murray_College" title="Murray College">Murray College</a> in <a href="/wiki/Sialkot" title="Sialkot">Sialkot</a>. As a student, he used to be published in <a href="/wiki/Daily_Imroze" title="Daily Imroze">Daily Imroze</a> and weekly <i>Lail-o-Nahar</i>. Later, he got admission in the <a href="/wiki/Punjab_University_Law_College" title="Punjab University Law College">Punjab University Law College</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-DAWN_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DAWN-2">[2]</a></sup> He was the editor of the college magazine <i>Al-Meezan</i>, while in law college.<sup id="cite_ref-DAWN2_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DAWN2-6">[6]</a></sup> He had a love marriage with his wife Ayesha Randhawa (d. 2016), who taught at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Agriculture_(Faisalabad)" title="University of Agriculture (Faisalabad)">University of Agriculture</a> in <a href="/wiki/Faisalabad" title="Faisalabad">Faisalabad</a>. Their only child was a son named Khurrum (d. 2014).<sup id="cite_ref-DAWN_2-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DAWN-2">[2]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Obituary_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Obituary-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biography">Biography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Afzal_Ahsan_Randhawa&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Biography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Randhawa was a left leaning politician and a lawyer.<sup id="cite_ref-DAWN_2-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DAWN-2">[2]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-HT_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HT-1">[1]</a></sup> In 1972, in a by-election he won the NA-49 seat in <a href="/wiki/Lyallpur" class="mw-redirect" title="Lyallpur">Lyallpur</a> (now Faisalabad) for the <a href="/wiki/National_Assembly_of_Pakistan" title="National Assembly of Pakistan">National Assembly</a> on <a href="/wiki/Pakistan_Peoples_Party" title="Pakistan Peoples Party">Pakistan Peoples Party</a>'s ticket.<sup id="cite_ref-HT_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HT-1">[1]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> He contributed to the framing of the <a href="/wiki/1973_Constitution_of_Pakistan" class="mw-redirect" title="1973 Constitution of Pakistan">1973 Constitution of Pakistan</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Obituary_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Obituary-7">[7]</a></sup> In 1977, during the martial law of General <a href="/wiki/Muhammad_Zia-ul-Haq" title="Muhammad Zia-ul-Haq">Zia ul Haq</a> has was disqualified from taking part in politics for seven years by the 'military courts' and later in 1981, he was detained for 'living beyond his means'.<sup id="cite_ref-DAWN_2-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DAWN-2">[2]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Notes_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Notes-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>Randhawa was popular among <a href="/wiki/Sikh" title="Sikh">Sikh</a> as he raised voice against 1984 Indian military <a href="/wiki/Operation_Bluestar" class="mw-redirect" title="Operation Bluestar">Operation Bluestar</a> on the <a href="/wiki/Golden_Temple" title="Golden Temple">Golden Temple</a>. He also wrote a poem on the operation <i>Navan Ghallughara</i> (new Holocaust) glorifying <a href="/wiki/Jarnail_Singh_Bhindranwale" title="Jarnail Singh Bhindranwale">Jarnail Singh Bhindranwale</a> as a great warrior. Hence, his works have been transcribed in <a href="/wiki/Gurmukhi_script" title="Gurmukhi script">Gurmukhi script</a> and published in the <a href="/wiki/Punjab,_India" title="Punjab, India">Indian Punjab</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-HT_1-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HT-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1986, Randhawa was awarded the Prof. <a href="/wiki/Piara_Singh_Gill" title="Piara Singh Gill">Piara Singh Gill</a> &amp; Karam Singh Sandhu Memorial <i>Antar-Rashtari Shiromani Sahitkaar/Kalakaar</i> Award by the International Association of Punjabi Authors and Artists.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> In 1996, the President of Pakistan conferred <a href="/wiki/Pride_of_Performance" title="Pride of Performance">Pride of Performance</a> award to him.<sup id="cite_ref-Obituary_7-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Obituary-7">[7]</a></sup> In 1999, he was bestowed upon with Kartar Singh Dhaliwal award by the Punjabi Sahit Akademi, Ludhiana.<sup id="cite_ref-HT_1-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HT-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Randhawa used to participate as a guest or a panelist at different literature festivals and writing conferences in Pakistan.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Aitzaz_Ahsan" title="Aitzaz Ahsan">Aitzaz Ahsan</a> a prominent lawyer, in his 1996 book (reprinted in 2005) titled 'The Indus Saga', quoted six verses of Randhawa's poem to depict the Indus man.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-DAWN_2-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DAWN-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2014, Randhawa was interviewed by Masood Malhi of <a href="/wiki/Special_Broadcasting_Service" title="Special Broadcasting Service">Special Broadcasting Service</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> In 2015, the <a href="/wiki/Pakistan_Academy_of_Letters" title="Pakistan Academy of Letters">Pakistan Academy of Letters</a> announced the 2013 <i>Kamal-e-Fun Award</i> – the highest literary award, to be presented to Randhawa along with a ₨. 500,000 prize money.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> In 2016, Randhawa opened the <i>Lyallpur Sulekh Mela</i> (Lyallpur Literary Festival) held in the <a href="/wiki/Faisalabad_Arts_Council" title="Faisalabad Arts Council">Faisalabad Arts Council</a> in Faisalabad.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Works">Works</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Afzal_Ahsan_Randhawa&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Works">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In Punjabi, Randhawa had contributed four novels, four collections of short stories, six collections of poetry, one collection of TV and radio dramas and three translated versions of an African novel, one collection of African poetry and one translation of interviews of world leaders. He also wrote a collection of <a href="/wiki/Urdu" title="Urdu">Urdu</a> poetry.<sup id="cite_ref-DAWN2_6-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DAWN2-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>His literary career began in 1961 with the publication of <i>Deeva tey Darya</i> – his first novel.<sup id="cite_ref-Obituary_7-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Obituary-7">[7]</a></sup> It became the first book by a Pakistani to be published in India. He was awarded the Adamjee Prize for Literature for 1961-62 by the Pakistan Writers' Guild for his novelette <i>Deeva tey Darya</i> and for his second Punjabi novel <i>Doaba</i> in 1981-82.<sup id="cite_ref-DAWN_2-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DAWN-2">[2]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-DAWN2_6-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DAWN2-6">[6]</a></sup> Randhawa's novel <i>Sooraj Grehan</i> published in 1984, is an exchange of letters between two lovers.<sup id="cite_ref-DAWN_2-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DAWN-2">[2]</a></sup> His fourth novel <i>Pundh</i> was published in 2001.<sup id="cite_ref-Notes_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Notes-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1965, he published a collection of poetry <i>Sheesha Aik Lashkaray Dou</i>; followed by a collection of short stories <i>Runn, Talwar Tay Ghora</i> published in 1973. Other short story collections, include <i>Randhawa Dian Kahanian</i> in 1988, <i>Munna Koh Lahore</i> in 1989 and <i>Illahi Mohar</i> in 2013. Randhawa’s further five poetry collections, include <i>Raat Daay Char Safar</i> in 1975; <i>Punjab Di Var</i> in 1979; <i>Mitti Di Mehek</i> in 1983; <i>Piyali Wich Aasmaan</i> in 1983; and <i>Chhewaan Darya</i> in 1997.<sup id="cite_ref-Obituary_7-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Obituary-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>He also translated <a href="/wiki/Chinua_Achebe" title="Chinua Achebe">Chinua Achebe</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Things_Fall_Apart" title="Things Fall Apart">Things Fall Apart</a> as <i>Tutt Bhaj</i> (1986) and <a href="/wiki/Gabriel_Garc%C3%ADa_M%C3%A1rquez" title="Gabriel García Márquez">Gabriel García Márquez</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Chronicle_of_a_Death_Foretold" title="Chronicle of a Death Foretold">Chronicle of a Death Foretold</a> as <i>Maut Da Roznamcha</i> (1993) into Punjabi.<sup id="cite_ref-Obituary_7-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Obituary-7">[7]</a></sup> In 2011, his collection of short stories <i>Elahi Mohr Tey Doojian Kahanian</i> was published.<sup id="cite_ref-DAWN2_6-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DAWN2-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Death">Death</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Afzal_Ahsan_Randhawa&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Death">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>On the evening of 18 September 2017, Randhawa died in Faisalabad, Pakistan, seventeen days after his 80th birthday. He was buried next to his son and wife in Qaim Sain Graveyard in <a href="/wiki/Ghulam_Muhammad_Abad" title="Ghulam Muhammad Abad">Ghulam Muhammad Abad</a>, Faisalabad on 20 September.<sup id="cite_ref-DAWN_2-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DAWN-2">[2]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Obituary_7-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Obituary-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>On his death, <a href="/wiki/Fakhar_Zaman" title="Fakhar Zaman">Fakhar Zaman</a> chairman of the World Punjabi Congress, said that "his poetry and short stories were equally trend setters and undoubtedly he was one of the very few writers who were equally popular in Pakistan and India".<sup id="cite_ref-Obituary_7-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Obituary-7">[7]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
