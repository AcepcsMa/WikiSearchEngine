<p>The <b>Association for Information Science and Technology</b> (<b>ASIS&amp;T</b>) is a <a href="/wiki/Non-profit_organization" class="mw-redirect" title="Non-profit organization">non-profit</a> <a href="/wiki/Membership_organization" title="Membership organization">membership organization</a> for <a href="/wiki/Information_professional" title="Information professional">information professionals</a>. Previously known as the <b>American Society for Information Science and Technology</b> (2000–2013), the organization sponsors an <a href="/wiki/Academic_conference" title="Academic conference">annual conference</a> as well as several <a href="/wiki/Periodical_literature" title="Periodical literature">serial publications</a>, including the <i><a href="/wiki/Journal_of_the_Association_for_Information_Science_and_Technology" title="Journal of the Association for Information Science and Technology">Journal of the Association for Information Science and Technology</a></i> (JASIST) and the association's <i>Bulletin</i>. The organization provides administration and communications support for its various divisions, known as special-interest groups or <b>SIGs</b>; provides administration for geographically defined chapters; connects job seekers with potential employers; and provides organizational support for <a href="/wiki/Continuing_education" title="Continuing education">continuing education</a> programs for information professionals.<sup id="cite_ref-asist-about_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-asist-about-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Association_for_Information_Science_and_Technology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Watson_Davis" title="Watson Davis">Watson Davis</a> formed the <i>Documentation Institute</i> in 1935, which changed its name to the American Documentation Institute on 1937 on 13 March 1937 with the collaboration of <a href="/wiki/Atherton_Seidell" title="Atherton Seidell">Atherton Seidell</a> and others.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> The organization was first concerned with <a href="/wiki/Microfilm" class="mw-redirect" title="Microfilm">microfilm</a> and its role as a vehicle for the dissemination of information.<sup id="cite_ref-history_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-history-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>ADI early on worked toward the development of microfilm readers and cameras. Their first microfilm laboratories were located in the U.S.<a href="/wiki/United_States_National_Agricultural_Library" title="United States National Agricultural Library">Department of Agriculture Library</a> in Washington, DC and the Institute distributed materials through the newly created Bibliofilm Service.</p>
<p>ADI established the Auxiliary Publication Program, which during its 30-year history released nearly 10,000 documents covering a wide range of subjects. The program enabled authors in the fields of physical, natural, social, historical and information sciences to publish and distribute research papers that were either too long, typographically complex or expensive to be published in journals using existing technology.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> In 1954, the Photoduplication Service at the Library of Congress took over the operation and became the source point for distributing ADI materials and in 2009 this material found its home in the Library's Technical Reports and Standards Unit.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>1950s was the transition to Modern Information Science:In 1952 the Bylaws were amended to allow individuals to become members due to the number of people that were engaged in the development of new principles and techniques. The goal was to make ADI a group that was concerned with all elements and problems of information science not just libraries. During this time there were increased interests and developments of automatic devices for searching, storage and retrieval.<sup id="cite_ref-asist-about_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-asist-about-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>In January 1968, ADI became the <b>American Society for Information Science</b>. The change was made to represent the organization’s interest in "all aspects of the information transfer process" such as, "designing, managing and using information systems and technology."<sup id="cite_ref-history_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-history-4">[4]</a></sup> In 2000 the organization updated its name, adding <i>Technology</i> to embrace the prevalence and increasing centrality of <a href="/wiki/Online_database" title="Online database">online databases</a> and other technical aspects of the information profession. In 2013 the organization was renamed as the Association for Information Science and Technology (ASIS&amp;T) to reflect its international membership. Today, the organization comprises professionals from various fields including engineering, linguistics, librarianship, education, chemistry, computer science and medicine. The members share "a common interest in improving the ways society stores, retrieves, analyzes, manages, archives and disseminates information ".<sup id="cite_ref-asist-about_2-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-asist-about-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>1970s was The Move to Online Information: During this time many institutions are making the move from batch processing to online modes, from mainframe computers to more modern computers. With the advancement of technology the traditional boundaries began to fade and library schools started to add "information" in the titles of their programs. ASIS sponsored a bicentennial conference which focused on the role of information in the country's development. The group also participated in the planning and implementation of the White House Conference on Library and Information Services. ".<sup id="cite_ref-asist-about_2-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-asist-about-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>1980s brings is the beginning of the popularity of Personal Computers: This is the first time individuals can access large databases, such as Grateful Med at the National Library of Medicine, and user-oriented services such as Dialog and Compuserve on their personal computers. ASIS created groups on office information, personal computers, international information issues and rural information services in response to the changing environment. Eventually other groups were created, such as: non-print media, social sciences, energy and the environment, and community information systems. ASIS also added its first non-North American Chapters. ".<sup id="cite_ref-asist-about_2-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-asist-about-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>ASIS&amp;T Today: The group continues to grow and change. In 2000's the groups name changes twice; first in 2000 to American Society for Information Science and Technology (ASIS&amp;T)in order to reflect the range of its members; then in 2013 to Association for Information Science and Technology (ASIS&amp;T) to reflect its growing international membership. ASIS&amp;T is involved in the forefront of in examining the technical bases, social consequences, and theoretical understanding of online databases. They also study the effects of widespread use of databases in government, industry, and education, and the development of information databases on the Internet and World Wide Web ".<sup id="cite_ref-asist-about_2-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-asist-about-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Mission">Mission</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Association_for_Information_Science_and_Technology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Mission">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In a world where "information is of central importance to personal, social, political, and economic progress", ASIS&amp;T seeks to advance the <a href="/wiki/Information_science" title="Information science">information sciences</a> and <a href="/wiki/Information_technology" title="Information technology">information technology</a> by providing focus, opportunity, and support to information professionals and <a href="/wiki/Information_organization" class="mw-redirect" title="Information organization">information organizations</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-missionvision_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-missionvision-8">[8]</a></sup> ASIS&amp;T seeks to advance knowledge "about information, its creation, properties, and use" as well as increase "public awareness of the information sciences and technologies and their benefits to society."<sup id="cite_ref-missionvision_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-missionvision-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Vision">Vision</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Association_for_Information_Science_and_Technology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Vision">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>To establish an information professionalism in the world by: Advancing knowledge about information; Providing analysis of ideas;; Valuing theory, research, applications, and service; Nurturing new perspectives, interests, and ideas; Increasing public awareness of the information sciences and technologies and their benefits to society."<sup id="cite_ref-missionvision_8-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-missionvision-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Membership">Membership</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Association_for_Information_Science_and_Technology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Membership">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Originally membership was based on representatives nominated by scientific societies, professional associations, foundations, and government agencies.<sup id="cite_ref-history_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-history-4">[4]</a></sup> Changes made to the bylaws in 1952 opened the organization to any individual with interest in the dissemination of information. Today, fee-based memberships can be either individual or institutional, with no formal requirements to join as an individual. Similar to most organizations of its kind, ASIS&amp;T offers benefits to its members in the form of subscriptions to publications, access to job assistance services (JobLine); and discounts to ASIS&amp;T-sponsored events.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Publications">Publications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Association_for_Information_Science_and_Technology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Publications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In addition to the scholarly <i><a href="/wiki/JASIST" class="mw-redirect" title="JASIST">JASIST</a></i>, the organization publishes the bi-monthly <i>Bulletin of the American Society for Information Science and Technology</i> focused on the latest developments and emerging issues in the field.</p>
<p>In 1966, ADI began publication of the <a href="/wiki/Annual_Review_of_Information_Science_and_Technology" title="Annual Review of Information Science and Technology">Annual Review of Information Science and Technology</a>. Its successor organizations continued publishing the annual review under that title until 2011.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-asist-pubs_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-asist-pubs-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="JASIST">JASIST</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Association_for_Information_Science_and_Technology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: JASIST">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>ADI decided in 1950 to create a journal modeled after the defunct <i>Journal of Documentary Reproduction</i>, which had been published by the <a href="/wiki/American_Library_Association" title="American Library Association">American Library Association</a> from 1938<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> to 1942.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> ADI published the journal <i>American Documentation</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> from 1950 until 1968, when ADI changed its name as an organization and renamed <i>American Documentation</i> as the <i>Journal of the American Society for Information Science</i>. In 2000, the society changed names again, and the journal followed suit, becoming the current <a href="/wiki/Journal_of_the_American_Society_for_Information_Science_and_Technology" class="mw-redirect" title="Journal of the American Society for Information Science and Technology">Journal of the American Society for Information Science and Technology</a>.</p>
