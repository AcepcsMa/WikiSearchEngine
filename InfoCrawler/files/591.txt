<p><b>Saint Clare of Assisi</b> (July 16, 1194 – August 11, 1253, born <b>Chiara Offreduccio</b> and sometimes spelled <b>Clair</b>, <b>Claire</b>, etc.) is an <a href="/wiki/Italians" title="Italians">Italian</a> <a href="/wiki/Saint" title="Saint">saint</a> and one of the first followers of <a href="/wiki/Saint_Francis_of_Assisi" class="mw-redirect" title="Saint Francis of Assisi">Saint Francis of Assisi</a>. She founded the Order of Poor Ladies, a <a href="/wiki/Monasticism" title="Monasticism">monastic</a> <a href="/wiki/Religious_order" title="Religious order">religious order</a> for women in the <a href="/wiki/Franciscan" class="mw-redirect" title="Franciscan">Franciscan</a> tradition, and wrote their Rule of Life,<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> the first set of monastic guidelines known to have been written by a woman. Following her death, the order she founded was renamed in her honor as the Order of Saint Clare, commonly referred to today as the <a href="/wiki/Poor_Clares" title="Poor Clares">Poor Clares</a>. Her feast day is on the 11th of August.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biography">Biography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Clare_of_Assisi&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Biography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:SDamiano-Clara_og_s%C3%B8stre.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/29/SDamiano-Clara_og_s%C3%B8stre.jpg/220px-SDamiano-Clara_og_s%C3%B8stre.jpg" width="220" height="175" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/29/SDamiano-Clara_og_s%C3%B8stre.jpg/330px-SDamiano-Clara_og_s%C3%B8stre.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/29/SDamiano-Clara_og_s%C3%B8stre.jpg/440px-SDamiano-Clara_og_s%C3%B8stre.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2415" data-file-height="1918" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:SDamiano-Clara_og_s%C3%B8stre.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>

<p>St. Clare was born in <a href="/wiki/Assisi" title="Assisi">Assisi</a>, the eldest daughter of Favorino Sciffi, Count of Sasso-Rosso and his wife <a href="/wiki/Ortolana" title="Ortolana">Ortolana</a>. Traditional accounts say that Clare's father was a wealthy representative of an ancient Roman family, who owned a large palace in Assisi and a castle on the slope of <a href="/wiki/Mount_Subasio" class="mw-redirect" title="Mount Subasio">Mount Subasio</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Robinson_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Robinson-2">[2]</a></sup> Ortolana belonged to the noble family of Fiumi, and was a very devout woman who had undertaken pilgrimages to <a href="/wiki/Rome" title="Rome">Rome</a>, <a href="/wiki/Santiago_de_Compostela" title="Santiago de Compostela">Santiago de Compostela</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Holy_Land" title="Holy Land">Holy Land</a>. Later in life, Ortolana entered Clare's monastery, as did Clare's sisters, Beatrix and Catarina (who took the name <a href="/wiki/Agnes_of_Assisi" title="Agnes of Assisi">Agnes</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>As a child, Clare was devoted to prayer. Although there is no mention of this in any historical record, it is assumed that Clare was to be married in line with the family tradition. However, at the age of 18 she heard Francis preach during a Lenten service in the church of San Giorgio at Assisi and asked him to help her to live after the manner of the Gospel. On the evening of <a href="/wiki/Palm_Sunday" title="Palm Sunday">Palm Sunday</a>, March 20, 1212, she left her father's house and accompanied by her aunt Bianca and another companion proceeded to the chapel of the <a href="/wiki/Porziuncula" class="mw-redirect" title="Porziuncula">Porziuncula</a> to meet Francis. There, her hair was cut, and she exchanged her rich gown for a plain robe and veil.<sup id="cite_ref-Robinson_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Robinson-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<p>Francis placed Clare in the convent of the <a href="/wiki/Benedictine" class="mw-redirect" title="Benedictine">Benedictine</a> nuns of San Paulo, near Bastia. Her father attempted to force her to return home. She clung to the altar of the church and threw aside her veil to show her cropped hair.<sup id="cite_ref-Foley_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Foley-4">[4]</a></sup> She resisted any attempt, professing that she would have no other husband but <a href="/wiki/Jesus" title="Jesus">Jesus Christ</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-SHMI_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SHMI-5">[5]</a></sup> In order to provide the greater solitude Clare desired, a few days later Francis sent her to Sant' Angelo in Panzo, another monastery of the Benedictine nuns on one of the flanks of Subasio. Clare was soon joined by her sister Catarina, who took the name Agnes. They remained with the Benedictines until a small dwelling was built for them next to the church of <a href="/wiki/San_Damiano,_Assisi" title="San Damiano, Assisi">San Damiano</a>, which Francis had repaired some years earlier.<sup id="cite_ref-Robinson_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Robinson-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Other women joined them, and they were known as the "Poor Ladies of San Damiano". They lived a simple life of poverty, austerity and seclusion from the world, according to a Rule which Francis gave them as a <a href="/wiki/Second_Order_(religious)" title="Second Order (religious)">Second Order</a> (Poor Clares).<sup id="cite_ref-Foley_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Foley-4">[4]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-SHMI_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SHMI-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>San Damiano became the center of Clare's new religious order, which was known in her lifetime as the "Order of Poor Ladies of San Damiano". San Damiano was long thought to be the first house of this order, however, recent scholarship strongly suggests that San Damiano actually joined an existing network of women's religious houses organized by Hugolino (who later became <a href="/wiki/Pope_Gregory_IX" title="Pope Gregory IX">Pope Gregory IX</a>). Hugolino wanted San Damiano as part of the order he founded because of the prestige of Clare's monastery.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> San Damiano emerged as the most important house in the order, and Clare became its undisputed leader. By 1263, just ten years after Clare's death, the order had become known as the <a href="/wiki/Order_of_Saint_Clare" class="mw-redirect" title="Order of Saint Clare">Order of Saint Clare</a>.</p>
<p>In 1228, when Gregory IX offered Clare a dispensation from the vow of strict poverty, she replied:<sup id="cite_ref-SHMI_5-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SHMI-5">[5]</a></sup></p>

<tr>
<td style="width: 20px; vertical-align: top; border: none; color: #B2B7F2; font-size: 40px; font-family: 'Times New Roman', Times, serif; font-weight: bold; line-height: .6em; text-align: left; padding: 10px 10px;">“</td>
<td style="vertical-align: top; border: none; padding: 4px 10px;">I need to be absolved from my sins, but not from the obligation of following Christ.</td>
<td style="width: 20px; vertical-align: bottom; border: none; color: #B2B7F2; font-size: 40px; font-family: 'Times New Roman', Times, serif; font-weight: bold; line-height: .6em; text-align: right; padding: 10px 10px;">”</td>
</tr>

<p>Accordingly, the Pope granted them the <i>Privilegium Pauperitatis</i> — that nobody could oblige them to accept any possession.<sup id="cite_ref-SHMI_5-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SHMI-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Unlike the <a href="/wiki/Franciscan_friars" class="mw-redirect" title="Franciscan friars">Franciscan friars</a>, whose members moved around the country to preach, Saint Clare's sisters lived in <a href="/wiki/Enclosed_religious_orders" title="Enclosed religious orders">enclosure</a>, since an itinerant life was hardly conceivable at the time for women. Their life consisted of manual labour<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> and prayer. The nuns went barefoot, slept on the ground, ate no meat and observed almost complete silence.<sup id="cite_ref-Foley_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Foley-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>For a short period, the order was directed by Francis himself.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Then in 1216, Clare accepted the role of <a href="/wiki/Abbess" title="Abbess">abbess</a> of San Damiano. As abbess, Clare had more authority to lead the order than when she was the <a href="/wiki/Prioress" class="mw-redirect" title="Prioress">prioress</a> and required to follow the orders of a priest heading the community.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Clare defended her order from the attempts of prelates to impose a rule on them that more closely resembled the <a href="/wiki/Rule_of_Saint_Benedict" title="Rule of Saint Benedict">Rule of Saint Benedict</a> than Francis' stricter vows. Clare sought to imitate Francis' virtues and way of life so much so that she was sometimes titled <i>alter Franciscus</i>, another Francis.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> She also played a significant role in encouraging and aiding Francis, whom she saw as a spiritual father figure, and she took care of him during his final illness.</p>
<p>After Francis's death, Clare continued to promote the growth of her order, writing letters to abbesses in other parts of Europe and thwarting every attempt by each successive <a href="/wiki/Pope" title="Pope">pope</a> to impose a rule on her order which weakened the radical commitment to <a href="/wiki/Corporate_poverty" title="Corporate poverty">corporate poverty</a> she had originally embraced. She did this despite enduring a long period of poor health until her death. Clare's Franciscan theology of joyous poverty in imitation of Christ is evident in the rule she wrote for her community and in her four letters to <a href="/wiki/Agnes_of_Prague" class="mw-redirect" title="Agnes of Prague">Agnes of Prague</a>.</p>
<p>In 1224, the army of <a href="/wiki/Frederick_II,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Frederick II, Holy Roman Emperor">Frederick II</a> came to plunder Assisi. Clare went out to meet them with the <a href="/wiki/Blessed_Sacrament" title="Blessed Sacrament">Blessed Sacrament</a> in her hands. Suddenly a mysterious terror seized the enemies, who fled without harming anybody in the city.<sup id="cite_ref-SHMI_5-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SHMI-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Before breathing her last in 1253, Clare said:<sup id="cite_ref-SHMI_5-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SHMI-5">[5]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Post-death">Post-death</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Clare_of_Assisi&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Post-death">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Santa-chiara.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7a/Santa-chiara.jpg/220px-Santa-chiara.jpg" width="220" height="203" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7a/Santa-chiara.jpg/330px-Santa-chiara.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7a/Santa-chiara.jpg/440px-Santa-chiara.jpg 2x" data-file-width="704" data-file-height="648" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Santa-chiara.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Basilica_of_Saint_Clare" class="mw-redirect" title="Basilica of Saint Clare">Basilica of Saint Clare</a>, <a href="/wiki/Assisi" title="Assisi">Assisi</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>On August 9, 1253, the <a href="/wiki/Papal_bull" title="Papal bull">papal bull</a> <i>Solet annuere</i> of <a href="/wiki/Pope_Innocent_IV" title="Pope Innocent IV">Pope Innocent IV</a> confirmed that Clare's rule would serve as the governing rule for Clare's Order of Poor Ladies. Two days later, on August 11, Clare died at the age of 59.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="(June 2017)">why?</span></a></i>]</sup> Her remains were interred at the chapel of <i>San Giorgio</i> while a church to hold her remains was being constructed. At her funeral, Pope Innocent IV insisted the friars perform the Office for the Virgin Saints as opposed to the Office for the Dead (Bartoli, 1993). This move by Pope Innocent ensured that the canonization process for Clare would begin shortly after her funeral. Pope Innocent was cautioned by multiple advisors against having the Office for the Virgin Saints performed at Clare's funeral (Bartoli, 1993). The most vocal of these advisors was Cardinal Raynaldus who would later become Pope Alexander IV, who in two years time would canonize Clare (Pattenden, 2008). At Pope Innocent's request the canonization process for Clare began immediately. While the whole process took two years, the examination of Clare's miracles took just six days. On September 26, 1255,<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Pope_Alexander_IV" title="Pope Alexander IV">Pope Alexander IV</a> canonized Clare as Saint Clare of Assisi. Construction of the <a href="/wiki/Basilica_of_Saint_Clare" class="mw-redirect" title="Basilica of Saint Clare">Basilica of Saint Clare</a> was completed in 1260, and on October 3 of that year Clare's remains were transferred to the newly completed basilica where they were buried beneath the <a href="/wiki/High_altar" class="mw-redirect" title="High altar">high altar</a>. In further recognition of the saint, <a href="/wiki/Pope_Urban_IV" title="Pope Urban IV">Pope Urban IV</a> officially changed the name of the Order of Poor Ladies to the <a href="/wiki/Order_of_Saint_Clare" class="mw-redirect" title="Order of Saint Clare">Order of Saint Clare</a> in 1263.</p>
<p>Some 600 years later in 1872, Saint Clare's <a href="/wiki/Relics" class="mw-redirect" title="Relics">relics</a> were transferred to a newly constructed shrine in the crypt of the Basilica of Saint Clare, where her relics can still be venerated today.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legacy">Legacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Clare_of_Assisi&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Legacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a href="/wiki/File:ChiaraAssisCos.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9e/ChiaraAssisCos.JPG/250px-ChiaraAssisCos.JPG" width="250" height="175" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9e/ChiaraAssisCos.JPG/375px-ChiaraAssisCos.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9e/ChiaraAssisCos.JPG/500px-ChiaraAssisCos.JPG 2x" data-file-width="550" data-file-height="386" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:ChiaraAssisCos.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The wax figure of Saint Clare of Assisi at <a href="/wiki/Basilica_di_Santa_Chiara" title="Basilica di Santa Chiara">Basilica of Saint Clare</a>, in Assisi</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Clare was canonized on August 15, 1255 by <a href="/wiki/Pope_Alexander_IV" title="Pope Alexander IV">Pope Alexander IV</a>, and her feast day was immediately inserted in the <a href="/wiki/General_Roman_Calendar" title="General Roman Calendar">General Roman Calendar</a> for celebration on August 12, the day following her death, as August 11 was already assigned to <a href="/wiki/Saints_Tiburtius_and_Susanna" class="mw-redirect" title="Saints Tiburtius and Susanna">Saints Tiburtius and Susanna</a>, two 3rd-century Roman martyrs. The celebration was <a href="/wiki/Ranking_of_liturgical_days_in_the_Roman_Rite" title="Ranking of liturgical days in the Roman Rite">ranked</a> as a Double (as in the <a href="/wiki/Tridentine_Calendar" title="Tridentine Calendar">Tridentine Calendar</a>) or, in the terminology adopted in 1960, a Third-Class Feast (as in the <a href="/wiki/General_Roman_Calendar_of_1960" title="General Roman Calendar of 1960">General Roman Calendar of 1960</a>). The <a href="/wiki/Mysterii_Paschalis" title="Mysterii Paschalis">1969 calendar revision</a> removed the feast of Tiburtius and Susanna from the calendar, finally allowing the <a href="/wiki/Memorial_(liturgy)" title="Memorial (liturgy)">memorial</a> of Saint Clare to be celebrated on August 11, the day of her death.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Basilica_di_Santa_Chiara" title="Basilica di Santa Chiara">Basilica di Santa Chiara</a> began construction a year after Clare's canonization, and her remains were transferred there on October 3, 1260 from the church of St George, also in Assisi. Her bones are now in the crypt at the Basilica, having been rediscovered in 1850.</p>
<p>In art, Clare is often shown carrying a <a href="/wiki/Monstrance" title="Monstrance">monstrance</a> or <a href="/wiki/Pyx" title="Pyx">pyx</a>, in commemoration of the occasion when she warded away the soldiers of Frederick II at the gates of her convent by displaying the Blessed Sacrament and kneeling in prayer.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Pope_Pius_XII" title="Pope Pius XII">Pope Pius XII</a> designated Clare as the <a href="/wiki/Patron_saint" title="Patron saint">patron saint</a> of television in 1958 on the basis that when she was too ill to attend <a href="/wiki/Mass_(liturgy)" title="Mass (liturgy)">Mass</a>, she had reportedly been able to see and hear it on the wall of her room.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>There are traditions of bringing offerings of eggs to the <a href="/wiki/Poor_Clares" title="Poor Clares">Poor Clares</a> for their intercessions for good weather, particularly for weddings.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> This tradition remains popular in the Philippines, particularly at the Real Monasterio de Santa Clara in <a href="/wiki/Quezon_City" title="Quezon City">Quezon City</a>. According to the Filipino essayist <a href="/wiki/Alejandro_Roces" title="Alejandro Roces">Alejandro Roces</a>, the practice arose because of Clare's name. In <a href="/wiki/Castilian_Spanish" title="Castilian Spanish">Castilian</a> <i>clara</i> refers to an interval of fair weather, and in <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">Spanish</a>, it also refers to the white or <a href="/wiki/Albumen" class="mw-redirect" title="Albumen">albumen</a> of the egg.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>Many places, including churches, convents, schools, hospitals, towns, and counties are named for St Clare. <a href="/wiki/Lake_Saint_Clair_(North_America)" class="mw-redirect" title="Lake Saint Clair (North America)">Lake Saint Clair</a> between <a href="/wiki/Ontario" title="Ontario">Ontario</a> and <a href="/wiki/Michigan" title="Michigan">Michigan</a> was navigated and named on her feast day in 1679. The <a href="/wiki/St._Clair_River" title="St. Clair River">Saint Clair River</a> and <a href="/wiki/St._Clair_County,_Michigan" title="St. Clair County, Michigan">St. Clair County, Michigan</a> were also consequently named for her. <a href="/wiki/Mission_Santa_Clara" class="mw-redirect" title="Mission Santa Clara">Mission Santa Clara</a>, founded by Spanish missionaries in <a href="/wiki/Northern_California" title="Northern California">northern California</a> in 1777, has given its name to the <a href="/wiki/Santa_Clara_University" title="Santa Clara University">university</a>, <a href="/wiki/Santa_Clara,_California" title="Santa Clara, California">city</a>, <a href="/wiki/Santa_Clara_County" class="mw-redirect" title="Santa Clara County">county</a> and <a href="/wiki/Santa_Clara_Valley" title="Santa Clara Valley">valley</a> in which it sits. Southern California's <a href="/wiki/Santa_Clara_River_(California)" title="Santa Clara River (California)">Santa Clara River</a> is hundreds of miles to the south and gave its name to the nearby city of <a href="/wiki/Santa_Clarita,_California" title="Santa Clarita, California">Santa Clarita</a>. <a href="/wiki/Santa_Clara_Pueblo,_New_Mexico" title="Santa Clara Pueblo, New Mexico">Santa Clara Pueblo, New Mexico</a> celebrates its Santa Clara Feast Day annually on August 12, as the feast was celebrated before the 1969 calendar change. It may be noted that the early California missions were founded by Franciscan Friars, who had a special devotion to Saint Clare.</p>

<p>Clare is one of five characters in the <a href="/wiki/Oratorio" title="Oratorio">oratorio</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Laudato_si%27_(oratorio)" title="Laudato si' (oratorio)">Laudato si'</a></i>, composed in 2016 by <a href="/wiki/Peter_Reulein" title="Peter Reulein">Peter Reulein</a> on a <a href="/wiki/Libretto" title="Libretto">libretto</a> by <a href="/wiki/Helmut_Schlegel" title="Helmut Schlegel">Helmut Schlegel</a>, the others being an angel, <a href="/wiki/Mary,_mother_of_Jesus" title="Mary, mother of Jesus">Mary</a>, Francis of Assisi and <a href="/wiki/Pope_Francis" title="Pope Francis">Pope Francis</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-LS-score_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-LS-score-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
