<p><b>CDU/CSU</b>, unofficially the <b>Union parties</b> (<a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a>: <span lang="de"><i>Unionsparteien</i></span>) or <b>Union</b>, is the <a href="/wiki/Christian_democracy" title="Christian democracy">Christian democratic</a> <a href="/wiki/Political_alliance" title="Political alliance">political alliance</a> of two <a href="/wiki/List_of_political_parties_in_Germany" title="List of political parties in Germany">political parties in Germany</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Christian_Democratic_Union_of_Germany" title="Christian Democratic Union of Germany">Christian Democratic Union of Germany</a> (CDU) and <a href="/wiki/Christian_Social_Union_in_Bavaria" title="Christian Social Union in Bavaria">Christian Social Union in Bavaria</a> (CSU).</p>
<p>Both Christian democratic parties share a common parliamentary group in the <a href="/wiki/Bundestag" title="Bundestag">Bundestag</a> known as the <b>CDU/CSU faction</b> (<i>CDU/CSU-Fraktion</i>) or <b>Union faction</b> (<i>Unionsfraktion</i>). According to German Federal Electoral Law, members of a parliamentary group which share the same basic political aims must not compete with one another in any federal state.<sup id="cite_ref-germanlawarchive_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-germanlawarchive-1">[1]</a></sup> The CSU is only organised and contests elections in <a href="/wiki/Bavaria" title="Bavaria">Bavaria</a>, while the CDU operates in the other 15 <a href="/wiki/States_of_Germany" title="States of Germany">states of Germany</a>. The CSU also reflects the particular concerns of the largely rural, Catholic south.<sup id="cite_ref-countrystudies_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-countrystudies-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Both the CDU and CSU are members of the <a href="/wiki/European_People%27s_Party" title="European People's Party">European People's Party</a> and <a href="/wiki/International_Democrat_Union" title="International Democrat Union">International Democrat Union</a>, and share a common youth organisation, the <a href="/wiki/Young_Union" title="Young Union">Young Union</a>. Both parties sit in the <a href="/wiki/European_People%27s_Party_group" title="European People's Party group">European People's Party group</a> in the <a href="/wiki/European_Parliament" title="European Parliament">European Parliament</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=CDU/CSU&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>Both the CDU and the CSU were established after <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a> and share a perspective based on <a href="/wiki/Christian_democracy" title="Christian democracy">Christian democracy</a> and <a href="/wiki/Conservatism" title="Conservatism">conservatism</a>, and hold the dominant <a href="/wiki/Centre-right_politics" title="Centre-right politics">centre-right</a> position in the German political spectrum. The CSU is usually considered the de facto successor of the <a href="/wiki/Weimar_Republic" title="Weimar Republic">Weimar Republic</a>–era <a href="/wiki/Bavarian_People%27s_Party" title="Bavarian People's Party">Bavarian People's Party</a>, which itself broke away from the all-German Catholic <a href="/wiki/Centre_Party_(Germany)" title="Centre Party (Germany)">Centre Party</a> after <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a>. The CDU's foundation however was the result of a major re-organisation of the centre-right political camp compared to the Weimar Republic. Though the CDU was largely built as the de facto successor the <a href="/wiki/Centre_Party_(Germany)" title="Centre Party (Germany)">Catholic Centre Party</a>, it successfully opened out to non-Catholic Christians, many of them affiliated with the <a href="/wiki/German_People%27s_Party" title="German People's Party">German People's Party</a> until 1933, and successfully asserted itself as the only major conservative party (outside of Bavaria) against initial competition from other Catholic, Protestant or nationalist conservative parties such as the <a href="/wiki/German_Party_(1947)" title="German Party (1947)">German Party</a> during the early years of the Federal Republic.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Political_stances">Political stances</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=CDU/CSU&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Political stances">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>The CDU and the CSU usually only differ slightly in their political stances.</p>
<p>The CSU is usually considered a bit more socially conservative (especially on family issues, e.g. the CSU favors providing infants' parents with compensation (Betreuungsgeld) if they intend not to use the public day nursery system to work<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> while the CDU favors public funding of day nurseries). The CSU government in Bavaria has implemented one of the strictest <a href="/wiki/Shopping_hours#Germany" title="Shopping hours">regulations for shopping hours in Germany</a> in order to protect employees. The CSU also strongly opposed ideas of an income unrelated system of contributions to public health insurances, a proposal which met a lot of approval in the CDU in 2010.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>CSU politicians often make their mark as self-declared defenders of Bavaria's state rights and cultural independence from federal or EU bureaucrats, even in times of conservative federal governments or conservative presidents of the <a href="/wiki/President_of_the_European_Commission" title="President of the European Commission">European Commission</a>. In 1998, then-Chancellor <a href="/wiki/Helmut_Kohl" title="Helmut Kohl">Helmut Kohl</a> of the CDU had to pressure the CSU intensely not to veto the introduction of the <a href="/wiki/Euro" title="Euro">Euro</a> in Germany.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> On the other hand the name "Euro" was the idea of former CSU chairman <a href="/wiki/Theo_Waigel" title="Theo Waigel">Theo Waigel</a> who served as finance minister when the Euro was introduced. The CSU strongly advocates the idea of a maximum number (Obergrenze) of 200,000 people per year to limit the number of asylum seekers. This is strongly opposed by the CDU.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>While both parties officially identify themselves as <a href="/wiki/Non-denominational_Christianity" class="mw-redirect" title="Non-denominational Christianity">non-denominational Christian</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Catholic_Church_in_Germany" title="Catholic Church in Germany">Catholic</a> influence on the CSU is far stronger than that on the CDU, since Bavaria is <a href="/wiki/Roman_Catholicism_in_Germany" class="mw-redirect" title="Roman Catholicism in Germany">predominantly Catholic</a>, while Christians in Germany as a whole are <a href="/wiki/Religion_in_Germany" title="Religion in Germany">approximately equally balanced between Catholics and Protestants</a>. There are nevertheless strong regional differences within Bavaria and Germany as a whole with large predominantly Protestant areas in northern Bavaria and large predominantly Catholic areas in <a href="/wiki/North_Rhine-Westphalia" title="North Rhine-Westphalia">North Rhine-Westphalia</a> and South Western Germany having a strong effect on CDU state politicians. E. g. <a href="/wiki/Saarland" title="Saarland">Saarland</a>'s CDU minister-president <a href="/wiki/Kramp-Karrenbauer" class="mw-redirect" title="Kramp-Karrenbauer">Kramp-Karrenbauer</a> heavily opposed same-sex marriages in July 2017 while the CDU in <a href="/wiki/Schleswig-Holstein" title="Schleswig-Holstein">Schleswig-Holstein</a> was in favor, Saarland having the largest share of Catholic Christians in any German state.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Tensions">Tensions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=CDU/CSU&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Tensions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The differences between the CDU and the somewhat more socially conservative CSU have sometimes led to conflicts in the past. These tensions climaxed during the 1970s, when <a href="/wiki/Helmut_Kohl" title="Helmut Kohl">Helmut Kohl</a> became CDU chairman in 1973, then considered a moderate or even progressive and also a personal foe of the right-wing then-CSU chairman <a href="/wiki/Franz_Josef_Strauss" title="Franz Josef Strauss">Franz Josef Strauss</a>, who had held that office since 1961.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Brief_1976_separation">Brief 1976 separation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=CDU/CSU&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Brief 1976 separation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>After the CDU/CSU narrowly lost the <a href="/wiki/West_German_federal_election,_1976" title="West German federal election, 1976">West German federal election of 1976</a> which had seen Kohl as the common chancellor candidate of the two parties, the CSU's future Bundestag representatives met on November 19, 1976 at a closed meeting at <a href="/wiki/Kreuth#Wildbad_Kreuth" title="Kreuth">Wildbad Kreuth</a>, at a <a href="/wiki/Hanns_Seidel_Foundation" title="Hanns Seidel Foundation">Hanns Seidel Foundation</a> compound, which is CSU's educational foundation. With a vote of 30–18 and one abstention (and one invalid vote), the CSU deputies decided to separate from their common faction with the CDU deputies in the <a href="/wiki/Bundestag" title="Bundestag">Bundestag</a>. The decision had been initiated by CSU chairman Strauss, then himself a Bundestag deputy.</p>
<p>The official reasons were to create a more effective opposition (the CDU would approach moderate conservatives, while the CSU would approach the right) and gain more speaking time in parliament.</p>
<p>As a party chairman, Strauss also announced that the CSU as a party would also terminate its self-restriction to Bavaria and foster the foundation of local CSU associations outside of the party's home state, running in all future German federal and state election against the CDU on a distinctly more conservative platform than the CDU's. Strauss therefore coined the term Vierte Partei (fourth party, after the CDU, the Social Democrats and the Free Democrats). This term was technically misleading, since the CSU had always been a distinct party from the CDU, therefore four parties had already been represented during previous Bundestag terms.</p>
<p>On December 12, 1976, the vote was rescinded after the CDU had threatened in turn to form local associations within Bavaria and to run in Bavarian elections against the CSU.</p>
