			<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Nuremberg eggs</h1>
									<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
									<div id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
								<div id="contentSub"></div>
												<div id="jump-to-nav" class="mw-jump">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>

				<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Nuernberger_Ei_offen.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e7/Nuernberger_Ei_offen.jpg/220px-Nuernberger_Ei_offen.jpg" width="220" height="287" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/e/e7/Nuernberger_Ei_offen.jpg 1.5x" data-file-width="278" data-file-height="363" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Nuernberger_Ei_offen.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A Nuremberg egg watch of the late 16th century</div>
</div>
</div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Henlein_Taschenuhr.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/65/Henlein_Taschenuhr.jpg/220px-Henlein_Taschenuhr.jpg" width="220" height="229" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/65/Henlein_Taschenuhr.jpg/330px-Henlein_Taschenuhr.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/65/Henlein_Taschenuhr.jpg/440px-Henlein_Taschenuhr.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1818" data-file-height="1893" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Henlein_Taschenuhr.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The oldest Nuremberg "clock-watch" preserved dates to c. 1550, after Henlein's death. Kept in <i><a href="/wiki/Germanisches_Nationalmuseum" title="Germanisches Nationalmuseum">Germanisches Nationalmuseum</a></i>, this is the so-called "Henlein-Uhr", and its association with Henlein's workshop, and even its authenticity, has long been controversial.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>A <b>Nuremberg egg</b> (<a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a>: <i><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/N%C3%BCrnberger_Ei" class="extiw" title="wikt:Nürnberger Ei">Nürnberger Ei</a></i>) is a type of small ornamental spring-driven <a href="/wiki/Clock" title="Clock">clock</a> made to be worn around the neck, produced in <a href="/wiki/Nuremberg" title="Nuremberg">Nuremberg</a> in the mid-to-late 16th century.</p>
<p>Their production was made possible by the miniaturisation of the <a href="/wiki/Torsion_pendulum_clock" title="Torsion pendulum clock">torsion pendulum</a> and <a href="/wiki/Coil_spring" title="Coil spring">coil spring</a> mechanism by Nuremberg clockmaker <a href="/wiki/Peter_Henlein" title="Peter Henlein">Peter Henlein</a> (d. 1542) at the beginning of the 16th century (c. 1505–10).<sup id="cite_ref-Rossum_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rossum-1">[1]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Fanthorpe_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Fanthorpe-2">[2]</a></sup> These portable watches were a novelty (although the oldest portable watch driven by wound-up <a href="/wiki/Clockwork" title="Clockwork">clockwork</a> rather than by weights dates to c. 1430, the so-called watch of <a href="/wiki/Philip_the_Good" title="Philip the Good">Philip the Good</a>), and their accuracy was very limited, so that they only had a single hand, showing hours.</p>
<p>There are three types of clock-watches made in early 16th-century Nuremberg:</p>
<ul>
<li>High cylindrical drum-watches (<i>Dosenuhr</i>) to be used as table clocks or to be carried in a purse</li>
<li>Flat cylindrical drum-watches (<i>Dosenuhr</i>) to be worn around the neck on a cord or necklace</li>
<li>Spherical watches in small pomanders (<i>Bisamapfeluhr</i>) that were worn on the wrist or around the neck</li>
</ul>
<p>The actual "eggs" (<i>Eierlein</i>) are a type of oval ("egg-shaped") form of the <i>Dosenuhr</i> which became popular from around 1580. Although they are associated with Henlein, and are a development of the watch-making tradition of Henlein's time, they thus become popular only several decades after his death. The German name <i>Eierlein</i> "little egg" is a corruption of a diminutive of <i>Uhr</i> (<a href="/wiki/Middle_Low_German" title="Middle Low German">Middle Low German</a> <i>ûr</i>, from Latin <i>hora</i>) "clock", <i>Aeurlein</i> or <i>Ueurlein</i> (Modern German <i>Ührlein</i>). The association with "eggs" may arise with a 1571 translation of <a href="/wiki/Rabelais" class="mw-redirect" title="Rabelais">Rabelais</a> by <a href="/wiki/Johann_Fischart" title="Johann Fischart">Johann Fischart</a> in 1571; Fischart translated as <i>Eierlein</i> an instance of <i>Ueurlein</i> in Rabelais. This form of the name may have played a part in inspiring the oval shape becoming popular in the 1580s.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Rossum_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rossum-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>It is known that Henlein first succeeded in producing a portable clock in 1510, and his contributions were clearly instrumental in giving rise to the later Nuremberg "neck-watch" industry. He is recorded to have sold a "gilded <a href="/wiki/Pomander" title="Pomander">Pomander</a> for all purposes with a clock-mechanism" (<i>vergulten pysn Apffel für all Ding mit einem Oraiologium</i>, i.e. a clock built into a precious perfume-box) for 15 <a href="/wiki/Goldgulden" class="mw-redirect" title="Goldgulden">gulden</a> to the city on 11 January 1524. Of the "pomander watch" type (<i>Bisamapfeluhr</i>), only two specimens are known to survive, one dated to 1505 and attributed to Henlein, the other dated 1530 (Walters Art Museum, Baltimore).</p>
