<p><b>Free content</b>, <b><a href="/wiki/Gratis_versus_libre" title="Gratis versus libre">libre</a> content</b>, or <b>free information</b>,<sup id="cite_ref-:0_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-1">[1]</a></sup> is any kind of functional work, <a href="/wiki/Work_of_art" title="Work of art">work of art</a>, or other creative <a href="/wiki/Content_(media_and_publishing)" class="mw-redirect" title="Content (media and publishing)">content</a> that meets the definition of a <a href="/wiki/Definition_of_Free_Cultural_Works" title="Definition of Free Cultural Works">free</a><sup id="cite_ref-:1_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-2">[2]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-:0_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-1">[1]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-:0_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-1">[1]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Definition_of_Free_Cultural_Works" title="Definition of Free Cultural Works">cultural work</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Definition">Definition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Free_content&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Definition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A <i>free cultural work</i> (free content) is, according to the <a href="/wiki/Definition_of_free_cultural_works" class="mw-redirect" title="Definition of free cultural works">Definition of free cultural works</a>, one that has no significant legal restriction on people's freedom to:</p>
<ul>
<li>use the content and benefit from using it,</li>
<li>study the content and apply what is learned,</li>
<li>make and distribute copies of the content,</li>
<li>change and improve the content and distribute these derivative works.<sup id="cite_ref-:1_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-2">[2]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<p>Free content encompasses all works in the <a href="/wiki/Public_domain" title="Public domain">public domain</a> and also those <a href="/wiki/Copyright" title="Copyright">copyrighted</a> works whose <a href="/wiki/License" title="License">licenses</a> honor and uphold the freedoms mentioned above. Because the <a href="/wiki/Berne_Convention" title="Berne Convention">Berne Convention</a> in most countries by default grants copyright holders <a href="/wiki/Monopoly" title="Monopoly">monopolistic control</a> over their creations, copyright content must be explicitly declared free, usually by the referencing or inclusion of licensing statements from within the work.</p>
<p>Although there are a great many different definitions in regular everyday use, free content is legally very similar, if not like an identical twin, to <a href="/wiki/Open_content" title="Open content">open content</a>. An analogy is the use of the rival terms free software and open source, which describe ideological differences rather than legal ones.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-raymondCall_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-raymondCall-6">[6]</a></sup> For instance, the <a href="/wiki/Open_Knowledge_Foundation" class="mw-redirect" title="Open Knowledge Foundation">Open Knowledge Foundation</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/The_Open_Definition" title="The Open Definition">Open Definition</a></i> describes "open" as synonymous to the definition of <i>free</i> in the "Definition of Free Cultural Works" (as also in the <a href="/wiki/Open_Source_Definition" class="mw-redirect" title="Open Source Definition">Open Source Definition</a> and <a href="/wiki/Free_Software_Definition" class="mw-redirect" title="Free Software Definition">Free Software Definition</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> For such free/open content both movements recommend the same three <a href="/wiki/Creative_Commons_license" title="Creative Commons license">Creative Commons licenses</a>, the <a href="/wiki/CC_BY" class="mw-redirect" title="CC BY">CC BY</a>, <a href="/wiki/CC_BY-SA" class="mw-redirect" title="CC BY-SA">CC BY-SA</a>, and <a href="/wiki/CC0" class="mw-redirect" title="CC0">CC0</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-WellcomeTrustReport_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WellcomeTrustReport-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legal_matters">Legal matters</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Free_content&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Legal matters">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Copyright">Copyright</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Free_content&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Copyright">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:102px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Copyright.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b0/Copyright.svg/100px-Copyright.svg.png" width="100" height="100" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b0/Copyright.svg/150px-Copyright.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b0/Copyright.svg/200px-Copyright.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="197" data-file-height="197" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Copyright.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Copyright" title="Copyright">copyright</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Copyright" title="Copyright">Copyright</a></div>
<p>Copyright is a legal concept, which gives the author or creator of a work legal control over the <a href="/wiki/Copying" title="Copying">duplication</a> and public performance of his or her work. In many jurisdictions, this is limited by a time period after which the works then enter the <a href="/wiki/Public_domain" title="Public domain">public domain</a>. Copyright laws are a balance between the rights of creators of intellectual and artistic works and the rights of others to build upon those works. During the time period of copyright the author's work may only be copied, modified, or publicly performed with the consent of the author, unless the use is a <a href="/wiki/Fair_use" title="Fair use">fair use</a>. Traditional copyright control limits the use of the work of the author to those who either pay royalties to the author for usage of the authors content, or limit their use to fair use. Secondly it limits the use of content whose author cannot be found.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Finally it creates a perceived barrier between authors by limiting derivative works, such as <a href="/wiki/Mashup_(music)" title="Mashup (music)">mashups</a> and collaborative content<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Public_domain">Public domain</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Free_content&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Public domain">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:102px;"><a href="/wiki/File:PD-icon.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/6/62/PD-icon.svg/100px-PD-icon.svg.png" width="100" height="100" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/6/62/PD-icon.svg/150px-PD-icon.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/6/62/PD-icon.svg/200px-PD-icon.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="196" data-file-height="196" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:PD-icon.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Public_domain" title="Public domain">public domain</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Public_domain" title="Public domain">Public domain</a></div>
<p>The public domain is a range of creative works whose <a href="/wiki/Copyright" title="Copyright">copyright</a> has expired, or was never established; as well as ideas and facts<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[nb 1]</a></sup> which are ineligible for copyright. A public domain work is a work whose author has either relinquished to the public, or no longer can claim control over, the distribution and usage of the work. As such any person may manipulate, distribute, or otherwise utilize the work, without legal ramifications. A work in the public domain or released under a permissive licence may be referred to as "<a href="/wiki/Copycenter" class="mw-redirect" title="Copycenter">copycenter</a>".<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Copyleft">Copyleft</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Free_content&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Copyleft">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Copyleft" title="Copyleft">Copyleft</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:102px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Copyleft.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8b/Copyleft.svg/100px-Copyleft.svg.png" width="100" height="100" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8b/Copyleft.svg/150px-Copyleft.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8b/Copyleft.svg/200px-Copyleft.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="512" data-file-height="512" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Copyleft.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Copyleft" title="Copyleft">copyleft</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Copyleft is a play on the word copyright and describes the practice of using copyright law to remove restrictions on distributing copies and modified versions of a work.<sup id="cite_ref-Dusollier_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dusollier-16">[15]</a></sup> The aim of copyleft is to use the legal framework of copyright to enable non-author parties to be able to reuse and, in many licensing schemes, modify content that is created by an author. Unlike works in the public domain, the author still maintains copyright over the material, however the author has granted a non-exclusive license to any person to distribute, and often modify, the work. Copyleft licenses require that any <a href="/wiki/Derivative_work" title="Derivative work">derivative works</a> be distributed under the same terms, and that the original copyright notices be maintained. A symbol commonly associated with copyleft is a reversal of the <a href="/wiki/Copyright_symbol" title="Copyright symbol">copyright symbol</a>, facing the other way; the opening of the C points left rather than right. Unlike the copyright symbol, the copyleft symbol does not have a codified meaning.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[16]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Usage">Usage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Free_content&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Usage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Projects that provide free content exist in several areas of interest, such as software, academic literature, general literature, music, images, video, and <a href="/wiki/Engineering" title="Engineering">engineering</a>. Technology has reduced the cost of publication and reduced the entry barrier sufficiently to allow for the production of widely disseminated materials by individuals or small groups. Projects to provide free literature and multimedia content have become increasingly prominent owing to the ease of dissemination of materials that is associated with the development of computer technology. Such dissemination may have been too costly prior to these technological developments.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Media">Media</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Free_content&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Media">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:102px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Cc.logo.circle.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a3/Cc.logo.circle.svg/100px-Cc.logo.circle.svg.png" width="100" height="100" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a3/Cc.logo.circle.svg/150px-Cc.logo.circle.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a3/Cc.logo.circle.svg/200px-Cc.logo.circle.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="64" data-file-height="64" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Cc.logo.circle.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Creative_Commons" title="Creative Commons">Creative Commons</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>In media, which includes textual, audio, and visual content, free licensing schemes such as some of the licenses made by <a href="/wiki/Creative_Commons_licenses" class="mw-redirect" title="Creative Commons licenses">Creative Commons</a> have allowed for the dissemination of works under a clear set of legal permissions. Not all of the Creative Commons’ licenses are entirely free: their permissions may range from very liberal general redistribution and modification of the work to a more restrictive redistribution-only licensing. Since February 2008, Creative Commons licenses which are entirely free carry a badge indicating that they are "approved for free cultural works".<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[17]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Digital_library" title="Digital library">Repositories</a> exist which exclusively feature free material provide content such as photographs, <a href="/wiki/Clip_art" title="Clip art">clip art</a>, music,<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[18]</a></sup> and literature,.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[19]</a></sup> While extensive reuse of free content from one website in another website is legal, it is usually not sensible because of the <a href="/wiki/Duplicate_content" title="Duplicate content">duplicate content</a> problem. <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia" title="Wikipedia">Wikipedia</a> is amongst the most well known databases of user uploaded free content on the web. While the vast majority of content on Wikipedia is free content, some copyrighted material is hosted under <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Non-free_content_criteria" title="Wikipedia:Non-free content criteria">Fair-use criteria</a>.</p>

<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Free_and_open-source_software" title="Free and open-source software">Free and open-source software</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Free_and_open_source_software" class="mw-redirect" title="Free and open source software">Free and open source software</a>, which is also often referred to as <a href="/wiki/Open_source_software" class="mw-redirect" title="Open source software">open source software</a> and <a href="/wiki/Free_software" title="Free software">free software</a>, is a maturing technology with major companies utilising free software to provide both services and technology to both end users and technical consumers. The ease of dissemination has allowed for increased modularity, which allows for smaller groups to contribute to projects as well as simplifying collaboration. Open source development models have been classified as having a similar peer-recognition and collaborative benefit incentives that are typified by more classical fields such as scientific research, with the social structures that result from this incentive model decreasing production cost.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[20]</a></sup> Given sufficient interest in a software component, by using <a href="/wiki/Peer-to-peer" title="Peer-to-peer">peer-to-peer</a> distribution methods, distribution costs of software may be reduced, removing the burden of infrastructure maintenance from developers. As distribution resources are simultaneously provided by consumers, these software distribution models are scalable, that is the method is feasible regardless of the number of consumers. In some cases, free software vendors may use peer-to-peer technology as a method of dissemination.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[21]</a></sup> In general, project hosting and code distribution is not a problem for the most of free projects as <a href="/wiki/Comparison_of_open_source_software_hosting_facilities" class="mw-redirect" title="Comparison of open source software hosting facilities">a number of providers</a> offer them these services free.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Engineering_and_technology">Engineering and technology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Free_content&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Engineering and technology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:102px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Open-source-hardware-logo.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fd/Open-source-hardware-logo.svg/100px-Open-source-hardware-logo.svg.png" width="100" height="105" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fd/Open-source-hardware-logo.svg/150px-Open-source-hardware-logo.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fd/Open-source-hardware-logo.svg/200px-Open-source-hardware-logo.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="512" data-file-height="538" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Open-source-hardware-logo.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Open_source_hardware" class="mw-redirect" title="Open source hardware">Open source hardware</a> logo</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Free content principles have been translated into fields such as engineering, where designs and engineering knowledge can be readily shared and duplicated, in order to reduce overheads associated with project development. <a href="/wiki/Open_design" title="Open design">Open design</a> principles can be applied in engineering and technological applications, with projects in <a href="/wiki/Mobile_telephony" title="Mobile telephony">mobile telephony</a>, small-scale manufacture,<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[22]</a></sup> the automotive industry,<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[23]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[24]</a></sup> and even agricultural areas.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[25]</a></sup> Technologies such as distributed manufacturing can allow <a href="/wiki/Computer-aided_manufacturing" title="Computer-aided manufacturing">computer-aided manufacturing</a> and <a href="/wiki/Computer-aided_design" title="Computer-aided design">computer-aided design</a> techniques to be able to develop small-scale production of components for the development of new, or repair of existing, devices. Rapid fabrication technologies underpin these developments, which allow end users of technology to be able to construct devices from pre-existing blueprints, using software and manufacturing hardware to convert information into physical objects.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Academia">Academia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Free_content&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Academia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:102px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Open_Access_logo_PLoS_white.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/25/Open_Access_logo_PLoS_white.svg/100px-Open_Access_logo_PLoS_white.svg.png" width="100" height="156" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/25/Open_Access_logo_PLoS_white.svg/150px-Open_Access_logo_PLoS_white.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/25/Open_Access_logo_PLoS_white.svg/200px-Open_Access_logo_PLoS_white.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="640" data-file-height="1000" /></a>

<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Open_access_(publishing)" class="mw-redirect" title="Open access (publishing)">Open access (publishing)</a></div>
<p>In academic work, the majority of works are not free, although the percentage of works that are open access is growing rapidly. <a href="/wiki/Open_access" title="Open access">Open access</a> refers to online <a href="/wiki/Research" title="Research">research</a> outputs that are free of all restrictions on access (e.g. access tolls) and free of many restrictions on use (e.g. certain copyright and license restrictions).<sup id="cite_ref-earlham.edu_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-earlham.edu-27">[26]</a></sup> Authors may see open access publishing as a method of expanding the audience that is able to access their work to allow for greater impact of the publication, or may support it for ideological reasons.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[27]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[28]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[29]</a></sup> Open access publishers such as <a href="/wiki/Public_Library_of_Science" class="mw-redirect" title="Public Library of Science">PLOS</a> and <a href="/wiki/Biomed_Central" class="mw-redirect" title="Biomed Central">Biomed Central</a> provide capacity for review and publishing of free works; though such publications are currently more common in science than humanities. Various funding institutions and governing research bodies have <a href="/wiki/Open_access_mandate" class="mw-redirect" title="Open access mandate">mandated</a> that academics must produce their works to be open-access, in order to qualify for funding, such as the <a href="/wiki/National_Institutes_of_Health" title="National Institutes of Health">National Institutes of Health</a>, <a href="/wiki/RCUK" class="mw-redirect" title="RCUK">RCUK</a> (effective 2016) and the EU (effective 2020).<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[30]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">[31]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">[32]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">[33]</a></sup> At an institutional level some universities, such as the <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts_Institute_of_Technology" title="Massachusetts Institute of Technology">Massachusetts Institute of Technology</a> (MIT), have adopted open access publishing by default by introducing their own mandates.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">[34]</a></sup> Some mandates may permit delayed publication and may charge researchers for open access publishing.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">[35]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">[36]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Open_content" title="Open content">Open content</a> publication has been seen as a method of reducing costs associated with information retrieval in research, as universities typically pay to subscribe for access to content that is published through traditional means<sup id="cite_ref-WellcomeTrustReport_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WellcomeTrustReport-11">[11]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">[37]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">[38]</a></sup> whilst improving journal quality by discouraging the submission of research articles of reduced quality.<sup id="cite_ref-WellcomeTrustReport_11-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WellcomeTrustReport-11">[11]</a></sup> Subscriptions for non-free content journals may be expensive for universities to purchase, though the article are written and peer-reviewed by academics themselves at no cost to the publisher. This has led to disputes between publishers and some universities over subscription costs, such as the one which occurred between the <a href="/wiki/Nature_Publishing_Group#Access_and_pricing" title="Nature Publishing Group">University of California and the Nature Publishing Group</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">[39]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">[40]</a></sup> For teaching purposes, some universities, including MIT, provide freely available course content, such as lecture notes, video resources and tutorials. This content is distributed via Internet resources to the general public. Publication of such resources may be either by a formal institution-wide program,<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">[41]</a></sup> or alternately via informal content provided by individual academics or departments.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Legislation">Legislation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Free_content&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Legislation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Any country has its own law and legal system, sustained by its legislation, a set of law-documents — documents containing <a href="/wiki/Statutory_law" title="Statutory law">statutory</a> <a href="/wiki/Category:Law_of_obligations" title="Category:Law of obligations">obligation rules</a>, usually <a href="/wiki/Law" title="Law">law</a> and created by <a href="/wiki/Legislature" title="Legislature">legislatures</a>. In a <a href="/wiki/Rule_of_law" title="Rule of law">democratic country</a>, each law-document is published as <a href="#Media">open media content</a>, is in principle a free content; but in general there are no explicit license attributed for each law-document, so the license must be interpreted, is a <i><a href="/wiki/Implied_license" title="Implied license">implied license</a></i>. Only few countries have explicit licenses in its law-documents, as the UK's <a href="/wiki/Open_Government_Licence" title="Open Government Licence">Open Government Licence</a> (a <a href="/wiki/CC-BY" class="mw-redirect" title="CC-BY">CC-BY</a> compatible license). In the other countries, the <i>implied license</i> comes from its proper rules (general laws and rules about copyright in government works). The automatic protection provided by <a href="/wiki/Berne_Convention" title="Berne Convention">Berne Convention</a> not apply to law-documents: Article 2.4 excludes the official texts from the automatic protection. It is also possible to "inherit" the license from context. The set of country's law-documents is made available through national repositories. Examples of law-document open repositories: <a href="/wiki/LexML_Brazil" class="mw-redirect" title="LexML Brazil">LexML Brazil</a>, <a href="/wiki/Legislation.gov.uk" title="Legislation.gov.uk">Legislation.gov.uk</a>, <a href="/wiki/EUR-Lex#N-Lex" title="EUR-Lex">N-Lex of EU countries</a>. In general a law-document is offered in more than one (open) official version, but the main one is that published by a <a href="/wiki/Government_gazette" title="Government gazette">government gazette</a>. So, law-documents can eventually inherit license expressed by the repository or by the gazette that contains it.</p>
