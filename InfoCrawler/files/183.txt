<p><b>Simon William "Bill" English</b> (born 30 December 1961) is the <a href="/wiki/List_of_Prime_Ministers_of_New_Zealand" title="List of Prime Ministers of New Zealand">39th Prime Minister of New Zealand</a> and leader of the <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand_National_Party" title="New Zealand National Party">National Party</a>, having taken office on 12 December 2016. He was previously <a href="/wiki/Deputy_Prime_Minister_of_New_Zealand" title="Deputy Prime Minister of New Zealand">Deputy Prime Minister</a> from 2008 to 2016.</p>
<p>A farmer and public servant before entering politics, English was elected to parliament <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand_general_election,_1990" title="New Zealand general election, 1990">in 1990</a> as the National Party's candidate in the <a href="/wiki/Wallace_(New_Zealand_electorate)" title="Wallace (New Zealand electorate)">Wallace electorate</a>. He was elevated to <a href="/wiki/Cabinet_of_New_Zealand" title="Cabinet of New Zealand">cabinet</a> in 1996 and in 1999 was made <a href="/wiki/Minister_of_Finance_(New_Zealand)" title="Minister of Finance (New Zealand)">Minister of Finance</a>, although he served for less than a year due to his party's loss at the <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand_general_election,_1999" title="New Zealand general election, 1999">1999 general election</a>. In October 2001, English replaced <a href="/wiki/Jenny_Shipley" title="Jenny Shipley">Jenny Shipley</a> as the leader of the National Party (and consequently as <a href="/wiki/Leader_of_the_Opposition_(New_Zealand)" title="Leader of the Opposition (New Zealand)">Leader of the Opposition</a>). The party lost the <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand_general_election,_2002" title="New Zealand general election, 2002">2002 general election</a>, and in October 2003 he was replaced as leader by <a href="/wiki/Don_Brash" title="Don Brash">Don Brash</a>. In November 2006, after Don Brash's resignation, English became deputy leader under <a href="/wiki/John_Key" title="John Key">John Key</a>.</p>
<p>After National's victory at the <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand_general_election,_2008" title="New Zealand general election, 2008">2008 general election</a>, English became Deputy Prime Minister and was also made Minister for Finance for a second time. He became a <a href="/wiki/List_MP" title="List MP">list-only MP</a> after stepping down as an electorate MP at the <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand_general_election,_2014" title="New Zealand general election, 2014">2014 general election</a>. In December 2016, John Key announced his intention to resign as prime minister. He endorsed English as his replacement, and English won <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand_National_Party_leadership_election,_2016" title="New Zealand National Party leadership election, 2016">the resulting leadership election</a> unopposed.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bill_English&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>English is the second-youngest of 12 children of Mervyn English and Norah (née O'Brien) English. His parents purchased <i>Rosedale</i>, a mixed sheep and cropping farm in <a href="/wiki/Dipton,_New_Zealand" title="Dipton, New Zealand">Dipton, Southland</a> from Mervyn's uncle, Vincent English, a bachelor, in 1944.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> English was born in the nearby town of <a href="/wiki/Lumsden,_New_Zealand" title="Lumsden, New Zealand">Lumsden</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>He attended St Thomas's School in <a href="/wiki/Winton,_New_Zealand" title="Winton, New Zealand">Winton</a>, then boarded at <a href="/wiki/St._Patrick%27s_College,_Silverstream" title="St. Patrick's College, Silverstream">St. Patrick's College</a> in <a href="/wiki/Upper_Hutt" title="Upper Hutt">Upper Hutt</a>, where he became <a href="/wiki/Head_boy" class="mw-redirect" title="Head boy">head boy</a>. He played in the first XV of the school's <a href="/wiki/Rugby_union" title="Rugby union">rugby</a> team. English went on to study commerce at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Otago" title="University of Otago">University of Otago</a>, where he was a resident at <a href="/wiki/Selwyn_College,_Otago" title="Selwyn College, Otago">Selwyn College</a>, and then completed an honours degree in English literature at <a href="/wiki/Victoria_University_of_Wellington" title="Victoria University of Wellington">Victoria University of Wellington</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-herbert_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-herbert-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>After finishing his studies, English returned to Dipton and farmed for a few years.<sup id="cite_ref-herbert_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-herbert-4">[4]</a></sup> From 1987 to 1989, he worked in <a href="/wiki/Wellington" title="Wellington">Wellington</a> as a policy analyst for the <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand_Treasury" title="New Zealand Treasury">New Zealand Treasury</a>, at a time when the <a href="/wiki/Free_market" title="Free market">free market</a> policies favoured by <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand_Labour_Party" title="New Zealand Labour Party">Labour</a>'s finance minister <a href="/wiki/Roger_Douglas" title="Roger Douglas">Roger Douglas</a> (known collectively as "<a href="/wiki/Rogernomics" title="Rogernomics">Rogernomics</a>") were being implemented.<sup id="cite_ref-rae_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rae-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>English joined the National Party in 1980, while at Victoria University. He served for a period as chairman of the Southland branch of the <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand_Young_Nationals" title="New Zealand Young Nationals">Young Nationals</a>, and became a member of the <a href="/wiki/Wallace_(New_Zealand_electorate)" title="Wallace (New Zealand electorate)">Wallace</a> electorate committee. After moving to Wellington, he served for periods on the <a href="/wiki/Island_Bay_(New_Zealand_electorate)" title="Island Bay (New Zealand electorate)">Island Bay</a> and <a href="/wiki/Miramar_(New_Zealand_electorate)" title="Miramar (New Zealand electorate)">Miramar</a> electorate committees, respectively.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>

<p>At the <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand_general_election,_1990" title="New Zealand general election, 1990">1990 general election</a>, English stood as the National candidate in Wallace, replacing the retiring <a href="/wiki/Derek_Angus" title="Derek Angus">Derek Angus</a>, and was elected with a large majority. He and three other newly elected National MPs (<a href="/wiki/Tony_Ryall" title="Tony Ryall">Tony Ryall</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nick_Smith_(New_Zealand_politician)" title="Nick Smith (New Zealand politician)">Nick Smith</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Roger_Sowry" title="Roger Sowry">Roger Sowry</a>) were soon identified as rising stars in New Zealand politics, and at various points were dubbed the "brat pack", the "gang of four", and the "Young Turks". In his first term in parliament, English chaired a <a href="/wiki/Select_committee" title="Select committee">select committee</a> into social services. He was made a <a href="/wiki/Parliamentary_secretary" title="Parliamentary secretary">parliamentary under-secretary</a> in 1993, serving under the Minister of Health.<sup id="cite_ref-herbert_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-herbert-4">[4]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span id="First_period_in_cabinet_(1996–1999)"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="First_period_in_cabinet_.281996.E2.80.931999.29">First period in cabinet (1996–1999)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bill_English&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: First period in cabinet (1996–1999)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In early 1996, English was elevated to <a href="/wiki/Cabinet_of_New_Zealand" title="Cabinet of New Zealand">cabinet</a> by Prime Minister <a href="/wiki/Jim_Bolger" title="Jim Bolger">Jim Bolger</a>, becoming the Minister for Crown Health Enterprises and Associate <a href="/wiki/Minister_of_Education_(New_Zealand)" title="Minister of Education (New Zealand)">Minister of Education</a> (to <a href="/wiki/Wyatt_Creech" title="Wyatt Creech">Wyatt Creech</a>). He was 34 at the time, becoming the cabinet's youngest member.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> After the <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand_general_election,_1996" title="New Zealand general election, 1996">1996 general election</a>, the National Party was forced into a coalition with <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand_First" title="New Zealand First">New Zealand First</a> to retain government. In the resulting cabinet reshuffle, English emerged as <a href="/wiki/Minister_of_Health_(New_Zealand)" title="Minister of Health (New Zealand)">Minister of Health</a>. However, as a condition of the coalition agreement, NZ First's <a href="/wiki/Neil_Kirton" title="Neil Kirton">Neil Kirton</a> (a first-term MP) was made Associate Minister of Health, effectively becoming English's deputy. This arrangement was described in the press as a "<a href="/wiki/Shotgun_marriage" class="mw-redirect" title="Shotgun marriage">shotgun marriage</a>", and there were frequent differences of opinion between the two ministers.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> After their relationship became unworkable, Kirton was sacked from the role in August 1997, with the agreement of NZ First leader <a href="/wiki/Winston_Peters" title="Winston Peters">Winston Peters</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>As Minister of Health, English was responsible for continuing the reforms to the public health system that National had begun after the <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand_general_election,_1990" title="New Zealand general election, 1990">1990 general election</a>. The reforms were unpopular, and health was perceived as one of the government's weaknesses, with the health portfolio consequently being viewed as a challenge.<sup id="cite_ref-cannan_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cannan-12">[12]</a></sup> English believed that the unpopularity of the reforms was in part due to a failure in messaging, and encouraged his National colleagues to avoid bureaucratic and money-focused language (such as references to "balance sheets" and "user charges") and instead talk about the improvements to services the government's reforms would bring.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> He also rejected the idea that public hospitals could be run as commercial enterprises, a view which some of his colleagues had previously promoted.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>By early 1997, as dissatisfaction with Bolger's leadership began to grow, English was being touted as a potential successor, along with <a href="/wiki/Jenny_Shipley" title="Jenny Shipley">Jenny Shipley</a> and <a href="/wiki/Doug_Graham" title="Doug Graham">Doug Graham</a>. His age (35) was viewed as the main impediment to a successful leadership run.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> National's leadership troubles were resolved in December 1997, when Bolger resigned and Shipley was elected to the leadership unopposed. English had been a supporter of Bolger as leader, but Shipley reappointed him Minister of Health in her new cabinet.<sup id="cite_ref-cannan_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cannan-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>English was promoted to <a href="/wiki/Minister_of_Finance_(New_Zealand)" title="Minister of Finance (New Zealand)">Minister of Finance</a> in a reshuffle in January 1999, a position which was at the time subordinate to the <a href="/wiki/Minister_of_Finance_(New_Zealand)#History" title="Minister of Finance (New Zealand)">Treasurer</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bill_Birch" title="Bill Birch">Bill Birch</a>. After a few months, the pair switched positions as part of Birch's transition to retirement, with English assuming the senior portfolio. In early interviews, he emphasised his wish to be seen as a pragmatist rather than an ideologue, and said that the initiatives of some of his predecessors (<a href="/wiki/Roger_Douglas" title="Roger Douglas">Roger Douglas</a>'s "<a href="/wiki/Rogernomics" title="Rogernomics">Rogernomics</a>" and <a href="/wiki/Ruth_Richardson" title="Ruth Richardson">Ruth Richardson</a>'s "<a href="/wiki/Ruthanasia" title="Ruthanasia">Ruthanasia</a>") had focused on "fruitless, theoretical debates" when "people just want to see problems solved".<sup id="cite_ref-rae_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rae-5">[5]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span id="Opposition_(1999–2008)"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Opposition_.281999.E2.80.932008.29">Opposition (1999–2008)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bill_English&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Opposition (1999–2008)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>After the National Party lost the <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand_general_election,_1999" title="New Zealand general election, 1999">1999 election</a> to <a href="/wiki/Helen_Clark" title="Helen Clark">Helen Clark</a>'s Labour Party, English continued on in the <a href="/wiki/Shadow_cabinet" class="mw-redirect" title="Shadow cabinet">shadow cabinet</a> as National's spokesperson for finance. He was elected deputy leader of the party in February 2001, following the resignation of <a href="/wiki/Wyatt_Creech" title="Wyatt Creech">Wyatt Creech</a>, with <a href="/wiki/Gerry_Brownlee" title="Gerry Brownlee">Gerry Brownlee</a> being his unsuccessful opponent.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Leader_of_the_Opposition">Leader of the Opposition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bill_English&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Leader of the Opposition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In October 2001, after months of speculation, <a href="/wiki/Jenny_Shipley" title="Jenny Shipley">Jenny Shipley</a> resigned as leader of the National Party after being told she no longer had the support of the party caucus. English <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand_National_Party_leadership_election,_2001" title="New Zealand National Party leadership election, 2001">was elected</a> as her replacement unopposed (with <a href="/wiki/Roger_Sowry" title="Roger Sowry">Roger Sowry</a> as his deputy), and consequently became <a href="/wiki/Leader_of_the_Opposition_(New_Zealand)" title="Leader of the Opposition (New Zealand)">Leader of the Opposition</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> However, he did not openly organise against Shipley, and according to <i><a href="/wiki/The_Southland_Times" title="The Southland Times">The Southland Times</a></i> "there was almost an element of 'aw, shucks, I'll do it then' about Mr English's ascension".<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>Aged 39 when he was elected, English became the second-youngest leader in the National Party's history, after <a href="/wiki/Jim_McLay" title="Jim McLay">Jim McLay</a> (who was 38 when elected in 1984). He also became only the third Southlander to lead a major New Zealand political party, after <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Ward" title="Joseph Ward">Joseph Ward</a> and <a href="/wiki/Adam_Hamilton" title="Adam Hamilton">Adam Hamilton</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> However, English failed to improve the party's performance. In the <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand_general_election,_2002" title="New Zealand general election, 2002">2002 elections</a>, National suffered its worst electoral defeat ever, gaining barely more than twenty percent of the vote. English described it as "the worst day of my political life". Both party insiders and the general public were split as to how much to blame him for the loss, but most of the party believed that English would be able to rebuild National's support.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>By late 2003, however, National's performance in opinion polls remained poor. The party had briefly increased its popularity in the year following the election, but by October its support had fallen to levels only slightly better than what it achieved in the last ballot. English also appeared in a boxing match for a charity against entertainer <a href="/w/index.php?title=Ted_Clarke&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Ted Clarke (page does not exist)">Ted Clarke</a>. This "stunt" did not boost his polling or that of the National party either, with suggestions that it devalued his image as a serious politician. <a href="/wiki/Don_Brash" title="Don Brash">Don Brash</a>, former governor of the <a href="/wiki/Reserve_Bank_of_New_Zealand" title="Reserve Bank of New Zealand">Reserve Bank</a> and a relative newcomer to politics, began to build up support to replace English. On 28 October, Brash gained sufficient backing in Caucus to defeat English in <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand_National_Party_leadership_election,_2003" title="New Zealand National Party leadership election, 2003">a leadership contest</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Shadow_cabinet_roles_and_deputy_leader">Shadow cabinet roles and deputy leader</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bill_English&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Shadow cabinet roles and deputy leader">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On 2 November 2003, when Brash announced changes in responsibilities for certain MPs, English became National's spokesman for education, ranked at fifth place in the party's parliamentary hierarchy. He remained in parliament after the <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand_general_election,_2005" title="New Zealand general election, 2005">2005 election</a>. In his new shadow education portfolio, English performed strongly, and remained a party favourite despite his election defeat as leader in 2002, eventually being returned to the finance portfolio in August 2004 as deputy spokesman (while still retaining responsibility for education).<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>After the resignation of <a href="/wiki/Don_Brash" title="Don Brash">Don Brash</a>, English aspired to replace <a href="/wiki/Gerry_Brownlee" title="Gerry Brownlee">Gerry Brownlee</a> as deputy leader. In November 2006, Brownlee announced that he was stepping aside and English was predicted to take over the deputy leadership and also the finance portfolio. This was confirmed the next day following a caucus meeting.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span id="Fifth_National_Government_(2008–present)"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Fifth_National_Government_.282008.E2.80.93present.29">Fifth National Government (2008–present)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bill_English&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Fifth National Government (2008–present)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<h3><span id="Deputy_Prime_Minister_and_Minister_of_Finance_(2008–2016)"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Deputy_Prime_Minister_and_Minister_of_Finance_.282008.E2.80.932016.29">Deputy Prime Minister and Minister of Finance (2008–2016)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bill_English&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Deputy Prime Minister and Minister of Finance (2008–2016)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>At the <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand_general_election,_2008" title="New Zealand general election, 2008">2008 election</a>, English was re-elected by his electorate, winning by a margin of about 15,500 votes.<sup id="cite_ref-election_results_2008_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-election_results_2008-25">[25]</a></sup> He became <a href="/wiki/Deputy_Prime_Minister_of_New_Zealand" title="Deputy Prime Minister of New Zealand">Deputy Prime Minister of New Zealand</a> and <a href="/wiki/Minister_of_Finance_(New_Zealand)" title="Minister of Finance (New Zealand)">Minister of Finance</a><sup id="cite_ref-Appt2008_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Appt2008-26">[26]</a></sup> in the fifth National Government, being sworn into office on 19 November 2008. He was also made <a href="/wiki/Minister_of_Infrastructure_(New_Zealand)" class="mw-redirect" title="Minister of Infrastructure (New Zealand)">Minister of Infrastructure</a>, an entirely new position,<sup id="cite_ref-Appt2008_26-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Appt2008-26">[26]</a></sup> although he held that title for only a single term of parliament.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p>The pairing of <a href="/wiki/John_Key" title="John Key">John Key</a> as leader of the National Party and English as his deputy has been compared to that of <a href="/wiki/Bob_Hawke" title="Bob Hawke">Bob Hawke</a> and <a href="/wiki/Paul_Keating" title="Paul Keating">Paul Keating</a> (in Australia) and <a href="/wiki/Tony_Blair" title="Tony Blair">Tony Blair</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gordon_Brown" title="Gordon Brown">Gordon Brown</a> (in the UK).<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<p>English acceded to the role of Finance Minister in the continuing wake of the <a href="/wiki/Financial_crisis_of_2007%E2%80%932008" title="Financial crisis of 2007–2008">financial crisis</a>. In response to New Zealand's rising debt, English made <a href="/wiki/Government_budget_balance" title="Government budget balance">budget deficit</a>-reduction his main priority. His <a href="/wiki/2009_New_Zealand_budget" title="2009 New Zealand budget">first budget</a> outlined three focuses in New Zealand's financial recovery: "improving the business environment and removing roadblocks to growth; investment in productive infrastructure; and improving the way government works".<sup id="cite_ref-09_budget_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-09_budget-29">[29]</a></sup> One of his first acts was creating the <a href="/w/index.php?title=National_Infrastructure_Unit&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="National Infrastructure Unit (page does not exist)">National Infrastructure Unit</a>, charged with formulating a plan for infrastructure projects and investments.<sup id="cite_ref-09_budget_29-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-09_budget-29">[29]</a></sup> He commissioned a government-wide spending review, with an aim to reducing government expenditure—with the exceptions of a two-year <a href="/wiki/Stimulus_(economics)" title="Stimulus (economics)">stimulus</a> package and long-term increases on infrastructure spending.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-09_budget_29-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-09_budget-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<p>In April 2011, the Opposition criticised English for suggesting that New Zealand businesses could use New Zealand's low wages to help it compete with Australia.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> The National Government campaigned for <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand_general_election,_2011" title="New Zealand general election, 2011">re-election in 2011</a> on its <a href="/wiki/Economy_of_New_Zealand" title="Economy of New Zealand">economic record</a>. The Government boasted growth for five consecutive quarters up to mid-2010, totalling 1.6% of <a href="/wiki/Real_gross_domestic_product" title="Real gross domestic product">real GDP</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<p>Strong growth resulted in a surplus of $473 million for the 2015/16 financial year, projected to rise to $8.5 billion by 2020/21. In his 2016, English stated that reducing debt and tackling the costs of the <a href="/wiki/Kaikoura_earthquake" class="mw-redirect" title="Kaikoura earthquake">Kaikoura earthquake</a> were higher priorities than reducing rates of tax.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<p>English announced in November 2013 that he would retire as an electorate MP at the <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand_general_election,_2014" title="New Zealand general election, 2014">2014 general election</a>, and contested the election as a <a href="/wiki/Party_lists_in_the_New_Zealand_general_election,_2014#National_Party" title="Party lists in the New Zealand general election, 2014">party-list candidate</a> only.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Allowances_issue">Allowances issue</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bill_English&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Allowances issue">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In 2009, the media, including <a href="/wiki/TVNZ" class="mw-redirect" title="TVNZ">TVNZ</a> and <a href="/wiki/TV3_(New_Zealand)" class="mw-redirect" title="TV3 (New Zealand)">TV3</a> revealed that English was receiving about NZ$900 a week as part of a living allowance for ministers, to live in his own NZ$1.2&#160;million Wellington home. At the time, English also received $276,200 in his annual salary as Deputy Prime Minister.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> It was also revealed other ministers with homes in the capital city were also claiming accommodation allowances.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> On 3 August 2009, Prime Minister <a href="/wiki/John_Key" title="John Key">John Key</a> announced a review of the housing allowances claimed by cabinet ministers.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup></p>
<p>English subsequently announced he would pay back $12,000 and only claim about $24,000 a year in living allowances.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> The Auditor-General's office said in September 2009 that they were making "preliminary enquiries" into parliamentary housing expenses in response to a letter of complaint from <a href="/wiki/Jim_Anderton%27s_Progressive_Party" title="Jim Anderton's Progressive Party">Progressive</a> party leader <a href="/wiki/Jim_Anderton" title="Jim Anderton">Jim Anderton</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> Two days later English announced that he would no longer take up any housing allowance and had paid back all the allowance he had received since the November 2008 election.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span id="Prime_Minister_(2016–present)"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Prime_Minister_.282016.E2.80.93present.29">Prime Minister (2016–present)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bill_English&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Prime Minister (2016–present)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p>On 5 December 2016, John Key announced he would resign on 12 December, and endorsed English as his successor in <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand_National_Party_leadership_election,_2016" title="New Zealand National Party leadership election, 2016">the resulting leadership election</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> English announced that he would be in the running on 6 December 2016.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> Following the drop-out of both <a href="/wiki/Judith_Collins" title="Judith Collins">Judith Collins</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jonathan_Coleman_(politician)" title="Jonathan Coleman (politician)">Jonathan Coleman</a> from the leadership election, he was sworn in as the 39th <a href="/wiki/Prime_Minister_of_New_Zealand" title="Prime Minister of New Zealand">Prime Minister of New Zealand</a> on 12 December 2016.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup></p>
<p>English appointed his first <a href="/wiki/Cabinet_of_New_Zealand" title="Cabinet of New Zealand">cabinet</a> on 18 December. In a <a href="/wiki/Cabinet_reshuffle" title="Cabinet reshuffle">reshuffle</a>, he appointed <a href="/wiki/Steven_Joyce" title="Steven Joyce">Steven Joyce</a> to succeed him as Finance Minister, while most ministerial portfolios remained the same.<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup></p>
<p>In January 2017, English announced that he would not attend <a href="/wiki/Waitangi_Day" title="Waitangi Day">Waitangi Day</a> commemorations at the historic <a href="/wiki/Waitangi,_Northland" title="Waitangi, Northland">treaty grounds</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Moir2017_48-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Moir2017-48">[48]</a></sup> It was reported that his absence was in response to the <a href="/wiki/Ng%C4%81puhi" title="Ngāpuhi">Ngāpuhi</a> <a href="/wiki/Iwi" title="Iwi">iwi</a>'s decision to stop the Prime Minister from speaking at the <a href="/wiki/Marae" title="Marae">marae</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Moir2017_48-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Moir2017-48">[48]</a></sup> Ngāpuhi have protested the Government's negotiation of the <a href="/wiki/Trans_Pacific_Partnership_Agreement" class="mw-redirect" title="Trans Pacific Partnership Agreement">Trans Pacific Partnership Agreement</a> (TPPA), which the iwi believe infringes upon Māori sovereignty, and thus does not adhere to the Treaty of Waitangi.<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup> English had been invited to attend in an official capacity; his non-attendance was criticised by a Ngāpuhi elder and Opposition leader <a href="/wiki/Andrew_Little_(New_Zealand_politician)" title="Andrew Little (New Zealand politician)">Andrew Little</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup></p>
<p>In his first overseas trip, the Prime Minister travelled to Europe to discuss trade ties, including a prospective New Zealand–European Union <a href="/wiki/Free_trade_agreement" class="mw-redirect" title="Free trade agreement">free trade agreement</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-foreign_visit_52-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-foreign_visit-52">[52]</a></sup> He first travelled to London on 13 January 2017 to meet British Prime Minister <a href="/wiki/Theresa_May" title="Theresa May">Theresa May</a>. Discussing trade relations, English said the two nations were "natural partners" and would "continue to forge ties" after the UK's <a href="/wiki/Brexit" title="Brexit">withdrawal from the EU</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup> He also arranged to meet with London Mayor <a href="/wiki/Sadiq_Khan" title="Sadiq Khan">Sadiq Khan</a>, Belgian Prime Minister <a href="/wiki/Charles_Michel" title="Charles Michel">Charles Michel</a> and German Chancellor <a href="/wiki/Angela_Merkel" title="Angela Merkel">Angela Merkel</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-foreign_visit_52-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-foreign_visit-52">[52]</a></sup> In a meeting with Merkel, English received crucial backing from Germany for a trade deal with the EU.<sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup> On 16 January, English stated that his Government would continue to promote TPPA, despite the United States' decision to withdraw from the agreement.<sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup> He explained that Southeast Asian countries would now be treated as a priority in negotiations—he also asserted that the United States was ceding influence to China by its rejection of the trade pact.<sup id="cite_ref-56" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup></p>
<p>At a press conference at the <a href="/wiki/Beehive_(New_Zealand)" title="Beehive (New Zealand)">Beehive</a> on 1 February 2017, English announced that the <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand_general_election,_2017" title="New Zealand general election, 2017">2017 general election</a> would be held on 23 September.<sup id="cite_ref-57" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup> The Prime Minister later confirmed that his party would approach <a href="/wiki/ACT_New_Zealand" title="ACT New Zealand">ACT</a>, <a href="/wiki/United_Future" title="United Future">United Future</a> and the <a href="/wiki/M%C4%81ori_Party" title="Māori Party">Māori Party</a> if <a href="/wiki/Confidence_and_supply" title="Confidence and supply">confidence and supply</a> agreements were required to form a government following the election.<sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup> In his second cabinet reshuffle on 24 April, English appointed <a href="/wiki/Gerry_Brownlee" title="Gerry Brownlee">Gerry Brownlee</a> as his new <a href="/wiki/Minister_of_Foreign_Affairs_(New_Zealand)" title="Minister of Foreign Affairs (New Zealand)">Foreign Affairs Minister</a>; he also promoted <a href="/wiki/Nikki_Kaye" title="Nikki Kaye">Nikki Kaye</a> to the portfolio of <a href="/wiki/Minister_of_Education_(New_Zealand)" title="Minister of Education (New Zealand)">Education Minister</a>, and moved <a href="/wiki/Mark_Mitchell_(politician)" title="Mark Mitchell (politician)">Mark Mitchell</a> into the cabinet to become <a href="/wiki/Minister_of_Defence_(New_Zealand)" title="Minister of Defence (New Zealand)">Defence Minister</a>. The reshuffle was perceived as an election preparation.<sup id="cite_ref-59" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup></p>
<p>On 13 February, English welcomed Australian Prime Minister <a href="/wiki/Malcolm_Turnbull" title="Malcolm Turnbull">Malcolm Turnbull</a> to Wellington. The two leaders reaffirmed their shared trade agenda, and discussed changes to the Australian citizenship pathway which will affect permanent residents originating from New Zealand.<sup id="cite_ref-60" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup></p>
<p>On 19 June 2017, it was reported that <a href="/wiki/Todd_Barclay" title="Todd Barclay">Todd Barclay</a>, who succeeded English as MP for <a href="/wiki/Clutha-Southland" title="Clutha-Southland">Clutha-Southland</a>, had clandestinely recorded one of his employee's conversations the previous year, and that John Key's leaders' budget was used to pay a confidential settlement after the employee resigned. English admitted that he been aware of the illegal recording and the settlement, and thus implicated in the scandal.<sup id="cite_ref-61" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-62" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup></p>

<p>English is regarded as more socially conservative than his predecessor, John Key.<sup id="cite_ref-63" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-EleanorAingeRoy_64-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EleanorAingeRoy-64">[64]</a></sup> He has stated his opposition to <a href="/wiki/Voluntary_euthanasia" title="Voluntary euthanasia">voluntary euthanasia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Physician_assisted_suicide" class="mw-redirect" title="Physician assisted suicide">physician assisted suicide</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-65" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-66" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Civil_union_in_New_Zealand" title="Civil union in New Zealand">same-sex civil unions</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-67" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup> and the decriminalisation of <a href="/wiki/Prostitution_in_New_Zealand" title="Prostitution in New Zealand">prostitution</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-68" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup> He also opposes any "liberalisation" of <a href="/wiki/Abortion_in_New_Zealand" title="Abortion in New Zealand">abortion law</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-69" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-69">[69]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2004, English voted against <a href="/wiki/Civil_Union_Act_2004" title="Civil Union Act 2004">a bill</a> to establish civil unions for both same-sex and opposite-sex couples.<sup id="cite_ref-70" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-70">[70]</a></sup> In 2005, he voted for the <a href="/wiki/Same-sex_marriage_in_New_Zealand#Marriage_.28Gender_Clarification.29_Amendment_Bill_2005" title="Same-sex marriage in New Zealand">Marriage (Gender Clarification) Amendment Bill</a>, which would have amended the Marriage Act to define marriage as only between a man and a woman.<sup id="cite_ref-71" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-71">[71]</a></sup> English voted against the <a href="/wiki/Marriage_(Definition_of_Marriage)_Amendment_Act_2013" title="Marriage (Definition of Marriage) Amendment Act 2013">Marriage (Definition of Marriage) Amendment Bill</a>, a bill that legalised <a href="/wiki/Same-sex_marriage_in_New_Zealand" title="Same-sex marriage in New Zealand">same-sex marriage in New Zealand</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-72" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-72">[72]</a></sup> However, in December 2016 he stated, "I'd probably vote differently now on the gay marriage issue, I don't think that gay marriage is a threat to anyone else's marriage".<sup id="cite_ref-73" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-73">[73]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2009, English voted against the <a href="/wiki/Cannabis_in_New_Zealand#Legality" title="Cannabis in New Zealand">Misuse of Drugs (Medicinal Cannabis) Amendment Bill</a>, a bill aimed at amending the Misuse of Drugs Act so that cannabis could be used for medical purposes.<sup id="cite_ref-HansardMedCannabisVote_74-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HansardMedCannabisVote-74">[74]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life">Personal life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bill_English&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Personal life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>English met his future wife, Mary Scanlon, at university. She was studying medicine at the time, and became a <a href="/wiki/General_practitioner" title="General practitioner">general practitioner</a>. Both her parents were immigrants, her father being Samoan and her mother Italian, born on the island of <a href="/wiki/Stromboli" title="Stromboli">Stromboli</a>. They have six children.<sup id="cite_ref-75" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-75">[75]</a></sup></p>
<p>English is a practising <a href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic" class="mw-redirect" title="Roman Catholic">Roman Catholic</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-EleanorAingeRoy_64-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EleanorAingeRoy-64">[64]</a></sup> but has stated that he considers his religious beliefs personal and thus separate from politics.<sup id="cite_ref-76" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-76">[76]</a></sup></p>
<p>In June 2002, English took part in <a href="/wiki/TV3_(New_Zealand)" class="mw-redirect" title="TV3 (New Zealand)">TV3's</a> <i>Fight For Life</i>, a celebrity <a href="/wiki/Boxing" title="Boxing">boxing</a> fundraiser to raise money for the Yellow Ribbon anti-youth-suicide campaign, influenced by the death of a teenage nephew in 1997. He lost a split decision to former university colleague Ted Clarke.<sup id="cite_ref-NZ_Herald_2045619_77-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NZ_Herald_2045619-77">[77]</a></sup></p>
