<p><b>Domesday Book</b> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'d' in 'dye'">d</span><span title="/uː/: 'oo' in 'goose'">uː</span><span title="'m' in 'my'">m</span><span title="'z' in 'zoom'">z</span><span title="'d' in 'dye'">d</span><span title="/eɪ/: 'a' in 'face'">eɪ</span></span>/</a></span></span> or <span class="nowrap"><small><a href="/wiki/American_English" title="American English">US</a>:</small> <span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'d' in 'dye'">d</span><span title="/oʊ/: 'o' in 'code'">oʊ</span><span title="'m' in 'my'">m</span><span title="'z' in 'zoom'">z</span><span title="'d' in 'dye'">d</span><span title="/eɪ/: 'a' in 'face'">eɪ</span></span>/</a></span></span>;<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a>: <i>Liber de Wintonia</i> "Book of <a href="/wiki/Winchester,_Hampshire" class="mw-redirect" title="Winchester, Hampshire">Winchester</a>") is a manuscript record of the "Great Survey" of much of England and parts of Wales completed in 1086 by order of King <a href="/wiki/William_the_Conqueror" title="William the Conqueror">William the Conqueror</a>. The <i><a href="/wiki/Anglo-Saxon_Chronicle" title="Anglo-Saxon Chronicle">Anglo-Saxon Chronicle</a></i> states:<sup id="cite_ref-asc_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-asc-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<blockquote>
<p>Then, at the midwinter [1085], was the king in <a href="/wiki/Gloucester" title="Gloucester">Glocester</a> with his council ... . After this had the king a large meeting, and very deep consultation with his council, about this land; how it was occupied, and by what sort of men. Then sent he his men over all England into each shire; commissioning them to find out "How many hundreds of hides were in the shire, what land the king himself had, and what stock upon the land; or, what dues he ought to have by the year from the shire."</p>
</blockquote>
<p>It was written in <a href="/wiki/Medieval_Latin" title="Medieval Latin">Medieval Latin</a>, was <a href="/wiki/Scribal_abbreviation" title="Scribal abbreviation">highly abbreviated</a>, and included some vernacular native terms without Latin equivalents.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> The survey's main purpose was to determine what taxes had been owed during the reign of King <a href="/wiki/Edward_the_Confessor" title="Edward the Confessor">Edward the Confessor</a>, which allowed William to reassert the rights of the Crown and assess where power lay after a wholesale redistribution of land following the <a href="/wiki/Norman_conquest" class="mw-redirect" title="Norman conquest">Norman conquest</a>.</p>
<p>The assessors' reckoning of a man's holdings and their values, as recorded in Domesday Book, was dispositive and without appeal. The name "Domesday Book" (<a href="/wiki/Middle_English" title="Middle English">Middle English</a> for "Doomsday Book") came into use in the 12th century.<sup id="cite_ref-Harvard_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Harvard-5">[5]</a></sup> As <a href="/wiki/Richard_FitzNeal" title="Richard FitzNeal">Richard FitzNeal</a> wrote in the <i><a href="/wiki/Dialogus_de_Scaccario" title="Dialogus de Scaccario">Dialogus de Scaccario</a></i> (<i>circa</i> 1179):<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<blockquote>
<p>for as the sentence of that strict and terrible last account cannot be evaded by any skilful subterfuge, so when this book is appealed to ... its sentence cannot be quashed or set aside with impunity. That is why we have called the book 'the Book of Judgement'<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>... because its decisions, like those of the <a href="/wiki/Last_Judgement" class="mw-redirect" title="Last Judgement">Last Judgement</a>, are unalterable.</p>
</blockquote>
<p>The manuscript is held at <a href="/wiki/The_National_Archives_(United_Kingdom)" title="The National Archives (United Kingdom)">The National Archives</a> at <a href="/wiki/Kew" title="Kew">Kew</a>, London. In 2011, the Open Domesday site made the manuscript available online.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>The book is an invaluable primary source for modern historians and historical economists. No survey approaching the scope and extent of Domesday Book was attempted again in Britain until the <a href="/wiki/Return_of_Owners_of_Land,_1873" title="Return of Owners of Land, 1873">1873 Return of Owners of Land</a> (sometimes termed the "Modern Domesday")<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> which presented the first complete, post-Domesday picture of the distribution of landed property in the British Isles.<sup id="cite_ref-Return_of_Owners_of_Land.2C_1873.2C_Wales.2C_Scotland.2C_Ireland_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Return_of_Owners_of_Land.2C_1873.2C_Wales.2C_Scotland.2C_Ireland-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Content_and_organisation">Content and organisation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Domesday_Book&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Content and organisation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:182px;"><a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Upload?wpDestFile=Doomsday_Book_-_Warwickshire.png" class="new" title="File:Doomsday Book - Warwickshire.png">File:Doomsday Book - Warwickshire.png</a>
<div class="thumbcaption">A page of Doomsday Book for <a href="/wiki/Warwickshire" title="Warwickshire">Warwickshire</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:132px;"><a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:Upload?wpDestFile=Doomsday_binding.jpg" class="new" title="File:Doomsday binding.jpg">File:Doomsday binding.jpg</a>
<div class="thumbcaption">Great Domesday in its "<a href="/wiki/Tudor_period" title="Tudor period">Tudor</a>" binding: a wood-engraving of the 1860s</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Domesday Book encompasses two independent works (in, originally, two physical volumes). These were "Little Domesday" (covering <a href="/wiki/Norfolk" title="Norfolk">Norfolk</a>, Suffolk, and <a href="/wiki/Essex" title="Essex">Essex</a>), and "Great Doomsday" (covering much of the remainder of England and parts of Wales‍—‌except for lands in the north which later became <a href="/wiki/Westmorland" title="Westmorland">Westmorland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cumberland" title="Cumberland">Cumberland</a>, Northumberland, and the <a href="/wiki/County_Palatine_of_Durham" title="County Palatine of Durham">County Palatine of Durham</a>). No surveys were made of the <a href="/wiki/City_of_London" title="City of London">City of London</a>, <a href="/wiki/Winchester" title="Winchester">Winchester</a>, or some other towns, probably due to their tax-exempt status. (Other areas of modern London were then in Middlesex, Kent, Essex, etc., and are included in Domesday Book.) Most of Cumberland and Westmorland are missing. <a href="/wiki/County_Durham" title="County Durham">County Durham</a> is missing because the <a href="/wiki/Bishop_of_Durham" title="Bishop of Durham">Bishop of Durham</a> (<a href="/wiki/William_de_St-Calais" title="William de St-Calais">William de St-Calais</a>) had the exclusive right to tax it; in addition, parts of north-east England were covered by the 1183 <i><a href="/wiki/Boldon_Book" title="Boldon Book">Boldon Book</a></i>, listing areas liable to tax by the Bishop of Durham. The omission of the other counties and towns is not fully explained, although in particular Cumberland and Westmorland had yet to be fully conquered<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>.</p>
<p>"Little Doomsday" – so named because its format is physically smaller than its companion's – is the more detailed survey, down to numbers of livestock. It may have represented the first attempt, resulting in a decision to avoid such level of detail in "Great Domesday".<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Both volumes are organised into a series of chapters (literally "headings", from Latin <i>caput</i>, "a head") listing the <a href="/wiki/Fiefdom" class="mw-redirect" title="Fiefdom">fees</a> (<a href="/wiki/Knight%27s_fee" title="Knight's fee">knight's fees</a> or <a href="/wiki/Fiefdom" class="mw-redirect" title="Fiefdom">fiefs</a>, broadly identical to <a href="/wiki/Manor" title="Manor">manors</a>), <a href="/wiki/Feudal_land_tenure_in_England" title="Feudal land tenure in England">held</a> by a named <a href="/wiki/Tenant-in-chief" title="Tenant-in-chief">tenant-in-chief</a> of the king (who formed the highest stratum of Norman feudal society below the king), namely religious institutions, Bishops, Norman warrior magnates and a few Saxon <a href="/wiki/Thegn" title="Thegn">thegns</a> who had made peace with the Norman regime. Some of the largest such magnates held several hundred fees, in a few cases in more than one county. For example, the chapter of the Doomsday Book <a href="/wiki/Devon" title="Devon">Devonshire</a> section concerning <a href="/wiki/Baldwin_the_Sheriff" class="mw-redirect" title="Baldwin the Sheriff">Baldwin the Sheriff</a> lists 176 holdings held in-chief by him. Only a few of the holdings of the large magnates were held <a href="/wiki/Demesne" title="Demesne">in demesne</a>, most having been <a href="/wiki/Subinfeudation" title="Subinfeudation">subinfeudated</a> to knights, generally military followers of the tenant-in-chief (often his feudal tenants from Normandy) which latter thus became their <a href="/wiki/Overlord" title="Overlord">overlord</a>. The fees listed within the chapter concerning a particular tenant-in-chief were usually ordered, but not in a systematic or rigorous fashion, by the <a href="/wiki/Hundred_(country_subdivision)" class="mw-redirect" title="Hundred (country subdivision)">Hundred Court</a> under the jurisdiction of which they were situated, not by geographic location. As a review of taxes owed, it was highly unpopular.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a href="/wiki/File:DevonDomesdayBook_TenantsInChief.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8b/DevonDomesdayBook_TenantsInChief.png/200px-DevonDomesdayBook_TenantsInChief.png" width="200" height="260" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8b/DevonDomesdayBook_TenantsInChief.png/300px-DevonDomesdayBook_TenantsInChief.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/8/8b/DevonDomesdayBook_TenantsInChief.png 2x" data-file-width="350" data-file-height="455" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">

<i>HIC ANNOTANTUR TENENTES TERRAS IN DEVENESCIRE</i> ("Here are noted (those) holding lands in Devonshire"). Detail from Domesday Book, list forming part of first page of king's holdings. There are 53 entries, including the first entry for the king himself followed by the <a href="/wiki/Devon_Domesday_Book_tenants-in-chief" title="Devon Domesday Book tenants-in-chief">Devon Domesday Book tenants-in-chief</a>. Each name has its own chapter to follow.</div>

<p>Each county's list opened with the king's <a href="/wiki/Demesne" title="Demesne">demesne</a> lands (which had possibly been the subject of separate inquiry). It should be borne in mind that under the feudal system the king was the only true "owner" of land in England, under his <a href="/wiki/Allodial_title" title="Allodial title">allodial title</a>. He was thus the ultimate overlord and even the greatest magnate could do no more than "hold" land from him as a <a href="/wiki/Leasehold_estate#history" title="Leasehold estate">tenant</a> (from the Latin verb <i>teneo</i>, "to hold") under one of the various contracts of <a href="/wiki/Feudal_land_tenure_in_England" title="Feudal land tenure in England">feudal land tenure</a>. Holdings of Bishops followed, then of the abbeys and religious houses, then of lay <a href="/wiki/Tenant-in-chief" title="Tenant-in-chief">tenants-in-chief</a> and lastly the king's serjeants (<i>servientes</i>), and Saxon <a href="/wiki/Thegn" title="Thegn">thegns</a> who had survived the <a href="/wiki/Norman_Conquest" class="mw-redirect" title="Norman Conquest">Conquest</a>, all in hierarchical order.</p>
<p>In some counties, one or more principal towns formed the subject of a separate section: in some the <i>clamores</i> (disputed titles to land) were also treated separately. This principle applies more specially to the larger volume: in the smaller one, the system is more confused, the execution less perfect.</p>
<p>Domesday names a total of 13,418 places.<sup id="cite_ref-tally_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-tally-11">[11]</a></sup> Apart from the wholly rural portions, which constitute its bulk, Domesday contains entries of interest concerning most of the towns, which were probably made because of their bearing on the fiscal rights of the crown therein. These include fragments of <a href="/wiki/Custumal" title="Custumal">custumals</a> (older customary agreements), records of the military service due, of markets, <a href="/wiki/Mint_(coin)" class="mw-redirect" title="Mint (coin)">mints</a>, and so forth. From the towns, from the counties as wholes, and from many of its ancient lordships, the crown was entitled to archaic dues in kind, such as <a href="/wiki/Honey" title="Honey">honey</a>. (In a parallel development, around 1100 the <a href="/wiki/Normans" title="Normans">Normans</a> in southern Italy completed their <i><a href="/wiki/Catalogus_Baronum" title="Catalogus Baronum">Catalogus Baronum</a></i> based on Domesday Book.) &#160;:3</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Name">Name</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Domesday_Book&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Name">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The manuscripts do not carry a formal title. The work is referred to internally as a <i>descriptio</i> (enrolling), and in other early administrative contexts as the king's <i>brevia</i> (writings). From about 1100, references appear to the <i>liber</i> (book) or <i>carta</i> (charter) of <a href="/wiki/Winchester,_Hampshire" class="mw-redirect" title="Winchester, Hampshire">Winchester</a>, its usual place of custody; and from the mid-12th to early 13th centuries, to the Winchester or king's <i>rotulus</i> (<a href="/wiki/Rotulus" title="Rotulus">roll</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-Hallam_1986.2C_pp._34.E2.80.935_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hallam_1986.2C_pp._34.E2.80.935-12">[12]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>To the English, however, who held the book in awe, it became known as "Domesday Book", in allusion to the <a href="/wiki/Last_Judgement" class="mw-redirect" title="Last Judgement">Last Judgement</a> and in specific reference to the definitive character of the record.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> The word "doom" was the usual <a href="/wiki/Old_English" title="Old English">Old English</a> term for a law or judgement; it did not carry the modern overtones of fatality or disaster.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Richard_FitzNeal" title="Richard FitzNeal">Richard FitzNeal</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lord_High_Treasurer" title="Lord High Treasurer">treasurer of England</a> under <a href="/wiki/Henry_II_of_England" title="Henry II of England">Henry II</a>, explained the name's connotations in detail in the <i><a href="/wiki/Dialogus_de_Scaccario" title="Dialogus de Scaccario">Dialogus de Scaccario</a></i> (c.1179):<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<blockquote>
<p>The book is metaphorically called by the native English, Domesday, i.e., the Day of Judgement. For as the sentence of that strict and terrible last account cannot be evaded by any skilful subterfuge, so when this book is appealed to on those matters which it contains, its sentence cannot be quashed or set aside with impunity. That is why we have called the book "the Book of Judgement",<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>... not because it contains decisions on various difficult points, but because its decisions, like those of the Last Judgement, are unalterable.</p>
</blockquote>
<p>The name "Domesday" was subsequently adopted by the book's custodians, being first found in an official document in 1221.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>Either through <a href="/wiki/False_etymology" title="False etymology">false etymology</a> or deliberate <a href="/wiki/Word_play" title="Word play">word play</a>, the name also came to be associated with the Latin phrase <i>Domus Dei</i> ("House of God"). Such a reference is found as early as the late 13th century, in the writings of <a href="/wiki/Adam_of_Damerham" title="Adam of Damerham">Adam of Damerham</a>; and in the 16th and 17th centuries, <a href="/wiki/Antiquarian" title="Antiquarian">antiquaries</a> such as <a href="/wiki/John_Stow" title="John Stow">John Stow</a> and Sir <a href="/wiki/Richard_Baker_(chronicler)" title="Richard Baker (chronicler)">Richard Baker</a> believed this was the name's origin, alluding to the church in Winchester in which the book had been kept.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> As a result, the alternative spelling "Domesdei" became popular for a while.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>The usual modern scholarly convention is to refer to the work as "Domesday Book" (or simply as "Domesday"), without a definite article. However, the form "the Domesday Book" is also found in both academic and non-academic contexts.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Survey">Survey</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Domesday_Book&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Survey">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <i>Anglo-Saxon Chronicle</i> states that planning for the survey was conducted in 1085, and the book's <a href="/wiki/Colophon_(publishing)" title="Colophon (publishing)">colophon</a> states the survey was completed in 1086. It is not known when exactly Domesday Book was compiled, but the entire copy of Great Domesday appears to have been copied out by one person on <a href="/wiki/Parchment" title="Parchment">parchment</a> (prepared sheepskin), although six scribes seem to have been used for Little Domesday. Writing in 2000, David Roffe argued that the inquest (survey) and the construction of the book were two distinct exercises. He believes the latter was completed, if not started, by <a href="/wiki/William_II_of_England" title="William II of England">William II</a> following his assumption of the English throne; William II quashed a rebellion that followed and was based on, though not consequent on, the findings of the inquest.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>Most shires were visited by a group of royal officers (<i>legati</i>), who held a public inquiry, probably in the great assembly known as the shire court. These were attended by representatives of every township as well as of the local lords. The unit of inquiry was the Hundred (a subdivision of the county, which then was an administrative entity). The return for each Hundred was sworn to by 12 local jurors, half of them English and half of them <a href="/wiki/Normans" title="Normans">Norman</a>.</p>
<p>What is believed to be a full transcript of these original returns is preserved for several of the <a href="/wiki/Cambridgeshire" title="Cambridgeshire">Cambridgeshire</a> Hundreds - the <a href="/wiki/Cambridge_Inquisition" title="Cambridge Inquisition">Cambridge Inquisition</a> - and is of great illustrative importance. The <a href="/wiki/Ely_Inquiry" title="Ely Inquiry"><i>Inquisitio Eliensis</i></a> is a record of the lands of <a href="/wiki/Ely_Abbey" class="mw-redirect" title="Ely Abbey">Ely Abbey</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> The <i><a href="/wiki/Liber_Exoniensis" title="Liber Exoniensis">Exon Domesday</a></i> (named because the volume was held at <a href="/wiki/Exeter" title="Exeter">Exeter</a>) covers <a href="/wiki/Cornwall" title="Cornwall">Cornwall</a>, Devon, <a href="/wiki/Dorset" title="Dorset">Dorset</a>, Somerset, and one manor of <a href="/wiki/Wiltshire" title="Wiltshire">Wiltshire</a>. Parts of Devon, Dorset, and Somerset are also missing. Otherwise, this contains the full details supplied by the original returns.</p>
<p>Through comparison of what details are recorded in which counties, six Great Domesday "circuits" can be determined (plus a seventh circuit for the Little Domesday shires).</p>
<ol>
<li><a href="/wiki/Berkshire" title="Berkshire">Berkshire</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hampshire" title="Hampshire">Hampshire</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kent" title="Kent">Kent</a>, <a href="/wiki/Surrey" title="Surrey">Surrey</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sussex" title="Sussex">Sussex</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Cornwall" title="Cornwall">Cornwall</a>, <a href="/wiki/Devon" title="Devon">Devon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dorset" title="Dorset">Dorset</a>, <a href="/wiki/Somerset" title="Somerset">Somerset</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wiltshire" title="Wiltshire">Wiltshire</a> (<a href="/wiki/Exon_Domesday" class="mw-redirect" title="Exon Domesday">Exon Domesday</a>)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Bedfordshire" title="Bedfordshire">Bedfordshire</a>, <a href="/wiki/Buckinghamshire" title="Buckinghamshire">Buckinghamshire</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cambridgeshire" title="Cambridgeshire">Cambridgeshire</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hertfordshire" title="Hertfordshire">Hertfordshire</a>, <a href="/wiki/Middlesex" title="Middlesex">Middlesex</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Leicestershire" title="Leicestershire">Leicestershire</a>, <a href="/wiki/Northamptonshire" title="Northamptonshire">Northamptonshire</a>, <a href="/wiki/Oxfordshire" title="Oxfordshire">Oxfordshire</a>, <a href="/wiki/Staffordshire" title="Staffordshire">Staffordshire</a>, <a href="/wiki/Warwickshire" title="Warwickshire">Warwickshire</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Cheshire" title="Cheshire">Cheshire</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gloucestershire" title="Gloucestershire">Gloucestershire</a>, <a href="/wiki/Herefordshire" title="Herefordshire">Herefordshire</a>, <a href="/wiki/Shropshire" title="Shropshire">Shropshire</a>, <a href="/wiki/Worcestershire" title="Worcestershire">Worcestershire</a>&#160;– the <a href="/wiki/Welsh_Marches" title="Welsh Marches">Marches</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Derbyshire" title="Derbyshire">Derbyshire</a>, <a href="/wiki/Huntingdonshire" title="Huntingdonshire">Huntingdonshire</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lincolnshire" title="Lincolnshire">Lincolnshire</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nottinghamshire" title="Nottinghamshire">Nottinghamshire</a>, <a href="/wiki/Yorkshire" title="Yorkshire">Yorkshire</a></li>
</ol>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Purpose">Purpose</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Domesday_Book&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Purpose">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Three sources discuss the goal of the survey:</p>
<ul>
<li>The <i>Anglo-Saxon Chronicle</i> tells why it was ordered:<sup id="cite_ref-asc_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-asc-3">[3]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<blockquote>
<p>After this had the king a large meeting, and very deep consultation with his council, about this land; how it was occupied, and by what sort of men. Then sent he his men over all England into each shire; commissioning them to find out 'How many hundreds of <a href="/wiki/Hide_(unit)" title="Hide (unit)">hides</a> were in the shire, what land the king himself had, and what stock upon the land; or, what dues he ought to have by the year from the shire.' Also he commissioned them to record in writing, 'How much land his archbishops had, and his diocesan bishops, and his abbots, and his earls;' and though I may be prolix and tedious, 'What, or how much, each man had, who was an occupier of land in England, either in land or in stock, and how much money it were worth.' So very narrowly, indeed, did he commission them to trace it out, that there was not one single hide, nor a yard of land, nay, moreover (it is shameful to tell, though he thought it no shame to do it), not even an ox, nor a cow, nor a swine was there left, that was not set down in his writ. And all the recorded particulars were afterwards brought to him.</p>
</blockquote>
<ul>
<li>The list of questions asked of the jurors was recorded in the <i>Inquisitio Eliensis</i>.</li>
<li>The contents of Domesday Book and the allied records mentioned above.</li>
</ul>
<p>The primary purpose of the survey was to ascertain and record the fiscal rights of the king. These were mainly:</p>
<ul>
<li>the national land-tax (<i>geldum</i>), paid on a fixed assessment,</li>
<li>certain miscellaneous dues, and</li>
<li>the proceeds of the crown lands.</li>
</ul>
<p>After a great political convulsion such as the Norman conquest, and the following wholesale confiscation of landed estates, William needed to reassert that the rights of the Crown, which he claimed to have inherited, had not suffered in the process. His Norman followers tended to evade the liabilities of their English predecessors. The <a href="/wiki/Trial_of_Penenden_Heath" title="Trial of Penenden Heath">successful trial</a> of <a href="/wiki/Odo_de_Bayeux" class="mw-redirect" title="Odo de Bayeux">Odo de Bayeux</a> at <a href="/wiki/Penenden_Heath" title="Penenden Heath">Penenden Heath</a> less than a decade after the conquest was one example of the Crown's growing discontent at the Norman land-grab of the years following the invasion. Historians believe the survey was to aid William in establishing certainty and a definitive reference point as to property holdings across the nation, in case such evidence was needed in disputes over Crown ownership.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Domesday survey therefore recorded the names of the new holders of lands and the assessments on which their tax was to be paid. But it did more than this; by the king's instructions, it endeavoured to make a national valuation list, estimating the annual value of all the land in the country, (1) at the time of <a href="/wiki/Edward_the_Confessor" title="Edward the Confessor">Edward the Confessor</a>'s death, (2) when the new owners received it, (3) at the time of the survey, and further, it reckoned, by command, the potential value as well. It is evident that William desired to know the financial resources of his kingdom, and it is probable that he wished to compare them with the existing assessment, which was one of considerable antiquity, though there are traces that it had been occasionally modified. The great bulk of Domesday Book is devoted to the somewhat arid details of the assessment and valuation of rural estates, which were as yet the only important source of national wealth. After stating the assessment of the <a href="/wiki/Manorialism" title="Manorialism">manor</a>, the record sets forth the amount of <a href="/wiki/Arable_land" title="Arable land">arable land</a>, and the number of plough teams (each reckoned at eight oxen) available for working it, with the additional number (if any) that might be employed; then the river-meadows, woodland, pasture, fisheries (i.e. <a href="/wiki/Fishing_weir" title="Fishing weir">fishing weirs</a>), <a href="/wiki/Watermill" title="Watermill">water-mills</a>, <a href="/wiki/Salt_evaporation_pond" title="Salt evaporation pond">salt-pans</a> (if by the sea) and other subsidiary sources of revenue; the peasants are enumerated in their several classes; and finally the annual value of the whole, past and present, is roughly estimated.</p>
<p>The organisation of the returns on a feudal basis, enabled the Conqueror and his officers to see the extent of a baron's possessions; and it also showed to what extent he had under-tenants, and the identities of the under-tenants. This was of great importance to William, not only for military reasons, but also because of his resolve to command the personal loyalty of the under-tenants (though the "men" of their lords) by making them swear allegiance to himself. As Domesday Book normally records only the Christian name of an under-tenant, it is not possible to search for the surnames of families claiming a Norman origin. Scholars, however, have worked to identify the under-tenants, most of whom have foreign Christian names.</p>
<p>The survey provided the King with information on potential sources of funds when he needed to raise money. It includes sources of income but not expenses, such as castles, unless they needed to be included to explain discrepancies between pre-and post-Conquest holdings of individuals. Typically, this happened in a town, where separately-recorded properties had been demolished to make way for a castle.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Custodial_history">Custodial history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Domesday_Book&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Custodial history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Domesday Book was preserved from the late 11th to the beginning of the 13th centuries in the royal <a href="/wiki/HM_Treasury" title="HM Treasury">Treasury</a> at <a href="/wiki/Winchester,_Hampshire" class="mw-redirect" title="Winchester, Hampshire">Winchester</a> (the Norman kings' capital). It was often referred to as the "Book" or "Roll" of Winchester.<sup id="cite_ref-Hallam_1986.2C_pp._34.E2.80.935_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hallam_1986.2C_pp._34.E2.80.935-12">[12]</a></sup> When the Treasury moved to the <a href="/wiki/Palace_of_Westminster" title="Palace of Westminster">Palace of Westminster</a>, probably under <a href="/wiki/John,_King_of_England" title="John, King of England">King John</a>, the book went with it. In the Middle Ages, the Book's evidence was frequently invoked in the law-courts. As recently as the 1960s, it was still referred to in court cases regarding ancient land and property rights.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<p>The two volumes (Great Domesday and Little Domesday) remained in Westminster until the 19th century, being held at different times in various offices of the <a href="/wiki/Exchequer" title="Exchequer">Exchequer</a> (the Chapel of the Pyx of <a href="/wiki/Westminster_Abbey" title="Westminster Abbey">Westminster Abbey</a>; the Treasury of Receipts; and the Tally Court).<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> On many occasions, however, the books were taken around the country with the Exchequer: for example to <a href="/wiki/York" title="York">York</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lincoln,_England" title="Lincoln, England">Lincoln</a> in 1300, to York again in 1303 and 1319, to <a href="/wiki/Hertford" title="Hertford">Hertford</a> in the 1580s or 1590s, and to <a href="/wiki/Nonsuch_Palace" title="Nonsuch Palace">Nonsuch Palace</a>, Surrey, in 1666, following the <a href="/wiki/Great_Fire_of_London" title="Great Fire of London">Great Fire of London</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<p>From the 1740s onwards they were held, with other Exchequer records, in the <a href="/wiki/Chapter_House" class="mw-redirect" title="Chapter House">Chapter House</a> of <a href="/wiki/Westminster_Abbey" title="Westminster Abbey">Westminster Abbey</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> In 1859 they were placed in the new <a href="/wiki/Public_Record_Office" title="Public Record Office">Public Record Office</a>, London.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> They are now held at <a href="/wiki/The_National_Archives_(United_Kingdom)" title="The National Archives (United Kingdom)">The National Archives</a> at Kew. The ancient Domesday chest, in which they were kept in the 17th and 18th centuries, is also preserved at Kew.</p>
<p>In modern times, the books have been removed from London on only a few exceptional occasions. In 1861–3 they were sent to <a href="/wiki/Southampton" title="Southampton">Southampton</a> for <a href="/wiki/Photozincography_of_Domesday_Book" title="Photozincography of Domesday Book">photozincographic reproduction</a>;<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> in 1918–19, during <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a>, they were evacuated (with other Public Record Office documents) to <a href="/wiki/Bodmin_Jail" title="Bodmin Jail">Bodmin Prison</a>, Cornwall; and similarly in 1939–45, during <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>, they were evacuated to <a href="/wiki/HM_Prison_Shepton_Mallet" title="HM Prison Shepton Mallet">Shepton Mallet Prison</a>, Somerset.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Binding">Binding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Domesday_Book&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Binding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The volumes have been rebound on several occasions. Little Domesday was rebound in 1320, its older oak boards being re-used. At a later date (probably in the <a href="/wiki/Tudor_period" title="Tudor period">Tudor period</a>) both volumes were given new covers. They were rebound twice in the 19th century, in 1819 and 1869, on the second occasion by the binder <a href="/wiki/Robert_Riviere" title="Robert Riviere">Robert Riviere</a>. In the 20th century, they were rebound in 1952, when their physical makeup was examined in greater detail; and yet again in 1986 for the survey's ninth centenary. On this last occasion Great Domesday was divided into two physical volumes, and Little Domesday into three volumes.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></p>

<p>The project to publish Domesday was begun by the government in 1773, and the book appeared in two volumes in 1783, set in "<a href="/wiki/Record_type" title="Record type">record type</a>" to produce a partial-<a href="/wiki/Facsimile" title="Facsimile">facsimile</a> of the manuscript. In 1811, a volume of indexes was added. In 1816 a supplementary volume, separately indexed, was published containing</p>
<ol>
<li>The <i><a href="/wiki/Exon_Domesday" class="mw-redirect" title="Exon Domesday">Exon Domesday</a></i>—for the south-western counties</li>
<li>The <i>Inquisitio Eliensis</i></li>
<li>The <i><a href="/wiki/Liber_Winton" class="mw-redirect" title="Liber Winton">Liber Winton</a></i>—surveys of Winchester late in the 12th century.</li>
<li>The <i><a href="/wiki/Boldon_Book" title="Boldon Book">Boldon Buke</a></i>—a survey of the bishopric of Durham a century later than Domesday</li>
</ol>
<p><a href="/wiki/Photozincography_of_Domesday_Book" title="Photozincography of Domesday Book">Photographic facsimiles</a> of Domesday Book, for each county separately, were published in 1861–1863, also by the government. Today, Domesday Book is available in numerous editions, usually separated by county and available with other <a href="/wiki/English_local_history" title="English local history">local history</a> resources.</p>
<p>In 1986, the <a href="/wiki/BBC" title="BBC">BBC</a> released the <i><a href="/wiki/BBC_Domesday_Project" title="BBC Domesday Project">BBC Domesday Project</a>,</i> the results of a project to create a survey to mark the 900th anniversary of the original Domesday Book. In August 2006 the contents of Domesday went online, with an English translation of the book's Latin. Visitors to the website are able to look up a place name and see the index entry made for the manor, town, city or village. They can also, for a fee, download the relevant page.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Importance">Importance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Domesday_Book&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Importance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Domesday_plaque.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e8/Domesday_plaque.JPG/220px-Domesday_plaque.JPG" width="220" height="218" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e8/Domesday_plaque.JPG/330px-Domesday_plaque.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e8/Domesday_plaque.JPG/440px-Domesday_plaque.JPG 2x" data-file-width="2207" data-file-height="2189" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Domesday_plaque.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
In 1986, memorial plaques were installed in settlements mentioned in Domesday Book</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Domesday Book is critical to understanding the period in which it was written. As H. C. Darby noted, anyone who uses it</p>
<blockquote>
<p>can have nothing but admiration for what is the oldest 'public record' in England and probably the most remarkable statistical document in the history of Europe. The continent has no document to compare with this detailed description covering so great a stretch of territory. And the geographer, as he turns over the folios, with their details of population and of arable, woodland, meadow and other resources, cannot but be excited at the vast amount of information that passes before his eyes.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>The author of the article on the book in the <a href="/wiki/Encyclop%C3%A6dia_Britannica_Eleventh_Edition" title="Encyclopædia Britannica Eleventh Edition">eleventh edition of the <i>Encyclopædia Britannica</i></a> noted, "To the topographer, as to the genealogist, its evidence is of primary importance, as it not only contains the earliest survey of each township or manor, but affords, in the majority of cases, a clue to its subsequent descent."</p>
<p>Darby also notes the inconsistencies, saying that "when this great wealth of data is examined more closely, perplexities and difficulties arise."<sup id="cite_ref-Darby-13_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Darby-13-35">[35]</a></sup> One problem is that the clerks who compiled this document "were but human; they were frequently forgetful or confused." The use of <a href="/wiki/Roman_numerals" title="Roman numerals">Roman numerals</a> also led to countless mistakes. Darby states, "Anyone who attempts an arithmetical exercise in Roman numerals soon sees something of the difficulties that faced the clerks."<sup id="cite_ref-Darby-13_35-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Darby-13-35">[35]</a></sup> But more important are the numerous obvious omissions, and ambiguities in presentation. Darby first cites <a href="/wiki/Frederic_William_Maitland" title="Frederic William Maitland">F. W. Maitland</a>'s comment following his compilation of a table of statistics from material taken from the Domesday Book survey, "it will be remembered that, as matters now stand, two men not unskilled in Domesday might add up the number of hides in a county and arrive at very different results because they would hold different opinions as to the meanings of certain formulas which are not uncommon."<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> Darby says that "it would be more correct to speak not of 'the Domesday geography of England', but of 'the geography of Domesday Book'. The two may not be quite the same thing, and how near the record was to reality we can never know."<sup id="cite_ref-Darby-13_35-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Darby-13-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
