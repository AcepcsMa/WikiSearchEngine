			<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Adam Kraft</h1>
									<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
									<div id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
								<div id="contentSub"></div>
												<div id="jump-to-nav" class="mw-jump">

Nuremberg sculptor Adam Kraft, self-portrait from <i>St Lorenz Church</i>, 1490s.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p><b>Adam Kraft</b> (or <b>Krafft</b>) (c. 1460?&#160;– January 1509) was a <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">German</a> stone <a href="/wiki/Sculpture" title="Sculpture">sculptor</a> and master <a href="/wiki/Architecture" title="Architecture">builder</a> of the late <a href="/wiki/Gothic_art" title="Gothic art">Gothic</a> period, based in <a href="/wiki/Nuremberg" title="Nuremberg">Nuremberg</a> and with a documented career there from 1490.</p>
<p>It is not known where Kraft was born and raised; his hand has been claimed to be evident as an assistant in works in <a href="/wiki/Ulm_Minster" title="Ulm Minster">Ulm Minster</a> (completed 1471) and the pulpit at <a href="/wiki/Strasbourg_Cathedral" title="Strasbourg Cathedral">Strasbourg Cathedral</a>, completed in 1485.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Kraft is believed to have married twice, but is not known to have produced any children. All his known works are in stone, but he may also have carved unidentified pieces in wood.</p>
<p>His <a href="/wiki/Masterpiece" title="Masterpiece">masterpiece</a> is considered to be the 18.7 meters (61 feet) tall <a href="/wiki/Church_tabernacle" title="Church tabernacle">tabernacle</a> at <a href="/wiki/St._Lorenz,_Nuremberg" title="St. Lorenz, Nuremberg">St. Lorenz, Nuremberg</a>. The tabernacle, that has the shape of a gothic tower reaching into the church's vault, is made up of tracery interspersed with figural scenes from Christ's Passion and was commissioned in 1493 by Hans Imhoff, a patrician from Nuremberg. The contract for the commission was preserved and stipulates details about the execution and finish of the work.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> The stone tower, which is supported by three figures, was lightly damaged during <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a> and restored afterwards. One of the supporting figures is a self-portrait by Kraft (at right). Another important work is a huge <a href="/wiki/Relief" title="Relief">relief</a> of 1490-92 depicting the <a href="/wiki/Crucifixion" title="Crucifixion">Crucifixion</a>, <a href="/wiki/Entombment_of_Christ" class="mw-redirect" title="Entombment of Christ">Entombment of Christ</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Resurrection_of_Christ" class="mw-redirect" title="Resurrection of Christ">Resurrection of Christ</a>, on the exterior of <a href="/wiki/St._Sebaldus_Church,_Nuremberg" title="St. Sebaldus Church, Nuremberg">St. Sebaldus Church, Nuremberg</a>.</p>
<p>Kraft is believed to have completed all of his sculpting work in Nuremberg and its environs in <a href="/wiki/Bavaria" title="Bavaria">Bavaria</a>, between the years 1490 and 1509, working with only a small complement of two or three assistants. His other significant works were the monumental reliefs in the various churches in Nuremberg. He produced the great Schreyer monument in 1492 for <a href="/wiki/St._Sebaldus_Church,_Nuremberg" title="St. Sebaldus Church, Nuremberg">St. Sebaldus Church</a> and <i>Christ bearing the Cross</i> above the altar of the same church. He also made various works for public and private buildings, such as the relief over the door of the Wagehaus, a <i><a href="/wiki/Saint_George_and_the_Dragon" title="Saint George and the Dragon">Saint George and the Dragon</a></i>, several <a href="/wiki/Madonna_(art)" title="Madonna (art)">Madonna (art)s</a>, and other purely decorative pieces. The great tabernacle, covered in statuettes, in <a href="/wiki/Ulm_Minster" title="Ulm Minster">Ulm Minster</a>, and the very spirited <i>Stations of the Cross</i>, on the road to the Nuremberg <a href="/wiki/Cemetery" title="Cemetery">cemetery</a>, are also his.<sup id="cite_ref-EB1911_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EB1911-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>He is buried in nearby <a href="/wiki/Schwabach" title="Schwabach">Schwabach</a>. Many of his pieces are exhibited at the Nuremberg museum, the <a href="/wiki/Germanisches_Nationalmuseum" title="Germanisches Nationalmuseum">Germanisches Nationalmuseum</a>.</p>
