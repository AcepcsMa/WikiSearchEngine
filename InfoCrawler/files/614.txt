<p><b>Nürnberg Hauptbahnhof</b><sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-lufthansa_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-lufthansa-5">[5]</a></sup> (<a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a> for <i>Nuremberg main station</i>) or <b>Nuremberg Central Station</b><sup id="cite_ref-lufthansa_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-lufthansa-5">[5]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> is the main railway station serving the city of <a href="/wiki/Nuremberg" title="Nuremberg">Nuremberg</a> in <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a>. It is the largest station in north <a href="/wiki/Bavaria" title="Bavaria">Bavaria</a> and belongs to the 20 stations in the highest <a href="/wiki/German_railway_station_categories" title="German railway station categories">category of importance</a> allocated by <a href="/wiki/DB_Station%26Service" title="DB Station&amp;Service">DB Station&amp;Service</a>.</p>
<p>It is a through station with 21 platforms and lies on major north–south and east–west transportation axes. It offers connections to the major German cities of <a href="/wiki/Leipzig" title="Leipzig">Leipzig</a>, <a href="/wiki/Berlin" title="Berlin">Berlin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Augsburg" title="Augsburg">Augsburg</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ingolstadt" title="Ingolstadt">Ingolstadt</a>, <a href="/wiki/Munich" title="Munich">Munich</a>, <a href="/wiki/W%C3%BCrzburg" title="Würzburg">Würzburg</a>, <a href="/wiki/Frankfurt" title="Frankfurt">Frankfurt</a> and <a href="/wiki/Regensburg" title="Regensburg">Regensburg</a>, as well as <a href="/wiki/Linz" title="Linz">Linz</a> and <a href="/wiki/Vienna" title="Vienna">Vienna</a> in <a href="/wiki/Austria" title="Austria">Austria</a> and <a href="/wiki/Prague" title="Prague">Prague</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Czech_Republic" title="Czech Republic">Czech Republic</a>. Over 450 trains stop here daily and more than 180,000 passengers use the station on average every day. It is also a major hub for public transport in Nuremberg.</p>
<p>The Hauptbahnhof is located on the southeastern perimeter of Nuremberg's <i><a href="/wiki/Altstadt" title="Altstadt">Altstadt</a></i>, immediately opposite the Königstor (King's Gate) where the streets of Marientorgraben, Frauentorgraben, and Bahnhofstraße meet. The <a href="/wiki/DB_Museum" class="mw-redirect" title="DB Museum">DB Museum</a>, the corporate museum of <a href="/wiki/Deutsche_Bahn_AG" class="mw-redirect" title="Deutsche Bahn AG">Deutsche Bahn AG</a> (formerly the <i>Verkehrsmuseum</i>), is close to the station, as is the <a href="/wiki/Staatstheater_N%C3%BCrnberg" title="Staatstheater Nürnberg">Staatstheater Nürnberg</a> opera house.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Location_in_the_railway_network">Location in the railway network</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nuremberg_Central_Station&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Location in the railway network">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Nuremberg_Aerial_Hauptbahnhof.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dc/Nuremberg_Aerial_Hauptbahnhof.JPG/220px-Nuremberg_Aerial_Hauptbahnhof.JPG" width="220" height="108" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dc/Nuremberg_Aerial_Hauptbahnhof.JPG/330px-Nuremberg_Aerial_Hauptbahnhof.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dc/Nuremberg_Aerial_Hauptbahnhof.JPG/440px-Nuremberg_Aerial_Hauptbahnhof.JPG 2x" data-file-width="2924" data-file-height="1436" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Nuremberg_Aerial_Hauptbahnhof.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Aerial photo 2009</div>

<p>Nürnberg Hauptbahnhof is an important hub in the south German railway network. Numerous lines meet here from all points of the compass. Amongst them is the <a href="/wiki/Nuremberg%E2%80%93Ingolstadt%E2%80%93Munich_high-speed_railway" class="mw-redirect" title="Nuremberg–Ingolstadt–Munich high-speed railway">Nuremberg–Ingolstadt–Munich high-speed railway</a>, opened in summer 2006 and which shortened journey times on many routes. The <i>Hauptbahnhof</i> will also be the departure point for the <a href="/wiki/Nuremberg%E2%80%93Erfurt_high-speed_railway" title="Nuremberg–Erfurt high-speed railway">Nuremberg–Erfurt high-speed line</a>, scheduled to open in December 2017.</p>
<p>West of another <a href="/wiki/F%C3%BCrth_(Bayern)_Hauptbahnhof" class="mw-redirect" title="Fürth (Bayern) Hauptbahnhof"><i>Hauptbahnhof</i> in neighbouring Fürth</a> the lines from <a href="/wiki/Nuremberg%E2%80%93W%C3%BCrzburg_railway" title="Nuremberg–Würzburg railway">Würzburg</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nuremberg%E2%80%93Bamberg_railway" title="Nuremberg–Bamberg railway">Bamberg</a> merge and then continue towards Nuremberg. In west Nuremberg they are joined by the railways from <a href="/wiki/Nuremberg%E2%80%93Crailsheim_railway" title="Nuremberg–Crailsheim railway">Crailsheim</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nuremberg%E2%80%93Roth_railway" title="Nuremberg–Roth railway">Roth</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nuremberg%E2%80%93Augsburg_railway" title="Nuremberg–Augsburg railway">Augsburg</a>. The four lines then enter together at the western approach to the station.</p>
<p>In southeast Nuremberg the <a href="/wiki/Nuremberg%E2%80%93Regensburg_railway" title="Nuremberg–Regensburg railway">line from Regensburg</a> and the high-speed link from Munich meet one another. At the eastern approach to the station they join the lines from <a href="/wiki/Nuremberg%E2%80%93Cheb_railway" title="Nuremberg–Cheb railway">Cheb</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nuremberg%E2%80%93Feucht_railway" title="Nuremberg–Feucht railway">Feucht</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nuremberg%E2%80%93Schwandorf_railway" title="Nuremberg–Schwandorf railway">Schwandorf</a>. They arrive together at the eastern end of the station.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Long-distance_services">Long-distance services</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nuremberg_Central_Station&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Long-distance services">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Hauptbahnhof is located at the intersection of various <a href="/wiki/Intercity-Express" title="Intercity-Express">Intercity-Express</a>, <a href="/wiki/Intercity_(Deutsche_Bahn)" title="Intercity (Deutsche Bahn)">InterCity</a> and <a href="/wiki/EuroCity" title="EuroCity">EuroCity</a> routes and is the only long-distance station in Nuremberg. These routes generally run to Nuremberg hourly, although some are more frequent as a result of overlapping services.</p>
<p>Long-distance trains travel from here to <a href="/wiki/Munich" title="Munich">Munich</a>, <a href="/wiki/Leipzig" title="Leipzig">Leipzig</a>, <a href="/wiki/Berlin" title="Berlin">Berlin</a>, into the <a href="/wiki/Rhine-Main_area" class="mw-redirect" title="Rhine-Main area">Rhine-Main</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Ruhrgebiet" class="mw-redirect" title="Ruhrgebiet">Ruhrgebiet</a>; to <a href="/wiki/Stuttgart" title="Stuttgart">Stuttgart</a>, <a href="/wiki/Karlsruhe" title="Karlsruhe">Karlsruhe</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hamburg" title="Hamburg">Hamburg</a> as well as <a href="/wiki/Passau" title="Passau">Passau</a> and <a href="/wiki/Vienna" title="Vienna">Vienna</a>. An <a href="/wiki/Interregio" class="mw-redirect" title="Interregio">Interregio-Express</a> link runs to <a href="/wiki/Chemnitz" title="Chemnitz">Chemnitz</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dresden" title="Dresden">Dresden</a>. <a href="/wiki/Sleeping_car" title="Sleeping car">Night trains</a> travel to various destinations including the <a href="/wiki/Baltic_Sea" title="Baltic Sea">Baltic Sea</a>, <a href="/wiki/Copenhagen" title="Copenhagen">Copenhagen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vienna" title="Vienna">Vienna</a>, <a href="/wiki/Prague" title="Prague">Prague</a> and <a href="/wiki/Moscow" title="Moscow">Moscow</a>.</p>
<p>Almost all long-distance trains travelling from Munich to the north of Germany run in combination to Nuremberg over the high-speed link and then divide up from here. So even for these trunk services there is a frequent service between the two cities. Once the high-speed link between Nuremberg and Leipzig is completed this one, and hence the link to Berlin, will also be considerably faster.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Local_services">Local services</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nuremberg_Central_Station&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Local services">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>Local services using <a href="/wiki/Regional-Express" title="Regional-Express">Regional-Express</a> and <a href="/wiki/Regionalbahn" title="Regionalbahn">Regionalbahn</a> trains mainly serve the Nuremberg area, but also travel to other parts of Bavaria as well as <a href="/wiki/Baden-W%C3%BCrttemberg" title="Baden-Württemberg">Baden-Württemberg</a>, <a href="/wiki/Thuringia" title="Thuringia">Thuringia</a> and into the <a href="/wiki/Czech_Republic" title="Czech Republic">Czech Republic</a> to <a href="/wiki/Prague" title="Prague">Prague</a>. Almost all lines are run at fixed frequencies, mainly hourly. More frequent services are laid on especially at peak times. The <a href="/wiki/Munich-Nuremberg_Express" class="mw-redirect" title="Munich-Nuremberg Express">Munich-Nuremberg Express</a> provides a fast local service between the two cities over the high-speed link to Ingolstadt and Munich, offering a cheaper alternative to the ICE.</p>
<p>Two of the four <a href="/wiki/Nuremberg_S-Bahn" title="Nuremberg S-Bahn">S-Bahn lines</a> also start from the Hauptbahnhof. The S-Bahn uses platforms 2 and 3 immediately next to the station building which significantly reduces changeover times to the city’s public transport systems.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="City_services">City services</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nuremberg_Central_Station&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: City services">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<div class="thumbcaption">

<p>Below the station is the Hauptbahnhof’s underground station on the <a href="/wiki/Nuremberg_U-Bahn" title="Nuremberg U-Bahn">Nuremberg U-Bahn</a>, one of the two crossing stations on the <i>U1</i>, <i>U2</i> and <i>U3</i> underground lines. The <i>U1</i> line goes to the Nuremberg <i>Messe</i> (exhibition hall) amongst others, line <i>U2</i> links the Hauptbahnhof with <a href="/wiki/N%C3%BCrnberg_Nordost_railway_station" class="mw-redirect" title="Nürnberg Nordost railway station">Nuremberg&#160;Nordost station</a>, the departure point for the <i><a href="/wiki/Gr%C3%A4fenberg_Railway" title="Gräfenberg Railway">Gräfenberg Railway</a></i> to <a href="/wiki/Franconian_Switzerland" title="Franconian Switzerland">Franconian Switzerland</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nuremberg_Airport" title="Nuremberg Airport">Nuremberg Airport</a>. On the station square are stops for the various city <a href="/wiki/Stadtbahn" title="Stadtbahn">tram</a> and bus lines. At weekends and on public holidays the station square is the meeting place and central changeover hub for the <i>NightLiner</i> night bus line network. East of that, about 200 m away, is the central <a href="/wiki/Bus_station" title="Bus station">bus station</a>, reopened in 2005 after being rebuilt. From here there are also international bus services to various European countries.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Infrastructure">Infrastructure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nuremberg_Central_Station&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Infrastructure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Facilities">Facilities</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nuremberg_Central_Station&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Facilities">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Because of its importance for long-distance and local services the station has a comprehensive range of facilities.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Deutsche_Bahn" title="Deutsche Bahn">Deutsche Bahn</a> has a travel centre and <i>Servicepoint</i> in the central hall (<i>Mittelhalle</i>). On the first floor is a DB lounge for <a href="/wiki/BahnCard#bahn.comfort" title="BahnCard">bahn.comfort</a> and 1st class passengers. Numerous electronic signboards in the halls and on the platforms inform passengers about the latest traffic situation, and recorded announcements are played automatically. Most of the station building and platforms have step-free access. All floors of the station building can be reached by escalators and lifts. The <a href="/w/index.php?title=Bahnhofsmission&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Bahnhofsmission (page does not exist)">Bahnhofsmission</a> at Nuremberg Hauptbahnhof is like a free first aid and support centre run by Christian charities and is located in the basement of the station ready to give advice and support to passengers, the homeless and those working at the station. On <a href="/wiki/Christmas_Eve" title="Christmas Eve">Christmas Eve</a> and <a href="/wiki/Maundy_Thursday" title="Maundy Thursday">Maundy Thursday</a> a service with live music is held in the central hall on the 1st floor.</p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Nuernberg-Hauptbahnhof_1.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/41/Nuernberg-Hauptbahnhof_1.JPG/220px-Nuernberg-Hauptbahnhof_1.JPG" width="220" height="142" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/41/Nuernberg-Hauptbahnhof_1.JPG/330px-Nuernberg-Hauptbahnhof_1.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/41/Nuernberg-Hauptbahnhof_1.JPG/440px-Nuernberg-Hauptbahnhof_1.JPG 2x" data-file-width="1402" data-file-height="908" /></a>

Taxicab stand</div>

<p>Numerous businesses for everyday requirements occupy about 20,000 square metres of floor in the station building; these include shops selling travel items, restaurants and fast-food outlets. A total of 55 shops are rented out. More shops and businesses are located in the <i>Königstor</i> passage adjoining the station.</p>
<p>At the beginning of 1999 a <a href="/wiki/Multi-storey_car_park" title="Multi-storey car park">multi-storey car park</a> was opened next to the east hall (<i>Osthalle</i>) with 487 bays. Deutsche Bahn initiated the planning, an insurance company provided the necessary investment.<sup id="cite_ref-etr-1999-177_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-etr-1999-177-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the Osthalle there is a service point for the InterCity courier service (<i>IC-Kurierdienst</i>) and a luggage locker room with 766 lockers. On the station forecourt there is a <a href="/wiki/Taxicab_stand" title="Taxicab stand">taxicab stand</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Signal_box">Signal box</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nuremberg_Central_Station&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Signal box">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>At the end of November 1988 a new <a href="/wiki/Signal_box" class="mw-redirect" title="Signal box">central signal box</a> went into service at Nürnberg Hauptbahnhof. At each end of the station there is a train dispatcher (<i>Fahrdienstleiter</i>) and assistant (<i>Fahrdiensthelfer</i>) to control the western and eastern approaches, supported by a train monitor (<i>Zugmelder</i>), a train announcer (<i>Zugansager</i>) and a <a href="/wiki/Pointsman" class="mw-redirect" title="Pointsman">pointsman</a> (<i>Wärter</i>) for the adjoining coach works. Its area of operations extends as far as Fürth and the station at Schweinau to the west, to Eibach station to the south and the stations of Dutzendteich and Mögeldorf to the east.<sup id="cite_ref-bundesbahn-1989-1-108_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bundesbahn-1989-1-108-13">[13]</a></sup> In all, 38.3 million <a href="/wiki/Deutschmarks" class="mw-redirect" title="Deutschmarks">deutschmarks</a> were invested. 800 train movements and 2,900 shunting movements are handled per day in the <i>Hauptbahnhof</i> area. Planning began in the 1970s, not just for rationalisation, but also to set out the requirements for the planned high-speed railway to Munich. Shortly before the signal box was opened, a new, million deutschmark, departure board went into service in the <i>Mittelhalle</i>, that was controlled by the new signal box using computers.<sup id="cite_ref-bundesbahn-1989-1-108_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bundesbahn-1989-1-108-13">[13]</a></sup> The new signal box replaced ten old ones that were about 70 years old; three more followed suit as part of the S-Bahn extension in the early 1990s. After the removal of these unfavourably sited, old signal boxes, which were not capable of expansion, five groups of storage sidings could be merged into one.</p>
<p>Around 1900, 116 railwaymen per shift worked the points and signals for about one hundred trains on the spot. In 1913, 122 trains stopped here daily, there were 40 workers per shift controlling the station from 14 signal boxes.<sup id="cite_ref-bundesbahn-1989-1-108_13-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bundesbahn-1989-1-108-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Architecture">Architecture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nuremberg_Central_Station&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Architecture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:N%C3%BCrnberg_Hauptbahnhof_bei_Nacht.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3f/N%C3%BCrnberg_Hauptbahnhof_bei_Nacht.jpg/220px-N%C3%BCrnberg_Hauptbahnhof_bei_Nacht.jpg" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3f/N%C3%BCrnberg_Hauptbahnhof_bei_Nacht.jpg/330px-N%C3%BCrnberg_Hauptbahnhof_bei_Nacht.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3f/N%C3%BCrnberg_Hauptbahnhof_bei_Nacht.jpg/440px-N%C3%BCrnberg_Hauptbahnhof_bei_Nacht.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1600" data-file-height="1200" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:N%C3%BCrnberg_Hauptbahnhof_bei_Nacht.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Middle section of Nuremberg Hauptbahnhof at night</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Nürnberg Hauptbahnhof, which had been originally built as in the <a href="/wiki/Neo-Gothic" class="mw-redirect" title="Neo-Gothic">neo-Gothic</a> style, was rebuilt by the architect, Karl Zenger, in 1900 largely in the <a href="/wiki/Baroque_Revival_architecture" title="Baroque Revival architecture">Neo-Baroque</a> style. The most striking feature is the <a href="/wiki/Muschelkalk" title="Muschelkalk">muschelkalk</a> which characterises the exterior façade. The portals to the individual halls are richly decorated and primarily depict symbols of technological progress, for example a winged wheel above the portal in the <i>Mittelhalle</i>. The lounge, in which the present-day travel centre is located, was built in 1904/1905 by <a href="/wiki/Bruno_Paul" title="Bruno Paul">Bruno Paul</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Jugendstil" class="mw-redirect" title="Jugendstil">Jugendstil</a>. Sections of the walls are decorated with fine <a href="/wiki/Mosaics" class="mw-redirect" title="Mosaics">mosaics</a>, the roof is ornamented with unobtrusive <a href="/wiki/Stucco" title="Stucco">stucco</a>. The Jugendstil lounge is one of the few areas of the station, which has survived the destruction of the Second World War. Above the main portal is an advertising column from the early 20th century.</p>
<p>In 1950 plans were made to change the neobaroque style to a neogothic style. Shortly before work began, however, it was stopped, so that only a few areas were changed.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nuremberg_Central_Station&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="From_opening_to_the_first_rebuilding">From opening to the first rebuilding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nuremberg_Central_Station&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: From opening to the first rebuilding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Between 1844 and 1847 Nürnberg Hauptbahnhof appeared in its present location during the construction of the state-owned <a href="/wiki/Ludwig_South-North_Railway" title="Ludwig South-North Railway">Ludwig South-North Railway</a> from <a href="/wiki/Lindau_(Bodensee)" class="mw-redirect" title="Lindau (Bodensee)">Lindau</a> to <a href="/wiki/Hof_(Saale)" class="mw-redirect" title="Hof (Saale)">Hof</a>. Because the area at Plärrer, the site of the station belonging to the privately owned <a href="/wiki/Bavarian_Ludwigsbahn" class="mw-redirect" title="Bavarian Ludwigsbahn">Ludwigsbahn</a> to Fürth, was not big enough, the state railway decided to build its own station in front of the <i>Frauentorgraben</i>. It was laid out as a terminal, something which was usual for the larges stations of that time. The station building was built in a <a href="/wiki/Neogothic" class="mw-redirect" title="Neogothic">neogothic</a> style. With the opening of the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Bavarian_State_Railways" title="Royal Bavarian State Railways">state railway lines</a> to <a href="/wiki/Schwabach" title="Schwabach">Schwabach</a> (1849) <a href="/wiki/Ansbach" title="Ansbach">Ansbach</a>, (1875) and <a href="/wiki/Bayreuth" title="Bayreuth">Bayreuth</a> (1877) as well as the <a href="/wiki/Bavarian_Eastern_Railway_Company" title="Bavarian Eastern Railway Company">Ostbahn lines</a> to <a href="/wiki/Hersbruck" title="Hersbruck">Hersbruck</a> (1859) and <a href="/wiki/Regensburg" title="Regensburg">Regensburg</a> (1871) the station became the central station for Nuremberg. The Ostbahn company built its own terminus in 1859 during the construction of the line to Hersbruck, east of the existing one, which was linked in 1876 with the western one by a through station.</p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:N%C3%BCrnberg_Hauptbahnhof_um_1900.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e4/N%C3%BCrnberg_Hauptbahnhof_um_1900.jpg/220px-N%C3%BCrnberg_Hauptbahnhof_um_1900.jpg" width="220" height="140" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e4/N%C3%BCrnberg_Hauptbahnhof_um_1900.jpg/330px-N%C3%BCrnberg_Hauptbahnhof_um_1900.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e4/N%C3%BCrnberg_Hauptbahnhof_um_1900.jpg/440px-N%C3%BCrnberg_Hauptbahnhof_um_1900.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2193" data-file-height="1400" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:N%C3%BCrnberg_Hauptbahnhof_um_1900.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The Station after the first rebuilding (1900–1906)</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Rising demand for railway traffic during the 1880s and the ever-growing number of tracks made the construction of the subways, which are still there today, necessary. Gradually the various underpasses - the <i>Tafelfeld</i>, <i>Karl-Bröger</i>, <i>Marien</i> and <i>Dürrenhof</i> tunnels as well as the <i>Celtis</i> and <i>Allersberg</i> subways were built. Between 1878 and 1880 the trackage for passenger traffic was again expanded and in 1880 goods traffic moved to the newly built Nürnberg Hauptgüterbahnhof (main goods station) at Kohlenhof. As early as 1897 new plans for the conversion of the <i>Hauptbahnhof</i> were proposed, which included rebuilding the station building, raising the entire track system by about 3.27&#160;m and building pedestrian underpasses (the <i>West</i> and <i>Ost</i> tunnels) under the tracks. Building work began on 19&#160;April 1900 and was finished on 10&#160;March 1906, the <i>Westtunnel</i> and the southern exit was not completed until 1927, however.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Destruction_and_rebuilding">Destruction and rebuilding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nuremberg_Central_Station&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Destruction and rebuilding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>As a result of allied bombing at the end of the <a href="/wiki/Second_World_War" class="mw-redirect" title="Second World War">Second World War</a> the station building, with the exception of the Jugendstil lounge suffered heavily and on 16&#160;March 1945 had to be closed for nine days. Its reconstruction took place between 1945 and 1956 and had to be simplified due to a lack of money. One new feature was the inclusion of a <a href="/wiki/Movie_theater" title="Movie theater">cinema</a>. In 1973 work began on an underground (U-Bahn) station underneath the <i>Hauptbahnhof</i>. This required the <i>Mittelhalle</i> to be dug out and stood on stilts. Between 1976 and 1984 new platform roofing was installed and platforms 1 to 15 raised to 76&#160;cm above the rail tops. The rebuilding of the third dome and the <i>Mittelhalle</i> was started in 1977. On 2&#160;April 1984 the restaurant established in 1906 was opened again. It covers an area of 390&#160;m², and is eight metres high.<sup id="cite_ref-etr-1984-717_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-etr-1984-717-14">[14]</a></sup> Finally plans were even proposed for a total rebuilding, but these did not come to fruition.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Rebuilding_of_the_eastern_approach_for_the_S-Bahn">Rebuilding of the eastern approach for the S-Bahn</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nuremberg_Central_Station&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Rebuilding of the eastern approach for the S-Bahn">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In summer 1988 major building work began to construct the main route of the Nuremberg S-Bahn in the area of the <i>Hauptbahnhof</i>. By building its own S-Bahn platforms in the northeast part of the <i>Hauptbahnhof</i> the S-Bahn stop could be moved from platforms 18/19 (on the far side from the city) to platforms 2/3. By using these platforms closer to the city the distance passengers had to walk between tramway, U-Bahn and Bus was significantly reduced. In addition the new S-Bahn station of Nuremberg-Dürrenhof was built.<sup id="cite_ref-bundesbahn-1988-7-655_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bundesbahn-1988-7-655-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>The rebuilding of the eastern track system, carried out in eight stages, cost around 100 million <a href="/wiki/Deutsche_Mark" title="Deutsche Mark">D-Marks</a> and was paid for by the Government, the state of Bavaria and the city of Nuremberg. Amongst other things, 16 kilometres of track and 60 points covering an area of 156,000 square metres were relaid, and two new fans of storage sidings (totalling 5.7&#160;km) controlled by a shunting signal box were built. To enable the line to Lauf to cross the line to Altdorf without a track crossing being required, a 60-metre-long (200&#160;ft) bridge crossing was built. In all ten bridges had to be altered or replaced and 640&#160;m of sound-damping walls were erected. The only examples in Europe of the wild flower, <i>Astragalus arenarius</i>, a plant related to the milk vetch, along with other groups of plants was transferred to a temporary nursery and later to areas around the railway. The conversion of the area with the platforms cost another 40 million D-Marks.<sup id="cite_ref-bundesbahn-1988-7-655_15-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bundesbahn-1988-7-655-15">[15]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Modernisation_at_the_turn_of_the_millennium">Modernisation at the turn of the millennium</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nuremberg_Central_Station&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Modernisation at the turn of the millennium">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>At the end of the 20th century the last major changes took place. The cinema was removed and the entire interior reworked. The intermediate floors of the station building were opened to the public and the whole area turned into a shopping mall. Ticket purchase and information was moved to the historic Jugendstil lounge. Three wall mosaics were made by the artist, Iris Rauh. The mosaic <i>Zeitreise</i> ("time journey"), which portrays the subject of travel during the course of time, became nationally noted. On 24&#160;June 2002 the opening ceremony of the station building, now placed under historical building protection, took place.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Planning">Planning</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nuremberg_Central_Station&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Planning">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:N%C3%BCrnberg_Hbf_TRS_2.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/14/N%C3%BCrnberg_Hbf_TRS_2.jpg/220px-N%C3%BCrnberg_Hbf_TRS_2.jpg" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/14/N%C3%BCrnberg_Hbf_TRS_2.jpg/330px-N%C3%BCrnberg_Hbf_TRS_2.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/14/N%C3%BCrnberg_Hbf_TRS_2.jpg/440px-N%C3%BCrnberg_Hbf_TRS_2.jpg 2x" data-file-width="4368" data-file-height="2912" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:N%C3%BCrnberg_Hbf_TRS_2.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Future will the S-Bahn to Neumarkt by platform 1 stop</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>As a result of the expansion of the Nuremberg S-Bahn network in the period to 2010, work needs to be carried out on the existing S-Bahn platforms and new ones need to be built. The eastern end of the home platform (no. 1) of the future S-Bahn to Neumarkt (S3) is being lengthened; it will then have 213&#160;m of usable length and a new height of 76&#160;cm above the rail tops. An extra exit to the <i>Osttunnel</i> is also being constructed.<sup id="cite_ref-DB-PB_S3_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DB-PB_S3-16">[16]</a></sup> For through services on the Hartmannshof – Nuremberg – Forchheim (S1) line, platforms 2 and 3 are being partially lowered to 76&#160;cm and linked by a ramp to the unchanged 96&#160;cm high existing structure.<sup id="cite_ref-DB-PB_S1_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DB-PB_S1-17">[17]</a></sup> The platform intended for the S-Bahn to Ansbach (S4), number 11 (tracks 22 and 23) will be raised from its present height of 38&#160;cm to 76&#160;cm; at the same time stairs and a lift will be provided and a new platform roof.<sup id="cite_ref-DB-PB_S4_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DB-PB_S4-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<div style="clear:right;"></div>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Operational_usage">Operational usage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nuremberg_Central_Station&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Operational usage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The station's footprint is rather large, numbered passenger tracks reach as far as 22. The station serves the entire <a href="/wiki/Nuremberg_U-Bahn" title="Nuremberg U-Bahn">Nuremberg U-Bahn</a> network, as all four lines interchange at the station together with the U3 which opened in 2008. A tram stop is situated in front of the station.</p>
