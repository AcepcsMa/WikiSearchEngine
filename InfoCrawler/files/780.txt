			<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en"><i>Catholic Encyclopedia</i></h1>

<p><i><b>The Catholic Encyclopedia: An International Work of Reference on the Constitution, Doctrine, Discipline, and History of the Catholic Church</b></i>,<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> also referred to as the <i><b>Old Catholic Encyclopedia</b></i> and the <i><b>Original Catholic Encyclopedia</b></i>,<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> is an English-language encyclopedia published in the United States. The first volume appeared in March 1907 and the last three volumes appeared in 1912, followed by a master index volume in 1914 and later supplementary volumes. It was designed "to give its readers full and authoritative information on the entire cycle of <a href="/wiki/Catholic" class="mw-redirect" title="Catholic">Catholic</a> interests, action and doctrine".<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <i>Catholic Encyclopedia</i> was published by the <a href="/wiki/Robert_Appleton_Company" class="mw-redirect" title="Robert Appleton Company">Robert Appleton Company</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Publishing_company" class="mw-redirect" title="Publishing company">publishing company</a> incorporated at <a href="/wiki/New_York_(state)" title="New York (state)">New York</a> in February 1905 for the express purpose of publishing the encyclopedia. The five members of the encyclopedia's Editorial Board also served as the directors of the company. In 1912 the company's name was changed to The Encyclopedia Press. Publication of the encyclopedia's volumes was the sole business conducted by the company during the project's lifetime.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Purpose">Purpose</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Catholic_Encyclopedia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Purpose">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The encyclopedia was designed to serve the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic_Church" class="mw-redirect" title="Roman Catholic Church">Roman Catholic Church</a>, concentrating on information related to the Church and explaining matters from the Catholic point of view. It records the accomplishments of Catholics and others in nearly all intellectual and professional pursuits, including artists, educators, poets and scientists. While more limited in focus than other general encyclopedias, it was far broader in scope than previous efforts at comprehensive Catholic encyclopedias, which had studied only internal Church affairs.</p>
<p>It offers in-depth portrayals of historical and philosophical ideas, persons and events, from a Catholic perspective, including issues that divide Catholicism from <a href="/wiki/Protestantism" title="Protestantism">Protestantism</a> and other faith communities. Since the encyclopedia was first published starting in 1907 and has never been updated (versus the <i><a href="/wiki/New_Catholic_Encyclopedia" title="New Catholic Encyclopedia">New Catholic Encyclopedia</a></i>), many of its entries may be out of date either with respect to the wider culture or to the Catholic <a href="/wiki/Ecclesiastical" class="mw-redirect" title="Ecclesiastical">ecclesiastical</a> world. In particular, it predates the creation of the <a href="/wiki/Vatican_City_State" class="mw-redirect" title="Vatican City State">Vatican City State</a> (1929) and the <a href="/wiki/Second_Vatican_Council" title="Second Vatican Council">Second Vatican Council</a> (1962–1965), which introduced many significant changes in Catholic practice: For example, the online version of the entries on <a href="/wiki/Judaism" title="Judaism">Judaism</a> and <a href="/wiki/Islam" title="Islam">Islam</a> at newadvent.org states in an editorial note: "To complement this article, which was taken from the 1910 Catholic Encyclopedia, New Advent recommends a prayerful reading of 'Nostra Aetate' from the Second Vatican Council."<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Catholic_Encyclopedia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The writing of the encyclopedia began on January 11, 1905, under the supervision of five editors:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Charles_George_Herbermann" title="Charles George Herbermann">Charles G. Herbermann</a>, Professor of <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> and librarian of the <a href="/wiki/City_College_of_New_York" title="City College of New York">College of the City of New York</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Edward_A._Pace" title="Edward A. Pace">Edward A. Pace</a>, Professor of Philosophy at <a href="/wiki/The_Catholic_University_of_America" class="mw-redirect" title="The Catholic University of America">The Catholic University of America</a> in Washington, D.C.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Cond%C3%A9_Benoist_Pallen" title="Condé Benoist Pallen">Condé B. Pallen</a>, editor</li>
<li>The Rev. <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Joseph_Shahan" title="Thomas Joseph Shahan">Thomas J. Shahan</a>, Professor of <a href="/wiki/History_of_the_Catholic_Church" title="History of the Catholic Church">Church History</a> at the Catholic University</li>
<li>The Rev. John J. Wynne, <a href="/wiki/Jesuit" class="mw-redirect" title="Jesuit">S.J.</a>, editor of <i><a href="/wiki/Messenger_of_the_Sacred_Heart" title="Messenger of the Sacred Heart">Messenger of the Sacred Heart</a></i></li>
</ul>

<p>The editors had their first editorial meeting at the office of <i>The Messenger</i>, on West 16th Street, <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a>. The text received a <i><a href="/wiki/Nihil_obstat" title="Nihil obstat">nihil obstat</a></i> from an official censor, Remy Lafort, on November 1, 1908 and an <a href="/wiki/Imprimatur" title="Imprimatur">imprimatur</a> from <a href="/wiki/John_Murphy_Farley" title="John Murphy Farley">John Murphy Farley</a>, <a href="/wiki/Archbishop_of_New_York" class="mw-redirect" title="Archbishop of New York">Archbishop of New York</a>. This review process was presumably accelerated by the reuse of older authorized publications. In addition to frequent informal conferences and constant communication by letters, the editors subsequently held 134 formal meetings to consider the plan, scope and progress of the work, culminating in publication on April 19, 1913. A first supplement was published in 1922; a second supplement in nine loose-leaf sections was published by The Gilmary Society between 1950 and 1958.</p>
<p>In 1912, a special completely illustrated commemorative volume was awarded to those patrons who contributed to the start of the enterprise by buying multiple encyclopedia sets early on.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>There was controversy over the presence of the <i>Catholic Encyclopedia</i> in public libraries in the United States with <a href="/wiki/Nativism_(politics)#Nativism_in_the_United_States" title="Nativism (politics)">nativist</a> protests that this violated the <a href="/wiki/Separation_of_church_and_state" title="Separation of church and state">separation of church and state</a>, including a successful appeal in <a href="/wiki/Belleville,_New_Jersey" title="Belleville, New Jersey">Belleville, New Jersey</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>The encyclopedia was later updated under the auspices of <a href="/wiki/The_Catholic_University_of_America" class="mw-redirect" title="The Catholic University of America">The Catholic University of America</a> and a 17-volume <i><a href="/wiki/New_Catholic_Encyclopedia" title="New Catholic Encyclopedia">New Catholic Encyclopedia</a></i> was first published in 1967, and then in 2002.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Authors_and_sources">Authors and sources</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Catholic_Encyclopedia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Authors and sources">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><i>The Catholic Encyclopedia and its makers</i> states that:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>The work is entirely new, and not merely a translation or a compilation from other encyclopedic sources. The editors have insisted that the articles should contain the latest and most accurate information to be obtained from the standard works on each subject.</p>
</blockquote>
<p>However, "from standard works" allows that some of the articles from European contributors such as <a href="/wiki/Pierre_Batiffol" title="Pierre Batiffol">Pierre Batiffol</a> (French), <a href="/wiki/Johann_Peter_Kirsch" title="Johann Peter Kirsch">Johann Peter Kirsch</a> (German) had previously been published in whole or in part in Europe and were translated and edited for the Encyclopedia.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Those who wrote new articles in English include <a href="/wiki/Anthony_Maas" title="Anthony Maas">Anthony Maas</a> and <a href="/wiki/Herbert_Thurston" title="Herbert Thurston">Herbert Thurston</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Online_versions">Online versions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Catholic_Encyclopedia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Online versions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Under <a href="/wiki/United_States_copyright_law" class="mw-redirect" title="United States copyright law">United States copyright law</a>, all works published in the United States before 1923 are in the <a href="/wiki/Public_domain" title="Public domain">public domain</a>. In 1993, Kevin Knight, then a 26-year-old resident of <a href="/wiki/Denver,_Colorado" class="mw-redirect" title="Denver, Colorado">Denver, Colorado</a>, decided, during the visit of <a href="/wiki/Pope_John_Paul_II" title="Pope John Paul II">Pope John Paul II</a> to that city for <a href="/wiki/World_Youth_Day" title="World Youth Day">World Youth Day</a>, to launch a project to publish the 1913 edition of the encyclopedia on the Internet. Knight founded the website <a href="/wiki/New_Advent" title="New Advent">New Advent</a> to house the undertaking. Volunteers from the United States, Canada, France, and <a href="/wiki/Brazil" title="Brazil">Brazil</a> helped in the transcription of the original material. The site went online in 1995, and transcription work ended in 1997.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[Volumes 1]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2007, <a href="/wiki/Catholic_Answers" title="Catholic Answers">Catholic Answers</a> published a watermarked version derived from page scans. This version has since been replaced with a transcription of the <i>Encyclopedia</i> similar to that found at the New Advent website.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[Volumes 2]</a></sup> The Catholic Answers transcription, however, is an exact transcription of the original text, whereas the New Advent version at times modernizes certain words (e.g., using the names of Old Testament books found in modern Bibles, such as "1 &amp; 2 Chronicles" and "Obadiah", in place of the <a href="/wiki/Vulgate" title="Vulgate">Vulgate</a>/<a href="/wiki/Douay%E2%80%93Rheims_Bible" title="Douay–Rheims Bible">Douay–Rheims</a> titles, such as "1 &amp; 2 Paralipomenon" and "Abdias") and Biblical citation formatting (i.e., the Catholic Answers version retains the original's usage of Roman numerals for chapter numbers [e.g., Genesis i, 1], while the New Advent version uses Arabic numerals throughout [e.g., Genesis 1:1]).</p>
<p>Other scanned copies of the 1913 Encyclopedia are available on <a href="/wiki/Google_Books" title="Google Books">Google Books</a>, at the <a href="/wiki/Internet_Archive" title="Internet Archive">Internet Archive</a>, and at <a href="/wiki/Wikimedia_Commons" title="Wikimedia Commons">Wikimedia Commons</a>. <a href="/wiki/Wikisource" title="Wikisource">Wikisource</a> also hosts a transcription project backed by the scans hosted at Commons.<sup id="cite_ref-list_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-list-13">[Volumes 3]</a></sup></p>
<p>The 1922 supplement to the Encyclopedia is also in the public domain and is available online. The <i>New Catholic Encyclopedia</i> also is available online at some libraries.</p>
