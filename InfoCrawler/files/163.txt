<p><i><b>Oxenfree</b></i> is a 2016 <a href="/wiki/Supernatural_fiction" title="Supernatural fiction">supernatural</a> <a href="/wiki/Mystery_fiction" title="Mystery fiction">mystery</a> <a href="/wiki/Graphic_adventure_game" class="mw-redirect" title="Graphic adventure game">graphic adventure</a> <a href="/wiki/Video_game" title="Video game">video game</a> developed and published by <a href="/wiki/Night_School_Studio" title="Night School Studio">Night School Studio</a>. The game was released for <a href="/wiki/Microsoft_Windows" title="Microsoft Windows">Microsoft Windows</a>, <a href="/wiki/OS_X" class="mw-redirect" title="OS X">OS X</a> and <a href="/wiki/Xbox_One" title="Xbox One">Xbox One</a> in January 2016; the game was released on <a href="/wiki/PlayStation_4" title="PlayStation 4">PlayStation 4</a> and <a href="/wiki/Linux" title="Linux">Linux</a> later in 2016 and on <a href="/wiki/IOS" title="IOS">iOS</a> and <a href="/wiki/Android_(operating_system)" title="Android (operating system)">Android</a> in 2017. In <i>Oxenfree</i>, players assume the role of teenager Alex on a weekend trip to a local island. There, seemingly supernatural events occur and Alex and her friends must unravel the secrets of the island.</p>
<p><i>Oxenfree</i> is developer Night School Studio's first game. Influenced by classic teen movies and <a href="/wiki/Coming-of-age_story" title="Coming-of-age story">coming-of-age shows</a>, the developers wanted to create a story-driven game without cutscenes and allowing players to freely roam the environment. The game's audiovisual presentation marries dark, organic, and analog elements with sharp, distorted, and digital ones. <i>Oxenfree</i> received generally positive reviews on release, with critics praising the presentation and characters, although some reviewers were left wanting more.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Gameplay">Gameplay</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Oxenfree&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Gameplay">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Oxenfree_overworld.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/6/68/Oxenfree_overworld.png/220px-Oxenfree_overworld.png" width="220" height="124" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/6/68/Oxenfree_overworld.png/330px-Oxenfree_overworld.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/6/68/Oxenfree_overworld.png/440px-Oxenfree_overworld.png 2x" data-file-width="540" data-file-height="304" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Oxenfree_overworld.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Alex (left) and <a href="/wiki/Non-player_character" title="Non-player character">non-player characters</a> Jonas and Ren converse while Alex tunes the radio. The player has three possible dialogue options visible.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p><i>Oxenfree</i> is a graphic adventure played from a <a href="/wiki/2.5D#2.5D_platform_games" title="2.5D">2.5D</a> perspective.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> The player controls the protagonist Alex, a teenager visiting a local island with a group of friends.<sup id="cite_ref-ign-review_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ign-review-2">[2]</a></sup> After accidentally unleashing a paranormal force on the island, Alex and company must figure out what the force is and how to stop it.<sup id="cite_ref-gamespot-review_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gamespot-review-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p><i>Oxenfree</i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span>s gameplay is built around the "walk and talk". Instead of dialogue occurring during cutscenes, <a href="/wiki/Speech_bubbles" class="mw-redirect" title="Speech bubbles">speech bubbles</a> appear over Alex's head, allowing the player to choose between two or three options what Alex will say next in the conversation. At the same time, Alex remains free to move around and navigate the game world.<sup id="cite_ref-ing-preview_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ing-preview-4">[4]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-metro-preview_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-metro-preview-5">[5]</a></sup> Players can select dialogue options at any time during conversations, choosing to wait for other characters to finish, choosing to interrupt, or remaining silent. Certain dialogue options cause a thought bubble with Alex inside it to appear over characters' heads, suggesting that the player's choice may have had an effect on the characters' relationship.<sup id="cite_ref-metro-preview_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-metro-preview-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Objects that can be interacted with in the game world display a small circle next to them.<sup id="cite_ref-gamespot-review_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gamespot-review-3">[3]</a></sup> Puzzles in the game are solved by finding the correct frequency on Alex's handheld radio, which can do everything from unlock doors to communicate with ghosts,<sup id="cite_ref-verge-review_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-verge-review-6">[6]</a></sup> and by winding up tape recorders at the correct speed.<sup id="cite_ref-gamespot-review_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gamespot-review-3">[3]</a></sup> <i>Oxenfree</i> does not have any "<a href="/wiki/Game_over" title="Game over">game over</a>" loss conditions; the player's choices and relationships with the characters determine which of several possible endings the player receives.<sup id="cite_ref-vice-ghostlytruth_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-vice-ghostlytruth-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Plot">Plot</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Oxenfree&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Plot">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Alex (<a href="/wiki/Erin_Yvette" title="Erin Yvette">Erin Yvette</a>), a teenager, is on the last ferry heading to the fictional Edwards Island for a weekend party. She is accompanied by Ren (Aaron Kuban), her <a href="/wiki/Cannabis_culture" title="Cannabis culture">stoner</a> friend, and Jonas (Gavin Hammon), her new stepbrother. On the island, Alex and company meet Clarissa (Avital Ash), Alex's late brother Michael's ex-girlfriend, and Nona (Brittani Johnson), Clarissa's best friend and Ren's love interest. Ren notes that the island's only permanent resident, Maggie Adler, has recently died. After camping on the beach, Alex, Ren, and Jonas explore the nearby caves, where it is rumored certain radio frequencies cause supernatural events. In a small cavern, Alex tunes her radio and unexpectedly forms a rift. A voice answers and the teens experience visions before passing out. Jonas and Alex awake in front of Harden Tower. Ren calls, having passed out in the woods, while Clarissa calls from the decommissioned military fort on the island.</p>
<p>In the woods, Jonas and Alex encounter supernatural events, including Alex's reflection in the water communicating with her, objects moving on their own, and time looping repeatedly. Time corrects when Alex plays a <a href="/wiki/Magnetophon" title="Magnetophon">magnetophon</a>. They spot Nona, who claims she saw an alternate Jonas and Alex. Alex and Jonas find Ren, but after another time loop they find him <a href="/wiki/Spirit_possession" title="Spirit possession">possessed</a> by a "ghost". Using the radio, Alex creates another rift which returns Ren to normal. Ren and Nona return to the tower.</p>
<p>At the military fort, Alex's reflection appears in a mirror, giving Alex advice. A ghost then questions them and possesses Jonas, revealing that the ghosts are the passengers of the submarine USS <i>Kanaloa</i>, thought lost at sea. Alex creates another rift that revives Jonas. They find Clarissa, but time loops to her jumping to her death, and then disappearing. Nona, Alex, Jonas and Ren regroup at Harden Tower. Unable to send a radio transmission off the island, Ren suggests finding the key to Maggie Adler's estate, where she kept a boat. The group go to Adler's estate. A possessed Clarissa talks with Ren; her conversations and scattered letters left behind by Maggie Adler reveal that Adler mistakenly interpreted scrambled messages from <i>Kanaloa</i> as an enemy transmission, and the submarine was sunk by friendly fire. The crew were not killed, however, but transported to another dimension due to the sub's experimental nuclear reactor. Adler and her friend Anna attempted to communicate with the <i>Kanaloa</i> crew via a radio, but Anna was absorbed through the gateway. The <i>Kanaloa</i> crew intend on using Alex and her friends' bodies to escape the dimension, keeping them on the island long enough for their possession attempts to be successful.</p>
<p>Alex and the group discover Maggie Adler's plan to fix the temporal tears—tranposing onto the other side and tuning within. Alex repeatedly has visions of the past, including talking with Michael. Alex and Jonas head to a lookout post to unlock a <a href="/wiki/Bomb_shelter" title="Bomb shelter">bomb shelter</a> to find another way into the cave. A ghost possesses Jonas and attempts to bargain with Alex, offering to spare her and the rest of her friends if they leave Clarissa behind. Inside the caves, Alex tunes into a rift that sends her into a void. Encountering the possessed Clarissa, Alex is warned that she will die if the rift is closed, but Clarissa will be kept if she goes through the portal. Alex can choose to leave, erasing Clarissa from existence; close the portal herself, trapping her with the ghosts; or, if she has found enough letters from Maggie Adler, Alex can appeal to the crew members directly, convincing them to let Clarissa and her go. The reflection of Alex seen throughout the game is revealed to be Alex from the future. She then loops back to a conversation to Michael, who admits that he and Clarissa are planning to move away from town. Time then corrects.</p>
<p>Alex wakes up with Jonas, Nona, Clarissa and Ren on the ferry back home, with Jonas informing her that whatever she did fixed the rift and that everything is normal. Everyone reveals that, for a limited period, they revisited past memories. They then swear to not tell anyone about their experiences. Nona takes a final group picture.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Endings">Endings</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Oxenfree&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Endings">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In an epilogue, Alex reveals the fate of the characters, with Nona's picture shown and the endings dependent on actions made during the game:</p>
<ul>
<li>Clarissa's friendship with Alex and Michael can be either preserved or destroyed, or Clarissa can be sacrificed to the rift, with no one remembering Clarissa except Alex herself. If she is saved she will either drop out of school or continue her studies.</li>
<li>Ren will either date or not date Nona depending on Alex's actions.</li>
<li>Nona goes to a ballet school, and may or may not be in a <a href="/wiki/Long-distance_relationship" title="Long-distance relationship">long-distance relationship</a> with Ren.</li>
<li>Jonas's relationship with Alex can be either preserved and destroyed.</li>
<li>Michael can be revived if Alex encourages him not to move away in the time loop. If so, Jonas and Alex will cease to be step-siblings. The player's actions can either destroy or preserve Michael's relationship with Clarissa.</li>
</ul>
<p>Delivering a statement on her own experience, Alex can ether go to college in or out of her hometown or take time off. Thereafter, the screen flickers and Alex tells the player she is going to meet up with Clarissa, Ren and Nona to go to Edward's Island and she is picking up Jonas, whom she has never met, revealing that time is still looping.</p>
<p>If the player continues the timeline in the <a href="/wiki/New_Game_Plus" title="New Game Plus">Game+</a> ending, Alex has the chance to send herself a message from the island. After the epilogue, a new scene plays of Alex, Jonas, and Ren at a supermarket waiting to buy alcohol before getting on the ferry. Tuning her radio, Alex receives the message her future self sent from the island. Depending on the message sent and how the player responds, Alex, Jonas, and Ren can decide not to go to Edwards Island at all, preventing the loop.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Development">Development</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Oxenfree&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Development">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Cousins Sean Krankel and Adam Hines founded <a href="/wiki/Night_School_Studio" title="Night School Studio">Night School Studio</a> in 2014,<sup id="cite_ref-techtimes-interview_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-techtimes-interview-8">[8]</a></sup> having long wanted to collaborate on a video game together.<sup id="cite_ref-skybound_oxenfree_p1_0m0s_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-skybound_oxenfree_p1_0m0s-9">[9]</a></sup> Hines had worked on <a href="/wiki/Telltale_Games" title="Telltale Games">Telltale Games</a>'s story-focused games like <i><a href="/wiki/Wolf_Among_Us" class="mw-redirect" title="Wolf Among Us">Wolf Among Us</a></i>, while Krankel had previously met or worked with many Night School members at <a href="/wiki/Disney" class="mw-redirect" title="Disney">Disney</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-latices-preview_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-latices-preview-10">[10]</a></sup> Hines and Krankel were interested in creating a game that told its story in a unique way.<sup id="cite_ref-skybound_oxenfree_p1_0m0s_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-skybound_oxenfree_p1_0m0s-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>Krankel and Hines looked at other story-focused games, and felt that they either were linear stories driven by <a href="/wiki/Set_piece" title="Set piece">set pieces</a>, or branching, player choice-influenced stories told through <a href="/wiki/Cut_scene" class="mw-redirect" title="Cut scene">cut scenes</a>. "We thought, why not let you move freely while communicating, interacting, and exploring a branching narrative?" Krankel recalled.<sup id="cite_ref-gamasutra-roadtotheIGF_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gamasutra-roadtotheIGF-11">[11]</a></sup> "The first thing we wanted to do is make a game where communication is really sort of a key mechanic, but that people have more freedom than they do with all the other narrative-focused games. At its core, we wanted to never have a cut scene in the game, where you could walk and move freely and communicate with your friends, and even communicate with other beings and other kind of strange things." Rather than developing the gameplay and attaching a story to it, Night School focused on developing gameplay that would support the story they wanted to tell.<sup id="cite_ref-techtimes-interview_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-techtimes-interview-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>Night School wanted the story to have scary and supernatural elements, without verging on horror. "We wanted to something a little bit more [...] <a href="/wiki/Steven_Spielberg" title="Steven Spielberg">Spielberg-ian</a>, and sort of give it a sense of wonder and have it, for awhile, just be actually interacting with these supernatural creatures where it's almost a little bit fun, and hopefully, gives you butterflies in your stomach before it gets pretty crazy," Krankel said. The developers were influenced by other coming-of-age stories like <i><a href="/wiki/Stand_by_Me_(film)" title="Stand by Me (film)">Stand By Me</a></i> when developing the characters and story;<sup id="cite_ref-techtimes-interview_8-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-techtimes-interview-8">[8]</a></sup> Krankel cited the character of <a href="/wiki/List_of_Freaks_and_Geeks_characters#Lindsay_Weir" title="List of Freaks and Geeks characters">Lindsay Weir</a> from <i><a href="/wiki/Freaks_and_Geeks" title="Freaks and Geeks">Freaks and Geeks</a></i> as the biggest outside influence on the character of Alex.<sup id="cite_ref-gamasutra-roadtotheIGF_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gamasutra-roadtotheIGF-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>After the founding of <a href="/wiki/Night_School_Studio" title="Night School Studio">Night School Studio</a>, the team set up a casting call for voice actors on <a href="/wiki/Backstage_(magazine)" title="Backstage (magazine)">Backstage</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Prior to actors being cast and their lines recorded, the team had placeholder subtitles in the game. Lead engineer Bryan Cannon recalled that without the voices, he was unsure whether the game would be fun to play; after the voices were added, he could see the characters as believable people having conversations. Hines directed recording sessions, allowing him to change dialogue on-the-fly if he felt material did not work when voiced by the actors. All of the actors voiced their lines separately, with their conversations in-game stitched together in post-production.<sup id="cite_ref-skybound-makingof-voices_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-skybound-makingof-voices-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p><i>Oxenfree</i> was developed primarily using the <a href="/wiki/Unity_(game_engine)" title="Unity (game engine)">Unity</a> game engine, <a href="/wiki/Adobe_Photoshop" title="Adobe Photoshop">Adobe Photoshop</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Autodesk_Maya" title="Autodesk Maya">Autodesk Maya</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-gamasutra-roadtotheIGF_11-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gamasutra-roadtotheIGF-11">[11]</a></sup> The game's artwork was made by Heather Gross. The game's camera distance from the player characters and 2D look were the result of functional necessity, as the team needed to show multiple characters, dialogue bubbles, and places to explore in the environment on the screen simultaneously. Working with these requirements, Gross created <a href="/wiki/Parallax_scrolling" title="Parallax scrolling">parallaxing</a>, painterly backgrounds. The dark, organic elements of the setting were put in opposition to the supernatural elements, which are bright and geometric.<sup id="cite_ref-gamasutra-roadtotheIGF_11-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gamasutra-roadtotheIGF-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Audio">Audio</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Oxenfree&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Audio">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Initially, Night School Studio did not know exactly what they wanted the music of <i>Oxenfree</i> to sound like. American film, TV, and game music composer and sound designer Andrew Rohrmann, known under his alias <a href="/wiki/Scntfc" title="Scntfc">scntfc</a>, composed the music and sound design of the game; Krankel knew Rohrmann from a friend of a friend and had not known of his game music pedigree. Hines mentioned that they gave Rohrmann "random" suggestions for the sound, including "<a href="/wiki/John_Carpenter" title="John Carpenter">John Carpenter</a> meets <a href="/wiki/Boards_of_Canada" title="Boards of Canada">Boards of Canada</a>", but were impressed with the music they got in response.<sup id="cite_ref-skybound-makingof-music_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-skybound-makingof-music-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>Krankel described the goal of the music to feel simultaneously analog and digital, "so that it's nostalgic without being set in a specific time in the past."<sup id="cite_ref-gamasutra-roadtotheIGF_11-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gamasutra-roadtotheIGF-11">[11]</a></sup> Rohrmann combined digital recording techniques and plugins with analog ones, running some sounds through old cassette decks and <a href="/wiki/Reel-to-reel_audio_tape_recording" title="Reel-to-reel audio tape recording">reel-to-reel tape</a>. The shortwave radio Alex uses in the game was created by recording sounds through a <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>-era radio set. Much of the music was not scored to specific scenes, but for certain moods; Rohrmann estimated 90% of the songs in the completed game were identical to his original demo recordings.<sup id="cite_ref-skybound-makingof-music_14-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-skybound-makingof-music-14">[14]</a></sup> The soundtrack was released on January 15, 2016 to accompany the game, with a <a href="/wiki/Gramophone_record" title="Gramophone record">vinyl</a> release on May 25.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<div style="clear:both;"></div>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Promotion_and_release">Promotion and release</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Oxenfree&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Promotion and release">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Night School posted a <a href="/wiki/Teaser_campaign" title="Teaser campaign">teaser</a> for the game on On March 1, 2015.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> The game was announced four days later at <a href="/wiki/Game_Developers_Conference" title="Game Developers Conference">Game Developers Conference</a> 2015.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> It was announced on October 23, 2015 that the game would be released in January 2016, with the second teaser from the game posted onto the YouTube account.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> After release, <a href="/wiki/Skybound_Entertainment" title="Skybound Entertainment">Skybound Entertainment</a> approached to help develop merchandise and the game for additional media.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>Night School partnered with <a href="/wiki/Iam8bit" title="Iam8bit">iam8bit</a> to create a collector's edition of the game, which featured items like a cassette tape demo of Ren's band in the game, a map of the island, poster, and code for the game.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/PlayStation_4" title="PlayStation 4">PlayStation 4</a> version of the game was announced on April 27 and released May 31. The PS4 version of the game added the New Game+ mode as well as platform-specific tweaks, like using the <a href="/wiki/DualShock_4" class="mw-redirect" title="DualShock 4">DualShock 4</a> controller to adjust the game's radio.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> An iOS version of the game was released on the <a href="/wiki/Apple_App_Store" class="mw-redirect" title="Apple App Store">Apple App Store</a> on March 16, 2017.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>The game itself also has an <a href="/wiki/Alternate_reality_game" title="Alternate reality game">alternate reality</a> counterpart. Within the game are radio frequencies that hint to a real phone number. This phone number led players to the Twitter account @xray9169363733. The account posted various cryptic, coded messages, all of which seemed to point to a real world location. On May 7, 2016, Youtuber Jesse Cox posted a video similar to the PS4 <i>Oxenfree</i> trailer, but with several letters highlighted in red. This led players to www.edwardsisland.com. Several messages were found, but most important was "MILNER IS WARD", confirming that a special object would be hidden at Fort Ward, WA.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> The object was revealed to be a box with letters by Alex from all the possible timelines from the game warning her not to go to Edwards island, and a manually operated tape player with two paper music tapes of songs from the game soundtrack.<sup id="cite_ref-killscreen-arg_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-killscreen-arg-25">[25]</a></sup></p>

<p><i>Oxenfree</i> received positive reviews from critics. <i><a href="/wiki/Wired_(magazine)" title="Wired (magazine)">Wired</a></i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span>s Chris Kohler wrote that "<i>Oxenfree</i> shows some smart thinking about the relationship between games and players," and that as Night School's first game, it was an "auspicious debut."<sup id="cite_ref-wired-review_37-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wired-review-37">[37]</a></sup> <i>Destructoid</i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span>s Nic Rowen praised the game for taking inspiration from old movies but still being "anything but generic"; "It dials into its own style and mood, tapping into something very heartfelt and special. It might just be the best 'horror' game I've played in years," he wrote.<sup id="cite_ref-destructoid-review_29-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-destructoid-review-29">[29]</a></sup> A less enthusiastic review was offered by Allegra Frank for <i><a href="/wiki/Polygon_(website)" title="Polygon (website)">Polygon</a></i>, who wrote that despite offering many promising attributes, "I finished <i>Oxenfree</i> with my mouth agape, feeling wholly unsatisfied."<sup id="cite_ref-polygon-review_35-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-polygon-review-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
<p>The game's audiovisual presentation was commonly considered a strength of the game. Rowen called the game's watercolor art style "gorgeous".<sup id="cite_ref-destructoid-review_29-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-destructoid-review-29">[29]</a></sup> Matt Buchholtz of <i><a href="/wiki/Electronic_Gaming_Monthly" title="Electronic Gaming Monthly">Electronic Gaming Monthly</a></i> favorably contrasted <i>Oxenfree</i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span>s traditional media style with the much more common <a href="/wiki/Retro_style" title="Retro style">retro</a> pixel styling of indie games. Rowen, Buchholtz, and others also noted that the contrast between the watercolor look of the environment and the sharp, digital effects of the paranormal. "The studio’s choice to completely split the art styles between the normal world and the mystical is a massive success." said Buchholtz.<sup id="cite_ref-ego-review_30-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ego-review-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<p>Frank credited <i>Oxenfree</i> with not relying on "lazy, clichéd 'young person' speech";<sup id="cite_ref-polygon-review_35-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-polygon-review-35">[35]</a></sup> reviewers also felt that the naturalistic dialogue of <i>Oxenfree</i> was a strength, while IGN's Kallie Plagge felt that characters such as Ren had grating or stilted dialogue.<sup id="cite_ref-ing-review_38-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ing-review-38">[38]</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/GamesTM" title="GamesTM">GamesTM</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/The_A.V._Club" title="The A.V. Club">The A.V. Club</a></i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span>s William Hughes credited the game with delivering organic dialogue options that lacked any clear good or bad associations.<sup id="cite_ref-avclub-review_39-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-avclub-review-39">[39]</a></sup> <i>GamesTM</i> and other reviewers, such as <a href="/wiki/VideoGamer.com" title="VideoGamer.com">VideoGamer.com</a>'s Tom Orry, felt the script sometimes failed to convey realistic panic or distress of the characters in their exceptional circumstances.<sup id="cite_ref-gamestm-review_40-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gamestm-review-40">[40]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-videogamer-review_36-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-videogamer-review-36">[36]</a></sup> Plagge noted that despite her efforts, some dialogue choices unavoidably hurt relationships in the game. "It’s an important reminder that you can’t “win” social situations, and that kept Oxenfree’s supernatural plot points grounded in reality," she wrote.<sup id="cite_ref-ing-review_38-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ing-review-38">[38]</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Game_Informer" title="Game Informer">Game Informer</a></i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span>s Kimberley Wallace felt that the opaque results of choices was sometimes a hindrance; "something feels fundamentally wrong when players think they're influencing events in one direction, and the exact opposite happens instead," she wrote.<sup id="cite_ref-gameinformer-review_31-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gameinformer-review-31">[31]</a></sup> In contrast, Corriea appreciated that some consequences of dialogue choices did not become clear until much later in the game, encouraging repeat playthroughs to try different approaches.<sup id="cite_ref-gamespot-review_3-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gamespot-review-3">[3]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Film_adaptation_and_web_series">Film adaptation and web series</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Oxenfree&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Film adaptation and web series">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
