			<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Victorian restoration</h1>
									<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
									<div id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
								<div id="contentSub"></div>

The amateur architect <a href="/wiki/Edmund_Beckett,_1st_Baron_Grimthorpe" title="Edmund Beckett, 1st Baron Grimthorpe">Lord Grimley</a>'s rebuilding of the west front destroyed the cathedral's perpendicular architectural features, replacing them with his own designs. These are considered unsympathetic to the fabric of the building, and were criticised by commentators even at the time.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></div>

<p><b>Victorian restoration</b> was the widespread and extensive <a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/refurbish" class="extiw" title="wikt:refurbish">refurbishment</a> and rebuilding of <a href="/wiki/Church_of_England" title="Church of England">Church of England</a> <a href="/wiki/Church_(building)" title="Church (building)">churches</a> and cathedrals that took place in England and Wales during the 19th-century <a href="/wiki/Victorian_era" title="Victorian era">reign of Queen Victoria</a>. It was not the same process as is understood today by the term <a href="/wiki/Building_restoration" title="Building restoration">building restoration</a>.</p>
<p>Against a background of poorly maintained church buildings; a reaction against the <a href="/wiki/Puritan" class="mw-redirect" title="Puritan">Puritan</a> ethic manifested in the <a href="/wiki/Gothic_Revival" class="mw-redirect" title="Gothic Revival">Gothic Revival</a>; and a shortage of churches where they were needed in cities, the <a href="/wiki/Cambridge_Camden_Society" title="Cambridge Camden Society">Cambridge Camden Society</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Oxford_Movement" title="Oxford Movement">Oxford Movement</a> advocated a return to a more medieval attitude to churchgoing. The change was embraced by the Church of England which saw it as a means of reversing the decline in <a href="/wiki/Church_attendance" title="Church attendance">church attendance</a>.</p>
<p>The principle was to "restore" a church to how it might have looked during the "<a href="/wiki/English_Gothic_architecture" title="English Gothic architecture">Decorated</a>" style of architecture which existed between 1260 and 1360, and many famous architects such as <a href="/wiki/George_Gilbert_Scott" title="George Gilbert Scott">George Gilbert Scott</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ewan_Christian" title="Ewan Christian">Ewan Christian</a> enthusiastically accepted commissions for restorations. It is estimated that around 80% of all Church of England churches were affected in some way by the movement, varying from minor changes to complete demolition and rebuilding.</p>
<p>Influential people like <a href="/wiki/John_Ruskin" title="John Ruskin">John Ruskin</a> and <a href="/wiki/William_Morris" title="William Morris">William Morris</a> were opposed to such large-scale restoration, and their activities eventually led to the formation of societies dedicated to building preservation, such as the <a href="/wiki/Society_for_the_Protection_of_Ancient_Buildings" title="Society for the Protection of Ancient Buildings">Society for the Protection of Ancient Buildings</a>. In retrospect, the period of Victorian restoration has been viewed in a generally unfavourable light.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Background">Background</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Victorian_restoration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Lich_Cath_Face.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5d/Lich_Cath_Face.jpg/200px-Lich_Cath_Face.jpg" width="200" height="298" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5d/Lich_Cath_Face.jpg/300px-Lich_Cath_Face.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5d/Lich_Cath_Face.jpg/400px-Lich_Cath_Face.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1438" data-file-height="2145" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Lich_Cath_Face.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The west front of <a href="/wiki/Lichfield_Cathedral" title="Lichfield Cathedral">Lichfield Cathedral</a> as restored by <a href="/wiki/George_Gilbert_Scott" title="George Gilbert Scott">George Gilbert Scott</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>A number of factors working together led to the spate of Victorian restoration.</p>
<p>From the time of the <a href="/wiki/English_Reformation" title="English Reformation">English Reformation</a> onwards, apart from necessary repairs so that buildings might remain in use, and the addition of occasional internal commemorative adornments, English churches and cathedrals were subjected to little building work and only piecemeal restoration. This situation lasted for about 250 years with the fabric of many churches and cathedrals suffering from neglect.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> The severity of the problem was demonstrated when the spire of <a href="/wiki/Chichester_Cathedral" title="Chichester Cathedral">Chichester Cathedral</a> suddenly telescoped in on itself in 1861.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Bramhope_Puritan_Chapel_-_geograph.org.uk_-_1603796.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4f/Bramhope_Puritan_Chapel_-_geograph.org.uk_-_1603796.jpg/220px-Bramhope_Puritan_Chapel_-_geograph.org.uk_-_1603796.jpg" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4f/Bramhope_Puritan_Chapel_-_geograph.org.uk_-_1603796.jpg/330px-Bramhope_Puritan_Chapel_-_geograph.org.uk_-_1603796.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4f/Bramhope_Puritan_Chapel_-_geograph.org.uk_-_1603796.jpg/440px-Bramhope_Puritan_Chapel_-_geograph.org.uk_-_1603796.jpg 2x" data-file-width="640" data-file-height="479" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Bramhope_Puritan_Chapel_-_geograph.org.uk_-_1603796.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The unrestored interior of the Puritan Chapel, <a href="/wiki/Bramhope" title="Bramhope">Bramhope</a>, West Yorkshire</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>In addition, ever since the mid-17th century <a href="/wiki/Puritan" class="mw-redirect" title="Puritan">Puritan</a> reforms which were typified by a minimum of ritual and decoration and by an unambiguous emphasis on preaching there had been an ongoing removal of any emotion or colour from English religious services as a means of distancing itself from what was seen as the excesses of Catholicism.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> But towards the end of the 18th century the burgeoning <a href="/wiki/Gothic_Revival" class="mw-redirect" title="Gothic Revival">Gothic Revival</a> and interest in <a href="/wiki/Medievalism" title="Medievalism">medievalism</a> encouraged people to seek more interest in their religious services. The popularity of the Gothic Revival was seen by Church officials as a way to reverse the decline in church attendance, and thereby start to reassert the Church's power, prosperity and influence. They therefore pushed for massive restoration programs.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>As a third factor, the industrial revolution had resulted in many people living in cities that had few churches to cater for their religious needs—for instance <a href="/wiki/Stockport" title="Stockport">Stockport</a> had a population of nearly 34,000 but church seating for only 2,500.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> The rise in <a href="/wiki/Dissenter" title="Dissenter">dissenter</a> <a href="/wiki/Religious_denomination" title="Religious denomination">denominations</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Methodism" title="Methodism">Methodism</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Religious_Society_of_Friends" class="mw-redirect" title="Religious Society of Friends">Religious Society of Friends</a>, was seen as further evidence of this shortfall.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> To fulfil this need, between 1818 and 1824 the Government had granted £1.5 million for building new churches.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Known as <a href="/wiki/Commissioners%27_church" title="Commissioners' church">Commissioners' churches</a>, most of them cost only £4,000 to £5,000 each to build, and dissatisfaction with their indifferent design and cheap construction provoked a strong reaction.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>Equivalent movements existed in most of Europe, especially northern Europe, with the French architect and architectural historian <a href="/wiki/Eug%C3%A8ne_Viollet-le-Duc" title="Eugène Viollet-le-Duc">Eugène Viollet-le-Duc</a> especially associated with the French manifestation.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Driving_forces">Driving forces</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Victorian_restoration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Driving forces">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Cambridge_Camden_Society">The Cambridge Camden Society</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Victorian_restoration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: The Cambridge Camden Society">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="quotebox" style="float: right; clear: right; margin: 0.5em 0 0.8em 1.4em; padding: 10px; border: 1px solid #aaa; font-size: 88%; background-color: #F9F9F9;">
<div class="" style="position: relative; text-align: left;">
<div>My first church dates from the same year with<br />
the foundation of the Cambridge Camden<br />
Society, to whom the honour of our recovery<br />
from the odious bathos is mainly due.<br />
I only wish I had known its founders at the time.</div>
</div>
<cite style="display: block; text-align: left;"><a href="/wiki/George_Gilbert_Scott" title="George Gilbert Scott">George Gilbert Scott</a>, Recollections, p.86.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></cite></div>
<p>One of the main driving forces for the restoration of churches was the <a href="/wiki/Cambridge_Camden_Society" title="Cambridge Camden Society">Cambridge Camden Society</a> (CCS), which was founded in 1839 by two <a href="/wiki/University_of_Cambridge" title="University of Cambridge">Cambridge</a> undergraduates, <a href="/wiki/John_Mason_Neale" title="John Mason Neale">John Mason Neale</a> and <a href="/w/index.php?title=Benjamin_Webb_(clergyman)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Benjamin Webb (clergyman) (page does not exist)">Benjamin Webb</a>, as a club for those who shared a common interest in Gothic church design.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> It rapidly became popular: its membership increased from 8 to 180 in its first 12 months.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> Although initially a society for recording and discussing medieval church features, the members of the CCS soon began to expostulate in their journal <i>The Ecclesiologist</i> and particularly in their <i>Few Words to Church-builders</i> of 1844 that the only "correct" form for a church building was the "middle pointed" or "<a href="/wiki/English_Gothic_architecture#Decorated_Gothic" title="English Gothic architecture">Decorated</a>" style,<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> in which churches had been built during the hundred years centred on 1300. Ecclesiology obviously struck a chord in society: it was closely linked with the ongoing interest in <a href="/wiki/Medievalism" title="Medievalism">medievalism</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Gothic_Revival" class="mw-redirect" title="Gothic Revival">Gothic Revival</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>The CCS's firm insistence on one style being correct proved to be a beacon for those who were no longer able to judge for themselves what was "good" in architecture—the certainties of the <a href="/wiki/Vitruvius" title="Vitruvius">Vitruvian</a> rules having lost their power during the <a href="/wiki/Romanticism" title="Romanticism">Romantic movement</a> that had been in vogue since the middle of the 18th century.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> The CCS stated that there were two possible ways in which a church could be restored. As <a href="/wiki/Kenneth_Clark" title="Kenneth Clark">Kenneth Clark</a> put it, they said that one could "either restore each of the various alterations and additions in its own style, or restore the whole church to the best and purest style of which traces remain".<sup id="cite_ref-Clark173_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Clark173-17">[17]</a></sup> The Society wholeheartedly recommended the second option and since virtually every medieval church had at least some small remnant of decorated style, maybe a porch or even just a window, the whole church would be "restored" to match it. And if the earliest portions were too late, then it was a candidate for a complete rebuild in the "correct" style.<sup id="cite_ref-Clark173_17-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Clark173-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>"To restore," <i>The Ecclesiologist</i> declared, "is to revive the original appearance&#160;... lost by decay, accident or ill-judged alteration". They did later admit, though, that such "restoration" might create an ideal state that the building had never been in.<sup id="cite_ref-ACR_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ACR-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Oxford_Movement">Oxford Movement</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Victorian_restoration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Oxford Movement">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Church restorations were also strongly influenced by the <a href="/wiki/Oxford_Movement" title="Oxford Movement">Oxford Movement</a>, which advocated moving the centre of importance in the church from preaching to the <a href="/wiki/Sacrament" title="Sacrament">sacrament</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Eucharist" title="Eucharist">Eucharist</a>: from the <a href="/wiki/Pulpit" title="Pulpit">pulpit</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Altar" title="Altar">altar</a>. Consequences of this included moving the pulpit from a more central position to the side of the church, replacing <a href="/wiki/Box_pew" title="Box pew">box pews</a> with open pews, creating a central <a href="/wiki/Aisle" title="Aisle">aisle</a> to give a better view of the altar, and the removal of galleries. Another consequence was that a larger <a href="/wiki/Chancel" title="Chancel">chancel</a> was required for the associated ritual.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>

<div class="thumbinner" style="width:182px;"><a href="/wiki/File:St._Peter%27s,_Berkhamsted_-_Nave_-_geograph.org.uk_-_780233.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/64/St._Peter%27s%2C_Berkhamsted_-_Nave_-_geograph.org.uk_-_780233.jpg/180px-St._Peter%27s%2C_Berkhamsted_-_Nave_-_geograph.org.uk_-_780233.jpg" width="180" height="240" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/64/St._Peter%27s%2C_Berkhamsted_-_Nave_-_geograph.org.uk_-_780233.jpg/270px-St._Peter%27s%2C_Berkhamsted_-_Nave_-_geograph.org.uk_-_780233.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/64/St._Peter%27s%2C_Berkhamsted_-_Nave_-_geograph.org.uk_-_780233.jpg/360px-St._Peter%27s%2C_Berkhamsted_-_Nave_-_geograph.org.uk_-_780233.jpg 2x" data-file-width="480" data-file-height="640" /></a>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Activities">Activities</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Victorian_restoration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Activities">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Persuaded by the Cambridge Camden Society that <a href="/wiki/English_Gothic_architecture#Decorated_Gothic" title="English Gothic architecture">Decorated Gothic</a> was the only correct style, and by the Oxford Movement's theories concerning the nature of worship, a spate of "restoration" was soon under way. Some figures give an idea of the scale. A total of 3,765 new and rebuilt churches were <a href="/wiki/Consecration" title="Consecration">consecrated</a> in the forty years up to 1875, with the most active decade being the 1860s in which there were more than 1,000 such consecrations.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> Over 7,000 parish churches in England and Wales — which is nearly 80% of all of them — were restored in some way between 1840 and 1875.<sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-21">[21]</a></sup> There were 150% more people identified as professional architects in the 1871 census than in 1851 — it is known that established architects passed small restoration jobs on to their newly qualified colleagues, since such work provided good practice.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>The retention of original material (carving, woodwork, etc.) tended to be of little importance to the early restorers: appearance was all, and much good old work was discarded to be replaced by modern replacement in the chosen style.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> Different architects had different degrees of sympathy with original material, and as the century progressed greater care was generally taken;<sup id="cite_ref-VW_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-VW-24">[24]</a></sup> this was at least partly as a result of the increasingly louder voices that were raised in opposition.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<p>As an example of the type of work undertaken in one church, in 1870–71 the <a href="/wiki/Church_of_St_Peter,_Great_Berkhamsted" title="Church of St Peter, Great Berkhamsted">Church of St Peter, Great Berkhamsted</a> was the subject of a restoration programme by <a href="/wiki/William_Butterfield" title="William Butterfield">William Butterfield</a>, whose other works included churches such as <a href="/wiki/All_Saints,_Margaret_Street" title="All Saints, Margaret Street">All Saints, Margaret Street</a> in London. Butterfield's restoration involved the removal of some original features, including the obliteration of paintings on the pillars. The most substantial structural changes involved raising both the roof and the floor of the chancel, raising the roof of the south transept to its original pitch, removing the vestry, incorporating the south porch into the south aisle and removing the door, re-flooring the nave, installing new oak benches and replacing an earlier gallery. Butterfield also installed clear windows in the clerestory, allowing more light to enter the nave. He extended the aisles by knocking down the dividing walls of two chambers at the west end. On the exterior of the church, Butterfield removed the crumbling <a href="/wiki/Stucco" title="Stucco">stucco</a> that had been added in 1820 and re-faced the church walls with <a href="/wiki/Flint" title="Flint">flint</a> <a href="/wiki/Flushwork" title="Flushwork">flushwork</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p>At <a href="/wiki/Lichfield_Cathedral" title="Lichfield Cathedral">Lichfield Cathedral</a>, the 18th century had been a period of decay: the 15th-century library was pulled down, most of the statues on the west front were removed, and the stonework covered with <a href="/wiki/Roman_cement" title="Roman cement">Roman cement</a>. After some structural work early in the 19th century by <a href="/wiki/James_Wyatt" title="James Wyatt">James Wyatt</a>, the ornate west front (pictured above) was restored by Sir <a href="/wiki/George_Gilbert_Scott" title="George Gilbert Scott">George Gilbert Scott</a>. It includes many ornate carved figures of kings, queens and saints, created from original materials where possible and new imitations and additions when the originals were not available. Wyatt's <a href="/wiki/Choir-screen" class="mw-redirect" title="Choir-screen">choir-screen</a> had utilised medieval stone-work which Scott in turn used to create the clergy's seats in the sanctuary. A new metal screen by <a href="/wiki/Francis_Skidmore" title="Francis Skidmore">Francis Skidmore</a> and <a href="/wiki/John_Birnie_Philip" title="John Birnie Philip">John Birnie Philip</a> to designs by Scott was installed, as was a <a href="/wiki/Mintons" title="Mintons">Minton tile</a> pavement stretching from choir screen to altar, inspired by medieval tiles found in the Choir foundations.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Practitioners">Practitioners</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Victorian_restoration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Practitioners">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Famous architects like <a href="/wiki/George_Gilbert_Scott" title="George Gilbert Scott">George Gilbert Scott</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ewan_Christian" title="Ewan Christian">Ewan Christian</a>, <a href="/wiki/William_Butterfield" title="William Butterfield">William Butterfield</a> and <a href="/wiki/George_Edmund_Street" title="George Edmund Street">George Edmund Street</a> became enthusiastic "restorers" and the wave of restoration spread across the country so that by 1875 something like 80% of all churches in England had been affected in some way.<sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_21-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-21">[21]</a></sup></p>

<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Bath_Abbey_Fan_Vaulting_-_July_2006_crop.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/67/Bath_Abbey_Fan_Vaulting_-_July_2006_crop.jpg/220px-Bath_Abbey_Fan_Vaulting_-_July_2006_crop.jpg" width="220" height="208" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/67/Bath_Abbey_Fan_Vaulting_-_July_2006_crop.jpg/330px-Bath_Abbey_Fan_Vaulting_-_July_2006_crop.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/67/Bath_Abbey_Fan_Vaulting_-_July_2006_crop.jpg/440px-Bath_Abbey_Fan_Vaulting_-_July_2006_crop.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2075" data-file-height="1965" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">

The nave of <a href="/wiki/Bath_Abbey" title="Bath Abbey">Bath Abbey</a>&#160;– Scott's stone <a href="/wiki/Fan_vaulting" class="mw-redirect" title="Fan vaulting">fan vaulting</a> that replaced the ancient wooden ceiling to the original design by <a href="/wiki/Robert_Vertue" title="Robert Vertue">Robert</a> and <a href="/wiki/William_Vertue" title="William Vertue">William Vertue</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-ingentaconnect_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ingentaconnect-29">[29]</a></sup></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>In 1850 Scott wrote a book <i>A plea for the faithful restoration of our Ancient Churches</i>, in which he stated that "as a general rule it is highly desirable to preserve those vestiges of the growth and history of buildings which are indicated by the various styles and irregularities of its parts". However he did not follow this principle in practice, generally sweeping away all later changes and reconstructing the church in a uniform early style, sometimes on the evidence of just one remaining early feature.<sup id="cite_ref-ACR_18-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ACR-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Opposition">Opposition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Victorian_restoration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Opposition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>There were opponents. Although <a href="/wiki/John_Ruskin" title="John Ruskin">John Ruskin</a> was generally in favour of new buildings in an early Gothic style,<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> in 1849 he wrote in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Seven_Lamps_of_Architecture" title="The Seven Lamps of Architecture">The Seven Lamps of Architecture</a></i> that it was not possible "to restore anything that has ever been great or beautiful in architecture". His attitude influenced the Society of Antiquaries of London, which urged in 1855 that "no restoration should ever be attempted, otherwise than&#160;... in the sense of preservation from further injuries".<sup id="cite_ref-ACR_18-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ACR-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>Another vociferous opponent was <a href="/wiki/William_Morris" title="William Morris">William Morris</a> who campaigned against the proposed restoration of <a href="/wiki/St_John_the_Baptist_Church,_Inglesham" title="St John the Baptist Church, Inglesham">St John the Baptist Church, Inglesham</a> in the 1880s and started the <a href="/wiki/Society_for_the_Protection_of_Ancient_Buildings" title="Society for the Protection of Ancient Buildings">Society for the Protection of Ancient Buildings</a> (SPAB) in 1877 when he heard of the proposed restoration of <a href="/wiki/Tewkesbury_Abbey" title="Tewkesbury Abbey">Tewkesbury Abbey</a> by Scott.<sup id="cite_ref-VW_24-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-VW-24">[24]</a></sup> The principles espoused by SPAB took some time to attract support, but the policy of putting "Protection in place of Restoration" eventually took hold, and are adhered to today.<sup id="cite_ref-ACR_18-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ACR-18">[18]</a></sup> Morris also wrote in 1877:</p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Wmmorris3248.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fa/Wmmorris3248.jpg/170px-Wmmorris3248.jpg" width="170" height="239" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fa/Wmmorris3248.jpg/255px-Wmmorris3248.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fa/Wmmorris3248.jpg/340px-Wmmorris3248.jpg 2x" data-file-width="416" data-file-height="585" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Wmmorris3248.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/William_Morris" title="William Morris">William Morris</a>, who strongly opposed restoration</div>
</div>
</div>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>"But of late years a great uprising of ecclesiastical zeal, coinciding with a great increase of study, and consequently of knowledge of medieval architecture has driven people into spending their money on these buildings, not merely with the purpose of repairing them, of keeping them safe, clean, and wind and water-tight, but also of "restoring" them to some ideal state of perfection; sweeping away if possible all signs of what has befallen them at least since the Reformation, and often since dates much earlier."<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>Despite his opposition, though, it is known that Morris profited greatly by his firm's provision of stained glass to many restoration projects,<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> and it has been noted that his criticism only started after his firm was securely established as a supplier to these projects.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<p>Further opposition came from evangelical Protestants, who believed that "ornamental carved work, decorative painting, encaustic tiles, and stained glass were foolish vanities which lead the heart astray",<sup id="cite_ref-E190_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-E190-34">[34]</a></sup> and from others who were concerned about the cost: "For the cost of one stone church with a groined roof, or even an open timbered roof, two might be built in brick with plaster ceilings; and who could dare to say that worship in the plainer building would be less devout or sincere than that which was offered in the other?"<sup id="cite_ref-E190_34-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-E190-34">[34]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
<p>Not all Catholics were in favour either: late in his life <a href="/wiki/Cardinal_Wiseman" class="mw-redirect" title="Cardinal Wiseman">Cardinal Wiseman</a> made it clear that his preference was for Renaissance art, as might be expected of a religious order of Italian origin.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_retrospect">In retrospect</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Victorian_restoration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: In retrospect">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="quotebox" style="float: right; clear: right; margin: 0.5em 0 0.8em 1.4em; padding: 10px; border: 1px solid #aaa; font-size: 88%; background-color: #F9F9F9;">
<div class="" style="position: relative; text-align: left;">
<div>The Church's Restoration<br />
In eighteen-eighty-three<br />
Has left for contemplation<br />
Not what there used to be.</div>
</div>
<cite style="display: block; text-align: left;">From <i>Hymn</i> by <a href="/wiki/John_Betjeman" title="John Betjeman">John Betjeman</a>,<br />
parody of <i>The Church's One Foundation</i><br />
(traditional tune settings include<br />
"Ellacombe" and "Aurelia").<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup></cite></div>
<p>From a 20th-century perspective the process of Victorian restoration has often been viewed unfavourably, with terms such as "ruthless", "insensitive" and "heavy-handed" being commonly used to describe the work done.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the introduction to his book <i>The Gothic Revival</i> (first published in 1928), <a href="/wiki/Kenneth_Clark" title="Kenneth Clark">Kenneth Clark</a> wrote "The real reason the Gothic Revival had been neglected is that it produced so little on which our eyes can rest without pain".<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> Clark also reckoned that Decorated Gothic was the worst of the <a href="/wiki/English_Gothic_architecture" title="English Gothic architecture">three possible styles</a> that could have been adopted—the others being Early English which had "very little detail which an ordinary craftsman could not manage", and Perpendicular which was "infinitely the most adaptable of medieval styles". Clark pointed out that Decorated was the most difficult to execute,<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> not least because of the complicated window <a href="/wiki/Tracery" title="Tracery">tracery</a> that set it apart from the other two Gothic styles.</p>
<p>However, not all restoration work was purely negative: a side effect of a number of restorations was the rediscovery of long-lost features, for instance Anglo-Saxon carving that had been incorporated into Norman foundations,<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> or wall-paintings that had been whitewashed over, at <a href="/wiki/St_Albans_Cathedral" title="St Albans Cathedral">St Albans Cathedral</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> It is also true to say that had they not been restored many churches would have fallen into disrepair.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup></p>
