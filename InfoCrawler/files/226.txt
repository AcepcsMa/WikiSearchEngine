<p><b>Kunar</b> (<a href="/wiki/Pashto_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Pashto language">Pashto</a>: <span lang="ps" dir="rtl" xml:lang="ps">کونړ</span>‎, <a href="/wiki/Persian_language" title="Persian language">Persian</a>: <span lang="fa" dir="rtl" xml:lang="fa">کنر</span>‎‎) is one of the 34 <a href="/wiki/Provinces_of_Afghanistan" title="Provinces of Afghanistan">provinces of Afghanistan</a>, located in the northeastern part of the country. Its capital is <a href="/wiki/Asadabad,_Afghanistan" title="Asadabad, Afghanistan">Asadabad</a>. It has a population of about 428,800.<sup id="cite_ref-cso_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cso-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>It is one of the four "<a href="/wiki/N2KL" title="N2KL">N2KL</a>" provinces (<a href="/wiki/Nangarhar_Province" title="Nangarhar Province">Nangarhar Province</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nuristan_Province" title="Nuristan Province">Nuristan Province</a>, Kunar Province and <a href="/wiki/Laghman_Province" title="Laghman Province">Laghman Province</a>). N2KL is the designation used by the US and Coalition Forces in Afghanistan for the rugged and very violent region along the <a href="/wiki/Durand_Line" title="Durand Line">Durand Line</a> border opposite Pakistan's <a href="/wiki/Federally_Administered_Tribal_Areas" title="Federally Administered Tribal Areas">Federally Administered Tribal Areas</a> and <a href="/wiki/Khyber_Pakhtunkhwa" title="Khyber Pakhtunkhwa">Khyber Pakhtunkhwa</a>. Kunar is the center of the N2KL region. Kunar is the birthplace of <a href="/w/index.php?title=Sayyed_Jamaluddin_Afghani&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Sayyed Jamaluddin Afghani (page does not exist)">Sayyed Jamaluddin Afghani</a> (al-Afghani), a very influential Muslim scholar and philosopher.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geography">Geography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kunar_Province&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Geography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Geography_of_Afghanistan" title="Geography of Afghanistan">Geography of Afghanistan</a></div>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Korengal_Valley-2009.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/28/Korengal_Valley-2009.jpg/220px-Korengal_Valley-2009.jpg" width="220" height="146" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/28/Korengal_Valley-2009.jpg/330px-Korengal_Valley-2009.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/28/Korengal_Valley-2009.jpg/440px-Korengal_Valley-2009.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2100" data-file-height="1391" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Korengal_Valley-2009.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
View of the <a href="/wiki/Korangal_Valley" title="Korangal Valley">Korangal Valley</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Kunar province is located in the northeast of Afghanistan. It borders with <a href="/wiki/Nangarhar_Province" title="Nangarhar Province">Nangarhar Province</a> to the south, <a href="/wiki/Nuristan_Province" title="Nuristan Province">Nuristan Province</a> to the north, <a href="/wiki/Laghman_Province" title="Laghman Province">Laghman Province</a> to the west and has a border with Pakistan in the east. The province covers an area of 4339&#160;km<sup>2</sup>. Nearly nine tenths (86%) of the province is mountainous or semi mountainous terrain while one eighth (12%) of the area is made up of relatively flat land. The primary geographic features of the province are (1) the lower <a href="/wiki/Hindu_Kush" title="Hindu Kush">Hindu Kush</a> mountains which are cut by the <a href="/wiki/Kunar_River" title="Kunar River">Kunar River</a> to form the <a href="/wiki/Kunar_Valley" title="Kunar Valley">Kunar Valley</a>. The river flows south and southwest from its source in the <a href="/wiki/Pamir_Mountains" title="Pamir Mountains">Pamir</a> area and is part of the <a href="/wiki/Indus_River" title="Indus River">Indus River</a> watershed via the <a href="/wiki/Kabul_River" title="Kabul River">Kabul River</a> which it meets at Jalalabad. The Kunar is a primary draining conduit for the Hindu Kush basin and several tributaries, including the <a href="/wiki/Pech_River" title="Pech River">Pech</a>, which form distinct and significant valleys in the area. The mountains, narrow valleys with steep walls, and rivers present formidable natural obstacles and have historically constrained all movement through the province. Even in the early 21st century, movement on foot, with pack animals, or with motorized vehicles is extremely limited and channeled due to the significant geographic restrictions.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_history">Early history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kunar_Province&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Early history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>While the <a href="/wiki/Diadochi" title="Diadochi">Diadochi</a> were warring amongst themselves, the <a href="/wiki/Maurya_Empire" title="Maurya Empire">Maurya Empire</a> was developing in the northern part of the <a href="/wiki/Indian_subcontinent" title="Indian subcontinent">Indian subcontinent</a>. The founder of the empire, <a href="/wiki/Chandragupta_Maurya" title="Chandragupta Maurya">Chandragupta Maurya</a>, confronted a Macedonian invasion force led by <a href="/wiki/Seleucus_I_Nicator" title="Seleucus I Nicator">Seleucus I</a> in 305 BC. Following a brief conflict, an agreement was reached as Seleucus ceded <a href="/wiki/Gandhara" title="Gandhara">Gandhara</a> and <a href="/wiki/Arachosia" title="Arachosia">Arachosia</a> (centered around ancient <a href="/wiki/Kandahar" title="Kandahar">Kandahar</a>) and areas south of <a href="/wiki/Bagram" title="Bagram">Bagram</a> (corresponding to the extreme south-east of modern Afghanistan) to the Mauryan. During the 120 years of the Mauryan rule in southern Afghanistan, <a href="/wiki/Buddhism" title="Buddhism">Buddhism</a> was introduced and eventually become a major religion alongside Zoroastrianism and local pagan beliefs. The ancient Grand Trunk Road was built, linking what is now Kabul to various cities in the Punjab and the Gangetic Plain. Commerce, art, and architecture flourished (the latter especially exemplified in the construction of <a href="/wiki/Stupa" title="Stupa">stupas</a>) developed during this period). It reached its high point under Emperor Ashoka, whose edicts, roads, and rest stops were found throughout the subcontinent. Although the vast majority of them throughout the subcontinent were written in Prakrit, Afghanistan is notable for the inclusion of two Greek and Aramaic records alongside the court language of the Mauryan.</p>
<p>Inscriptions made by the Mauryan Emperor <a href="/wiki/Ashoka" title="Ashoka">Ashoka</a>, a fragment of <a href="/wiki/Edicts_of_Ashoka" title="Edicts of Ashoka">Edict 13</a> in Greek, as well as a full Edict, written in both Greek and Aramaic, has been discovered in <a href="/wiki/Kandahar" title="Kandahar">Kandahar</a>. It is said to be written in excellent Classical Greek, using sophisticated philosophical terms. In this Edict, Ashoka uses the word <a href="/wiki/Eusebeia" title="Eusebeia">Eusebeia</a> ("<a href="/wiki/Piety" title="Piety">Piety</a>") as the Greek translation for the ubiquitous "<a href="/wiki/Dharma" title="Dharma">Dharma</a>" of his other Edicts written in <a href="/wiki/Prakrit" title="Prakrit">Prakrit</a>:</p>
<dl>
<dd>"Ten years (of reign) having been completed, King Piodasses (Ashoka) made known (the doctrine of) Piety (<i>εὐσέβεια</i>, <a href="/wiki/Eusebeia" title="Eusebeia">Eusebeia</a>) to men; and from this moment he has made men more pious, and everything thrives throughout the whole world. And the king abstains from (killing) living beings, and other men and those who (are) huntsmen and fishermen of the king have desisted from hunting. And if some (were) intemperate, they have ceased from their intemperance as was in their power; and obedient to their father and mother and to the elders, in opposition to the past also in the future, by so acting on every occasion, they will live better and more happily." (Trans. by G.P. Carratelli<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>)</dd>
</dl>
<p>The last ruler in the region was probably <a href="/wiki/Subhagasena" class="mw-redirect" title="Subhagasena">Subhagasena</a> (<a href="/wiki/Sophagasenus" title="Sophagasenus">Sophagasenus</a> of <a href="/wiki/Polybius" title="Polybius">Polybius</a>), who, in all probability, belonged to the <a href="/wiki/Ashvaka" class="mw-redirect" title="Ashvaka">Ashvaka</a> (q.v.) background.</p>
<p>The region has been part of many empires in the past, from the Maurya Empire to the latest Afghan <a href="/wiki/Durrani_Empire" title="Durrani Empire">Durrani Empire</a>. Many famous historical figures are believed to have visited the area, including <a href="/wiki/Alexander_the_Great" title="Alexander the Great">Alexander the Great</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mahmud_Ghaznavi" class="mw-redirect" title="Mahmud Ghaznavi">Mahmud Ghaznavi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Xuanzang" title="Xuanzang">Xuanzang</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ibn_Battuta" title="Ibn Battuta">Ibn Battuta</a>, and others.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Archaeologists have dated to AD 800-1000 a fortification system overlooking a Muslim cemetery at Chaga Serai (near the Pech-Kunar confluence).<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Babur" title="Babur">Babur</a> wrote about Kunar in <i><a href="/wiki/Baburnama" title="Baburnama">Baburnama</a></i>. He claimed that there was a shrine in Kunar of a preacher and poet Said-Ul-Auliya <a href="/wiki/Mir_Sayyid_Ali_Hamadani" title="Mir Sayyid Ali Hamadani">Mir Sayyid Ali Hamadani</a>, who is said to have died there in 1384 AD (786 AH). Ali hamadani is founder of <a href="/wiki/Cashmere_wool" title="Cashmere wool">cashmere wool</a> first time he found soft goat in kashmir.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> He also describes agriproducts citron, oranges, coriander, orchards, strong yellow wines, and a burial custom wherein a woman whose corpse moved was considered to have done good things in life. He mentions <a href="/wiki/Chaghan-Sarai" class="mw-redirect" title="Chaghan-Sarai">Chaghan-Sarai</a> as a small town, and describes the towns folk as Muslims who mixed with the Kafirs of nearby <a href="/wiki/Kafiristan" title="Kafiristan">Kafiristan</a> and followed some of their customs. He also claims to have later captured the town, even as the Pech river Kafirs tried to help the Chaghan Sarai residents repel his attack.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Walter Hamilton's writing in 1828 mentions that the <a href="/wiki/Padishah" title="Padishah">padishah</a> of Cooner was joined in alliance with the neighboring <a href="/wiki/Nuristanis" title="Nuristanis">Kafirs (non Muslims) of Nuristan</a> in battles against Muslim invaders.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> The Kafirs were forcibly converted by <a href="/wiki/Abdur_Rahman_Khan" title="Abdur Rahman Khan">Abdur Rahman Khan</a> in the 1890s.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>Some British sources from the <a href="/wiki/Great_Game" class="mw-redirect" title="Great Game">Great Game</a> period (1800s) go into more detail about Kunar. For example, one from 1881 describes the various Kunar Chiefs and their internecine wars, the conflict with <a href="/wiki/Dost_Mohammad_Khan" class="mw-redirect" title="Dost Mohammad Khan">Dost Mohammad Khan</a>, their relations with the British, etc.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Names vary greatly, with Kunar sometimes being called Kama, or Kashkote, and the capital being listed as "Pashoot", which is not on modern maps.</p>

<p>An 1891 book described the Kunar region as split between the lower river area, controlled by Afghan chiefs, and the upstream area, where the Kunar river was actually referred to as the <a href="/wiki/Chitral" title="Chitral">Chitral</a> river. The major town of Chitral (in modern Pakistan) was the base of a badshah, who ruled under the <a href="/wiki/Royal_House_of_Jammu_and_Kashmir" class="mw-redirect" title="Royal House of Jammu and Kashmir">Maharajah of Kashmir</a><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="20th_century">20th century</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kunar_Province&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: 20th century">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>According to a US Army paper, the Pashtuns of Kunar and the Kafirs of Kunar/Nuristan eventually joined together in the 20th century. Fundamentalist religion came to the region in the 1950s but the heavy unification happened during the <a href="/wiki/Soviet%E2%80%93Afghan_War" title="Soviet–Afghan War">Soviet–Afghan War</a> (1979–88). Some of the first anti-government forces (lashkar) rose in the Kunar region.<sup id="cite_ref-ctc1_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ctc1-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>Kerala, a town near Asadabad, was the site of the 1979 Kerala Massacre, where the male population of a village was allegedly murdered by the <a href="/wiki/People%27s_Democratic_Party_of_Afghanistan" title="People's Democratic Party of Afghanistan">People's Democratic Party of Afghanistan</a> and its Soviet advisors.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>Later, over ten-thousand Soviet and Afghan communist troops invaded the region, resulting in a massive <a href="/wiki/Afghans_in_Pakistan" title="Afghans in Pakistan">refugee</a> flow of the populace into <a href="/wiki/Khyber_Pakhtunkhwa" title="Khyber Pakhtunkhwa">Khyber Pakhtunkhwa</a>, Pakistan.<sup id="cite_ref-ctc1_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ctc1-9">[9]</a></sup> There were <a href="/wiki/Spetsnaz" title="Spetsnaz">Spetsnaz</a> units based in <a href="/wiki/Asadabad,_Afghanistan" title="Asadabad, Afghanistan">Asadabad</a> (where the Pech meets the Kunar), in <a href="/wiki/Jalalabad" title="Jalalabad">Jalalabad</a> (where the Kunar meets the Kabul), and other towns.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Islamic_Unity_of_Afghanistan_Mujahideen" title="Islamic Unity of Afghanistan Mujahideen">major mujahideen groups</a> had representation in the area, and were successful enough to confine the Communist troops for the most part to their fortifications in the major towns of the Kunar valley.<sup id="cite_ref-ctc1_9-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ctc1-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>Eventually one of the Mujahideen leaders, <a href="/wiki/Jamil_al-Rahman" title="Jamil al-Rahman">Jamil al-Rahman</a>, formed <a href="/wiki/Jamaat_al_Dawa_al_Quran" title="Jamaat al Dawa al Quran">a movement</a> that had a very strict interpretation of Islam, along the lines of <a href="/wiki/Wahhabism" title="Wahhabism">Wahhabism</a> and/or <a href="/wiki/Salafism" class="mw-redirect" title="Salafism">Salafism</a>. He was supported by elements in <a href="/wiki/Saudi_Arabia" title="Saudi Arabia">Saudi Arabia</a>, and later attracted many Saudis and Egyptians who had come to Afghanistan to fight Jihad.</p>
<p>When the Soviets left in 1988, the leader of the Mujahideen group <a href="/wiki/Hizb-i-Islami" class="mw-redirect" title="Hizb-i-Islami">Hizb-i-Islami</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gulbuddin_Hekmatyar" title="Gulbuddin Hekmatyar">Gulbuddin Hekmatyar</a>, began to fight with Jamil al-Rahman over control of the area. Hekmatyar was victorious and eventually his troops sacked <a href="/wiki/Asadabad,_Afghanistan" title="Asadabad, Afghanistan">Asadabad</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-ctc1_9-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ctc1-9">[9]</a></sup> By 1996 however, <a href="/wiki/Mullah_Omar" class="mw-redirect" title="Mullah Omar">Mullah Omar</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Islamic_Emirate_of_Afghanistan" title="Islamic Emirate of Afghanistan">Taliban</a> had invaded Kunar and driven out Hekmatyar.<sup id="cite_ref-ctc1_9-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ctc1-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="21st_century">21st century</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kunar_Province&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: 21st century">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Further information: <a href="/wiki/International_Security_Assistance_Force" title="International Security Assistance Force">International Security Assistance Force</a> and <a href="/wiki/Presidency_of_Hamid_Karzai" title="Presidency of Hamid Karzai">Presidency of Hamid Karzai</a></div>

<p>After the <a href="/wiki/September_11_attacks" title="September 11 attacks">September 11 attacks</a> of 2001, Afghanistan was invaded by United States and other NATO countries provided direct support to the Northern Alliance forces fighting the Taliban regime, which was quickly toppled and fled to remote areas. It was part of the <a href="/wiki/War_on_Terror" title="War on Terror">War on Terror</a> and to assist the new <a href="/wiki/Presidency_of_Hamid_Karzai" title="Presidency of Hamid Karzai">government</a> that was led by <a href="/wiki/Hamid_Karzai" title="Hamid Karzai">Hamid Karzai</a>.</p>
<p>During the 19th century British military expeditions, the 1980s Soviet occupation, and the latest conflict, Kunar has been a favoured spot of <a href="/wiki/Taliban_insurgency" title="Taliban insurgency">insurgent groups</a>. Its impenetrable terrain, cave networks and border with the semi-autonomous Pakistani Khyber Pakhtunkhwa provides significant advantages for unconventional warfare and militant groups. The province is informally known as "Enemy Central" and "Indian Country" by Western armed forces serving in Afghanistan. Between January 2006 and March 2010 more than 65% of all insurgent incidents in Afghanistan occurred in Kunar province.</p>
<p>Like many of the mountainous eastern provinces of Afghanistan, the groups involved in armed conflict vary greatly in strength and purpose. Native <a href="/wiki/Taliban" title="Taliban">Taliban</a> forces mingle with foreign <a href="/wiki/Al-Qaeda" title="Al-Qaeda">al-Qaeda</a> fighters, while former mujahadeen militias, such as Hekmatyar's <a href="/wiki/Hezb-e-Islami_Gulbuddin" class="mw-redirect" title="Hezb-e-Islami Gulbuddin">Hezb-e-Islami Gulbuddin</a>, continue to operate as they did in the chaotic post-Soviet years. Another strong militia in the region is the <a href="/wiki/Hezbi_Islami" title="Hezbi Islami">Hezbi Islami</a> faction of the late <a href="/wiki/Mulavi_Younas_Khalis" class="mw-redirect" title="Mulavi Younas Khalis">Mulavi Younas Khalis</a>, who had his headquarters in neighboring <a href="/wiki/Nuristan_Province" title="Nuristan Province">Nuristan Province</a>.</p>
<p>Compounding the problems of the province is an extensive cross-border trade in contraband which includes lumber, semi-precious stones and other natural resources.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> This smuggling activity is often organized along tribal lines, and has led to intense deforestation in some areas.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The rugged border area has many illegal and hidden crossing points making Kunar a high traffic route for illegal arms transporting, insurgent movement as well as other smuggling activities as mentioned above.</p>

<p>The province, relatively small compared to others, has among the highest concentration of both US and Afghan security forces. Special Operations Forces operate extensively throughout the area.</p>
<p>As of September 2015 the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> has awarded twelve <a href="/wiki/Medal_of_Honor" title="Medal of Honor">Medals of Honor</a> for actions in Afghanistan. Six of these have been awarded for actions in Kunar province, and an additional four have been awarded for neighboring <a href="/wiki/Nuristan_Province" title="Nuristan Province">Nuristan Province</a>.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Operation_Mountain_Resolve" title="Operation Mountain Resolve">Operation Mountain Resolve</a> in 2003 involved the <a href="/wiki/10th_Mountain_Division_(United_States)" class="mw-redirect" title="10th Mountain Division (United States)">10th Mountain Division</a> air dropping into <a href="/wiki/Nuristan" class="mw-redirect" title="Nuristan">Nuristan</a> and traveling dozens of kilometers, including in Kunar, to fight what was termed "Anti Coalition Milita" (ACM) activity.<sup id="cite_ref-ctc1_9-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ctc1-9">[9]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2005 <a href="/wiki/Operation_Red_Wings" title="Operation Red Wings">Operation Red Wings</a> set out with the intent to disrupt ACM activity in the region in order to further aid the stabilization efforts of the region for the upcoming <a href="/wiki/Afghan_parliamentary_election,_2005" title="Afghan parliamentary election, 2005">September 18, 2005 Afghan National Parliamentary Elections</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-macmannis_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-macmannis-15">[15]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Ed_Darack_Victory_Point_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ed_Darack_Victory_Point-16">[16]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Ed_Darack_Marine_Corps_Gazette_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ed_Darack_Marine_Corps_Gazette-17">[17]</a></sup> Anti-Coalition Militia activity in the region was carried out at the time most notably by a small group led by a local man named <a href="/wiki/Ahmad_Shah_(Taliban)" title="Ahmad Shah (Taliban)">Ahmad Shah</a> (from <a href="/wiki/Nangarhar_Province" title="Nangarhar Province">Nangarhar Province</a>) who had aspirations of regional <a href="/wiki/Islamic_fundamentalist" class="mw-redirect" title="Islamic fundamentalist">Islamic fundamentalist</a> prominence, hence he and his small group would be one of the primary targets of the operation. A team of four Navy SEALs, tasked for <a href="/wiki/Reconnaissance" title="Reconnaissance">surveillance and reconnaissance</a> of a group of structures known to be used by <a href="/wiki/Ahmad_Shah_(Taliban)" title="Ahmad Shah (Taliban)">Ahmad Shah</a> and his men, fell into an ambush by Shah and his group just hours after inserting by <a href="/wiki/Fastrope" class="mw-redirect" title="Fastrope">fastrope</a> from an <a href="/wiki/MH-47" class="mw-redirect" title="MH-47">MH-47</a> helicopter in the area.<sup id="cite_ref-Ed_Darack_Victory_Point_16-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ed_Darack_Victory_Point-16">[16]</a></sup> Three of the four SEALs were killed in the ambush; a <a href="/wiki/Quick_reaction_force" title="Quick reaction force">quick reaction force</a> helicopter sent in for their aid was subsequently shot down with an <a href="/wiki/RPG-7" title="RPG-7">RPG-7 rocket propelled grenade</a> by one of Shah's men, 19 American Forces were killed when their <a href="/wiki/CH-47_Chinook" class="mw-redirect" title="CH-47 Chinook">CH-47 Chinook</a> <a href="/wiki/Helicopter" title="Helicopter">helicopter</a> was shot down, representing the second biggest loss of American forces since their invasion of the country.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>According to Pakistan's <a href="/wiki/Interior_Minister_of_Pakistan" class="mw-redirect" title="Interior Minister of Pakistan">Interior Minister</a> <a href="/wiki/Rehman_Malik" title="Rehman Malik">Rehman Malik</a>, <a href="/wiki/Osama_bin_Laden" title="Osama bin Laden">Osama bin Laden</a> was most likely hiding in Kunar Province in the spring and summer of 2009: "According to our information Osama is in Afghanistan, probably Kunar, as most of the activities against Pakistan are being directed from Kunar."<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> Bin Laden was later found and killed in <a href="/wiki/Abbottabad" title="Abbottabad">Abbottabad</a>, Pakistan, contrary to what the Pakistani government had previously stated. He had been living there since 2005.</p>

<p>The current <a href="/wiki/List_of_governors_of_Kunar" title="List of governors of Kunar">governor</a> of Kunar province is <a href="/w/index.php?title=Wahidullah_Kalimzai&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Wahidullah Kalimzai (page does not exist)">Wahidullah Kalimzai</a>. His predecessor was <a href="/wiki/Shuja_ul-Mulk_Jalala" title="Shuja ul-Mulk Jalala">Shuja ul-Mulk Jalala</a>.</p>
<p>The city of <a href="/wiki/Asadabad,_Afghanistan" title="Asadabad, Afghanistan">Asadabad</a> is the capital of Kunar. All <a href="/wiki/Law_enforcement_in_Afghanistan" title="Law enforcement in Afghanistan">law enforcement</a> activities throughout the province are controlled by the <a href="/wiki/Afghan_National_Police" title="Afghan National Police">Afghan National Police</a> (ANP). Kunar's border with neighboring <a href="/wiki/Khyber_Pakhtunkhwa" title="Khyber Pakhtunkhwa">Khyber Pakhtunkhwa</a> province of Pakistan is monitored and protected by the <a href="/wiki/Afghan_Border_Police" title="Afghan Border Police">Afghan Border Police</a> (ABP), which is part of the ANP. The border is called the <a href="/wiki/Durand_Line" title="Durand Line">Durand Line</a> and is known to be one of the most dangerous in the world due to heavy militant activities and illegal smugglings. A provincial police chief is assigned to lead both the ANP and ABP. The police chief represents the <a href="/wiki/Ministry_of_the_Interior_(Afghanistan)" class="mw-redirect" title="Ministry of the Interior (Afghanistan)">Ministry of the Interior</a> in <a href="/wiki/Kabul" title="Kabul">Kabul</a>. The ANP is backed by other <a href="/wiki/Afghan_National_Security_Forces" title="Afghan National Security Forces">Afghan National Security Forces</a> (ANSF), including the NATO-led forces.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>

<p>According to latest statistics, the total population of Kunar province was estimated to be around 428,800.<sup id="cite_ref-cso_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cso-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Around 96% of the population of Kunar lives in rural districts while 4% lives in urban areas.<sup id="cite_ref-mrrd_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mrrd-20">[20]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reconstruction_and_international_assistance">Reconstruction and international assistance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kunar_Province&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Reconstruction and international assistance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Saw_Bridge_in_Kunar.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d3/Saw_Bridge_in_Kunar.jpg/220px-Saw_Bridge_in_Kunar.jpg" width="220" height="148" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d3/Saw_Bridge_in_Kunar.jpg/330px-Saw_Bridge_in_Kunar.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d3/Saw_Bridge_in_Kunar.jpg/440px-Saw_Bridge_in_Kunar.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1500" data-file-height="1009" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Saw_Bridge_in_Kunar.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A bridge in Kunar province.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Asadabad,_Afghanistan" title="Asadabad, Afghanistan">Asadabad</a> hosts both an American <a href="/wiki/Provincial_Reconstruction_Team" title="Provincial Reconstruction Team">Provincial Reconstruction Team</a> at nearby Forward Operating Base Wright and a <a href="/wiki/UNAMA" class="mw-redirect" title="UNAMA">UNAMA</a> development office. Additionally, representatives of the United States Agency for International Development (USAID), US State and Agriculture Departments advise the local government. A US Army Agribusiness Development Team (ADT) was deployed to the province in late 2009. Successive ADTs continue to serve in the province.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_media">In media</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kunar_Province&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: In media">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The 2010 documentary <i><a href="/wiki/Restrepo_(film)" title="Restrepo (film)">Restrepo</a></i> was filmed in the <a href="/wiki/Korengal_Valley" class="mw-redirect" title="Korengal Valley">Korengal Valley</a> of Kunar Province.</p>
<p>The book <i>Siren's Song: The Allure of War</i> was published in 2012. It depicts the story of an American platoon at COP Honaker Miracle, Pesh valley, Kunar Province.</p>
<p>Documentary author James F. Christ has published numerous titles about the fighting in Kunar and Nuristan provinces, mainly from 2005 to 2007 with the 10th Mountain Division and Afghan National Army ETT advisors.</p>
