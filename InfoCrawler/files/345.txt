<p>The <b>March of the Nordgau</b> (<a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a>: <span lang="de" xml:lang="de"><i>Markgrafschaft Nordgau</i></span>) or <b>Bavarian Nordgau</b> (<i>Bayerischer Nordgau</i>) was a medieval administrative unit (<i><a href="/wiki/Gau_(territory)" title="Gau (territory)">Gau</a></i>) on the frontier of the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Germany" title="Kingdom of Germany">German</a> <a href="/wiki/Stem_duchy" title="Stem duchy">stem duchy</a> of <a href="/wiki/Duchy_of_Bavaria" title="Duchy of Bavaria">Bavaria</a>. It comprised the region north of the <a href="/wiki/Danube" title="Danube">Danube</a> and <a href="/wiki/Regensburg" title="Regensburg">Regensburg</a> (Ratisbon), roughly covered by the modern <a href="/wiki/Upper_Palatinate" title="Upper Palatinate">Upper Palatinate</a> stretching up to the river <a href="/wiki/Main_(river)" title="Main (river)">Main</a><sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> and, especially after 1061, into the <a href="/wiki/Egerland" title="Egerland">Egerland</a> on the border with <a href="/wiki/Duchy_of_Bohemia" title="Duchy of Bohemia">Bohemia</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=March_of_the_Nordgau&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/History_of_Bavaria" title="History of Bavaria">History of Bavaria</a></div>
<p>The area east of <a href="/wiki/Franconia" title="Franconia">Franconia</a> proper up to the <a href="/wiki/Bohemian_Forest" title="Bohemian Forest">Bohemian Forest</a> had been settled by Germanic <a href="/wiki/Varisci" title="Varisci">Varisci</a> and <a href="/wiki/Armalausi" title="Armalausi">Armalausi</a> tribes in ancient times; after the <a href="/wiki/Migration_Period" title="Migration Period">Migration Period</a> it was occupied by the <a href="/wiki/Merovingian_dynasty" title="Merovingian dynasty">Merovingian</a> king <a href="/wiki/Chlodio" title="Chlodio">Chlodio</a>. From the mid 6th century onwards, the region was Christianised by several <a href="/wiki/Episcopi_vagantes" title="Episcopi vagantes">wandering bishops</a>, among them Saints <a href="/wiki/Saint_Boniface" title="Saint Boniface">Boniface</a> and <a href="/wiki/Emmeram_of_Regensburg" title="Emmeram of Regensburg">Emmeram of Regensburg</a>. In 739, the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic_Diocese_of_Regensburg" title="Roman Catholic Diocese of Regensburg">Diocese of Regensburg</a> was founded. The great fortress of <a href="/wiki/Wogastisburg" class="mw-redirect" title="Wogastisburg">Wogastisburg</a> was built at the insistence of <a href="/wiki/Saint_Boniface" title="Saint Boniface">Saint Boniface</a> by <a href="/wiki/Charles_Martel" title="Charles Martel">Charles Martel</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Thompson.2C_619_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Thompson.2C_619-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>When King <a href="/wiki/Charlemagne" title="Charlemagne">Charlemagne</a> deposed Duke <a href="/wiki/Tassilo_III,_Duke_of_Bavaria" title="Tassilo III, Duke of Bavaria">Tassilo III of Bavaria</a> in 788, he entered the Nordgau and brought the <a href="/wiki/Carolingian_Empire" title="Carolingian Empire">Carolingian Empire</a> into contact with <a href="/wiki/Bohemia" title="Bohemia">Bohemia</a> permanently. For this reason, the incorporated Nordgau has been called the <b>Bohemian March</b> on occasion, although this term is also reserved for the later <a href="/wiki/Margraviate_of_Moravia" title="Margraviate of Moravia">Margraviate of Moravia</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Thompson.2C_619_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Thompson.2C_619-2">[2]</a></sup> By a 806 deed issued at <a href="/wiki/Thionville" title="Thionville">Thionville</a>, Charlemagne separated the Bavarian lands left of the Danube including the ducal residences of Regensburg and <a href="/wiki/Lauterhofen" title="Lauterhofen">Lauterhofen</a>, called <i>pagum, qui dicitur Northgowe</i>. His chancellor <a href="/wiki/Einhard" title="Einhard">Einhard</a> reached the confirmation by <a href="/wiki/Pope_Leo_III" title="Pope Leo III">Pope Leo III</a>.</p>
<p>The Nordgau was again separated from Bavaria following the death of the <a href="/wiki/Luitpoldings" title="Luitpoldings">Luitpolding</a> duke <a href="/wiki/Arnulf,_Duke_of_Bavaria" title="Arnulf, Duke of Bavaria">Arnulf</a> in 937. There has been some confusion over whether or not the Nordgau was separated from Bavaria at this date or only as late as 976, when the East Franconain count <a href="/wiki/Berthold_of_Schweinfurt" title="Berthold of Schweinfurt">Berthold of Schweinfurt</a>, who had helped to suppress the rebellion of the Bavarian duke <a href="/wiki/Henry_II,_Duke_of_Bavaria" title="Henry II, Duke of Bavaria">Henry the Wrangler</a> against Emperor <a href="/wiki/Otto_II,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Otto II, Holy Roman Emperor">Otto II</a>, was appointed <a href="/wiki/Margrave" title="Margrave">margrave</a> to administrate the region as a distinct <a href="/wiki/March_(territorial_entity)" title="March (territorial entity)">march</a>.</p>
<p>Likewise, Berthold's son Count <a href="/wiki/Henry_of_Schweinfurt" title="Henry of Schweinfurt">Henry of Schweinfurt</a> appeared as Margrave of the Nordgau in 994. He backed the <a href="/wiki/Prince-elector" title="Prince-elector">election</a> of Duke <a href="/wiki/Henry_II,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Henry II, Holy Roman Emperor">Henry IV of Bavaria</a> as <a href="/wiki/King_of_the_Romans" title="King of the Romans">King of the Romans</a> in 1002, however, he the new king (Henry II) responded with ingratitude. Count Henry soon after joined a revolt (<i>Schweinfurter Fehde</i>) led by the Polish ruler <a href="/wiki/Boles%C5%82aw_I_the_Brave" title="Bolesław I the Brave">Bolesław I the Brave</a>, after which he was deposed and arrested. In 1004, King Henry II installed his brother-in-law Count <a href="/wiki/Henry_V,_Duke_of_Bavaria" title="Henry V, Duke of Bavaria">Henry of Luxembourg</a> as Bavarian duke and gave the temporal authority of the Nordgau region over to the <a href="/wiki/Prince-Bishopric_of_Bamberg" title="Prince-Bishopric of Bamberg">Bishopric of Bamberg</a>, which he heavily favoured throughout his career. However, the margravial title survived in a succession of families in the region. Count Henry's possessions were partly restored and his descendants used the title "Margrave of Schweinfurt".</p>
<p>Throughout the 11th and 12th centuries, the Nordgau was a pathway for invading armies from Bohemia and <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Hungary" title="Kingdom of Hungary">Hungary</a> and for the countering armies of the <a href="/wiki/Holy_Roman_Empire" title="Holy Roman Empire">Holy Roman Empire</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> In the mid 11th century, the <a href="/wiki/Salian_dynasty" title="Salian dynasty">Salian</a> emperor <a href="/wiki/Henry_III,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Henry III, Holy Roman Emperor">Henry III</a> had an Imperial castle erected at <a href="/wiki/Cham,_Germany" title="Cham, Germany">Cham</a> to encounter the forces of Duke <a href="/wiki/Bretislaus_I,_Duke_of_Bohemia" class="mw-redirect" title="Bretislaus I, Duke of Bohemia">Bretislaus I of Bohemia</a>. It became the residence of the <i>Rapotonen</i> noble family, who eventually received the margravial title by royal grant about 1073, passing it down as "Margrave of <a href="/wiki/Vohburg_an_der_Donau" class="mw-redirect" title="Vohburg an der Donau">Vohburg</a>" to their descendants.</p>
<p>Margrave <a href="/wiki/Diepold_III,_Margrave_of_Vohburg" title="Diepold III, Margrave of Vohburg">Diepold III</a> (d. 1146) was a loyal supporter of Emperor <a href="/wiki/Henry_V,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Henry V, Holy Roman Emperor">Henry V</a> and founder of the abbeys in <a href="/wiki/Reichenbach_Abbey_(Bavaria)" title="Reichenbach Abbey (Bavaria)">Reichenbach</a> and <a href="/wiki/Waldsassen_Abbey" title="Waldsassen Abbey">Waldsassen</a>. From about 1125, he had the fortress of <a href="/wiki/Cheb" title="Cheb">Eger</a> (Cheb) erected at the border with Bohemia. His daughter <a href="/wiki/Adelaide_of_Vohburg" title="Adelaide of Vohburg">Adelaide</a> married the <a href="/wiki/Duchy_of_Swabia" title="Duchy of Swabia">Swabian</a> duke and later emperor <a href="/wiki/Frederick_I,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Frederick I, Holy Roman Emperor">Frederick Barbarossa</a> in 1147.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Egerland" title="Egerland">Egerland</a> was incorporated as a <a href="/wiki/House_of_Hohenstaufen" class="mw-redirect" title="House of Hohenstaufen">Hohenstaufen</a> imperial territory upon Diepold's death and the town of Eger itself was inherited by Emperor Frederick Barbarossa in 1167. It attained the <a href="/wiki/Imperial_immediacy" title="Imperial immediacy">immediate</a> status of an <a href="/wiki/Imperial_city" class="mw-redirect" title="Imperial city">Imperial city</a>, while large parts of the Norgau were controlled by the Bavarian <a href="/wiki/House_of_Wittelsbach" title="House of Wittelsbach">House of Wittelsbach</a> from the mid 13th century onwards. In 1322 the Wittelsbach emperor <a href="/wiki/Louis_IV,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Louis IV, Holy Roman Emperor">Louis IV</a> gave the Egerland in pawn to the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Bohemia" title="Kingdom of Bohemia">Kingdom of Bohemia</a>. By the 1329 <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Pavia_(1329)" title="Treaty of Pavia (1329)">Treaty of Pavia</a>, he ceded the remaining Nordgau region to his <a href="/wiki/Electoral_Palatinate" title="Electoral Palatinate">Palatinate</a> nephews <a href="/wiki/Rudolf_II,_Count_Palatine_of_the_Rhine" title="Rudolf II, Count Palatine of the Rhine">Rudolf II</a> and <a href="/wiki/Rupert_I,_Elector_Palatine" title="Rupert I, Elector Palatine">Rupert I</a>, whereafter it became known as the "Upper Palatinate".</p>
