<p><b>St. Sebaldus Church</b> (<i>St. Sebald</i>, <i>Sebalduskirche</i>) is a <a href="/wiki/Medieval_era" class="mw-redirect" title="Medieval era">medieval</a> <a href="/wiki/Church_(building)" title="Church (building)">church</a> in <a href="/wiki/Nuremberg" title="Nuremberg">Nuremberg</a>, <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a>. Along with <a href="/wiki/Frauenkirche,_Nuremberg" title="Frauenkirche, Nuremberg">Frauenkirche</a> (Our Lady's Church) and <a href="/wiki/St._Lorenz,_Nuremberg" title="St. Lorenz, Nuremberg">St. Lorenz</a>, it is one of the most important churches of the city, and also one of the oldest. It is located at the Albrecht-Dürer-Platz, in front of the old city hall. It takes its name from <a href="/wiki/Sebaldus" title="Sebaldus">Sebaldus</a>, an 8th-century <a href="/wiki/Hermit" title="Hermit">hermit</a> and <a href="/wiki/Missionary" title="Missionary">missionary</a> and <a href="/wiki/Patron_saint" title="Patron saint">patron saint</a> of Nuremberg. It has been a <a href="/wiki/Lutheran" class="mw-redirect" title="Lutheran">Lutheran</a> parish church since the <a href="/wiki/Reformation" title="Reformation">Reformation</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=St._Sebaldus_Church,_Nuremberg&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The construction of the building began in 1225. the church achieved parish church status in 1255 and was completed by 1273-75. It was originally built as a <a href="/wiki/Romanesque_architecture" title="Romanesque architecture">Romanesque</a> <a href="/wiki/Basilica" title="Basilica">basilica</a> with two <a href="/wiki/Choir_(architecture)" title="Choir (architecture)">choirs</a>. During the 14th century several important changes to the construction were made: first the side <a href="/wiki/Aisle" title="Aisle">aisles</a> were widened and the <a href="/wiki/Steeple_(architecture)" class="mw-redirect" title="Steeple (architecture)">steeples</a> made higher (1309–1345), then the late gothic hall <a href="/wiki/Chancel" title="Chancel">chancel</a> was built (1358–1379). The two towers were added in the 15th century. In the middle 17th century galleries were added and the interior was remodelled in the <a href="/wiki/Baroque" title="Baroque">Baroque</a> fashion. The church suffered serious damage during <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a> and was subsequently restored. Some of the old interior undamaged includes the Shrine of St. Sebaldus, works by <a href="/wiki/Veit_Stoss" title="Veit Stoss">Veit Stoss</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Stained_glass" title="Stained glass">stained glass</a> windows. In the church the famous epitaph of the <a href="/wiki/Tucher_von_Simmelsdorf" title="Tucher von Simmelsdorf">Tucher family</a> can be found.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Organ">Organ</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=St._Sebaldus_Church,_Nuremberg&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Organ">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Nuremberg_-_St._Sebald_church_-_interior_13.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5f/Nuremberg_-_St._Sebald_church_-_interior_13.JPG/220px-Nuremberg_-_St._Sebald_church_-_interior_13.JPG" width="220" height="293" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5f/Nuremberg_-_St._Sebald_church_-_interior_13.JPG/330px-Nuremberg_-_St._Sebald_church_-_interior_13.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5f/Nuremberg_-_St._Sebald_church_-_interior_13.JPG/440px-Nuremberg_-_St._Sebald_church_-_interior_13.JPG 2x" data-file-width="2448" data-file-height="3264" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Nuremberg_-_St._Sebald_church_-_interior_13.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The organ of 1975 by Peter of Köln</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The church had an organ by the 14th century, and another by the 15th. The main organ had been built in 1440–41 by <a href="/wiki/Heinrich_Traxdorf" title="Heinrich Traxdorf">Heinrich Traxdorf</a>, who also built two small organs for Nuremberg's Frauenkirche. Until its destruction in the 20th century it was one of the oldest playable organs in the world, and all the more notable because Traxdorf was one of the first organ builders to depart from the gothic Blockwerk organ by dividing the windchests and separating the front <a href="/wiki/Organ_stop" title="Organ stop">stops</a> into Flute (Principal) and Octave (see <a href="/wiki/Pipe_organ" title="Pipe organ">pipe organ</a>). The Traxdorf organ was rebuilt in 1691. The modified case was destroyed by the <a href="/wiki/Allies_of_World_War_II" title="Allies of World War II">Allied forces</a> during a bombing raid on 2 January 1945.</p>
<p>The new 4 manual, 122 rank, 84 stop organ by <i>Peter</i> of Köln was installed in 1975.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Organists">Organists</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=St._Sebaldus_Church,_Nuremberg&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Organists">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The position of organist of St. Sebaldus was the most important one of this kind in Nuremberg, and several important composers occupied this post. Organists who worked at St. Sebaldus include the following (almost all held the post until their death, except where stated otherwise):</p>
<ul>
<li>1446-1450: <a href="/wiki/Conrad_Paumann" title="Conrad Paumann">Conrad Paumann</a>, secretly left for <a href="/wiki/Munich" title="Munich">Munich</a></li>
<li>1522-1524: Hans Seber</li>
<li>1564-1561: Sebald Heyden</li>
<li>1567-1571: Hans Haiden</li>
<li>1596-1616: Hans Christoph Haiden, dismissed for <a href="/wiki/Adultery" title="Adultery">adultery</a></li>
<li>1616-1618: <a href="/wiki/Kasper_Hassler" title="Kasper Hassler">Kasper Hassler</a></li>
<li>1618-1634: <a href="/wiki/Johann_Staden" title="Johann Staden">Johann Staden</a></li>
<li>1634-1658: Valentin Dretzel</li>
<li>1658-1686: Paul Hainlein</li>
<li>1686-1695: <a href="/wiki/Georg_Caspar_Wecker" title="Georg Caspar Wecker">Georg Caspar Wecker</a></li>
<li>1695-1706: <a href="/wiki/Johann_Pachelbel" title="Johann Pachelbel">Johann Pachelbel</a></li>
<li>1706-1719: Johann Siegmund Richter</li>
<li>1719-1764: <a href="/wiki/Wilhelm_Hieronymus_Pachelbel" title="Wilhelm Hieronymus Pachelbel">Wilhelm Hieronymus Pachelbel</a> (Johann's son)</li>
<li>1764-1775: <a href="/wiki/Cornelius_Heinrich_Dretzel" title="Cornelius Heinrich Dretzel">Cornelius Heinrich Dretzel</a></li>
<li>1783-1810: Egidius Bauer</li>
<li>1969-1991: <a href="/w/index.php?title=Werner_Jacob&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Werner Jacob (page does not exist)">Werner Jacob</a> (left)</li>
</ul>
