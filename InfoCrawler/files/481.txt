<p><b>Langwasser</b> is a district of <a href="/wiki/Nuremberg" title="Nuremberg">Nuremberg</a> in the southeastern area of the city. It was developed as a prototype of the <a href="/wiki/Satellite_town" title="Satellite town">satellite town</a> concept in the 1960s and is primarily a suburban residential area. The name Langwasser (translated as "long water") comes from a small stream bordering the area on its eastern edge.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Location">Location</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Langwasser&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Location">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Langwasser is located in the southeastern area of Nuremberg.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Langwasser&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>At the beginning of the 20th century, the area that would become Langwasser was heavily wooded and part of the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Nuremberg_Reichswald&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Nuremberg Reichswald (page does not exist)">Imperial Forest</a>. After devastating forest fires between 1917 and 1919 the area was cleared and used for farming.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Nazi_era">Nazi era</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Langwasser&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Nazi era">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Prior to <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>, the area which had been cleared by fire became an important site for the <a href="/wiki/Nazi" class="mw-redirect" title="Nazi">Nazi</a> movement. Beginning in 1934 it was the site of various tent cities and encampments. The area originally housed a tent encampment of the <a href="/wiki/Reich_Labor_Service" class="mw-redirect" title="Reich Labor Service">Reich Labor Service</a> (RAD) and later the <a href="/wiki/Hitler_Youth" title="Hitler Youth">Hitler Youth</a> (HJ). Permanent camps for the SS, SA, HJ, and RAD were built near the Nuremberg Rally Grounds. The Langwasser camp, with space for 200,000, was the largest. At the outbreak of World War II, party rallies ceased and the compound was converted into a <a href="/wiki/Prisoner-of-war_camp" title="Prisoner-of-war camp">prisoner-of-war camp</a> known as <a href="/wiki/Stalag_XIII-D" title="Stalag XIII-D">Stalag XIII-D</a>, which housed up to 150,000 prisoners until closing in 1940. In this camp during August 1940, prisoners of war celebrated a "special Olympics" called International Prisoner-of-War Olympic Games where prisoners of Belgium, France, Great Britain, Norway, Poland, Russia and Yugoslavia took part.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> United States military records<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> report that 6,676 American <a href="/wiki/POW" class="mw-redirect" title="POW">POWs</a> were transferred there late in the war.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Post-war_period">Post-war period</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Langwasser&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Post-war period">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The history of Langwasser as a district begins after the Second World War in 1949. For ten years the area was a mixture of <a href="/wiki/Refugee_camp" title="Refugee camp">refugee camps</a> and temporary housing developments. Construction of the first permanent housing developments did not begin until the 1950s. Many German refugees from <a href="/wiki/Silesia" title="Silesia">Silesia</a> and from the <a href="/wiki/Sudetenland" title="Sudetenland">Sudetenland</a> made Langwasser their new home.</p>
<p>The facilities were used as <a href="/wiki/Internment" title="Internment">internment</a> camp by the US occupation army. During the <a href="/wiki/Nuremberg_trials" title="Nuremberg trials">Nuremberg trials</a> 21 witnesses - members of the <a href="/wiki/Schutzstaffel" title="Schutzstaffel">SS</a> - were moved to the camp on July 17, 1946, from the Palace of Justice to the camp.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Modern_times">Modern times</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Langwasser&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Modern times">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The modern history of Langwasser begins with the decision of the city of Nuremberg to develop a planned community in the area marketed with the slogan "living in the country". An architectural competition was held in 1956, with construction beginning the following year. Construction was not fully completed until the 1990s.</p>
<p>A part of Langwasser Nordost around Euckenweg is apparently carfree, although with underground garages.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
