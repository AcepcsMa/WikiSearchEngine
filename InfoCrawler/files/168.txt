			<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Oreste Pucciani</h1>
									<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
									<div id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
								<div id="contentSub"></div>
												<div id="jump-to-nav" class="mw-jump">

				</div>
				<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p><b>Oreste Francesco Pucciani</b> (April 7, 1916 – April 28, 1999) was a pioneer teacher of <a href="/wiki/Jean-Paul_Sartre" title="Jean-Paul Sartre">Jean-Paul Sartre</a>'s philosophy at <a href="/wiki/UCLA" class="mw-redirect" title="UCLA">UCLA</a>. He was the last partner of Rudi Gernreich, fashion designer, and at the latter's death, established the ACLU Rudi Gernreich-Oreste Pucciani Endowment Fund to support the fight for LGBT rights.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Oreste_Pucciani&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Oreste Francesco Pucciani was born in <a href="/wiki/Cleveland,_Ohio" class="mw-redirect" title="Cleveland, Ohio">Cleveland, Ohio</a>, on April 7, 1916.<sup id="cite_ref-Papers_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Papers-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>He attended public schools in Cleveland and graduated from Adelbert College, Western Reserve University (later <a href="/wiki/Case_Western_Reserve_University" title="Case Western Reserve University">Case Western Reserve University</a>) in 1939. He was part of the <a href="/wiki/Phi_Beta_Kappa_Society" title="Phi Beta Kappa Society">Phi Beta Kappa</a>. He then completed his education with a doctorate in Romance languages from <a href="/wiki/Harvard_University" title="Harvard University">Harvard University</a> in 1943. His doctoral dissertation analyzed <a href="/wiki/Walt_Whitman" title="Walt Whitman">Walt Whitman</a>'s reception in France.<sup id="cite_ref-Papers_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Papers-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>From 1946 to 1947, Pucciani obtained a Sheldon Traveling Fellowship and studied in France and Italy. He met <a href="/wiki/Jean-Paul_Sartre" title="Jean-Paul Sartre">Jean-Paul Sartre</a> and <a href="/wiki/Simone_de_Beauvoir" title="Simone de Beauvoir">Simone de Beauvoir</a>, at the time Sartre's companion.<sup id="cite_ref-Papers_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Papers-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Career">Career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Oreste_Pucciani&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1947 Oreste Puccini returned to the United States and at first lectured at Harvard, and in 1948 he joined <a href="/wiki/University_of_California,_Los_Angeles" title="University of California, Los Angeles">University of California, Los Angeles</a>, at first as Assistant Professor of French. In 1954 he became Associate Professor and in 1960 he was named full Professor. From 1961 to 1966 he was chairman of the French department, and became professor emeritus in 1979.<sup id="cite_ref-Papers_1-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Papers-1">[1]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Obituary_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Obituary-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1954 he published <i>The French Theater since 1930</i>, a still used anthology of French theater between <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a> and <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>. In 1961 he published a translation of <a href="/wiki/Jean_Racine" title="Jean Racine">Jean Racine</a>'s <i>Phédre</i>: Pucciani was a pioneer teacher on Jean-Paul Sartre's philosophy at UCLA even before Sartre was translated into English.<sup id="cite_ref-Papers_1-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Papers-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Pucciani studied with <a href="/wiki/Emile_B._De_Sauz%C3%A9" title="Emile B. De Sauzé">Emile B. De Sauzé</a> at Adelbert College, and was a supporter of Sauzé's "<a href="/wiki/Direct_method_(education)" title="Direct method (education)">direct method</a>" of language teaching, which he introduced in the late 1950s to the UCLA French Department's language program: he banned the use of English from his classrooms; although today this is common practice, at the time was a revolutionary method in the United States, where foreign languages were taught using English translations and learning by memory already built sentences.<sup id="cite_ref-Obituary_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Obituary-2">[2]</a></sup> In 1967 Pucciani together with Jacqueline Hamel published <i>Langue et Langage</i>, a textbook describing the experience of the UCLA French Department with the direct method.<sup id="cite_ref-Papers_1-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Papers-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1965 Pucciani was awarded the Knight's Cross of the <a href="/wiki/Legion_of_Honour" title="Legion of Honour">Legion of Honour</a> by the French government for his services to French language and culture.<sup id="cite_ref-Papers_1-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Papers-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life">Personal life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Oreste_Pucciani&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Personal life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Pucciani was the last partner of <a href="/wiki/Rudi_Gernreich" title="Rudi Gernreich">Rudi Gernreich</a>, fashion designer, and at the latter death, established the ACLU Rudi Gernreich-Oreste Pucciani Endowment Fund to support the fight for <a href="/wiki/LGBT_rights_in_the_United_States" title="LGBT rights in the United States">LGBT rights</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Papers_1-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Papers-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>He died in Los Angeles on April 28, 1999.<sup id="cite_ref-Papers_1-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Papers-1">[1]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Obituary_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Obituary-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
