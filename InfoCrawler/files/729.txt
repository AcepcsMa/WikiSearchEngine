			<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Caritas Pirckheimer</h1>

<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:N%C3%BCrnberg_K%C3%B6nigstra%C3%9Fe_70_Ecke_Luitpoldstra%C3%9Fe_Caritas_Pirckheimer.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Caritas Pirckheimer</div>
</div>
</div>
<p><b>Caritas Pirckheimer</b> (born 21 March 1467, <a href="/wiki/Eichst%C3%A4tt" title="Eichstätt">Eichstätt</a> - died 19 August 1532, <a href="/wiki/Nuremberg" title="Nuremberg">Nuremberg</a>) was <a href="/wiki/Abbess" title="Abbess">Abbess</a> of Saint Clara's convent in <a href="/wiki/Nuremberg" title="Nuremberg">Nuremberg</a> at the time of the <a href="/wiki/Protestant_Reformation" class="mw-redirect" title="Protestant Reformation">Reformation</a>, which she opposed due to the threat posed by <a href="/wiki/Martin_Luther" title="Martin Luther">Martin Luther</a> to Catholic houses of worship and religious buildings, including her own convent.</p>
<p>Born as <b>Barbara Pirckheimer</b>, she was the eldest of 12 children, nine of whom would survive to adulthood and one of whom was the prominent humanist <a href="/wiki/Willibald_Pirckheimer" title="Willibald Pirckheimer">Willibald Pirckheimer</a>. Until her mother's death in 1488, she reportedly received a <a href="/wiki/Humanism" title="Humanism">humanist</a> education at home, where she became fluent in Latin. At the age of 12 she went to school at the Franciscan monastery of Saint Clara at <a href="/wiki/Nuremberg" title="Nuremberg">Nuremberg</a>. When she was about 16 years old, she joined the order, taking the name of <b>Caritas</b> (or <b>Charitas</b>).</p>
<p>Already in her 50s at the time, Sister Caritas apparently received support in her struggle for the survival of the monastery from <a href="/wiki/Philipp_Melanchthon" class="mw-redirect" title="Philipp Melanchthon">Philipp Melanchthon</a>, formerly a close friend of Luther.</p>
<p>She had maintained a chronicle during her abbacy of events at the monastery during the period of upheaval (1524–1528), <i>"including letters to and from the city council and written transcripts of conversations. Later a final section was added, perhaps written after her death but including passages from Caritas' later letters."</i><sup id="cite_ref-infionline_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-infionline-1">[1]</a></sup> The fate of the convent, as it transpires, was that the women of Saint Clara's were allowed to stay in the monastery until their deaths but no novices were to be received. By 1591 the monastery and cloister had ceased to exist as Catholic houses of worship.<sup id="cite_ref-infionline_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-infionline-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
