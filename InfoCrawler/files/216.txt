<p>The <b>Battle of Polygon Wood</b> took place during the second phase of the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Passchendaele" title="Battle of Passchendaele">Third Battle of Ypres</a> in <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a> and was fought near <a href="/wiki/Ypres" title="Ypres">Ypres</a> in <a href="/wiki/Belgium" title="Belgium">Belgium</a> <span class="nowrap">26 September</span> &#160;– <span class="nowrap">3 October</span>   1917, in the area from the Menin Road to Polygon Wood and thence north, to the area beyond St Julien.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[a]</a></sup> Much of the woodland had been destroyed by the huge quantity of shellfire from both sides since 16 July and the area had changed hands several times. General <a href="/wiki/Herbert_Plumer" class="mw-redirect" title="Herbert Plumer">Herbert Plumer</a> continued the series of British general attacks with limited objectives. The British attacks were led by lines of skirmishers, followed by small infantry columns organised in depth, (a formation which had been adopted by the Fifth Army in August) with a vastly increased amount of artillery support, the infantry advancing behind five layers of creeping bombardment on the Second Army front.</p>
<p>The advance was planned to cover 1,000–1,500&#160;yd (910–1,370&#160;m) and stop on reverse slopes which were easier to defend, enclosing ground which gave observation of German reinforcement routes and counter-attack assembly areas. Preparations were then made swiftly to defeat German counter-attacks, by mopping-up and consolidating the captured ground with defences in depth. The attack inflicted a severe blow on the German 4th Army, causing many losses, capturing a significant portion of the <span lang="de" xml:lang="de"><i>Flandern I Stellung</i></span>, which threatened the German hold on Broodseinde ridge. The better weather continued to benefit the British attackers by drying the ground, raising mist which obscured British infantry attacks made around dawn, then clearing to reveal German <span lang="de" xml:lang="de"><i>Eingreif</i></span> formations to air and ground observation, well in advance of their arrival on the battlefield.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEEdmonds1991290_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEEdmonds1991290-3">[2]</a></sup> German defensive arrangements were changed hastily after the battle to try to counter British offensive superiority.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESheldon2007185.E2.80.93186_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESheldon2007185.E2.80.93186-4">[3]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Background">Background</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Battle_of_Polygon_Wood&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Tactical_developments">Tactical developments</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Battle_of_Polygon_Wood&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Tactical developments">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>The preliminary operation to capture <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Messines_(1917)" title="Battle of Messines (1917)">Messines ridge</a> <span class="nowrap">(7–14 June)</span> had been followed by a strategic pause as the British repaired their communications behind Messines ridge, completed the building of the infrastructure necessary for a much larger force in the Ypres area and moved troops and equipment north from the Arras front.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEEdmonds199124.E2.80.9325_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEEdmonds199124.E2.80.9325-5">[4]</a></sup> After delays caused by local conditions, the Battles of Ypres had begun on 31 July with the Battle of Pilckem Ridge, which was a substantial local success for the British, taking a large amount of ground and inflicting many casualties on the German defenders.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPriorWilson199695.E2.80.9396_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPriorWilson199695.E2.80.9396-6">[5]</a></sup> The German defence had nonetheless recovered some of the lost ground in the middle of the attack front and restricted the British advance on the Gheluvelt Plateau further south. British attacks had then been seriously hampered by unseasonal heavy rain during August and had not been able to retain much of the additional ground captured on the plateau on 10, 16–18, 22–24 and 27 August due to the determined German defence, mud and poor visibility.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEEdmonds1991183.E2.80.93213_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEEdmonds1991183.E2.80.93213-7">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>Sir Douglas Haig ordered artillery to be transferred from the southern flank of the Second Army and more artillery to be brought into Flanders from the armies further south, to increase the weight of the attack on the Gheluvelt Plateau.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[b]</a></sup> The principal role was changed from the Fifth to the Second Army and the boundary between the Second and Fifth armies was moved north towards the Ypres–Roulers railway, to narrow the frontages of the Second Army divisions on the Gheluvelt Plateau. A pause in British attacks was used to reorganise and to improve supply routes behind the front line, to carry forward 54,572 long tons (55,448&#160;t) of ammunition above normal expenditure, guns were moved forward to new positions and the infantry and artillery reinforcements which arrived, practised for the next attack. The unseasonal rains stopped, the ground began to dry and the cessation of British attacks misled the Germans, who risked moving some units away from Flanders.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTETerraine1977257_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTETerraine1977257-10">[8]</a></sup> The offensive had resumed on 20 September, using similar step-by-step methods to those of the Fifth Army after 31 July, with a further evolution of technique, based on the greater mass of artillery made available, to enable the consolidation of captured ground with sufficient strength and organisation to defeat German counter-attacks.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEEdmonds1991239.E2.80.93242.2C_452.E2.80.93464_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEEdmonds1991239.E2.80.93242.2C_452.E2.80.93464-11">[9]</a></sup> At the Battle of the Menin Road Ridge, most of the British objectives had been captured and held, with substantial losses being inflicted on the six German ground-holding divisions and their three supporting <span lang="de" xml:lang="de"><i>Eingreif</i></span> divisions.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEEdmonds1991253.E2.80.93279_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEEdmonds1991253.E2.80.93279-12">[10]</a></sup> British preparations for the next step began immediately and both sides studied the effect of the battle and the implications it had for their dispositions.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEEdmonds1991280.E2.80.93289.2C_290.E2.80.93295_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEEdmonds1991280.E2.80.93289.2C_290.E2.80.93295-13">[11]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERogers2011168.E2.80.93170_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERogers2011168.E2.80.93170-14">[12]</a></sup></p>

<p>On 21 September, Haig instructed the Fifth and Second armies to make the next step across the Gheluvelt Plateau, on a front of 8,500&#160;yd (7,800&#160;m). The <a href="/wiki/I_ANZAC_Corps" title="I ANZAC Corps">I ANZAC Corps</a> would conduct the main advance of about 1,200&#160;yd (1,100&#160;m), to complete the occupation of Polygon Wood and the south end of Zonnebeke village.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEEdmonds1991280_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEEdmonds1991280-16">[14]</a></sup> The Second Army altered its corps frontages soon after the attack of 20 September so that each attacking division could be concentrated on a 1,000&#160;yd (910&#160;m) front. Roads and light railways were built behind the new front line to allow artillery and ammunition to be moved forward, beginning on 20 September; in fine weather this was finished in four days. As before Menin Road, bombardment and counter-battery fire began immediately, with practice barrages fired daily as a minimum. Artillery from the <a href="/wiki/VIII_Corps_(United_Kingdom)" title="VIII Corps (United Kingdom)">VIII</a> and <a href="/wiki/IX_Corps_(United_Kingdom)" title="IX Corps (United Kingdom)">IX</a> Corps in the south conducted bombardments to simulate attack preparations on Zandvoorde and Warneton. Haig intended that later operations would capture the rest of the ridge from Broodseinde, giving the Fifth Army scope to advance beyond the ridge north-eastwards and allow the commencement of <a href="/wiki/Operation_Hush" title="Operation Hush">Operation Hush</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEEdmonds1991280_16-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEEdmonds1991280-16">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>The huge amounts of shellfire from both sides had cut up the ground and destroyed roads. New road circuits were built to carry supplies forward, especially artillery ammunition. Heavier equipment bogged in churned mud so had to be brought forward by wagons along roads and tracks, many of which were under German artillery observation from Passchendaele ridge, rather than being moved cross-country. The I Anzac Corps had <span class="nowrap">205 heavy</span> artillery pieces, one gun for every 9 metres (9.8&#160;yd) of front and many field artillery brigades with <span class="nowrap">18-pdr</span> guns and <span class="nowrap">4.5-inch</span> howitzers, which with the guns of the other attacking corps were moved forward 2,000&#160;yd (1,800&#160;m) from <span class="nowrap">20–24 September.</span> Assembled forward of the artillery were heavy <a href="/wiki/Vickers_machine_guns" class="mw-redirect" title="Vickers machine guns">Vickers machine-guns</a> of the divisional machine-gun companies, <span class="nowrap">56 for</span> the creeping machine-gun barrage and <span class="nowrap">64 "SOS"</span> guns for emergency barrages against German counter–attacks and to prolong the barrage towards the final objective.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBean1941791.E2.80.93796_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBean1941791.E2.80.93796-17">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>The frontages of VIII and IX Corps were moved northwards so that X Corps could take over 600&#160;yd (550&#160;m) of front up to the southern edge of Polygon Wood, which kept each of the frontages of the two Australian divisions of I Anzac Corps to 1,000&#160;yd (910&#160;m). The <a href="/wiki/39th_Division_(United_Kingdom)" title="39th Division (United Kingdom)">39th Division</a> took over from the <a href="/wiki/41st_Division_(United_Kingdom)" title="41st Division (United Kingdom)">41st Division</a> ready to attack Tower Hamlets (on the Bassevillebeke spur), the <a href="/wiki/33rd_Division_(United_Kingdom)" title="33rd Division (United Kingdom)">33rd Division</a> replaced the <a href="/wiki/23rd_Division_(United_Kingdom)" title="23rd Division (United Kingdom)">23rd Division</a> beyond the Menin Road and the <a href="/wiki/5th_Division_(Australia)" title="5th Division (Australia)">5th</a> and <a href="/wiki/4th_Division_(Australia)" title="4th Division (Australia)">4th</a> Australian divisions replaced the <a href="/wiki/1st_Division_(Australia)" title="1st Division (Australia)">1st</a> and <a href="/wiki/2nd_Division_(Australia)" title="2nd Division (Australia)">2nd</a> Australian divisions in Polygon Wood.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEEdmonds1991282_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEEdmonds1991282-18">[16]</a></sup> A German attack on 25 September between Menin Road and Polygon Wood occurred as the 33rd Division was taking over from the 23rd Division and for a time threatened to delay preparations for the British operation, due next day. Some ground was captured by the Germans and part of it was then recaptured by the 33rd Division. Plumer ordered that the flank guard protecting the I Anzac Corps on 26 September be formed by the 98th Brigade of the 33rd Division while the 100th Brigade recaptured the lost ground.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEEdmonds1991284_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEEdmonds1991284-19">[17]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Plan_of_attack">Plan of attack</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Battle_of_Polygon_Wood&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Plan of attack">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Dispersed and camouflaged German defences, using shell-hole positions, pillboxes and the holding back of much of the German infantry for counter-attacks, had meant that as British advances became weaker and disorganised by losses, fatigue, poor visibility and the channelling effect of waterlogged ground, they met more and fresher German defenders.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEEdmonds1991456.E2.80.93459_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEEdmonds1991456.E2.80.93459-20">[18]</a></sup> To overcome the German defensive system, which had been made more formidable by the unusually rainy weather in August, making movement much more difficult and forcing the British to keep to duck board tracks, easy to identify and bombard, objectives had been chosen which provided British infantry with good positions from which to face German counter-attacks, rather than to advance to the maximum distance before digging-in. The Fifth Army had set objectives much closer than 3,000–3,500&#160;yd (2,700–3,200&#160;m) after 31 July and the Second Army methods of September were based on SS 144 <i>The Normal Formation for the Attack</i> (February 1917), reflecting the experience of the fighting in August and to exploit opportunities made possible by the reinforcement of the Flanders front with another <span class="nowrap">626 artillery</span> pieces by Sir Douglas Haig, during the operational pause before 20 September.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEEdmonds1991183.E2.80.93213.2C_449.E2.80.93452_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEEdmonds1991183.E2.80.93213.2C_449.E2.80.93452-21">[19]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDavidson201044.E2.80.9348_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDavidson201044.E2.80.9348-22">[20]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[c]</a></sup> The methods based on the Second Army <i>Note</i> of 31 August, had proved themselves on 20 September and were to be repeated.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEEdmonds1991459.E2.80.93464_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEEdmonds1991459.E2.80.93464-31">[28]</a></sup></p>
<p>The attack of 20 September had used the extra infantry made available by narrowing attack frontages and increasing the number of divisions, to have greater depth than those of August, with several widely spaced lines of infantry in front of section columns, snaking round shell-holes and patches of mud, ready to surround German pillboxes, with increased numbers of support waves ready to leap-frog through and reserves ready to intervene, during delays and German counter-attacks.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEEdmonds1991231.E2.80.93279_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEEdmonds1991231.E2.80.93279-32">[29]</a></sup> Intermediate objectives were chosen which required a shorter distance to be covered and the number of infantry attacking the first objective were reduced, since the German garrisons in the forward defended areas were small and dispersed. British troops involved in the first advance were lightly equipped, to allow them to move more quickly, to avoid German counter-barrages and get through the relatively empty area up to the first objective. The number of units leapfrogging through to the next objective was increased and the distance to the final objective further reduced, to match the increasing density of German defences; the creeping barrage was arranged to move more slowly to the final objective.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEEdmonds1991459_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEEdmonds1991459-33">[30]</a></sup> Particular units were allotted to "mop-up" and occupy areas behind the most advanced troops, to make certain that pockets of Germans overrun by the foremost troops were killed or captured, before they could emerge from shelter and re-join the battle. The formation used by the infantry was altered, so that those in the leading waves were further apart and followed by files or small groups, ready to swarm around German defences uncovered by the skirmish lines, each unit keeping a sub-unit in close reserve, brigades a reserve battalion, battalions a reserve company and companies a reserve platoon.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEEdmonds1991240.E2.80.93242_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEEdmonds1991240.E2.80.93242-34">[31]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">[d]</a></sup> Increased emphasis was placed on Lewis-guns, rifle-fire and rifle-grenades. Hand-grenades were given less emphasis in favour of more rifle training. The proportion of smoke ammunition for rifle grenades and Stokes mortars was increased, to blind the occupants of German pillboxes as they were being surrounded. All units were required to plan an active defence against counter-attack, using the repulse of German infantry as an opportunity to follow up and inflict more casualties.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEEdmonds1991460.E2.80.93461_38-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEEdmonds1991460.E2.80.93461-38">[34]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/X_Corps_(United_Kingdom)" title="X Corps (United Kingdom)">X Corps</a> was to advance to create a defensive flank on the right, attacking with the 33rd and 39th divisions either side of the Menin road. The I Anzac Corps with the 5th and 4th Australian divisions would make the main attack on the remainder of Polygon Wood and the southern part of Zonnebeke village in two stages, 800–900&#160;yd (730–820&#160;m) to the Butte and Tokio pillbox and after a one-hour pause for consolidation, make a final advance beyond the <span lang="de" xml:lang="de"><i>Flandern I Stellung</i></span> and the Tokio spur. To the north, V Corps of the Fifth Army with the 3rd and 59th divisions was to reach a line from Zonnebeke, to Hill 40 and Kansas Farm crossroads, using the smoke and high explosive barrage (rather than shrapnel) demonstrated by the 9th Division on 20 September.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEEwing2001237_39-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEEwing2001237-39">[35]</a></sup> A brigade of the 58th Division, (XVIII Corps) was to attack up Gravenstafel spur towards Aviatik Farm. The relief of <a href="/wiki/V_Corps_(United_Kingdom)" title="V Corps (United Kingdom)">V Corps</a> by II Anzac Corps, to bring the ridge as far north as Passchendaele into the Second Army area was delayed, as the 1st and 2nd Australian divisions were still battleworthy.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEEdmonds1991281_40-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEEdmonds1991281-40">[36]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="German_defensive_preparations">German defensive preparations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Battle_of_Polygon_Wood&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: German defensive preparations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>Tower Hamlets spur overlooked the ground south towards Zandvoorde. The upper valleys of the Reutelbeek and Polygonebeek further north commanded the German counter-attack assembly areas in the low ground north of the Menin road. The large mound in Polygon Wood, the <span lang="fr" xml:lang="fr"><i>Butte de Polygone</i></span> gave observation of the east end of the Gheluvelt Plateau towards Becelaere and Broodseinde and had been fortified with machine gun emplacements and <a href="/wiki/Barbed_wire" title="Barbed wire">barbed wire</a> obstacles.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBean1941795_41-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBean1941795-41">[37]</a></sup> From mid-1917, the area east of Ypres was defended by six German defensive positions, the front position, <i>Albrecht Stellung</i> (second position), <i>Wilhelm Stellung</i> (third position), <i>Flandern I Stellung</i> (fourth position), <i>Flandern II Stellung</i> (fifth position) and <i>Flandern III Stellung</i> (under construction). In between the German defence positions lay the Belgian villages of <a href="/wiki/Zonnebeke" title="Zonnebeke">Zonnebeke</a> and Passchendaele.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWynne1976284_42-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWynne1976284-42">[38]</a></sup></p>
<p>The German defences had been arranged as a "forward zone", "main battle zone" and "rearward battle zone".<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELupfer198114_43-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELupfer198114-43">[39]</a></sup> Polygon Wood formed part of the <span lang="de" xml:lang="de"><i>Wilhelmstellung</i></span>; large numbers of dugouts and <a href="/wiki/Defensive_fighting_position" title="Defensive fighting position">foxholes</a> were constructed within it.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBean1941795_41-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBean1941795-41">[37]</a></sup> After the defeat of 20 September, the 4th Army made changes to its defensive methods. At a conference on 22 September, it was decided to increase the artillery effort between battles, half for counter-battery fire and half against British infantry. The accuracy of German artillery fire was to be improved by increasing the amount of artillery observation available to direct fire during British attacks. Infantry raiding was to be stepped up and counter-attacks to be made more quickly.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEEdmonds1991295_44-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEEdmonds1991295-44">[40]</a></sup> By 26 September the ground-holding divisions had been reorganised so that the regiments were side-by-side, covering a front of about 1,000&#160;yd (910&#160;m) each with the battalions one-behind-the-other, the first in the front line, one in support and the third in reserve, over a depth of 3,000&#160;yd (2,700&#160;m).<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERogers2011169.E2.80.93170_45-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERogers2011169.E2.80.93170-45">[41]</a></sup> Each of the three ground-holding divisions on the Gheluvelt Plateau had an <span lang="de" xml:lang="de"><i>Eingreif</i></span> division in support, double the ratio on 20 September.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEEdmonds1991293_46-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEEdmonds1991293-46">[42]</a></sup></p>
<p>On 25 September, a German attack on the front of the 20th Division (XIV Corps) was prevented by artillery fire but on the X Corps front south of <a href="/wiki/I_Anzac_Corps" class="mw-redirect" title="I Anzac Corps">I Anzac Corps</a>, a bigger German attack took place.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMoorhouse2003174.E2.80.93175_47-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMoorhouse2003174.E2.80.93175-47">[43]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBean1941799.E2.80.93809_48-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBean1941799.E2.80.93809-48">[44]</a></sup> Crown Prince Rupprecht had ordered the attack to recover ground on the Gheluvelt Plateau and to try to gain time for reinforcements to be brought into the battle zone to bolster the defensive system.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESheldon2007166_49-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESheldon2007166-49">[45]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEEdmonds1991283_50-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEEdmonds1991283-50">[46]</a></sup> Two regiments of the 50th Reserve Division attacked either side of the Reutelbeek, with the support of <span class="nowrap">44 field</span> and <span class="nowrap">20 heavy</span> batteries of artillery, four times the usual amount of artillery for one division.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEEdmonds1991283_50-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEEdmonds1991283-50">[46]</a></sup> The attack on a 1,800-yard (1,600&#160;m) front from the Menin road to Polygon Wood, to recapture pillboxes and shelters in the <i>Wilhelmstellung</i> 500&#160;yd (460&#160;m) away, had been due to begin at <span class="nowrap">5:15 a.m.</span> but the barrage fell short onto the German assembly area and the German infantry had to fall back until it began to creep forward at <span class="nowrap">5:30 a.m.</span><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEEdmonds1991283_50-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEEdmonds1991283-50">[46]</a></sup> The German infantry managed to advance on the flanks, about 100&#160;yd (91&#160;m) near the Menin road and 600&#160;yd (550&#160;m) north of the Reutelbeek, close to Black Watch Corner, with the help of a number of observation and ground-attack aircraft and a box-barrage, which obstructed the supply of ammunition to the British defenders, before fire from the 33rd Division troops being attacked and the <a href="/wiki/15th_Brigade_(Australia)" title="15th Brigade (Australia)">15th Australian Brigade</a> along the southern edge of Polygon wood, forced them under cover, after recapturing some of the <i>Wilhelmstellung</i> pillboxes near Black Watch Corner.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEEdmonds1991283_50-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEEdmonds1991283-50">[46]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBean1941794_51-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBean1941794-51">[47]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESheldon2007167_52-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESheldon2007167-52">[48]</a></sup> A number of attempts to reinforce the attacking troops failed, due to British artillery observers isolating the advanced German troops with artillery barrages.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEEdmonds1991284_19-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEEdmonds1991284-19">[17]</a></sup> The Australian advance the next day began with uncertainty as to the security of the right flank.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBean1941809.E2.80.93813_53-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBean1941809.E2.80.93813-53">[49]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Battle">Battle</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Battle_of_Polygon_Wood&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Battle">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Second_Army">Second Army</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Battle_of_Polygon_Wood&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Second Army">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>In X Corps, the 19th Division of IX Corps provided flanking artillery fire, machine-gun fire and a smoke screen for the 39th Division, keeping a very thinly occupied front line which in consequence received much German retaliatory artillery fire at first, which fell on unoccupied ground, then diminished and became inaccurate during the day.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWyrall2009118_54-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWyrall2009118-54">[50]</a></sup> The 39th Division attacked at <span class="nowrap">5:50 a.m.</span> with two brigades. The "Quadrilateral" further down Bassevillebeek spur, which commanding the area around Tower Hamlets was captured; the right brigade had been caught in the boggy ground of the Bassevillebeek, its two tanks in support got stuck near Dumbarton Lakes. Soon after arriving in the "Quadrilateral" it was counter-attacked by part of the German <a href="/wiki/25th_Division_(German_Empire)" title="25th Division (German Empire)">25th Division</a> and pushed back 200&#160;yd (180&#160;m).<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEEdmonds1991288_55-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEEdmonds1991288-55">[51]</a></sup> The left brigade passed through Tower Hamlets to reach the final objective and consolidated behind Tower Trench, with an advanced post in the north-west of Gheluvelt Wood.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMcCarthy199582.E2.80.9383_56-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMcCarthy199582.E2.80.9383-56">[52]</a></sup></p>
<p>The right brigade of the 33rd Division advanced to recapture the ground lost in the German attack the day before and was stopped 50&#160;yd (46&#160;m) short of its objective, until a reserve company assisted and gained touch with the left brigade of the 39th Division to the south. On the left of the brigade the old front line was regained by <span class="nowrap">1:30 p.m.</span> and posts established beyond the Reutelbeek.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMcCarthy199583.E2.80.9384_57-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMcCarthy199583.E2.80.9384-57">[53]</a></sup> The 98th Brigade on the left attacked with reinforcements from the reserve brigade at <span class="nowrap">5:15 a.m.</span> so as to advance 500&#160;yd (460&#160;m) with the troops at Black Watch Corner in action the previous day. At <span class="nowrap">2:20 a.m.</span> the brigade had gained Jerk House and met the 5th Australian Division to the north. A German barrage forced a delay until <span class="nowrap">5:30 a.m.</span> but the German bombardment increased in intensity and the advance lost the barrage, reaching only as far as Black Watch Corner. A reserve battalion was sent through the 5th Australian Division sector, to attack to the south-east at noon, which enabled the brigade to regain most of the ground lost the day before, although well short of the day's objectives. A German counter-attack at <span class="nowrap">2:30 p.m.</span> was driven off and more ground re-taken by the 100th Brigade on the right. A pillbox near the Menin road taken at <span class="nowrap">4:00 p.m.</span> was the last part of the area captured by the German attack the previous day to be re-taken. A German counter-attack at <span class="nowrap">5:00 p.m.</span> was stopped by artillery fire.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMcCarthy199583.E2.80.9385_58-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMcCarthy199583.E2.80.9385-58">[54]</a></sup></p>

<p>I Anzac Corps attacked with the 5th Australian Division on the right. In the 15th Australian Brigade the battalions were to advance successively but bunched up near the first objective and were stopped by pillboxes at the "racecourse" and fire from the 33rd Division area to the south. At <span class="nowrap">7:30 a.m.</span> the right-hand battalion dug in at the boundary with the 33rd Division and the other two advanced to the second objective by <span class="nowrap">11:00 a.m.</span> The left brigade assembled in twelve waves on a strip of ground 60&#160;yd (55&#160;m) deep and avoided the German barrage fired at <span class="nowrap">4:00 a.m.</span> which fell behind them and advanced through the fog 500&#160;yd (460&#160;m) almost unopposed to <i>The Butte</i>. At some pillboxes there was resistance but many German soldiers surrendered when they were rapidly surrounded.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEEllis1920245_59-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEEllis1920245-59">[55]</a></sup> <i>The Butte</i> was rushed and was found to be full of German dugouts.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBean1941826_60-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBean1941826-60">[56]</a></sup> Two battalions passed through at <span class="nowrap">7:30 a.m.</span> towards the second objective, a 1,000-yard (910&#160;m) stretch of the <span lang="de" xml:lang="de"><i>Flandern I Stellung</i></span> and some pillboxes, until held up by fire from a German battalion headquarters on the Polygonebeek. A reserve battalion overran the dugouts and more pillboxes nearby, advancing to just beyond the final objective, at the junction with the 4th Australian Division to the north, taking <span class="nowrap">200 prisoners</span> and <span class="nowrap">34 machine-guns.</span> An attempted German counter-attack by part of the 17th Division, was dispersed by artillery and machine-gun fire.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBean1941826.E2.80.93827_61-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBean1941826.E2.80.93827-61">[57]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMcCarthy199585.E2.80.9387_62-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMcCarthy199585.E2.80.9387-62">[58]</a></sup></p>
<p>The 4th Australian Division assembled well forward and avoided the German barrage by squeezing up into an area 150&#160;yd (140&#160;m) deep and attacked at <span class="nowrap">6:45 a.m.</span> with two brigades. The right brigade attacked through a mist, took the first objective with only short delays to capture pillboxes but then mistakenly advanced into the standing barrage, which had paused for twice as long as usual, to assist the 3rd Division advance through muddier conditions to the north and had to be brought back until the barrage moved forward. The brigade reached the final objective from just short of the <span lang="de" xml:lang="de"><i>Flandern I Stellung</i></span> on the right and the edge of Zonnebeke on the left and gained touch with the 5th Australian Division further south. At <span class="nowrap">1:20 p.m.</span> air reconnaissance reported German troops east of Broodseinde ridge and at <span class="nowrap">3:25 p.m.</span> as the German force from the 236th Division, massed to counter-attack it was dispersed by artillery fire. The northern brigade advanced to the final objective against minor opposition, moving beyond the objective to join with the 3rd Division to the north, which had pressed on into Zonnebeke. Attempts by the Germans to counter-attack at <span class="nowrap">4:00 p.m.</span> and <span class="nowrap">6:00 p.m.</span> were stopped by the protective barrage and machine-gun fire.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBean1941829.2C_830_63-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBean1941829.2C_830-63">[59]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMcCarthy199587.E2.80.9389_64-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMcCarthy199587.E2.80.9389-64">[60]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fifth_Army">Fifth Army</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Battle_of_Polygon_Wood&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Fifth Army">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The southern boundary of the Fifth Army lay approximately 800&#160;yd (730&#160;m) south of the Ypres–Roulers railway, in the V Corps area. The <a href="/wiki/3rd_Mechanised_Division_(United_Kingdom)" class="mw-redirect" title="3rd Mechanised Division (United Kingdom)">3rd Division</a> attacked either side of the line at <span class="nowrap">5:50 a.m.</span> The right brigade met little resistance but was briefly held up, when crossing the Steenbeek. The advance slowed under machine-gun fire from Zonnebeke station on the far side of the railway as Zonnebeke was entered. North of the embankment the left brigade attacked at <span class="nowrap">5:30 a.m.</span> in a mist. The attack reached the first objective, despite crossing severely boggy ground at <span class="nowrap">7:00 a.m.</span> The advance resumed and reached the western slope of Hill 40, just short of the final objective. A German counter-attack began at <span class="nowrap">2:30 p.m.</span> but was stopped easily. A bigger attempt at <span class="nowrap">6:30 p.m.</span> was defeated with rifle and machine-gun fire, as the British attack on Hill 40 resumed, eventually leaving both sides still on the western slope.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMcCarthy199589.E2.80.9392_65-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMcCarthy199589.E2.80.9392-65">[61]</a></sup></p>
<p>59th Division attacked with two brigades, the right brigade advancing until held up by its own barrage and took Dochy Farm at <span class="nowrap">7:50 a.m.</span> One battalion found a German barrage laid behind the British creeping barrage, which crept back with it and caused many casualties.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEOates2010167_66-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEOates2010167-66">[62]</a></sup> The advance continued beyond the final objective to Riverside and Otto Farms but when the protective barrage fell short, Riverside was abandoned. The left brigade advanced and took Schuler Farm, Cross Cottages, Kansas, Martha, Green and Road Houses then Kansas Cross and Focker pillboxes. As the brigade reached the final objective Riverside, Toronto and Deuce Houses were captured. A German counter-attack between <span class="nowrap">5:30 p.m.</span> and <span class="nowrap">6:50 p.m.</span> pushed back some advanced posts, which with reinforcements were regained by <span class="nowrap">11:00 p.m.</span><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMcCarthy199589.E2.80.9392_65-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMcCarthy199589.E2.80.9392-65">[61]</a></sup></p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/XVIII_Corps_(United_Kingdom)" title="XVIII Corps (United Kingdom)">XVIII Corps</a>, the <a href="/wiki/58th_(2/1st_London)_Division" title="58th (2/1st London) Division">58th Division</a> attacked with one brigade at <span class="nowrap">5:50 a.m.</span> In a thick mist some of the British troops lost direction and were then held up by fire from Dom Trench and a pillbox, after these were captured the advance resumed until stopped at Dear House, Aviatik Farm and Vale House, about 400&#160;yd (370&#160;m) short of the final objective. A German counter-attack pushed the British back from Aviatik Farm and Dale House and an attempt to regain them failed. Another attack at <span class="nowrap">6:11 p.m.</span> reached Nile on the divisional boundary with the 3rd Division. German troops trickling forward to Riverside and Otto pillboxes were stopped by artillery and machine-gun fire.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMcCarthy199593_67-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMcCarthy199593-67">[63]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Air_operations">Air operations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Battle_of_Polygon_Wood&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Air operations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>Aircraft of the <a href="/wiki/Australian_Air_Force#World_War_I" class="mw-redirect" title="Australian Air Force">Australian Flying Corps</a> flew over the infantry on contact patrol, the aeroplanes were distinguished by black streamers on the rear edge of their left wings and called for signals from the ground by sounding a klaxon horn or dropping lights, to which infantry responded with red <a href="/wiki/Flares" class="mw-redirect" title="Flares">flares</a> to communicate their position; the pilot would report to the Australian divisional headquarters.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEEllis1920245_59-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEEllis1920245-59">[55]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Royal_Flying_Corps" title="Royal Flying Corps">Royal Flying Corps</a> (RFC) began operations on the night of <span class="nowrap">25–26 September</span> when <a href="/wiki/No._100_Squadron_RAF" title="No. 100 Squadron RAF">100</a> and <a href="/wiki/No._101_Squadron_RAF" title="No. 101 Squadron RAF">101</a> squadrons attacked German billets and railway stations. Mist rose before dawn and ended night flying early; there was a low cloud present at <span class="nowrap">5:50 a.m.</span> when the infantry advanced, which made observation difficult. Contact-patrol and artillery observers managed to observe progress on the ground and reported <span class="nowrap">193 German</span> artillery batteries to British artillery. Fighters flying at about 300&#160;ft (91&#160;m) attacked German infantry and artillery; German aircraft tried this against British troops with some success, although ground fire shot five of them down. Six more German aircraft were shot down by RFC and <a href="/wiki/Royal_Naval_Air_Service" title="Royal Naval Air Service">Royal Naval Air Service</a> ("RNAS") pilots over the battlefield.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEJones2002191.E2.80.93192_68-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJones2002191.E2.80.93192-68">[64]</a></sup> Operations further afield were reduced due to the low cloud but three German airfields were attacked and an offensive patrol over the front line intercepted German bombers and escorts and drove them off.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEJones2002193.E2.80.93194_69-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJones2002193.E2.80.93194-69">[65]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="German_4th_Army">German 4th Army</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Battle_of_Polygon_Wood&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: German 4th Army">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Despite difficulties at the extremities of the attack front, by mid-morning most British objectives had been gained and consolidated. The Germans launched several counter-attacks, with the <span lang="de" xml:lang="de"><i>Eingreif</i></span> divisions supported by the equivalent of ten normal divisional artilleries.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEEdmonds1991293_46-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEEdmonds1991293-46">[42]</a></sup> Clearing weather assisted early observation of the German counter-attacks, most of which were repulsed by accurate and heavy artillery and small-arms fire, causing many German casualties.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEEdmonds1991290.E2.80.93293_70-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEEdmonds1991290.E2.80.93293-70">[66]</a></sup> At Zonnebeke a local counter-attack by the 34th Fusilier Regiment (3rd Reserve Division) was attempted around <span class="nowrap">6:45 a.m.</span>, with part of the 2nd battalion (in support) advancing to reinforce the 3rd Battalion holding the front line and the reserve battalion (1st) joining the counter-attack, after advancing west over Broodseinde ridge.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERogers2011172_71-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERogers2011172-71">[67]</a></sup> The order reached the troops south of the Ypres–Roulers railway quickly, who attacked immediately. The companies south of Zonnebeke advanced and were overrun by British troops on the Grote Molen spur and taken prisoner. Closer to the railway, troops reached the lake near Zonnebeke church and were pinned down by a British machine-gun already dug-in nearby. The counter-attack order was delayed north of the railway and the counter-attack there did not begin until the 1st Battalion (in reserve) arrived. The battalion was able to descend the slope from Broodseinde covered by mist and smoke, which led to few losses but some units losing direction. The British barrage near the village caused many casualties but the survivors pressed on through it and at <span class="nowrap">7:30 a.m.</span> reached the remnants of the 3rd Battalion near the level crossing north of the village, just in time to hold off a renewed British attack 200&#160;yd (180&#160;m) short of their position, as stray German troops trickled in. By mid-morning the mist had cleared, allowing German machine-gunners and artillery to pin the British down around Grote Molen spur and Frezenburg ridge, forestalling a British attack intended for <span class="nowrap">10:00 a.m.</span>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERogers2011173_72-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERogers2011173-72">[68]</a></sup></p>
<p>Around noon, British aircraft on counter-attack patrols, began to send wireless messages warning of German infantry advancing towards all of the front attacked. Similar reports from the ground began in the early afternoon. In the centre German infantry from the 236th Division, 234th Division and 4th Bavarian Division were advancing north of Becelaere–Broodseinde–Passchendaele Ridge, while the 17th Division advanced south of Polygon wood.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBean1941823_73-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBean1941823-73">[69]</a></sup> British artillery immediately bombarded these areas, disrupting the German deployment and causing the German attacks to be disjointed. A German officer later wrote of severe delays and disorganisation caused to German <span lang="de" xml:lang="de"><i>Eingreif</i></span> units by British artillery-fire and air attacks.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESheldon2007167.E2.80.93168_74-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESheldon2007167.E2.80.93168-74">[70]</a></sup> A counter-attack either side of Molenaarelsthoek was stopped dead at <span class="nowrap">3:25 p.m.</span> At <span class="nowrap">4:00 p.m.</span> German troops advancing around Reutel to the south were heavily bombarded, as were German artillery positions in Holle Bosch, ending the German advance. A German attack then developed near Polderhoek, whose survivors managed to reach the British infantry and were seen off in bayonet fighting. Observation aircraft found German troops massing against Tower Hamlets, on the Bassevillebeek spur and artillery and machine-gun barrages stopped the attack before it reached the British infantry. At <span class="nowrap">6:50 p.m.</span> the Germans managed to organise an attack from Tower Hamlets to north of Polygon Wood; such German infantry as got through the barrages were "annihilated" by the British infantry.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESheldon2007173_75-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESheldon2007173-75">[71]</a></sup> German counter-attacks were only able to reach the new front line and reinforce the remnants of the front divisions.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEEdmonds1991291.E2.80.93292_76-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEEdmonds1991291.E2.80.93292-76">[72]</a></sup></p>
<p>The 236th Division (<span lang="de" xml:lang="de"><i>Eingreif</i></span>) attacked south of the Ypres–Roulers railway and 4th Bavarian Division (<span lang="de" xml:lang="de"><i>Eingreif</i></span>) for 2,000&#160;yd (1,800&#160;m) to the north, with field artillery and twelve aircraft attached to each division and the 234th Division in support.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERogers2011174_77-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERogers2011174-77">[73]</a></sup> British counter-attack patrols easily observed the advance and as the lines of German troops breasted Broodseinde ridge at <span class="nowrap">2:30 p.m.</span>, a huge bombardment enveloped them. German field artillery with the infantry was hit by artillery-fire, which blocked the roads, causing delays and disorganisation. German infantry had many casualties, as they advanced down the slope in good visibility. The 236th Division lost so many men that it was only able to reinforce the troops of the 3rd Reserve Division, found east of the Zonnebeke–Haus Kathé road on Grote Molen spur, chasing a few Australian souvenir hunters out of Molenaarelsthoek.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBean1941830_78-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBean1941830-78">[74]</a></sup> The 4th Bavarian Division had to find a way across the mud and floodings of the Paddebeek east of Kleinmolen spur, losing <span class="nowrap">1,340 casualties</span> to reach the survivors of the 3rd Reserve Division (Polygon Wood–Kleinmolen) and the 23rd Reserve Division (Kleinmolen–St Julien). A renewal of the British attack at <span class="nowrap">6:00 p.m.</span> and the German counter-attack over Hill 40 and Kleinmolen met, the mélee leaving both sides where they began.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERogers2011175_79-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERogers2011175-79">[75]</a></sup></p>
<p>British artillery fire slowed the advance of the <span lang="de" xml:lang="de"><i>Eingreif</i></span> units, which took up to two hours to cover one kilometre and arrived at the front line exhausted. The 17th Division had replaced the 16th Bavarian Division as the <span lang="de" xml:lang="de"><i>Eingreif</i></span> division covering the forces near Zandvoorde, just before the battle began. At <span class="nowrap">10:00 a.m.</span> movement orders arrived and parts of the division advanced north-west towards Terhand, where the first layer of the British barrage (directed by artillery-observation aircraft) was met, delaying the arrival of advanced units in their assembly areas until <span class="nowrap">1:00 p.m.</span>. The order to advance took until <span class="nowrap">2:00 p.m.</span> to reach all units and then the advance resumed through crater fields and the British bombardment, having to disperse to avoid swamps and the worst of the British artillery fire. Polderhoek was not reached until <span class="nowrap">4:10 p.m.</span> and as soon as the first battalions crossed the skyline near Polderhoek Château they were hit by artillery and machine-gun fire from three sides and the counter-attack "withered away".<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESheldon2007169.E2.80.93171_80-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESheldon2007169.E2.80.93171-80">[76]</a></sup> The German attacks stopped at <span class="nowrap">8:30 p.m.</span> and after a quiet night, troops from X and I Anzac corps occupied Cameron House and the head of the Reutelbeek valley near Cameron Covert. The German Official History later recorded that the German counter-attacks found well-dug-in (<span lang="de" xml:lang="de"><i>eingenistete</i></span>) infantry and in places more British attacks.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEEdmonds1991292_81-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEEdmonds1991292-81">[77]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Aftermath">Aftermath</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Battle_of_Polygon_Wood&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Aftermath">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Analysis">Analysis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Battle_of_Polygon_Wood&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Analysis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>Each of the three German ground-holding divisions attacked on 26 September had an <span lang="de" xml:lang="de"><i>Eingreif</i></span> division in support, which was twice the ratio of 20 September. No ground captured by the British had been regained and the counter-attacks had managed only to reach ground held by the remnants of the front-line divisions. Second Army Intelligence estimated that ten divisional artilleries had supported the German troops defending the Gheluvelt Plateau, doubling the Royal Artillery casualties compared to the previous week.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEEdmonds1991293_46-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEEdmonds1991293-46">[42]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Casualties">Casualties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Battle_of_Polygon_Wood&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Casualties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The British had <span class="nowrap">15,375 casualties;</span> <span class="nowrap">1,215</span> being killed. In <span lang="de" xml:lang="de"><i>Der Weltkrieg</i></span> the German official historians recorded <span class="nowrap">13,500 casualties</span> from <span class="nowrap">21–30 September,</span> to which <a href="/wiki/James_Edward_Edmonds" title="James Edward Edmonds">J. E. Edmonds</a>, the British official historian controversially added 30 percent for lightly wounded.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEReichsarchiv201296_82-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEReichsarchiv201296-82">[78]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEEdmonds1991293_46-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEEdmonds1991293-46">[42]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMcRandleQuirk2006667.E2.80.93701_83-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMcRandleQuirk2006667.E2.80.93701-83">[79]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-85" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-85">[e]</a></sup> The 4th Australian Division suffered <span class="nowrap">1,717 casualties</span> and the 5th Australian Division had <span class="nowrap">5,471 dead</span> and wounded from <span class="nowrap">26–28 September.</span><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBean1941795_41-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBean1941795-41">[37]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Subsequent_operations">Subsequent operations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Battle_of_Polygon_Wood&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Subsequent operations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On 27 September in the X Corps area, the 39th Division stopped three German counter-attacks with artillery fire. In the 33rd Division area, after a report that Cameron House had been captured, a battalion attacked past it and reached the blue line. The 98th Brigade to the north attacked towards the 5th Australian Division against determined German resistance, reaching the Australians at Cameron Covert at <span class="nowrap">3:50 p.m.</span> After an intense barrage on the 3rd Division in the V Corps area, astride the Ypres–Roulers railway, the Germans attacked at Bostin Farm and were repulsed after severe fighting. A lull followed until 30 September, when a morning attack by regiments of the fresh 8th and 45th Reserve divisions and the 4th Army <span lang="de" xml:lang="de"><i>Sturmbattalion</i></span>, with flame-throwers and a smoke screen, on the 23rd Division (X Corps) front, north of the Menin Road was defeated.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEEdmonds1991302_86-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEEdmonds1991302-86">[81]</a></sup> Another German attempt at <span class="nowrap">5.30 a.m.</span> on 1 October, with support from ground-attack aircraft, pushed two battalions back 150&#160;yd (140&#160;m); three later attacks were repulsed. Further north the Germans attacked the 7th Division at <span class="nowrap">6:15 a.m.</span> and were stopped by artillery and small arms fire. A renewed attack at <span class="nowrap">9:00 a.m.</span> also failed and when preparations for a third attack were seen at Cameron Covert and Joist Trench, an artillery bombardment stopped all activity. Joist Farm was lost by the 21st Division during a German attack on Polygon Wood and Black Watch Corner and the line stabilised east of Cameron House. German attacks near the Menin Road on the 37th Division front in IX Corps and the 5th Division (X Corps) on 3 October failed.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMcCarthy199593.E2.80.9396_87-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMcCarthy199593.E2.80.9396-87">[82]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Commemoration">Commemoration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Battle_of_Polygon_Wood&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Commemoration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Though smaller than in 1917, Polygon Wood is still large; the remains of three German pillboxes captured by the Australians lie deep among the trees but few trench lines remain. The Butte is still prominent and mounted on top of it is the 5th Australian Divisional memorial. There are two Commonwealth War Graves Commission (CWGC) cemeteries in the vicinity of Polygon Wood, the CWGC Polygon Wood Cemetery and the CWGC Buttes New British Cemetery.<a rel="nofollow" class="external autonumber" href="https://www.cwgc.org/find-a-cemetery/cemetery/55100/polygon-wood-cemetery/">[1]</a><a rel="nofollow" class="external autonumber" href="https://www.cwgc.org/find-a-cemetery/cemetery/55101/BUTTES%20NEW%20BRITISH%20CEMETERY,%20POLYGON%20WOOD">[2]</a> Within Buttes New British Cemetery is the CWGC Polygon Wood New Zealand Memorial to the Missing.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBean1941794_51-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBean1941794-51">[47]</a></sup><a rel="nofollow" class="external autonumber" href="https://www.cwgc.org/find-a-cemetery/cemetery/166200/buttes-new-british-cemetery-(n.z.)-memorial,-polygon-wood/">[3]</a></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Notes">Notes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Battle_of_Polygon_Wood&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Notes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="reflist" style="list-style-type: lower-alpha;">
<div class="mw-references-wrap">
<ol class="references">
<li id="cite_note-2"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-2">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">The name Polygon Wood (German-<span lang="de" xml:lang="de"><i>Polygonwald</i></span> or French-<span lang="fr" xml:lang="fr"><i>Bois de Polygone</i></span>) was derived from the shape of a plantation forest that lay along the axis of the Australian advance on 26 September 1917. The wood was sometimes known as <i>Racecourse Wood</i>, as there was a track within it. Before the Great War, Polygon Wood was used by the Belgian Army and within it stands a large mound, known as the Butte, which was used as a rifle range; there was also a small airfield near the area.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGibbs2009359_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGibbs2009359-1">[1]</a></sup></span></li>
<li id="cite_note-9"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-9">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">From 28 August – 12 September, <span class="nowrap">240 × field</span> guns and howitzers, <span class="nowrap">386 × medium</span> and heavy guns and howitzers arrived from the Third and Fourth armies.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEEdmonds1991244_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEEdmonds1991244-8">[7]</a></sup></span></li>
<li id="cite_note-30"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-30">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">Several writers have followed the Official Historian, <a href="/wiki/James_Edward_Edmonds" title="James Edward Edmonds">J. E. Edmonds</a> (1991 [1948]) in ascribing these changes to the influence of Plumer and the Second Army staff, once Haig had transferred the main offensive effort from the Fifth Army in late August.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEEdmonds1991239_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEEdmonds1991239-23">[21]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELiddle199737.2C_52.E2.80.9354.2C_111.E2.80.93112.2C_233_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELiddle199737.2C_52.E2.80.9354.2C_111.E2.80.93112.2C_233-24">[22]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPriorWilson1996113.E2.80.93114_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPriorWilson1996113.E2.80.93114-25">[23]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESheffieldTodman2004126.E2.80.93127.2C_182.E2.80.93184_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESheffieldTodman2004126.E2.80.93127.2C_182.E2.80.93184-26">[24]</a></sup> The narrative of the Official History makes it clear, that the Fifth Army methods used in July and August and those of the Second Army from September to the end of the battle were similar and that the changes were evolutionary.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESimpson2001133.E2.80.93136_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESimpson2001133.E2.80.93136-27">[25]</a></sup> The practice of troops behind the most advanced infantry moving forward in artillery formation or columns, had been used on 31 July at the Battle of Pilckem Ridge and the attacks of <span class="nowrap">10 and 16–18 August</span> were strictly limited attacks to reach the black and green lines (second and third objectives) of the 31 July attack, on the II and XIX corps fronts.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPriorWilson199681_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPriorWilson199681-28">[26]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEEdmonds1991184.E2.80.93202_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEEdmonds1991184.E2.80.93202-29">[27]</a></sup></span></li>
<li id="cite_note-37"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-37">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">SS 144 (February 1917) reiterated the platoon organisation laid down in SS 143 and recommended an attacking frontage of 200&#160;yd (180&#160;m) for a battalion, with wide intervals between each man, line and wave to create a dispersed attack in depth. Where one or two objectives were to be captured, the first wave should advance to the final objective, with troops in following waves to mop-up and occupy captured ground. Where more objectives were set, the first wave was to stop at the first objective, mop-up and dig in, ready to receive German counter-attacks, as following waves leapfrogged beyond them to the further objectives and did the same, particularly when enough artillery was present to provide covering fire for all of the depth of the attack.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBond199985.E2.80.9386_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBond199985.E2.80.9386-35">[32]</a></sup> The leap-frog method was chosen for the September attacks, whereas in July and August both methods had been used.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBewsher1921200.E2.80.93201_36-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBewsher1921200.E2.80.93201-36">[33]</a></sup></span></li>
<li id="cite_note-85"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-85">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">236th (<span lang="de" xml:lang="de"><i>Eingreif</i></span>), 10th Ersatz, 50th Reserve Division (<span class="nowrap">1,850 casualties</span>) 23rd Reserve (<span lang="de" xml:lang="de"><i>Eingreif</i></span>), 17th (<span lang="de" xml:lang="de"><i>Eingreif</i></span>), 19th Reserve and 4th Bavarian (<span lang="de" xml:lang="de"><i>Eingreif</i></span>) divisions.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEUSWD1920_84-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEUSWD1920-84">[80]</a></sup></span></li>
