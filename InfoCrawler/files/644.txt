			<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Airline hub</h1>
									<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
									<div id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
								<div id="contentSub"><span class="mw-redirectedfrom">  (Redirected from <a href="/w/index.php?title=Focus_city&amp;redirect=no" class="mw-redirect" title="Focus city">Focus city</a>)</span></div>

<p><b>Airline hubs</b> or <b>hub airports</b> are used by one or more <a href="/wiki/Airlines" class="mw-redirect" title="Airlines">airlines</a> to concentrate passenger traffic and flight operations at a given <a href="/wiki/Airport" title="Airport">airport</a>. They serve as transfer (or stop-over) points to get passengers to their final destination.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[a]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[b]</a></sup> It is part of the <a href="/wiki/Spoke-hub_distribution_paradigm" class="mw-redirect" title="Spoke-hub distribution paradigm">hub-and-spoke system</a>. An airline operates flights from several non-hub (spoke) cities to the hub airport, and passengers traveling between spoke cities need to connect through the hub. This paradigm creates <a href="/wiki/Economies_of_scale" title="Economies of scale">economies of scale</a> that allow an airline to serve (via an intermediate connection) city-pairs that could otherwise not be economically served on a <a href="/wiki/Non-stop_flight" title="Non-stop flight">non-stop</a> basis. This system contrasts with the <a href="/wiki/Point-to-point_transit" title="Point-to-point transit">point-to-point model</a>, in which there are no hubs and nonstop flights are instead offered between spoke cities. Hub airports also serve origin and destination (O&amp;D) traffic.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Focus_city">Focus city</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Airline_hub&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Focus city">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Airline" title="Airline">airline</a> industry, a <b>focus city</b> is a destination from which an airline operates several <a href="/wiki/Point-to-point_transit" title="Point-to-point transit">point-to-point</a> routes.<sup id="cite_ref-encyclo_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-encyclo-6">[4]</a></sup> Thus, a focus city primarily caters to the local market rather than to connecting passengers.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[5]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>However, with the term's expanded usage, a focus city may also function as a small-scale <a class="mw-selflink selflink">hub</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-encyclo_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-encyclo-6">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Allegiant_Air" title="Allegiant Air">Allegiant Air</a>, <a href="/wiki/JetBlue" title="JetBlue">JetBlue</a> and <a href="/wiki/Southwest_Airlines" title="Southwest Airlines">Southwest Airlines</a> are examples of airlines that consider some of their destinations to be focus cities.<sup id="cite_ref-jetblue_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-jetblue-5">[3]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Analysis">Analysis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Airline_hub&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Analysis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The hub-and-spoke system allows an airline to serve fewer routes, so fewer aircraft are needed.<sup id="cite_ref-erau_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-erau-10">[8]</a></sup> The system also increases passenger loads; a flight from a hub to a spoke carries not just passengers originating at the hub, but also passengers originating at multiple spoke cities.<sup id="cite_ref-hofstra_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hofstra-11">[9]</a></sup> However, the system is costly. Additional employees and facilities are needed to cater to connecting passengers. To serve spoke cities of varying populations and demand, an airline requires several aircraft types, and specific training and equipment are necessary for each type.<sup id="cite_ref-erau_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-erau-10">[8]</a></sup> In addition, airlines may experience capacity constraints as they expand at their hub airports.<sup id="cite_ref-hofstra_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hofstra-11">[9]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>For the passenger, the hub-and-spoke system offers one-stop air service to a wide array of destinations.<sup id="cite_ref-erau_10-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-erau-10">[8]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-govt_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-govt-13">[11]</a></sup> However, having to regularly make connections en route to their final destination increases travel time.<sup id="cite_ref-govt_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-govt-13">[11]</a></sup> Additionally, airlines can come to monopolise their hubs, allowing them to freely increase fares.<sup id="cite_ref-hofstra_11-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hofstra-11">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Banking">Banking</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Airline_hub&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Banking">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Airlines may operate banks of flights at their hubs, in which several flights arrive and depart within short periods of time. The banks may be known as "peaks" of activity at the hubs and the non-banks as "valleys". Banking allows for short connection times for passengers.<sup id="cite_ref-dallas_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dallas-14">[12]</a></sup> However, an airline must assemble a large number of resources to cater to the influx of flights during a bank, and having several aircraft on the ground at the same time can lead to congestion and delays.<sup id="cite_ref-belobaba_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-belobaba-15">[13]</a></sup> In addition, banking could result in inefficient aircraft utilisation, with aircraft waiting at spoke cities for the next bank.<sup id="cite_ref-belobaba_15-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-belobaba-15">[13]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-american2002_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-american2002-16">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>Instead, some airlines have debanked their hubs, introducing a "rolling hub" in which flight arrivals and departures are spread throughout the day. This phenomenon is also known as "depeaking".<sup id="cite_ref-american2002_16-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-american2002-16">[14]</a></sup> While costs may decrease, connection times are longer at a rolling hub.<sup id="cite_ref-belobaba_15-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-belobaba-15">[13]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/American_Airlines" title="American Airlines">American Airlines</a> was the first to depeak its hubs,<sup id="cite_ref-belobaba_15-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-belobaba-15">[13]</a></sup> trying to improve profitability following the <a href="/wiki/September_11_attacks" title="September 11 attacks">September 11 attacks</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-dallas_14-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dallas-14">[12]</a></sup> It rebanked its hubs in 2015, however, feeling the gain in connecting passengers would outweigh the rise in costs.<sup id="cite_ref-dallas_14-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dallas-14">[12]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cargo_hub">Cargo hub</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Airline_hub&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Cargo hub">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The hub-and-spoke system is also used by some <a href="/wiki/Cargo_airline" title="Cargo airline">cargo airlines</a>. <a href="/wiki/FedEx_Express" title="FedEx Express">FedEx Express</a> established its main hub in <a href="/wiki/Memphis_International_Airport" title="Memphis International Airport">Memphis</a> in 1973, prior to the deregulation of the air cargo industry in the United States. The system has created an efficient delivery system for the airline.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[15]</a></sup> Other airlines that use this system include <a href="/wiki/UPS_Airlines" title="UPS Airlines">UPS Airlines</a>, <a href="/wiki/TNT_Airways" class="mw-redirect" title="TNT Airways">TNT Airways</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cargolux" title="Cargolux">Cargolux</a> and <a href="/wiki/DHL_Aviation" title="DHL Aviation">DHL Aviation</a>, which operate their primary hubs at <a href="/wiki/Louisville_International_Airport" title="Louisville International Airport">Louisville</a>, <a href="/wiki/Li%C3%A8ge_Airport" title="Liège Airport">Liège</a>, <a href="/wiki/Luxembourg_Findel_Airport" class="mw-redirect" title="Luxembourg Findel Airport">Luxembourg</a> and <a href="/wiki/Leipzig/Halle_Airport" title="Leipzig/Halle Airport">Leipzig</a> respectively.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[16]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Focus_city_2">Focus city</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Airline_hub&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Focus city">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Although the term <i>focus city</i> used to mainly refer to an airport from which an airline operates several point-to-point routes, its usage has expanded to refer to a small-scale hub as well.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[17]</a></sup> For example, <a href="/wiki/JetBlue" title="JetBlue">JetBlue</a>'s <a href="/wiki/John_F._Kennedy_International_Airport" title="John F. Kennedy International Airport">New York–JFK</a> focus city, which is the airline's busiest operation, functions like a hub.<sup id="cite_ref-belobaba_15-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-belobaba-15">[13]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fortress_hub">Fortress hub</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Airline_hub&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Fortress hub">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A fortress hub exists when an airline controls a significant majority of the market at one of its hubs. Competition is particularly difficult at fortress hubs.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[18]</a></sup> Examples include <a href="/wiki/Delta_Air_Lines" title="Delta Air Lines">Delta Air Lines</a>' hub at <a href="/wiki/Hartsfield-Jackson_Atlanta_International_Airport" class="mw-redirect" title="Hartsfield-Jackson Atlanta International Airport">Atlanta</a>, <a href="/wiki/American_Airlines" title="American Airlines">American Airlines</a>' hub at <a href="/wiki/Dallas_Fort_Worth_International_Airport" class="mw-redirect" title="Dallas Fort Worth International Airport">Dallas</a>, and <a href="/wiki/United_Airlines" title="United Airlines">United Airlines</a>' hub at <a href="/wiki/George_Bush_Intercontinental_Airport" title="George Bush Intercontinental Airport">Houston–Intercontinental</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[19]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Flag_carrier" title="Flag carrier">Flag carriers</a> have historically enjoyed similar dominance at the main airport of their countries and some still do. Examples include Lufthansa at Frankfurt Airport, KLM at Amsterdam Schiphol, British Airways at London Heathrow, Air France at Paris Orly and Charles de Gaulle airports.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Primary_and_secondary_hubs">Primary and secondary hubs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Airline_hub&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Primary and secondary hubs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A primary hub is the main hub for an airline. However, as an airline expands operations at its primary hub to the point that it experiences capacity limitations, it may elect to open secondary hubs. Examples of such hubs are <a href="/wiki/Turkish_Airlines" title="Turkish Airlines">Turkish Airlines</a>' <a href="/wiki/Sabiha_G%C3%B6k%C3%A7en_International_Airport" title="Sabiha Gökçen International Airport">Istanbul–Sabiha Gökçen</a> hub, <a href="/wiki/British_Airways" title="British Airways">British Airways</a>' hub at <a href="/wiki/Gatwick_Airport" title="Gatwick Airport">London-Gatwick</a>, <a href="/wiki/Air_India" title="Air India">Air India</a>'s hub at <a href="/wiki/Chhatrapati_Shivaji_International_Airport" title="Chhatrapati Shivaji International Airport">Mumbai</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lufthansa" title="Lufthansa">Lufthansa</a>'s hub at <a href="/wiki/Munich_Airport" title="Munich Airport">Munich</a>. By operating multiple hubs, airlines can expand their geographic reach.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[20]</a></sup> They can also better serve spoke–spoke markets, providing more itineraries with connections at different hubs.<sup id="cite_ref-holloway_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-holloway-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Reliever_hub">Reliever hub</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Airline_hub&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Reliever hub">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A given hub's capacity may become exhausted or capacity shortages may occur during peak periods of the day, at which point airlines may be compelled to shift traffic to a reliever hub. A reliever hub has the potential to serve several functions for an airline: it can bypass the congested hub, it can absorb excess demand for flights that could otherwise not be scheduled at the congested hub, and it can schedule new O&amp;D city pairs for connecting traffic.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Scissor_hub">Scissor hub</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Airline_hub&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Scissor hub">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A scissor hub occurs when an airline operates multiple flights to an airport that arrive at the same time, swap passengers, and then continue to their final destination.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[21]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Jet_Airways" title="Jet Airways">Jet Airways</a> has a scissors hub in <a href="/wiki/Amsterdam_Airport_Schiphol" title="Amsterdam Airport Schiphol">Amsterdam</a>, where passengers fly in from <a href="/wiki/Delhi" title="Delhi">Delhi</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mumbai" title="Mumbai">Mumbai</a> to connect onto the flight to <a href="/wiki/Toronto" title="Toronto">Toronto</a> and vice versa.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[22]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Air_India" title="Air India">Air India</a> briefly operated a scissors hub at <a href="/wiki/Frankfurt_Airport" title="Frankfurt Airport">Frankfurt</a> until 2010.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[23]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[24]</a></sup> An international scissor hub could be used for <a href="/wiki/Freedoms_of_the_air#Third_and_fourth_freedom" title="Freedoms of the air">third and fourth freedom</a> flights or it could be used for <a href="/wiki/Freedoms_of_the_air#Fifth_freedom" title="Freedoms of the air">fifth freedom</a> flights, for which a precursor is a <a href="/wiki/Bilateral_treaty" title="Bilateral treaty">bilateral treaty</a> between two country pairs.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/WestJet_Airlines" class="mw-redirect" title="WestJet Airlines">WestJet Airlines</a> uses St. John's as a scissor hub during its summer schedule for flights inbound from Ottawa and Toronto and outbound to Dublin and London Gatwick.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Moonlight_hub">Moonlight hub</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Airline_hub&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Moonlight hub">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In past history, carriers have maintained niche, time-of-day operations at hubs. The most notable is <a href="/wiki/America_West" class="mw-redirect" title="America West">America West</a>'s use of <a href="/wiki/McCarran_International_Airport" title="McCarran International Airport">McCarran International Airport</a> in Las Vegas as a primary night-flight hub to increase aircraft utilization rates far beyond those of competing carriers.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Airline_hub&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Middle_East">Middle East</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Airline_hub&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Middle East">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Emirates_tails_(8499979565).jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/81/Emirates_tails_%288499979565%29.jpg/220px-Emirates_tails_%288499979565%29.jpg" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/81/Emirates_tails_%288499979565%29.jpg/330px-Emirates_tails_%288499979565%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/81/Emirates_tails_%288499979565%29.jpg/440px-Emirates_tails_%288499979565%29.jpg 2x" data-file-width="4342" data-file-height="2894" /></a>

<p>In 1974, the governments of <a href="/wiki/Bahrain" title="Bahrain">Bahrain</a>, <a href="/wiki/Oman" title="Oman">Oman</a>, <a href="/wiki/Qatar" title="Qatar">Qatar</a> and the <a href="/wiki/United_Arab_Emirates" title="United Arab Emirates">United Arab Emirates</a> took control of <a href="/wiki/Gulf_Air" title="Gulf Air">Gulf Air</a> from the <a href="/wiki/British_Overseas_Airways_Corporation" title="British Overseas Airways Corporation">British Overseas Airways Corporation</a> (BOAC). Gulf Air became the <a href="/wiki/Flag_carrier" title="Flag carrier">flag carrier</a> of the four Middle Eastern nations. It linked Oman, Qatar and the UAE to its Bahrain hub, from which it offered flights to destinations throughout Europe and Asia. In the UAE, Gulf Air focused on <a href="/wiki/Abu_Dhabi" title="Abu Dhabi">Abu Dhabi</a> rather than <a href="/wiki/Dubai" title="Dubai">Dubai</a>, contrary to the aspirations of UAE Prime Minister <a href="/wiki/Mohammed_bin_Rashid_Al_Maktoum" title="Mohammed bin Rashid Al Maktoum">Mohammed bin Rashid Al Maktoum</a> to transform the latter into a world-class metropolis. Sheikh Mohammed proceeded to establish a new airline based in Dubai, <a href="/wiki/Emirates_(airline)" title="Emirates (airline)">Emirates</a>, which launched operations in 1985.<sup id="cite_ref-mit_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mit-27">[25]</a></sup></p>
<p>Observing the success of Emirates, Qatar and Oman decided to create their own airlines as well. <a href="/wiki/Qatar_Airways" title="Qatar Airways">Qatar Airways</a> and <a href="/wiki/Oman_Air" title="Oman Air">Oman Air</a> were both founded in 1993, with hubs at <a href="/wiki/Doha" title="Doha">Doha</a> and <a href="/wiki/Muscat,_Oman" class="mw-redirect" title="Muscat, Oman">Muscat</a> respectively. As the new airlines grew, their home nations relied less on Gulf Air to provide air service. Qatar withdrew its share in Gulf Air in 2002. In 2003, the UAE formed another national airline, <a href="/wiki/Etihad_Airways" title="Etihad Airways">Etihad Airways</a>, which is based in Abu Dhabi. The country exited Gulf Air in 2006, and Oman followed in 2007.<sup id="cite_ref-mit_27-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mit-27">[25]</a></sup></p>
<p>Emirates, Qatar Airways and Etihad Airways have since established large hubs at their respective home airports. The hubs, which benefit from their proximity to large population centres,<sup id="cite_ref-mit_27-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mit-27">[25]</a></sup> have become popular stopover points on trips between Europe and Asia, for example.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[26]</a></sup> Their rapid growth has impacted the development of traditional hubs, such as <a href="/wiki/London" title="London">London</a> and <a href="/wiki/Paris" title="Paris">Paris</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[27]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="United_States">United States</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Airline_hub&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: United States">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Before the US airline industry was <a href="/wiki/Airline_Deregulation_Act" title="Airline Deregulation Act">deregulated</a> in 1978, most airlines operated under the <a href="/wiki/Point-to-point_transit" title="Point-to-point transit">point-to-point system</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-hofstra_11-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hofstra-11">[9]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Civil_Aeronautics_Board" title="Civil Aeronautics Board">Civil Aeronautics Board</a> dictated which routes an airline could fly. At the same time, however, some airlines began to experiment with the hub-and-spoke system. <a href="/wiki/Delta_Air_Lines" title="Delta Air Lines">Delta Air Lines</a> was the first to implement such a system, providing service to remote spoke cities from its <a href="/wiki/Hartsfield%E2%80%93Jackson_Atlanta_International_Airport" title="Hartsfield–Jackson Atlanta International Airport">Atlanta</a> hub.<sup id="cite_ref-govt_13-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-govt-13">[11]</a></sup> After deregulation, many airlines quickly established hub-and-spoke route networks of their own.<sup id="cite_ref-erau_10-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-erau-10">[8]</a></sup></p>
