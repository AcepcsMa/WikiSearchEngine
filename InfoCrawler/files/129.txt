<p><b><a href="/wiki/Mathematics" title="Mathematics">Mathematics</a></b> is the study of <a href="/wiki/Number" title="Number">numbers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Quantity" title="Quantity">quantity</a>, <a href="/wiki/Space" title="Space">space</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mathematical_structure" title="Mathematical structure">structure</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Calculus" title="Calculus">change</a>. Mathematics is used throughout the world as an essential tool in many fields, including <a href="/wiki/Natural_science" title="Natural science">natural science</a>, <a href="/wiki/Engineering" title="Engineering">engineering</a>, <a href="/wiki/Medicine" title="Medicine">medicine</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Social_sciences" class="mw-redirect" title="Social sciences">social sciences</a>. <a href="/wiki/Applied_mathematics" title="Applied mathematics">Applied mathematics</a>, the branch of mathematics concerned with application of mathematical knowledge to other fields, inspires and makes use of new mathematical discoveries and sometimes leads to the development of entirely new mathematical disciplines, such as <a href="/wiki/Statistics" title="Statistics">statistics</a> and <a href="/wiki/Game_theory" title="Game theory">game theory</a>. Mathematicians also engage in <a href="/wiki/Pure_mathematics" title="Pure mathematics">pure mathematics</a>, or mathematics for its own sake, without having any application in mind. There is no clear line separating pure and applied mathematics, and practical applications for what began as pure mathematics are often discovered.</p>

<p><b>Euclid's <i>Elements</i></b> (<a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a>: Στοιχεῖα) is a <a href="/wiki/Mathematics" title="Mathematics">mathematical</a> and <a href="/wiki/Geometry" title="Geometry">geometric</a> treatise, consisting of 13 books, written by the <a href="/wiki/Hellenistic" class="mw-redirect" title="Hellenistic">Hellenistic</a> <a href="/wiki/Mathematician" title="Mathematician">mathematician</a> <a href="/wiki/Euclid" title="Euclid">Euclid</a> in <a href="/wiki/Egypt" title="Egypt">Egypt</a> during the early <a href="/wiki/3rd_century_BC" title="3rd century BC">3rd century BC</a>. It comprises a collection of definitions, postulates (<a href="/wiki/Axiom" title="Axiom">axioms</a>), propositions (<a href="/wiki/Theorem" title="Theorem">theorems</a>) and proofs thereof. Euclid's books are in the fields of <a href="/wiki/Euclidean_geometry" title="Euclidean geometry">Euclidean geometry</a>, as well as the ancient <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a> version of <a href="/wiki/Number_theory" title="Number theory">number theory</a>. The <i>Elements</i> is one of the oldest extant axiomatic deductive treatments of <a href="/wiki/Geometry" title="Geometry">geometry</a>, and has proven instrumental in the development of <a href="/wiki/Logic" title="Logic">logic</a> and modern <a href="/wiki/Science" title="Science">science</a>.</p>
<p>It is considered one of the most successful textbooks ever written: the <i>Elements</i> was one of the very first books to go to press, and is second only to the <a href="/wiki/Bible" title="Bible">Bible</a> in number of editions published (well over 1000). For centuries, when the <a href="/wiki/Quadrivium" title="Quadrivium">quadrivium</a> was included in the curriculum of all university students, knowledge of at least part of Euclid's <i>Elements</i> was required of all students. Not until the 20th century did it cease to be considered something all educated people had read. It is still (though rarely) used as a basic introduction to geometry today.</p>

<p>This is <a href="/wiki/Francis_Galton" title="Francis Galton">Francis Galton's</a> original 1889 drawing of three versions of a "<a href="/wiki/Bean_machine" title="Bean machine">bean machine</a>", now commonly called a "Galton box" (another name is a <i><a href="/wiki/Quincunx" title="Quincunx">quincunx</a></i>), a real-world device that can be used to illustrate the <b><a href="/wiki/De_Moivre%E2%80%93Laplace_theorem" title="De Moivre–Laplace theorem">de Moivre–Laplace theorem</a></b> of <a href="/wiki/Probability_theory" title="Probability theory">probability theory</a>, which states that the <a href="/wiki/Normal_distribution" title="Normal distribution">normal distribution</a> is a good approximation to the <a href="/wiki/Binomial_distribution" title="Binomial distribution">binomial distribution</a> provided that the number of repeated "trials" associated with the latter distribution is sufficiently large. As the "bean" (i.e., a small ball) falls through the box (the design of which is quite similar to the popular Japanese game <a href="/wiki/Pachinko" title="Pachinko">Pachinko</a>), it can fall to the left or right of each pin it approaches. Since each lower pin is centered horizontally beneath a pair of higher pins (or a higher pin and the side of the box), the bean has the same probability of falling either way, and each such outcome is approximately independent of the others. Each row of pins thus corresponds to a <a href="/wiki/Bernoulli_trial" title="Bernoulli trial">Bernoulli trial</a> with "success" probablility 0.5 ("success" is defined as falling a particular direction—say, to the right—each time). This makes the final position of the bean at the bottom of the box the sum of several approximately-independent <a href="/wiki/Bernoulli_distribution" title="Bernoulli distribution">Bernoulli random variables</a>, and therefore approximately a random observation from a binomial distribution. (Note that because the bean may reach the side of the box and at that point only be able to fall in one direction, this sequence of Bernoulli random variables might be interrupted by a <i>non-random</i> movement back towards the center; this would not be a problem if the box were wide enough to prevent the bean from reaching the side of the box, as in the top half of Fig.&#160;8—see also <a href="/wiki/File:Planche_de_Galton.jpg" title="File:Planche de Galton.jpg">this photograph</a>.) The box on the left, in Fig.&#160;7, has 23 rows of pins (not counting the first row which is positioned in such a way that the bean always passes between two particular pins) and a final row of slots, so the number of trials in that case is 24. This is large enough that the resulting columns of beans collected at the bottom of the box show the classic "bell curve" shape of the normal distribution. While a level box gives a probability of 0.5 to fall either way at each pin, a tilted box results in asymmetric probabilities, and thus a <a href="/wiki/Skewness" title="Skewness">skewed distribution</a> (see <a href="/wiki/File:Galton_box_3.jpg" title="File:Galton box 3.jpg">this other photograph</a>). Published in 1738 by <a href="/wiki/Abraham_de_Moivre" title="Abraham de Moivre">Abraham de Moivre</a> in the second edition of his textbook <i><a href="/wiki/The_Doctrine_of_Chances" title="The Doctrine of Chances">The Doctrine of Chances</a></i>, the de Moivre–Laplace theorem is today recognized as a special case of the more familiar <a href="/wiki/Central_limit_theorem" title="Central limit theorem">central limit theorem</a>. Together these results underlie a great many <a href="/wiki/Statistics" title="Statistics">statistical procedures</a> widely used today in science, technology, business, and government to analyze data and make decisions.</p>

<ul>
<li>...that the <a href="/wiki/Axiom_of_choice" title="Axiom of choice">axiom of choice</a> is <a href="/wiki/Logically_independent" class="mw-redirect" title="Logically independent">logically independent</a> of the other axioms of <a href="/wiki/Zermelo%E2%80%93Fraenkel_set_theory" title="Zermelo–Fraenkel set theory">Zermelo–Fraenkel set theory</a>?</li>
<li>...that the <a href="/wiki/Pythagorean_theorem#Similar_figures_on_the_three_sides" title="Pythagorean theorem">Pythagorean Theorem</a> generalizes to any three similar shapes on the three sides of a right-angled triangle?</li>

<li>...that the <a href="/wiki/Summation" title="Summation">sum</a> of the first <i>n</i> <a href="/wiki/Odd_numbers" class="mw-redirect" title="Odd numbers">odd numbers</a> divided by the sum of the next <i>n</i> odd numbers is always equal to one third?</li>
<li>...that <i>i</i> to the power of <i>i</i>, where <i>i</i> is the <a href="/wiki/Imaginary_unit" title="Imaginary unit">square root of -1</a>, is a real number?</li>
