			<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Parish</h1>
									<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">

<p>A <b>parish</b> is a church territorial unit constituting a division within a <a href="/wiki/Diocese" title="Diocese">diocese</a>. A parish is under the pastoral care and clerical jurisdiction of a <a href="/wiki/Clergy#Christianity" title="Clergy">parish priest</a>, who might be assisted by one or more <a href="/wiki/Curates" class="mw-redirect" title="Curates">curates</a>, and who operates from a <a href="/wiki/Parish_church" title="Parish church">parish church</a>. Historically, a parish often covered the same geographical area as a <a href="/wiki/Manor" title="Manor">manor</a> (its association with the parish church remaining paramount).<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>By extension the term <i>parish</i> refers not only to the territorial unit but to the people of its community or congregation as well as to church property within it. In England this church property was technically in ownership of the parish priest <i>ex-officio</i>, vested in him on his institution to that parish.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology_and_use">Etymology and use</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Parish&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology and use">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>First attested in English in the late 13th&#160;century, the word <i>parish</i> comes from the <a href="/wiki/Old_French" title="Old French">Old French</a> <i>paroisse</i>, in turn from <a href="/wiki/Latin_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Latin language">Latin</a>: <span lang="la" xml:lang="la"><i>paroecia</i></span>,<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> the <a href="/wiki/Latinisation_(literature)" class="mw-redirect" title="Latinisation (literature)">latinisation</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">Ancient&#160;Greek</a>: <span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc">παροικία</span>&#160;<span title="Ancient Greek transliteration" class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none"><i>paroikia</i></span>, "sojourning in a foreign land",<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> itself from <span lang="el" xml:lang="el">πάροικος</span> (<i>paroikos</i>), "dwelling beside, stranger, sojourner",<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> which is a compound of <span lang="el" xml:lang="el">παρά</span> (<i>pará</i>), "beside, by, near"<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> and οἶκος (<i>oîkos</i>), "house".<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>As an ancient concept, the term "parish" occurs in the long-established Christian denominations: <a href="/wiki/Catholic_Church" title="Catholic Church">Roman Catholic</a>, <a href="/wiki/Anglican_Communion" title="Anglican Communion">Anglican Communion</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Orthodox_Church" title="Eastern Orthodox Church">Eastern Orthodox Church</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Lutheranism" title="Lutheranism">Lutheran</a> churches, and in some <a href="/wiki/Methodist" class="mw-redirect" title="Methodist">Methodist</a> and <a href="/wiki/Presbyterianism" title="Presbyterianism">Presbyterian</a> administrations.</p>
<p>The eighth Archbishop of Canterbury <a href="/wiki/Theodore_of_Tarsus" title="Theodore of Tarsus">Theodore of Tarsus</a> (c. 602–690) appended the parish structure to the <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Saxons#Culture" title="Anglo-Saxons">Anglo-Saxon township</a> unit, where it existed, and where <a href="/wiki/Minster_(church)" title="Minster (church)">minsters</a> catered to the surrounding district.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Church_territorial_structure">Church territorial structure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Parish&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Church territorial structure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Broadly speaking, the parish is the standard unit in <a href="/wiki/Episcopal_polity" title="Episcopal polity">episcopal polity</a> of church administration, although parts of a parish may be subdivided as a <i><a href="/wiki/Chapelry" title="Chapelry">chapelry</a></i>, with a <a href="/wiki/Chapel_of_ease" title="Chapel of ease">chapel of ease</a> or <a href="/wiki/Filial_church" title="Filial church">filial church</a> serving as the local place of worship in cases of difficulty to access the main parish church.</p>
<p>In the wider picture of ecclesiastical polity, a <i>parish</i> comprises a division of a <a href="/wiki/Diocese" title="Diocese">diocese or see</a>. Parishes within a diocese may be grouped into a <a href="/wiki/Deanery" title="Deanery">deanery</a> or <i><a href="/wiki/Vicar#Roman_Catholic" title="Vicar">vicariate forane</a></i> (or simply <i>vicariate</i>), overseen by a <a href="/wiki/Dean_(Christianity)" title="Dean (Christianity)">dean</a> or <i>vicar forane</i>, or in some cases by an <a href="/wiki/Archpriest" title="Archpriest">archpriest</a>. Some churches of the <a href="/wiki/Anglican_Communion" title="Anglican Communion">Anglican Communion</a> have deaneries as units of an <a href="/wiki/Archdeaconry" class="mw-redirect" title="Archdeaconry">archdeaconry</a>.</p>

<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Parish_(Catholic_Church)" class="mw-redirect" title="Parish (Catholic Church)">Parish (Catholic Church)</a></div>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Team_of_priests_in_solidum" title="Team of priests in solidum">Team of priests in solidum</a></div>
<p>In the Roman Catholic Church, each parish normally has its own parish priest (in some countries called <a href="/wiki/Pastor" title="Pastor">pastor</a>), who has responsibility and canonical authority over the parish.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>What in most English-speaking countries is termed the "parish priest" is referred to as the "pastor" in the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>, where the term "parish priest" is used of any priest assigned to a parish even in a subordinate capacity. These are called "assistant priests",<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> "parochial vicars",<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> "<a href="/wiki/Curate#Catholic_Church" title="Curate">curates</a>", or, in the United States, "associate pastors" and "assistant pastors".</p>
<p>Each diocese (administrative region) is divided into parishes, each with their own central church called the <a href="/wiki/Parish_church" title="Parish church">parish church</a>, where religious services take place. Some larger parishes or parishes that have been combined under one pastor may have two or more such churches, or the parish may be responsible for <a href="/wiki/Chapels" class="mw-redirect" title="Chapels">chapels</a> (or <a href="/wiki/Chapels_of_ease" class="mw-redirect" title="Chapels of ease">chapels of ease</a>) located at some distance from the <a href="/wiki/Mother_church" title="Mother church">mother church</a> for the convenience of distant parishioners.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>Normally, a parish comprises all Catholics living in its area, but parishes can also be established within a defined geographical region on a personal basis for Catholics of a particular <a href="/wiki/Particular_Church" class="mw-redirect" title="Particular Church">rite</a>, language, nationality or the like.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> An example is that of personal parishes established in accordance with the 7 July 2007 motu proprio <i><a href="/wiki/Summorum_Pontificum" title="Summorum Pontificum">Summorum Pontificum</a></i> for those attached to the older form of the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Rite" title="Roman Rite">Roman Rite</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>Most Catholic parishes are part of <a href="/wiki/Latin_Rite" class="mw-redirect" title="Latin Rite">Latin Rite</a> <a href="/wiki/Diocese" title="Diocese">dioceses</a>, which together cover the whole territory of a country. There can also be overlapping parishes of eparchies of <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Catholic_Churches" title="Eastern Catholic Churches">Eastern Catholic Churches</a>, <a href="/wiki/Personal_ordinariate" title="Personal ordinariate">personal ordinariates</a> or <a href="/wiki/Military_ordinariate" title="Military ordinariate">military ordinariates</a>.</p>

<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Advowson" title="Advowson">Advowson</a></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Church_of_England" title="Church of England">Church of England</a> geographical structure uses the local parish church as its basic unit. The parish system survived the <a href="/wiki/Protestant_Reformation" class="mw-redirect" title="Protestant Reformation">Reformation</a> with the Anglican Church's <a href="/wiki/Church_of_England#Secession_from_Rome" title="Church of England">secession from Rome</a> remaining largely untouched, thus it shares its roots with the <a href="/wiki/Catholic_Church" title="Catholic Church">Catholic Church</a>'s system described above. Parishes may extend into different counties or hundreds and historically many parishes comprised extra outlying portions in addition to its principal district, usually being described as 'detached' and intermixed with the lands of other parishes. Church of England parishes nowadays all lie within one of <a href="/wiki/List_of_Church_of_England_dioceses" title="List of Church of England dioceses">44 dioceses</a> divided between the provinces of <a href="/wiki/Province_of_Canterbury" title="Province of Canterbury">Canterbury</a>, 30 and <a href="/wiki/Province_of_York" title="Province of York">York</a>, 14.</p>
<p>Each parish normally has its own parish priest (either a <a href="/wiki/Vicar" title="Vicar">vicar</a> or <a href="/wiki/Rector_(ecclesiastical)" title="Rector (ecclesiastical)">rector</a>, owing to the vagaries of the feudal <a href="/wiki/Tithe" title="Tithe">tithe</a> system: rectories usually having had greater income) and perhaps supported by one or more <a href="/wiki/Curate" title="Curate">curates</a> or <a href="/wiki/Deacon" title="Deacon">deacons</a> - although as a result of ecclesiastical <a href="/wiki/Benefice#Pluralism_in_England" title="Benefice">pluralism</a> some parish priests might have held more than one parish <a href="/wiki/Advowson" title="Advowson">living</a>, placing a curate in charge of those where they do not reside. Now, however, it is common for a number of neighbouring parishes to be placed under one <a href="/wiki/Benefice" title="Benefice">benefice</a> in the charge of a priest who conducts services by rotation, with additional services being provided by <a href="/wiki/Lay_reader" title="Lay reader">lay readers</a> or other non-ordained members of the church community.</p>

<p>A <a href="/wiki/Chapelry" title="Chapelry">chapelry</a> was a subdivision of an <a href="/wiki/Ecclesiastical_parish" class="mw-redirect" title="Ecclesiastical parish">ecclesiastical parish</a> in <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a>, and parts of <a href="/wiki/Lowland_Scotland" class="mw-redirect" title="Lowland Scotland">Lowland Scotland</a> up to the mid 19th century.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> It had a similar status to a <a href="/wiki/Township_(England)" title="Township (England)">township</a> but was so named as it had a <a href="/wiki/Chapel_of_ease" title="Chapel of ease">chapel</a> which acted as a subsidiary place of worship to the main parish church.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>In England <a href="/wiki/Civil_parishes_in_England#History" class="mw-redirect" title="Civil parishes in England">civil parishes</a> and their governing <a href="/wiki/Parish_councils_in_England" title="Parish councils in England">parish councils</a> evolved in the 19th century as ecclesiastical parishes began to be relieved of what became considered to be civic responsibilities. Thus their boundaries began to diverge. The word "parish" acquired a secular usage. Since 1895, a parish council elected by public vote or a (civil) parish meeting administers a civil parish and is formally recognised as the level of local government below a <a href="/wiki/Districts_of_England" title="Districts of England">district council</a>.</p>
<p>The traditional structure of the Church of England with the parish as the basic unit has been exported to other countries and churches throughout the <a href="/wiki/Anglican_Communion" title="Anglican Communion">Anglican Communion</a> and <a href="/wiki/Commonwealth" title="Commonwealth">Commonwealth</a> but does not necessarily continue to be administered in the same way.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Church_of_Scotland">Church of Scotland</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Parish&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Church of Scotland">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><span id="Scotland"></span> <span id="Church_of_Scotland"></span></p>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/History_of_local_government_in_Scotland" title="History of local government in Scotland">History of local government in Scotland</a>, <a href="/wiki/List_of_Church_of_Scotland_parishes" title="List of Church of Scotland parishes">List of Church of Scotland parishes</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Civil_parishes_in_Scotland" title="Civil parishes in Scotland">Civil parishes in Scotland</a></div>
<p>The parish is also the basic level of church administration in the <a href="/wiki/Church_of_Scotland" title="Church of Scotland">Church of Scotland</a>. Spiritual oversight of each parish church in <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a> is responsibility of the congregation's <a href="/wiki/Kirk_Session" class="mw-redirect" title="Kirk Session">Kirk Session</a>. Patronage was regulated in 1711 (<a href="/wiki/Church_Patronage_(Scotland)_Act_1711" title="Church Patronage (Scotland) Act 1711">Patronage Act</a>) and abolished in 1874, with the result that <a href="/wiki/Minister_(Christianity)" title="Minister (Christianity)">ministers</a> must be elected by members of the congregation. Many parish churches in Scotland today are "linked" with neighbouring parish churches served by a single minister. Since the <a href="/wiki/Local_Government_(Scotland)_Act_1929" title="Local Government (Scotland) Act 1929">abolition of parishes as a unit of civil government in Scotland</a> in 1929, Scottish parishes have purely ecclesiastical significance and the boundaries may be adjusted by the local Presbytery.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Methodist_Church">Methodist Church</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Parish&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Methodist Church">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Although they are more often simply called congregations and have no geographic boundaries, in the <a href="/wiki/United_Methodist_Church" title="United Methodist Church">United Methodist Church</a> congregations are called parishes. A prominent example of this usage comes in <i>The Book of Discipline of The United Methodist Church</i>, in which the committee of every local congregation that handles staff support is referred to as the committee on Pastor-Parish Relations. This committee gives recommendations to the bishop on behalf of the parish/congregation since it is the United Methodist Bishop of the <a href="/wiki/Episcopal_area_(United_Methodist_Church)" title="Episcopal area (United Methodist Church)">episcopal area</a> who appoints a pastor to each congregation. The same is true in the <a href="/wiki/African_Methodist_Episcopal_Church" title="African Methodist Episcopal Church">African Methodist Episcopal Church</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Christian_Methodist_Episcopal_Church" title="Christian Methodist Episcopal Church">Christian Methodist Episcopal Church</a>.</p>
<p>In New Zealand, a local grouping of Methodist churches that share one or more <a href="/wiki/Minister_of_religion" class="mw-redirect" title="Minister of religion">ministers</a> (which in the United Kingdom would be called a <a href="/wiki/Methodist_Circuit" class="mw-redirect" title="Methodist Circuit">circuit</a>) is referred to as a parish.</p>
