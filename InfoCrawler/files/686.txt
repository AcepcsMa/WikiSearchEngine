<p><b>Córdoba</b> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'k' in 'kind'">k</span><span title="/ɔːr/: 'ar' in 'war'">ɔːr</span><span title="'d' in 'dye'">d</span><span title="/ə/: 'a' in 'about'">ə</span><span title="'b' in 'buy'">b</span><span title="/ə/: 'a' in 'about'">ə</span></span>/</a></span></span>, <small>Spanish:&#160;</small><span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Spanish" title="Help:IPA/Spanish">[ˈkoɾðoβa]</a></span>),<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> also called <b>Cordova</b> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'k' in 'kind'">k</span><span title="/ɔːr/: 'ar' in 'war'">ɔːr</span><span title="'d' in 'dye'">d</span><span title="/ə/: 'a' in 'about'">ə</span><span title="'v' in 'vie'">v</span><span title="/ə/: 'a' in 'about'">ə</span></span>/</a></span></span>) in <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">English</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> is a city in <a href="/wiki/Andalusia" title="Andalusia">Andalusia</a>, southern Spain, and the capital of the <a href="/wiki/C%C3%B3rdoba_(Spanish_province)" class="mw-redirect" title="Córdoba (Spanish province)">province of Córdoba</a>. It was a Roman settlement, then colonized by Muslim armies in the eighth century. It became the capital of the Islamic <a href="/wiki/Emirate_of_C%C3%B3rdoba" title="Emirate of Córdoba">Emirate</a>, and then of the <a href="/wiki/Caliphate_of_C%C3%B3rdoba" title="Caliphate of Córdoba">Caliphate of Córdoba</a>, including most of the <a href="/wiki/Iberian_Peninsula" title="Iberian Peninsula">Iberian Peninsula</a>.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Caliph" class="mw-redirect" title="Caliph">Caliph</a> <a href="/wiki/Al_Hakam_II" class="mw-redirect" title="Al Hakam II">Al Hakam II</a> opened many libraries in addition to the many medical schools and universities which existed at the time, making Córdoba a centre for education. During these centuries it became the center of a society ruled by Muslims, in which all other groups had a <a href="/wiki/Dhimmi" title="Dhimmi">second-class status</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> It was recaptured by Christian forces in 1236, during the <i><a href="/wiki/Reconquista" title="Reconquista">Reconquista</a></i>. Today it is a moderately sized modern city; its population in 2011 was about 330,000.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Historic_centre_of_C%C3%B3rdoba" title="Historic centre of Córdoba">historic centre</a> was named a <a href="/wiki/UNESCO" title="UNESCO">UNESCO</a> <a href="/wiki/World_Heritage_Site" title="World Heritage Site">World Heritage Site</a>.</p>
<p>Córdoba has the highest summer temperatures in Spain and Europe, with average high temperatures around 37&#160;°C (99&#160;°F) in July and August.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=C%C3%B3rdoba,_Spain&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Timeline_of_C%C3%B3rdoba,_Spain" title="Timeline of Córdoba, Spain">Timeline of Córdoba, Spain</a></div>
<h3><span id="Prehistory,_antiquity_and_Roman_foundation_of_the_city"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Prehistory.2C_antiquity_and_Roman_foundation_of_the_city">Prehistory, antiquity and Roman foundation of the city</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=C%C3%B3rdoba,_Spain&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Prehistory, antiquity and Roman foundation of the city">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The first traces of human presence in the area are remains of a <a href="/wiki/Neanderthal_Man" class="mw-redirect" title="Neanderthal Man">Neanderthal Man</a>, dating to c. 42,000 to 35,000 BC<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> In the 8th century BC, during the ancient <a href="/wiki/Tartessos" title="Tartessos">Tartessos</a> period, a pre-urban settlement existed.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The population gradually learned copper and silver metallurgy.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The first historical mention of a settlement dates to the <a href="/wiki/Carthaginian" class="mw-redirect" title="Carthaginian">Carthaginian</a> expansion across the <a href="/wiki/Guadalquivir" title="Guadalquivir">Guadalquivir</a>, when general <a href="/wiki/Hamilcar_Barca" title="Hamilcar Barca">Hamilcar Barca</a> renamed it <i>Kartuba</i>, from <i>Kart-Juba</i>, meaning "the City of <a href="/wiki/Juba_I_of_Numidia" title="Juba I of Numidia">Juba</a>", a <a href="/wiki/Numidian" class="mw-redirect" title="Numidian">Numidian</a> commander who had died in a battle nearby.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Córdoba was conquered by the <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Rome" title="Ancient Rome">Romans</a> in 206 BC.</p>
<p>In 169 Roman consul M. Claudius Marcellus, grandson of <a href="/wiki/Marcus_Claudius_Marcellus" title="Marcus Claudius Marcellus">Marcus Claudius Marcellus</a>, who had governed both Further and Hither Spain, founded a Latin colony alongside the pre-existing Iberian settlement.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Between 143 and 141 BC the town was besieged by <a href="/wiki/Viriatus" class="mw-redirect" title="Viriatus">Viriatus</a>. A Roman forum is known to have existed in the city in 113 BC.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The famous <a href="/wiki/Cordoba_Treasure" title="Cordoba Treasure">Cordoba Treasure</a>, with mixed local and Roman artistic traditions, was buried in the city at this time; it is now in the <a href="/wiki/British_Museum" title="British Museum">British Museum</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>It became a <i>colonia</i> with the title <i>Patricia</i>, between 46 and 45 B.C.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> It was sacked by Caesar in 45 due to its Pompeian allegiance, and settled with veterans by Augustus. It became capital of Baetica and had a colonial and provincial forum and many temples. It was the chief center of Roman intellectual life in Hispania Ulterior (Further Spain). Its republican poets were succeeded by the Senecas and Lucan.</p>
<p>At the time of <a href="/wiki/Julius_Caesar" title="Julius Caesar">Julius Caesar</a>, Córdoba was the capital of the Roman province of <i><a href="/wiki/Hispania_Baetica" title="Hispania Baetica">Hispania Ulterior / Baetica</a></i>. Great Roman philosophers such as Lucius Annaeus <a href="/wiki/Seneca_the_Younger" title="Seneca the Younger">Seneca the Younger</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> orators such as <a href="/wiki/Seneca_the_Elder" title="Seneca the Elder">Seneca the Elder</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> and poets such as <a href="/wiki/Marcus_Annaeus_Lucanus" class="mw-redirect" title="Marcus Annaeus Lucanus">Lucan</a><sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> came from Roman Cordoba.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>In the late Roman period, its bishop Hosius (Ossius) was the dominant figure of the western Church throughout the earlier 4th cent.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> Later, it occupied an important place in the <i>Provincia Hispaniae</i> of the Byzantine Empire (552–572) and under the <a href="/wiki/Visigoths" title="Visigoths">Visigoths</a>, who conquered it in the late 6th century.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Islamic_rule">Islamic rule</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=C%C3%B3rdoba,_Spain&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Islamic rule">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Emirate_of_C%C3%B3rdoba" title="Emirate of Córdoba">Emirate of Córdoba</a> and <a href="/wiki/Caliphate_of_C%C3%B3rdoba" title="Caliphate of Córdoba">Caliphate of Córdoba</a></div>
<p>Córdoba was captured in 711<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> by a Moorish army. Unlike other Iberian towns, no capitulation was signed and the position was taken by storm. Córdoba was in turn governed by direct Moorish rule. The new Moorish commanders established themselves within the city and in 716 it became a provincial capital, subordinate to the <a href="/wiki/Caliphate_of_Damascus" class="mw-redirect" title="Caliphate of Damascus">Caliphate of Damascus</a>; in Arabic it was known as <b><span lang="ar">قرطبة</span></b> (Qurṭubah).</p>
<p>Different areas were allocated for services in the Saint Vincent Church shared by Christian and Moors, until construction of the <a href="/wiki/Mosque%E2%80%93Cathedral_of_C%C3%B3rdoba#Origins" title="Mosque–Cathedral of Córdoba">Córdoba Mosque</a> started on the same spot under <a href="/wiki/Abd-ar-Rahman_I" class="mw-redirect" title="Abd-ar-Rahman I">Abd-ar-Rahman I</a>. Abd al-Rahman allowed the Christians to rebuild their ruined churches and purchased the Christian half of the church of St Vincent. In May 766 Córdoba was chosen as the capital of the independent Muslim emirate, later <a href="/wiki/Caliphate_of_Cordoba" class="mw-redirect" title="Caliphate of Cordoba">caliphate</a>, of <a href="/wiki/Al-Andalus" title="Al-Andalus">al-Andalus</a>. By 800 the <a href="/wiki/Megacity" title="Megacity">megacity</a> of Cordoba supported over 200,000 residents, 0.1 per cent of the global population. During the apogee of the caliphate (1000 AD), Córdoba had a population of about 500,000 inhabitants;<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> estimates range from 350,000 to 1,000,000. In the 10th and 11th centuries Córdoba was one of the most advanced cities in the world,and a great cultural, political, financial and economic centre.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Great_Mosque_of_C%C3%B3rdoba" class="mw-redirect" title="Great Mosque of Córdoba">Great Mosque of Córdoba</a> dates back to this time. After a change of rulers the situation changed quickly. The vizier al-Mansur–the unofficial ruler of al-Andalus from 976 to 1002—burned most of the books on philosophy to please the Moorish clergy; most of the others were sold off or perished in the civil strife not long after.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the ninth and tenth centuries, Córdoba was "one of the most important cities in the history of the world." In it, "Christians and Jews were involved in the Royal Court and the intellectual life of the city."<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>Regarding Córdoba's importance, Reinhardt Dozy wrote:<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>The fame of Córdoba penetrated even distant Germany: the Saxon nun <a href="/wiki/Hroswitha" class="mw-redirect" title="Hroswitha">Hroswitha</a>, famous in the last half of the 10th century for her Latin poems and dramas, called it the Ornament of the World.</p>
<div class="templatequotecite"><cite>— Reinhardt Dozy</cite></div>
</blockquote>
<p>Córdoba had a prosperous economy with its "skilled artisans and agricultural infrastructure," The manufactured goods for sale included "leather and metal work, glazed tiles and textiles." The agricultural produce included fruits, vegetables, spices, herbs, and raw materials such as "cotton, flax and silk."<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>Córdoba was also famous as "a centre of learning." Education was "taken seriously." <a href="/wiki/Al-Hakam_II" title="Al-Hakam II">Al-Hakam II</a> had a large library. Knowledge in the fields of "medicine, mathematics, astronomy, botany" exceeded the rest of Europe.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Roger_Collins" title="Roger Collins">Roger Collins</a> wrote:<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>The Arab conquest created the conditions for a state of almost permanent warfare in the Iberian Peninsula ... and in scale and intensity exceeded anything to be found elsewhere in Western Europe in these centuries.</p>
<div class="templatequotecite"><cite>— <a href="/wiki/Roger_Collins" title="Roger Collins">Roger Collins</a> in "Caliphs and Kings: Spain, 796-1031"</cite></div>
</blockquote>
<p>In 1002 <a href="/wiki/Almanzor" title="Almanzor">Al-Mansur</a> was returning to Córdoba from an expedition in the area of <a href="/wiki/La_Rioja_(Spain)" title="La Rioja (Spain)">Rioja</a> when he died. His death was the beginning of the end of Córdoba. <a href="/wiki/Abd_al-Malik_al-Muzaffar" title="Abd al-Malik al-Muzaffar">Abd al-Malik al-Muzaffar</a>, al-Mansur's older son, succeeded to his father’s authority, but he died in 1008, possibly assassinated. <a href="/wiki/Sanchuelo" class="mw-redirect" title="Sanchuelo">Sanchuelo</a>, Abd al-Malik’s younger brother succeeded him. While Sanchuelo was away fighting <a href="/wiki/Alfonso_V_of_Leon" class="mw-redirect" title="Alfonso V of Leon">Alfonso V of Leon</a>, a revolution made Mohammed II al-Mahdi the Caliph. Sanchuelo sued for pardon but he was killed when he returned to Cardova. The slaves revolted against Mahdi, killed him in 1009, and replaced him with <a href="/wiki/Hisham_II" title="Hisham II">Hisham II</a> in 1010. Hisham II wore a veil, used makeup, kept a male harem, and was forced out of office. In 1012 the <a href="/wiki/Berbers" title="Berbers">Berbers</a> "sacked Cardova." In 1016 the slaves captured Cardova and searched for Hisham II, but he had escaped to Asia. This event was followed by a fight for power until <a href="/wiki/Hisham_III" class="mw-redirect" title="Hisham III">Hisham III</a>, the last of the <a href="/wiki/Umayyads" class="mw-redirect" title="Umayyads">Umayyads</a>, was routed from Córdoba in 1031.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<p>After 1031, Córdoba lost its prosperity and fame and became an isolated city. The "ruling elite" were well known for their "disinterest in the outside world ... and intellectual laziness." <sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Modern_history">Modern history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=C%C3%B3rdoba,_Spain&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Modern history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Plano_de_C%C3%B3rdoba_1851.jpeg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/57/Plano_de_C%C3%B3rdoba_1851.jpeg/200px-Plano_de_C%C3%B3rdoba_1851.jpeg" width="200" height="156" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/57/Plano_de_C%C3%B3rdoba_1851.jpeg/300px-Plano_de_C%C3%B3rdoba_1851.jpeg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/57/Plano_de_C%C3%B3rdoba_1851.jpeg/400px-Plano_de_C%C3%B3rdoba_1851.jpeg 2x" data-file-width="3165" data-file-height="2462" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Plano_de_C%C3%B3rdoba_1851.jpeg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A city map of Córdoba, Andalusia in 1851 <b>(Spanish language edition)</b></div>

</div>
<p>During the process known as the Spanish <i>Reconquista</i>, Córdoba was captured by King <a href="/wiki/Ferdinand_III_of_Castile" title="Ferdinand III of Castile">Ferdinand III of Castile</a> on 29 June 1236, after a siege of several months. The city was divided into 14 <i>colaciones</i>, and numerous new church buildings were added. The centre of the mosque was converted into a large Catholic cathedral.</p>
<p>The city declined, especially after <a href="/wiki/Renaissance" title="Renaissance">Renaissance</a> times. In the 18th century it was reduced to just 20,000 inhabitants. The population and economy started to increase again only in the early 20th century.</p>
<p>With the most extensive historical heritages in the world declared <a href="/wiki/World_Heritage_Site" title="World Heritage Site">World Heritage Site</a> by <a href="/wiki/UNESCO" title="UNESCO">UNESCO</a> (on 17 December 1984), the city also has a number of modern areas, including the districts of Zoco and the railway station district.</p>
<p>The regional government (the <a href="/wiki/Junta_de_Andaluc%C3%ADa" class="mw-redirect" title="Junta de Andalucía">Junta de Andalucía</a>) has for some time<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Dates_and_numbers#Chronological_items" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Dates and numbers"><span title="The time period mentioned near this tag is ambiguous. (August 2017)">when?</span></a></i>]</sup> been studying the creation of a <i>Córdoba Metropolitan Area</i> that would comprise, in addition to the capital itself, the towns of <a href="/wiki/Villafranca_de_C%C3%B3rdoba" title="Villafranca de Córdoba">Villafranca de Córdoba</a>, <a href="/wiki/Obejo" title="Obejo">Obejo</a>, <a href="/wiki/La_Carlota,_C%C3%B3rdoba,_Spain" class="mw-redirect" title="La Carlota, Córdoba, Spain">La Carlota</a>, <a href="/wiki/Villaharta" title="Villaharta">Villaharta</a>, <a href="/wiki/Villaviciosa,_C%C3%B3rdoba" class="mw-redirect" title="Villaviciosa, Córdoba">Villaviciosa</a>, <a href="/wiki/Almod%C3%B3var_del_R%C3%ADo" title="Almodóvar del Río">Almodóvar del Río</a> and <a href="/wiki/Guadalc%C3%A1zar_(C%C3%B3rdoba)" title="Guadalcázar (Córdoba)">Guadalcázar</a>. The combined population of such an area would be around 351,000.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geography">Geography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=C%C3%B3rdoba,_Spain&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Geography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The city is on the banks of the <a href="/wiki/Guadalquivir" title="Guadalquivir">Guadalquivir</a> river, and its easy access to the mining resources of the <a href="/wiki/Sierra_Morena" title="Sierra Morena">Sierra Morena</a> (coal, lead, zinc) satisfies the population's needs.</p>
<p>The city is in a depression of the valley of the Guadalquivir. In the north is the Sierra Morena, which defines the borders of the municipal area.</p>
<p>Córdoba is <a href="/wiki/Antipodes#Cities" title="Antipodes">one of the few cities</a> in the world that has a near-exact <a href="/wiki/Antipodes" title="Antipodes">antipodal</a> city – <a href="/wiki/Hamilton,_New_Zealand" title="Hamilton, New Zealand">Hamilton</a>, New Zealand.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Climate">Climate</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=C%C3%B3rdoba,_Spain&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Climate">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Córdoba has a <a href="/wiki/Subtropical" class="mw-redirect" title="Subtropical">Subtropical</a>-<a href="/wiki/Mediterranean_climate" title="Mediterranean climate">Mediterranean climate</a> (<a href="/wiki/K%C3%B6ppen_climate_classification" title="Köppen climate classification">Köppen climate classification</a> <i>Csa</i>).<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> It has the highest summer average daily temperatures in Europe (averaging 36.9&#160;°C (98&#160;°F) in July) and days with temperatures over 40&#160;°C (104&#160;°F) are common in the summer months. August's 24-hour average of 28.0&#160;°C (82&#160;°F) is also among the highest in Europe, despite relatively cool nightly temperatures.</p>
<p>Winters are mild, yet cooler than other low lying cities in southern Spain due to its interior location, wedged between the Sierra Morena and the Penibaetic System. Precipitation is concentrated in the coldest months; this is due to the Atlantic coastal influence. Precipitation is generated by storms from the west that occur most frequently from December to February. This Atlantic characteristic then gives way to a hot summer with significant drought more typical of Mediterranean climates. Annual rain surpasses 600&#160;mm (24&#160;in), although it is recognized to vary from year to year.</p>
<p>Registered maximum temperatures at the <a href="/wiki/C%C3%B3rdoba_Airport" title="Córdoba Airport">Córdoba Airport</a> (6 kilometres (4 miles) from the city) are 46.9&#160;°C (116.4&#160;°F) (13 July 2017) and 46.6&#160;°C (115.9&#160;°F) (23 July 1995). The lowest temperature ever recorded was −8.2&#160;°C (17.2&#160;°F) (28 January 2005).<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p>

<p>Córdoba has the second largest Old town in Europe, the largest urban area in the world declared World Heritage by UNESCO. The most important building and symbol of the city, the Great Mosque of Córdoba and current cathedral, and the <a href="/wiki/Roman_bridge_of_C%C3%B3rdoba" title="Roman bridge of Córdoba">Roman bridge</a>, are the city's best-known features. Other Roman remains include the <a href="/wiki/Roman_temple_of_C%C3%B3rdoba" title="Roman temple of Córdoba">Roman Temple</a>, the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Roman_theatre_of_C%C3%B3rdoba&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Roman theatre of Córdoba (page does not exist)">Theatre</a>, <a href="/wiki/Roman_mausoleum_of_C%C3%B3rdoba" title="Roman mausoleum of Córdoba">Mausoleum</a>, the Colonial Forum, the <i>Forum Adiectum</i>, an amphitheater and the remains of the Palace of Emperor <a href="/wiki/Maximian" title="Maximian">Maximian</a> in the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Archaeological_site_of_Cercadilla&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Archaeological site of Cercadilla (page does not exist)">Archaeological site of Cercadilla</a>.</p>
<p>Near the cathedral is the old <a href="/w/index.php?title=Jewish_quarter_of_C%C3%B3rdoba&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Jewish quarter of Córdoba (page does not exist)">Jewish quarter</a>, which consists of many irregular streets, such as <i>Calleja de las Flores</i> and <i>Calleja del Pañuelo</i>, and which is home to the <a href="/wiki/C%C3%B3rdoba_Synagogue" title="Córdoba Synagogue">Synagogue</a> and the Sephardic House. In the extreme southwest of the Old Town is the <a href="/wiki/Alc%C3%A1zar_de_los_Reyes_Cristianos" title="Alcázar de los Reyes Cristianos">Alcázar de los Reyes Cristianos</a>, a former royal property and the seat of the <a href="/wiki/Spanish_Inquisition" title="Spanish Inquisition">Inquisition</a>; adjacent to it are the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Stables_of_C%C3%B3rdoba" title="Royal Stables of Córdoba">Royal Stables</a>, where <a href="/wiki/Andalusian_horse" title="Andalusian horse">Andalusian horses</a> are bred. Near the stables are located, along the walls, the medieval Baths of the Caliphate. In the south of the Old town and east of the great cathedral, in the <a href="/wiki/Plaza_del_Potro" title="Plaza del Potro">Plaza del Potro</a>, is the <i>Posada del Potro</i>, a row of <a href="/wiki/Inn" title="Inn">inns</a> mentioned in literary works including <i>Don Quixote</i> and <i>La Feria de los Discretos</i>, and which remained active until 1972. Both the plaza and the inn get their name from the fountain in the centre of the plaza, which represents a foal (<i>potro</i>). Not far from this plaza is the <i>Arco del Portillo</i> (a 14th-century arch).</p>
<p>Along the banks of the <a href="/wiki/Guadalquivir" title="Guadalquivir">Guadalquivir</a> are the <a href="/wiki/Mills_of_the_Guadalquivir" title="Mills of the Guadalquivir">Mills of the Guadalquivir</a>, Moorish-era buildings that used the water flow to grind flour. They include the Albolafia, Alegría, Carbonell, Casillas, Enmedio, Lope García, Martos, Pápalo, San Antonio, San Lorenzo and San Rafael mills.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Surrounding the large Old town are the <a href="/wiki/Roman_walls_of_C%C3%B3rdoba" title="Roman walls of Córdoba">Roman walls</a>: gates include the Puerta de Almodóvar, the Puerta de Sevilla and <a href="/wiki/Puerta_del_Puente" title="Puerta del Puente">Puerta del Puente</a>, which are the only three gates remaining from the original thirteen. Towers and fortresses include the <a href="/wiki/Torre_de_la_Malmuerta" title="Torre de la Malmuerta">Malmuerta Tower</a>, the Belén Tower and the Puerta del Rincón's Tower, and the fortress of the <a href="/wiki/Calahorra_Tower" title="Calahorra Tower">Calahorra Tower</a> and of the Donceles Tower.</p>
<p>Palace buildings in the Old Town include the <i>Palacio de Viana</i> (14th century) and the <i><a href="/wiki/Palacio_de_la_Merced" title="Palacio de la Merced">Palacio de la Merced</a></i> among others. On the outskirts of the city lies the archaeological site of the city of <a href="/wiki/Medina_Azahara" title="Medina Azahara">Medina Azahara</a>, which, together with the <a href="/wiki/Alhambra" title="Alhambra">Alhambra</a> in <a href="/wiki/Granada" title="Granada">Granada</a>, is one of the main examples of Spanish-Muslim architecture in Spain.</p>
<p>Other sights are the <i>Cuesta del Bailío</i> (a staircase connecting the upper and lower part of the city) and the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Minaret_of_San_Juan&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Minaret of San Juan (page does not exist)">Minaret of San Juan</a>, once part of a mosque.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sculptures_and_memorials">Sculptures and memorials</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=C%C3%B3rdoba,_Spain&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Sculptures and memorials">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Scattered throughout the city are ten statues of the <a href="/wiki/Raphael_(archangel)" title="Raphael (archangel)">Archangel Raphael</a>, protector and custodian of the city. These are called <a href="/w/index.php?title=Triumphs_of_Saint_Raphael&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Triumphs of Saint Raphael (page does not exist)">Triumphs of Saint Raphael</a>, and are located in landmarks such as the Roman Bridge, the Puerta del Puente and the Plaza del Potro.</p>
<p>In the western part of the Historic Centre are the statue of Seneca (near the <i>Puerta de Almodóvar</i>, a gate of Islamic origin, (the Statue of <a href="/wiki/Averroes" title="Averroes">Averroes</a> (next to the <i>Puerta de la Luna</i>), and <a href="/wiki/Maimonides" title="Maimonides">Maimonides</a> (in the plaza de Tiberiades). Further south, near the Puerta de Sevilla, are the sculpture to the poet <a href="/wiki/Ibn_Zayd%C3%BAn" title="Ibn Zaydún">Ibn Zaydún</a> and the sculpture of the writer and poet <a href="/wiki/Ibn_Hazm" title="Ibn Hazm">Ibn Hazm</a> and, inside the <a href="/wiki/Alc%C3%A1zar_de_los_Reyes_Cristianos" title="Alcázar de los Reyes Cristianos">Alcázar de los Reyes Cristianos</a>, the monument to the <a href="/wiki/Catholic_Monarchs" title="Catholic Monarchs">Catholic Monarchs</a> and Christopher Columbus.</p>
<p>There are also several sculptures in plazas of the Old Town. In the central <a href="/w/index.php?title=Plaza_de_las_Tendillas&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Plaza de las Tendillas (page does not exist)">Plaza de las Tendillas</a> is the equestrian statue of <a href="/wiki/Gonzalo_Fern%C3%A1ndez_de_C%C3%B3rdoba" title="Gonzalo Fernández de Córdoba">Gonzalo Fernández de Córdoba</a>, in the Plaza de Capuchinos is the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Cristo_de_los_Faroles&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Cristo de los Faroles (page does not exist)">Cristo de los Faroles</a>, in Plaza de la Trinidad is the statue of <a href="/wiki/Luis_de_G%C3%B3ngora" title="Luis de Góngora">Luis de Góngora</a>, in the Plaza del Cardenal Salazar is the bust of Ahmad ibn Muhammad abu Yafar al-Gafiqi, in the Plaza de Capuchinas is the statue to the bishop Osio, in Plaza del Conde de Priego is the monument to <a href="/wiki/Manolete" title="Manolete">Manolete</a> and the Campo Santo de los Mártires is a statue to <a href="/wiki/Al-Hakam_II" title="Al-Hakam II">Al-Hakam II</a> and the monument to the lovers.</p>
<p>In the Jardines de la Agricultura is the monument to the painter <a href="/wiki/Julio_Romero_de_Torres" title="Julio Romero de Torres">Julio Romero de Torres</a>, a bust by sculptor <a href="/wiki/Mateo_Inurria" title="Mateo Inurria">Mateo Inurria</a>, a bust of the poet <a href="/wiki/Julio_Aumente" title="Julio Aumente">Julio Aumente</a> and the sculpture dedicated to the gardener Aniceto García Roldán, who was killed in the park. Further south, in the Gardens of the Duke of Rivas, is a statue of writer and poet <a href="/wiki/%C3%81ngel_de_Saavedra,_3rd_Duke_of_Rivas" title="Ángel de Saavedra, 3rd Duke of Rivas">Ángel de Saavedra, 3rd Duke of Rivas</a> by sculptor <a href="/wiki/Mariano_Benlliure" title="Mariano Benlliure">Mariano Benlliure</a>.</p>
<p>In the Guadalquivir river, near the San Rafael Bridge is the Island of the sculptures, an artificial island with a dozen stone sculptures executed during the International Sculpture Symposium. Up the river, near the Miraflores bridge, is the "<a href="/w/index.php?title=Hombre_R%C3%ADo&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Hombre Río (page does not exist)">Hombre Río</a>", a sculpture of a swimmer looking to the sky and whose orientation varies depending from the current.</p>

Scattered throughout the garden are numerous sculptures such as the sculpture in memory of <a href="/wiki/Julio_Romero_de_Torres" title="Julio Romero de Torres">Julio Romero de Torres</a>, the sculpture to the composer <a href="/wiki/Julio_Aumente" title="Julio Aumente">Julio Aumente</a> and the bust of <a href="/wiki/Mateo_Inurria" title="Mateo Inurria">Mateo Inurria</a>. <li>Parque Cruz Conde, located southwest of the city, is an open park and barrier-free park in <a href="/wiki/English_garden" class="mw-redirect" title="English garden">English gardens</a> style.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></li>
<li><i>Paseo de Cordoba</i>. Located on the underground train tracks, it is a long tour of several km in length with more than 434,000&#160;m². The tour has numerous fountains, including six formed by a portico of falling water which form a waterfall to a pond with four levels. Integrated into the tour is a pond of water from the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Empire" title="Roman Empire">Roman era</a>, and the building of the old train station of RENFE, now converted into offices of <a href="/wiki/Canal_Sur" title="Canal Sur">Canal Sur</a>.</li>
<li>Jardines Juan Carlos I, in the Ciudad Jardín neighborhood. It is a fortress which occupies an area of about 12,500 square metres.</li>
<li>Jardines del Conde de Vallellano, located on both sides of the avenue of the same name. It includes a large L-shaped pond with a capacity of 3,000&#160;m<sup>3</sup> (105,944.00&#160;cu&#160;ft) and archaeological remains embedded in the gardens, among which is a Roman cistern from the second half of the 1st century BC.</li>
<li><i>Parque de la Asomadilla</i>, with a surface of 27 hectares, is the second largest park in Andalusia.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> The park recreates a Mediterranean forest vegetation, such as hawthorn, pomegranate, hackberry, oak, olive, tamarisk, cypress, elms, pines, oaks and carob trees among others.</li>
<li><i>Balcón del Guadalquivir</i></li>
<li><i>Jardines de Colón'</i></li>
<li><i>Sotos de la Albolafia</i>. Declared Natural monument by the Andalusian Autonomous Government, it is located in a stretch of the Guadalquivir river from the Roman Bridge and the San Rafael Bridge, with an area of 21.36 hectares.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> Host a large variety of birds and is an important point of migration for many birds.</li>
<li><i>Parque periurbano Los Villares</i></li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Bridges">Bridges</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=C%C3%B3rdoba,_Spain&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Bridges">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Córdoba has seven bridges.</p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Spain_Andalusia_Cordoba_BW_2015-10-27_12-11-37.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a2/Spain_Andalusia_Cordoba_BW_2015-10-27_12-11-37.jpg/200px-Spain_Andalusia_Cordoba_BW_2015-10-27_12-11-37.jpg" width="200" height="310" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a2/Spain_Andalusia_Cordoba_BW_2015-10-27_12-11-37.jpg/300px-Spain_Andalusia_Cordoba_BW_2015-10-27_12-11-37.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a2/Spain_Andalusia_Cordoba_BW_2015-10-27_12-11-37.jpg/400px-Spain_Andalusia_Cordoba_BW_2015-10-27_12-11-37.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2438" data-file-height="3778" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Spain_Andalusia_Cordoba_BW_2015-10-27_12-11-37.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The <a href="/wiki/Tower_of_Calahorra" class="mw-redirect" title="Tower of Calahorra">Tower of Calahorra</a> to one side of the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Bridge_of_C%C3%B3rdoba" class="mw-redirect" title="Roman Bridge of Córdoba">Roman Bridge</a>.</div>
</div>
</div>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Roman_bridge_of_C%C3%B3rdoba" title="Roman bridge of Córdoba">Roman Bridge</a>, over the <a href="/wiki/Guadalquivir" title="Guadalquivir">Guadalquivir</a> River. It links the area of Campo de la Verdad with Barrio de la Catedral. It was the only bridge of the city for twenty centuries, until the construction of the San Rafael Bridge in the mid-20th century. Built in the early 1st century BC, during the period of Roman rule in Córdoba, probably replacing a more primitive wooden one, it has a length of about 250&#160;m and has 16 arches.</li>
<li>San Rafael Bridge, consisting of eight arches of 25&#160;m span and a length of 217&#160;m. The width is between parapets, divided into 12&#160;m of cobblestone for four circulations and two tiled concrete sidewalks. It was inaugurated on 29 April 1953 joining the Avenue Corregidor with Plaza de Andalucía. In January 2004 the plaques reading "His Excellency the Head of State and Generalissimo of all the Armies, Francisco Franco Bahamonde, opened this bridge of the Guadalquivir on 29 April 1953", which were on both sides of each of the entrances of the bridge, were removed.</li>
<li>Andalusia Bridge, a suspension bridge.</li>
<li><i>Puente de Miraflores</i>, known as "the rusty bridge". This bridge links the Street San Fernando and Ronda de Isasa with the Miraflores peninsula. It was designed by Herrero, Suárez and Casado and inaugurated on 2 May 2003. At first, in 1989, a proposal by architect-engineer <a href="/wiki/Santiago_Calatrava" title="Santiago Calatrava">Santiago Calatrava</a> was considered<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> that would look like the Lusitania Bridge of <a href="/wiki/M%C3%A9rida,_Spain" title="Mérida, Spain">Mérida</a>, but this was eventually discarded because its height would obscure the view of the Great Mosque.</li>
<li>Autovía del Sur Bridge.</li>
<li>Abbas Ibn Firnas Bridge, Inaugurated in January 2011 It is part of the variant west of Córdoba.</li>
<li><i>Puente del Arenal</i>, connecting Avenue Campo de la Verdad with the Recinto Ferial (fairground) of Cordoba.</li>
</ul>

<p>As of 2017<sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=C%C3%B3rdoba,_Spain&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup> <a href="/w/index.php?title=Isabel_Ambrosio&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Isabel Ambrosio (page does not exist)">Isabel Ambrosio</a> (<a href="/w/index.php?title=Spanish_Social_Worker_Party_(Spain)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Spanish Social Worker Party (Spain) (page does not exist)">PSOE</a>) was the mayor of Córdoba.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="May_celebrations">May celebrations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=C%C3%B3rdoba,_Spain&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: May celebrations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Ferria_de_Cordoba.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/08/Ferria_de_Cordoba.jpg/220px-Ferria_de_Cordoba.jpg" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/08/Ferria_de_Cordoba.jpg/330px-Ferria_de_Cordoba.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/08/Ferria_de_Cordoba.jpg/440px-Ferria_de_Cordoba.jpg 2x" data-file-width="4320" data-file-height="3240" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Ferria_de_Cordoba.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
This picture was taken during the <i>feria de Cordoba,</i> or the festival of Cordoba.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Tourism is especially intense in Córdoba during May because of the weather and as this month hosts three festivals.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup></p>
<p>The May Crosses Festival takes place at the beginning of the month. During three or four days, crosses of around 3 m height are placed in many squares and streets and decorated with flowers and a contest is held to choose the most beautiful one. Usually there is regional food and music near the crosses.</p>
<p>The Patios Festival is celebrated during the second and third week of the month. Many houses of the historic centre open their private patios to the public and compete in a contest. Both the architectonic value and the floral decorations are taken into consideration to choose the winners. It is usually very difficult and expensive to find accommodation in the city during the festival.</p>
<p>Córdoba's Fair takes place at the ending of the month and is similar to the better known <a href="/w/index.php?title=Sevilla_Fair&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Sevilla Fair (page does not exist)">Sevilla Fair</a> with some differences, mainly that the Sevilla one is private, while the Cordoba one is not.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="People">People</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=C%C3%B3rdoba,_Spain&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: People">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Córdoba was the birthplace of the following philosophers and religious scholars:</p>
<ul>
<li>In Roman times the <a href="/wiki/Stoicism" title="Stoicism">Stoic</a> philosopher <a href="/wiki/Lucius_Annaeus_Seneca" class="mw-redirect" title="Lucius Annaeus Seneca">Seneca</a></li>
<li>In Islamic times
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Abd_Allah_al-Qaysi" title="Abd Allah al-Qaysi">Abd Allah al-Qaysi</a>, an early jurist responsible for spreading the <a href="/wiki/Zahiri" class="mw-redirect" title="Zahiri">Zahirite</a> school</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Ibn_Hazm" title="Ibn Hazm">Ibn Hazm</a>, a major Muslim theologian and legal jurist,</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Averroes" title="Averroes">Averroes</a>, an important figure in both Muslim and Western philosophy,</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Mundhir_bin_Sa%27%C4%ABd_al-Ball%C5%AB%E1%B9%AD%C4%AB" class="mw-redirect" title="Mundhir bin Sa'īd al-Ballūṭī">Mundhir bin Sa'īd al-Ballūṭī</a>, a prominent judge for the <a href="/wiki/Caliph_of_Cordoba" class="mw-redirect" title="Caliph of Cordoba">Caliph of Cordoba</a>,</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Ibn_Ma%E1%B8%8D%C4%81%CA%BE" title="Ibn Maḍāʾ">Ibn Maḍāʾ</a>, the first <a href="/wiki/Linguist" class="mw-redirect" title="Linguist">linguist</a> to write about <a href="/wiki/Dependency_grammar" title="Dependency grammar">dependency grammar</a>,</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Al-Qurtubi" title="Al-Qurtubi">al-Qurtubi</a>, a leading jurist of the <a href="/wiki/Maliki" title="Maliki">Maliki</a> <a href="/wiki/Madhhab" title="Madhhab">school</a>, and</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Moses_Maimonides" class="mw-redirect" title="Moses Maimonides">Moses Maimonides</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Rabbi" title="Rabbi">rabbi</a> who radically changed the direction of Jewish philosophy.</li>
</ul>
</li>
</ul>
<p>Córdoba was also the birthplace of</p>
<ul>
<li>The Roman poet <a href="/wiki/Lucan_(poet)" class="mw-redirect" title="Lucan (poet)">Lucan</a>,</li>
<li>The medieval Spanish poet <a href="/wiki/Juan_de_Mena" title="Juan de Mena">Juan de Mena</a></li>
<li>The Renaissance poet <a href="/wiki/Luis_de_G%C3%B3ngora" title="Luis de Góngora">Luis de Góngora</a>, who lived most of his life and wrote all his most important works but one in Córdoba.</li>
</ul>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Renaissance" title="Renaissance">Renaissance</a> philosopher <a href="/wiki/Abraham_Cohen_de_Herrera" title="Abraham Cohen de Herrera">Abraham Cohen de Herrera</a> and the Jewish mystic <a href="/wiki/Moses_ben_Jacob_Cordovero" title="Moses ben Jacob Cordovero">Moses ben Jacob Cordovero</a> both descended from families which lived in Córdoba before the <a href="/wiki/Expulsion_of_the_Jews_from_Spain" class="mw-redirect" title="Expulsion of the Jews from Spain">expulsion of the Jews from Spain</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Transport">Transport</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=C%C3%B3rdoba,_Spain&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Transport">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The city is connected by high speed trains to the following Spanish cities: <a href="/wiki/Madrid" title="Madrid">Madrid</a>, <a href="/wiki/Barcelona" title="Barcelona">Barcelona</a>, <a href="/wiki/Seville" title="Seville">Seville</a>, <a href="/wiki/M%C3%A1laga" title="Málaga">Málaga</a> and <a href="/wiki/Zaragoza" title="Zaragoza">Zaragoza</a>. More than 20 trains per day connect the downtown area, in 54 minutes, with Málaga María Zambrano station, which provides interchange capability to destinations along the Costa del Sol, including <a href="/wiki/M%C3%A1laga_Airport" title="Málaga Airport">Málaga Airport</a>. The city is also well connected by highways with the rest of the country and Portugal.</p>
