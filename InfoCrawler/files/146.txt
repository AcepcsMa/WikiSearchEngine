<p>The <b>Bishop of Lincoln</b> is the <a href="/wiki/Ordinary_(officer)" title="Ordinary (officer)">ordinary</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Church_of_England" title="Church of England">Church of England</a> <a href="/wiki/Diocese_of_Lincoln" title="Diocese of Lincoln">Diocese of Lincoln</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Province_of_Canterbury" title="Province of Canterbury">Province of Canterbury</a>.</p>
<p>The present diocese covers the county of <a href="/wiki/Lincolnshire" title="Lincolnshire">Lincolnshire</a> and the unitary authority areas of North Lincolnshire and North East Lincolnshire. The bishop's seat (<i><a href="/wiki/Cathedra" title="Cathedra">cathedra</a></i>) is located in the <a href="/wiki/Lincoln_Cathedral" title="Lincoln Cathedral">Cathedral Church of the Blessed Virgin Mary</a> in the city of <a href="/wiki/Lincoln,_England" title="Lincoln, England">Lincoln</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Cathedral" title="Cathedral">cathedral</a> was originally a <a href="/wiki/Minster_(church)" title="Minster (church)">minster</a> church founded around 653 and refounded as a cathedral in 1072. Until the 1530s the bishops were in full communion with the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic_Church" class="mw-redirect" title="Roman Catholic Church">Roman Catholic Church</a>.</p>
<p>The historic medieval Bishop's Palace lies immediately to the south of the cathedral in Palace Yard; managed by English Heritage, it is open to visitors.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> An adjacent later residence (first used by <a href="/wiki/Edward_King_(Bishop_of_Lincoln)" class="mw-redirect" title="Edward King (Bishop of Lincoln)">Bishop Edward King</a> in 1885<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>) was converted from office accommodation to reopen in 2009 as a 16-bedroom conference centre and wedding venue.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> It is now known as Edward King House and provides offices for the bishops, archdeacons and diocesan staff. A 14-bedroomed house on Eastgate was the official residence in use from 1948 until 2011, when the bishop's office staff and home were separated, allowing the incoming bishop, <a href="/wiki/Christopher_Lowson" title="Christopher Lowson">Christopher Lowson</a>, to live in a modern five-bedroom house. <sup id="cite_ref-bishop72_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bishop72-5">[5]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bishop_of_Lincoln&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>The <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Saxons" title="Anglo-Saxons">Anglo-Saxon</a> dioceses of <a href="/wiki/Diocese_of_Lindsey" class="mw-redirect" title="Diocese of Lindsey">Lindsey</a> and <a href="/wiki/Diocese_of_Leicester" title="Diocese of Leicester">Leicester</a> were established when the large <a href="/wiki/Diocese_of_Mercia" class="mw-redirect" title="Diocese of Mercia">Diocese of Mercia</a> was divided in the late 7th century into the bishoprics of <a href="/wiki/Bishop_of_Lichfield" title="Bishop of Lichfield">Lichfield</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bishop_of_Leicester" title="Bishop of Leicester">Leicester</a> (for <a href="/wiki/Mercia" title="Mercia">Mercia</a> itself), <a href="/wiki/Bishop_of_Worcester" title="Bishop of Worcester">Worcester</a> (for the <a href="/wiki/Hwicce" title="Hwicce">Hwicce</a>), <a href="/wiki/Bishop_of_Hereford" title="Bishop of Hereford">Hereford</a> (for the <a href="/wiki/Magons%C3%A6te" title="Magonsæte">Magonsæte</a>) and <a href="/wiki/Bishop_of_Lindsey" title="Bishop of Lindsey">Lindsey</a> (for the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Lindsey" title="Kingdom of Lindsey">Lindisfaras</a>).</p>
<p>The historic Bishop of Dorchester was a <a href="/wiki/Prelate" title="Prelate">prelate</a> who administered the <a href="/wiki/Mercian_dioceses_of_Dorchester" class="mw-redirect" title="Mercian dioceses of Dorchester">Diocese of Dorchester</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Saxons" title="Anglo-Saxons">Anglo-Saxon</a> period. The bishop's seat, or <a href="/wiki/Cathedra" title="Cathedra">cathedra</a>, was at <a href="/wiki/Dorchester_Abbey" title="Dorchester Abbey">the cathedral</a> in <a href="/wiki/Dorchester,_Oxfordshire" class="mw-redirect" title="Dorchester, Oxfordshire">Dorchester-on-Thames</a> in <a href="/wiki/Oxfordshire" title="Oxfordshire">Oxfordshire</a>.</p>
<p>In the 660s the seat at Dorchester-on-Thames was abandoned, but briefly in the late 670s it was once more a bishop's seat under <a href="/wiki/%C3%86tla" title="Ætla">Ætla</a>, under Mercian control.<sup id="cite_ref-Kirby48_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kirby48-6">[6]</a></sup> The town of Dorchester again became the seat of a bishop in around 875, when the <a href="/wiki/Mercia" title="Mercia">Mercian</a> <a href="/wiki/Bishop_of_Leicester" title="Bishop of Leicester">Bishop of Leicester</a> transferred his seat there. The diocese merged with that of <a href="/wiki/Bishop_of_Lindsey" title="Bishop of Lindsey">Lindsey</a> in 971; the bishop's seat was moved to <a href="/wiki/Lincoln,_Lincolnshire" class="mw-redirect" title="Lincoln, Lincolnshire">Lincoln</a> in 1072 and thus the Mercian Bishops of Dorchester were succeeded by the Bishops of Lincoln.</p>
<p>The first bishops of Leicester were originally <a href="/wiki/Prelate" title="Prelate">prelates</a> who administered an <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Saxons" title="Anglo-Saxons">Anglo-Saxon</a> <a href="/wiki/Diocese" title="Diocese">diocese</a> between the 7th and 9th centuries. The bishopric fell victim to the invasion by the <a href="/wiki/Danelaw" title="Danelaw">Danes</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Episcopal_see" title="Episcopal see">episcopal see</a> was transferred to <a href="/wiki/Dorchester-on-Thames" class="mw-redirect" title="Dorchester-on-Thames">Dorchester-on-Thames</a> in Oxfordshire.<sup id="cite_ref-LeiCathHistory_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-LeiCathHistory-7">[7]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-handbook238_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-handbook238-8">[8]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-dpkirby_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dpkirby-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>The dioceses of Lindsey and Leicester continued until the <a href="/wiki/Denmark" title="Denmark">Danish</a> <a href="/wiki/Viking" class="mw-redirect" title="Viking">Viking</a> invasions and establishment of the <a href="/wiki/Danelaw" title="Danelaw">Danelaw</a> in the 9th century. The <a href="/wiki/Episcopal_see" title="Episcopal see">see</a> of Leicester was transferred to Dorchester, now in <a href="/wiki/Oxfordshire" title="Oxfordshire">Oxfordshire</a>, sometime between 869 and 888. After an interruption, the see of Lindsey was resumed until it was united with the bishopric of Dorchester in the early 11th century. The diocese was the largest in England, extending from the <a href="/wiki/River_Thames" title="River Thames">River Thames</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Humber_Estuary" class="mw-redirect" title="Humber Estuary">Humber Estuary</a>.</p>
<p>In 1072, <a href="/wiki/Remigius_de_F%C3%A9camp" title="Remigius de Fécamp">Remigius de Fécamp</a> moved the see of Dorchester to Lincoln, but the bishops of Lincoln retained significant landholdings within <a href="/wiki/Oxfordshire" title="Oxfordshire">Oxfordshire</a>. Because of this historic link, for a long time <a href="/wiki/Banbury" title="Banbury">Banbury</a> remained a "<a href="/wiki/Royal_Peculiar" title="Royal Peculiar">peculiar</a>" of the Bishop of Lincoln.</p>
<p>Until the 1530s the bishops were in full communion with the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic_Church" class="mw-redirect" title="Roman Catholic Church">Roman Catholic Church</a>. During the <a href="/wiki/English_Reformation" title="English Reformation">English Reformation</a> they changed their allegiance back and forth between the <a href="/wiki/The_Crown" title="The Crown">crown</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Papacy" class="mw-redirect" title="Papacy">papacy</a>. Under <a href="/wiki/Henry_VIII_of_England" title="Henry VIII of England">Henry VIII</a> and <a href="/wiki/Edward_VI_of_England" title="Edward VI of England">Edward VI</a>, the bishops conformed to the <a href="/wiki/Church_of_England" title="Church of England">Church of England</a>, but under <a href="/wiki/Mary_I_of_England" title="Mary I of England">Mary I</a> they adhered to the Roman Catholic Church. Since the <a href="/wiki/English_Reformation" title="English Reformation">English Reformation</a>, the bishops and diocese of Lincoln have been part of the reformed Church of England and the <a href="/wiki/Anglican_Communion" title="Anglican Communion">Anglican Communion</a>.</p>
<p>The dioceses of <a href="/wiki/Diocese_of_Oxford" title="Diocese of Oxford">Oxford</a> and <a href="/wiki/Anglican_Diocese_of_Peterborough" title="Anglican Diocese of Peterborough">Peterborough</a> were created in 1541 out of parts of the <a href="/wiki/Diocese_of_Lincoln" title="Diocese of Lincoln">Diocese of Lincoln</a>. The county of <a href="/wiki/Leicestershire" title="Leicestershire">Leicestershire</a> was transferred from Lincoln to Peterborough in 1837.</p>
