<p>The <b>Pegnitz</b> is a small river in <a href="/wiki/Franconia" title="Franconia">Franconia</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">German</a> <a href="/wiki/Federal_state" class="mw-redirect" title="Federal state">federal state</a> of <a href="/wiki/Bavaria" title="Bavaria">Bavaria</a>. The Pegnitz has its source in the <a href="/wiki/Pegnitz_(city)" class="mw-redirect" title="Pegnitz (city)">city</a> of the same name at an altitude of 425&#160;m (1,394&#160;ft) and meets the <a href="/wiki/Rednitz" title="Rednitz">Rednitz</a> at 283&#160;m (928&#160;ft) in <a href="/wiki/F%C3%BCrth" title="Fürth">Fürth</a>. From that point on the river is called <a href="/wiki/Regnitz" title="Regnitz">Regnitz</a>. But often esp. by local people the 23 km long Fichtenohe is regarded as upper course of the Pegnitz. The Fichtenohe source is in Lindenhardt Forest north of Pegnitz town.</p>
<p>The Pegnitz is about 79&#160;km (49&#160;mi) long, with Fichtenohe 102 km</p>
<p>The river is inhabited by numerous <a href="/wiki/Ducks" class="mw-redirect" title="Ducks">ducks</a>, <a href="/wiki/Coots" class="mw-redirect" title="Coots">coots</a>, <a href="/wiki/Swan" title="Swan">swans</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Gulls" class="mw-redirect" title="Gulls">gulls</a>.</p>

<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:IM001357.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/77/IM001357.JPG/220px-IM001357.JPG" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/77/IM001357.JPG/330px-IM001357.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/77/IM001357.JPG/440px-IM001357.JPG 2x" data-file-width="1280" data-file-height="960" /></a>

<p>From source to mouth:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Pegnitz_(city)" class="mw-redirect" title="Pegnitz (city)">Pegnitz</a></li>
<li>Neuhaus on the Pegnitz</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Hersbruck" title="Hersbruck">Hersbruck</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Lauf_an_der_Pegnitz" title="Lauf an der Pegnitz">Lauf an der Pegnitz</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/R%C3%B6thenbach_an_der_Pegnitz" title="Röthenbach an der Pegnitz">Röthenbach an der Pegnitz</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Nuremberg" title="Nuremberg">Nuremberg</a>: Within city limits (about 14&#160;km), the river forms secondary arms. Numerous <a href="/wiki/Bridge" title="Bridge">bridges</a> and footbridges cross the river, such as the unchanged <a href="/wiki/Renaissance" title="Renaissance">Renaissance</a> bridge <i><a href="/wiki/Fleischbr%C3%BCcke" class="mw-redirect" title="Fleischbrücke">Fleischbrücke</a></i> (1598) and the iron <a href="/wiki/Suspension_bridge" title="Suspension bridge">suspension bridge</a> <a href="/wiki/Chain_Bridge_(Nuremberg)" title="Chain Bridge (Nuremberg)">Chain Bridge</a> (<i>Kettensteg</i>), dating from 1824. To the west of the Maxbrücke there is a <a href="/wiki/Weir" title="Weir">weir</a>. In the western part of the city, the river's ecological status was restored one section at a time from 1998 to 2001, using the historical <a href="/wiki/River_bed" class="mw-redirect" title="River bed">river bed</a> as an orientation.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/F%C3%BCrth" title="Fürth">Fürth</a>: Numerous bridges and footbridges exist. As part of project <i>Uferstadt</i> (riverside city), the redesign of the former Grundig site, the river bed was restored to being closer to nature in 2003. On a length of one kilometer, two new loops and shallowed banks have been created, and part of the former river bed have been kept in the form of backwaters. These activities were cofinanced by the <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">European Union</a>.</li>
</ul>

<p>After the big <a href="/wiki/Flood" title="Flood">flood</a> in February 1909, straightening has shortened the river by four kilometers within the Nuremberg city limits.</p>
<p>Since 1996 planning and actions are progressing trying to lengthen the course of the river between Nuremberg and Fürth again and to shape it in a nature-oriented way.</p>
