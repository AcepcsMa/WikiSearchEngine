<p><b><a href="/wiki/Mathematics" title="Mathematics">Mathematics</a></b> is the study of <a href="/wiki/Number" title="Number">numbers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Quantity" title="Quantity">quantity</a>, <a href="/wiki/Space" title="Space">space</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mathematical_structure" title="Mathematical structure">structure</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Calculus" title="Calculus">change</a>. Mathematics is used throughout the world as an essential tool in many fields, including <a href="/wiki/Natural_science" title="Natural science">natural science</a>, <a href="/wiki/Engineering" title="Engineering">engineering</a>, <a href="/wiki/Medicine" title="Medicine">medicine</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Social_sciences" class="mw-redirect" title="Social sciences">social sciences</a>. <a href="/wiki/Applied_mathematics" title="Applied mathematics">Applied mathematics</a>, the branch of mathematics concerned with application of mathematical knowledge to other fields, inspires and makes use of new mathematical discoveries and sometimes leads to the development of entirely new mathematical disciplines, such as <a href="/wiki/Statistics" title="Statistics">statistics</a> and <a href="/wiki/Game_theory" title="Game theory">game theory</a>. Mathematicians also engage in <a href="/wiki/Pure_mathematics" title="Pure mathematics">pure mathematics</a>, or mathematics for its own sake, without having any application in mind. There is no clear line separating pure and applied mathematics, and practical applications for what began as pure mathematics are often discovered.</p>

<p><b>Leonhard Euler</b> (pronounced oiler; <a href="/wiki/International_Phonetic_Alphabet" title="International Phonetic Alphabet">IPA</a> <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/ˈɔɪlər/</span>) (April 15, 1707 <a href="/wiki/Basel,_Switzerland" class="mw-redirect" title="Basel, Switzerland">Basel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Switzerland" title="Switzerland">Switzerland</a> - September 18, 1783 <a href="/wiki/St_Petersburg" class="mw-redirect" title="St Petersburg">St Petersburg</a>, <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russia</a>) was a <a href="/wiki/Ancien_R%C3%A9gime_of_Switzerland" class="mw-redirect" title="Ancien Régime of Switzerland">Swiss</a> <a href="/wiki/Mathematician" title="Mathematician">mathematician</a> and <a href="/wiki/Physicist" title="Physicist">physicist</a>. He is considered to be the dominant mathematician of the <a href="/wiki/18th_century" title="18th century">18th century</a> and one of the greatest mathematicians of all time; he is certainly among the most prolific, with collected works filling over 70 volumes.</p>
<p>Euler developed many important concepts and <a href="/wiki/Mathematical_proof" title="Mathematical proof">proved</a> numerous lasting <a href="/wiki/Theorem" title="Theorem">theorems</a> in diverse areas of <a href="/wiki/Mathematics" title="Mathematics">mathematics</a>, from <a href="/wiki/Calculus" title="Calculus">calculus</a> to <a href="/wiki/Number_theory" title="Number theory">number theory</a> to <a href="/wiki/Topology" title="Topology">topology</a>. In the course of this work, he introduced many of modern mathematical terminologies, defining the concept of a <i><a href="/wiki/Function_(mathematics)" title="Function (mathematics)">function</a></i>, and its notation, such as <i>sin</i>, <i>cos</i>, and <i>tan</i> for the <a href="/wiki/Trigonometric_function" class="mw-redirect" title="Trigonometric function">trigonometric functions</a>.</p>

<p><b><a href="/wiki/Pi" title="Pi">Pi</a></b>, represented by the Greek letter <a href="/wiki/Pi_(letter)" title="Pi (letter)">π</a>, is a <a href="/wiki/Mathematical_constant" title="Mathematical constant">mathematical constant</a> whose value is the <a href="/wiki/Ratio" title="Ratio">ratio</a> of any <a href="/wiki/Circle" title="Circle">circle</a>'s circumference to its diameter in <a href="/wiki/Euclidean_geometry" title="Euclidean geometry">Euclidean space</a> (i.e., on a flat <a href="/wiki/Plane_(geometry)" title="Plane (geometry)">plane</a>); it is also the ratio of a circle's area to the square of its radius. (These facts are reflected in the familiar formulas from <a href="/wiki/Geometry" title="Geometry">geometry</a>, <span class="texhtml"><i>C</i> = π <i>d</i></span> and <span class="texhtml"><i>A</i> = π <i>r</i><sup>2</sup></span>.) In this animation, the circle has a diameter of 1 unit, giving it a circumference of π. The rolling shows that the distance a point on the circle moves linearly in one complete revolution is equal to π. <a href="/wiki/Proof_that_%CF%80_is_irrational" title="Proof that π is irrational">Pi is an irrational number</a> and so cannot be expressed as the ratio of two <a href="/wiki/Integer" title="Integer">integers</a>; as a result, the decimal expansion of π is nonterminating and nonrepeating. To 50 decimal places, π&#160;<abbr title="is approximately equal to">≈</abbr>&#160;3.14159 26535 89793 23846 26433 83279 50288 41971 69399 37510, a value of sufficient precision to allow the calculation of the volume of a <a href="/wiki/Sphere" title="Sphere">sphere</a> the size of the orbit of <a href="/wiki/Neptune" title="Neptune">Neptune</a> around the Sun (assuming an exact value for this radius) to within 1 cubic <a href="/wiki/Angstrom" class="mw-redirect" title="Angstrom">angstrom</a>. According to the <a href="/wiki/Lindemann%E2%80%93Weierstrass_theorem" title="Lindemann–Weierstrass theorem">Lindemann–Weierstrass theorem</a>, first proved in 1882, π is also a <a href="/wiki/Transcendental_number" title="Transcendental number">transcendental</a> (or non-<a href="/wiki/Algebraic_number" title="Algebraic number">algebraic</a>) number, meaning it is not the <a href="/wiki/Zero_of_a_function" title="Zero of a function">root</a> of any non-zero <a href="/wiki/Polynomial" title="Polynomial">polynomial</a> with <a href="/wiki/Rational_number" title="Rational number">rational</a> coefficients. (This implies that it cannot be expressed using any <a href="/wiki/Closed-form_expression" title="Closed-form expression">closed-form</a> <a href="/wiki/Algebraic_expression" title="Algebraic expression">algebraic expression</a>—and also that solving the ancient problem of <a href="/wiki/Squaring_the_circle" title="Squaring the circle">squaring the circle</a> using a <a href="/wiki/Compass_and_straightedge_construction" class="mw-redirect" title="Compass and straightedge construction">compass and straightedge construction</a> is impossible). Perhaps the simplest non-algebraic closed-form expression for π is <span class="texhtml">4 arctan 1</span>, based on the <a href="/wiki/Inverse_trigonometric_functions" title="Inverse trigonometric functions">inverse tangent function</a> (a <a href="/wiki/Transcendental_function" title="Transcendental function">transcendental function</a>). There are also many <a href="/wiki/Infinite_series" class="mw-redirect" title="Infinite series">infinite series</a> and some <a href="/wiki/Infinite_product" title="Infinite product">infinite products</a> that converge to π or to a simple function of it, like 2/π; one of these is <a href="/wiki/Leibniz_formula_for_%CF%80" title="Leibniz formula for π">the infinite series representation</a> of the inverse-tangent expression just mentioned. Such iterative approaches to <a href="/wiki/Approximations_of_%CF%80" title="Approximations of π">approximating π</a> first appeared in 15th-century India and were later rediscovered (perhaps not independently) in 17th- and 18th-century Europe (along with several <a href="/wiki/Continued_fractions" class="mw-redirect" title="Continued fractions">continued fractions</a> representations). Although these methods often suffer from an impractically slow convergence rate, one modern infinite series that converges to 1/π very quickly is given by the <a href="/wiki/Chudnovsky_algorithm" title="Chudnovsky algorithm">Chudnovsky algorithm</a>, first published in 1989; each term of this series gives an astonishing 14 additional decimal places of accuracy. In addition to <a href="/wiki/Geometry" title="Geometry">geometry</a> and <a href="/wiki/Trigonometry" title="Trigonometry">trigonometry</a>, π appears in many other areas of mathematics, including <a href="/wiki/Number_theory" title="Number theory">number theory</a>, <a href="/wiki/Calculus" title="Calculus">calculus</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Probability" title="Probability">probability</a>.</p>
