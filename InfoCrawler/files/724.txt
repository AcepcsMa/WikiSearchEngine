			<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Kunz Lochner</h1>

<p><b>Kunz (Konrad) Lochner</b> (1510–1567) was an eminent master <a href="/wiki/Plate_armour" title="Plate armour">plate armourer</a> from <a href="/wiki/Nuremberg" title="Nuremberg">Nuremberg</a>, Germany. He was the son of an armourer with the same name, and his two brothers Heinrich and Hans were also <a href="/wiki/Blacksmiths" class="mw-redirect" title="Blacksmiths">blacksmiths</a>. In 1543, Lochner started working for the Holy Roman Emperor <a href="/wiki/Ferdinand_I,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Ferdinand I, Holy Roman Emperor">Ferdinand I</a>, and the following year he began his service at the future <a href="/wiki/Maximilian_II,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Maximilian II, Holy Roman Emperor">Maximilian II</a>'s court.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Lochner's workshop produced some of the most magnificent plate armours made during the 16th-century <a href="/wiki/Renaissance" title="Renaissance">renaissance</a> period for mounted warfare, <a href="/wiki/Tournament_(medieval)" title="Tournament (medieval)">tourney</a> and ceremonial occasions. Lochner's patrons included <a href="/wiki/Royal_family" title="Royal family">royalty</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nobility" title="Nobility">nobility</a> from all over Europe.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Armour bearing Lochner's <a href="/wiki/Hallmark" title="Hallmark">hallmark</a>, can be found preserved in museums such as the Swedish Royal Armoury (<a href="/wiki/Livrustkammaren" title="Livrustkammaren">Livrustkammaren</a>) and the <a href="/wiki/Metropolitan_Museum_of_Art" title="Metropolitan Museum of Art">Metropolitan Museum of Art</a>.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Parade_Armour_for_man_and_horse">Parade Armour for man and horse</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kunz_Lochner&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Parade Armour for man and horse">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Royal_armoury_Stockholm_1.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/43/Royal_armoury_Stockholm_1.jpg/220px-Royal_armoury_Stockholm_1.jpg" width="220" height="282" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/43/Royal_armoury_Stockholm_1.jpg/330px-Royal_armoury_Stockholm_1.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/4/43/Royal_armoury_Stockholm_1.jpg 2x" data-file-width="375" data-file-height="480" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Royal_armoury_Stockholm_1.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Royal armoury Stockholm 1</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The armour is made in 1550 for <a href="/wiki/Sigismund_II_Augustus" title="Sigismund II Augustus">Sigismund II Augustus</a> of Poland. He was the last king of the old <a href="/wiki/Jagiellonian_dynasty" title="Jagiellonian dynasty">Jagiellonian dynasty</a>. In his wills he divided his legacy between his sisters. One of them was <a href="/wiki/Catherine_Jagiellon" title="Catherine Jagiellon">Catherine Jagiellon</a>, queen of Sweden, married to <a href="/wiki/John_III_of_Sweden" title="John III of Sweden">John III of Sweden</a>. Since Poland didn't have a given heir to the throne, John III wanted his son <a href="/wiki/Sigismund_III_Vasa" title="Sigismund III Vasa">Sigismund</a> to be the next successor. In the complicated process of choosing a new king, Catherine's sister Anna sent this armour to calm John III concerning the heritage and to ensure herself of support from Sweden. The armour remains of a full plate armour and a horse armour.</p>
