<p><b>Veit Stoss</b> (also: <i>Veit Stoß</i>; <a href="/wiki/Polish_language" title="Polish language">Polish</a>: <span lang="pl" xml:lang="pl"><i>Wit Stwosz</i></span>; before 1450 – about 20 September 1533) was a leading <a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a> sculptor, mostly in wood, whose career covered the transition between the late <a href="/wiki/Gothic_art" title="Gothic art">Gothic</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Northern_Renaissance" title="Northern Renaissance">Northern Renaissance</a>. His style emphasized pathos and emotion, helped by his virtuoso carving of billowing drapery; it has been called "late Gothic <a href="/wiki/Baroque" title="Baroque">Baroque</a>".<sup id="cite_ref-Snyder_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Snyder-1">[1]</a></sup> He had a large workshop and in addition to his own works there are a number by pupils. He is best known for the <a href="/wiki/Altarpiece_of_Veit_Stoss" class="mw-redirect" title="Altarpiece of Veit Stoss">altarpiece</a> in <a href="/wiki/St._Mary%27s_Basilica,_Krak%C3%B3w" title="St. Mary's Basilica, Kraków">St. Mary's Basilica</a> in <a href="/wiki/Krak%C3%B3w" title="Kraków">Kraków</a>, <a href="/wiki/Poland" title="Poland">Poland</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Life">Life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Veit_Stoss&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Stoss was born at <a href="/wiki/Horb_am_Neckar" title="Horb am Neckar">Horb am Neckar</a> before 1450; his exact date of birth is unknown though it may have been in 1447. Nothing about his life is known for certain before 1473 when he moved to <a href="/wiki/Nuremberg" title="Nuremberg">Nuremberg</a> in <a href="/wiki/Franconia" title="Franconia">Franconia</a> and married Barbara Hertz. Their eldest son Andreas was born there before 1477, when Stoss moved to Kraków, the royal capital of Poland, where he was commissioned to produce the enormous <a href="/wiki/Polychrome" title="Polychrome">polychrome</a> wooden <a href="/wiki/Altar_of_Veit_Stoss" class="mw-redirect" title="Altar of Veit Stoss">Altar of Veit Stoss</a> (Ołtarz Wita Stwosza) at <a href="/wiki/St._Mary%27s_Basilica,_Krak%C3%B3w" title="St. Mary's Basilica, Kraków">St Mary's Church</a> in Kraków. His son <a href="/wiki/Stanis%C5%82aw_Stwosz" title="Stanisław Stwosz">Stanisław</a> was also a sculptor. Veit lived and worked in Krakow for the next twenty years. His name is usually <a href="/wiki/Polish_language" title="Polish language">polonized</a> as Wit Stwosz.<sup id="cite_ref-wzks_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wzks-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span id="In_Kraków"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="In_Krak.C3.B3w">In Kraków</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Veit_Stoss&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: In Kraków">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The altar in Kraków was completed in 1489, and was the largest <a href="/wiki/Triptych" title="Triptych">triptych</a> of its time. Like Stoss' other large works, it required a large workshop including specialized painters and gilders.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Other important works from Stoss' period in Poland were the tomb of <a href="/wiki/Casimir_IV_Jagiellon" title="Casimir IV Jagiellon">Casimir IV</a> in <a href="/wiki/Wawel_Cathedral" title="Wawel Cathedral">Wawel Cathedral</a>, the marble tomb of <a href="/wiki/Zbigniew_Ole%C5%9Bnicki_(primate_of_Poland)" title="Zbigniew Oleśnicki (primate of Poland)">Zbigniew Oleśnicki</a> in <a href="/wiki/Gniezno" title="Gniezno">Gniezno</a>, and the altar of <a href="/wiki/Stanislaus_of_Szczepan%C3%B3w" title="Stanislaus of Szczepanów">Saint Stanislaus</a>. The Polish court was more aware of Italian styles than Nuremberg patrons of that time, and some of Stoss' Polish work used Renaissance classical ornament.<sup id="cite_ref-staropolska_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-staropolska-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>During World War II, on the order of <a href="/wiki/Hans_Frank" title="Hans Frank">Hans Frank</a> – the Governor-General of <a href="/wiki/General_Government" title="General Government">that region</a> of <a href="/wiki/Occupation_of_Poland_(1939%E2%80%931945)" title="Occupation of Poland (1939–1945)">occupied Poland</a> – the dismantled Altar was shipped to the <a href="/wiki/Third_Reich" class="mw-redirect" title="Third Reich">Third Reich</a> around 1941. It was rediscovered in 1945 in <a href="/wiki/Bavaria" title="Bavaria">Bavaria</a>, hidden in the basement of the heavily bombed <a href="/wiki/Nuremberg_Castle" title="Nuremberg Castle">Nuremberg Castle</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> The High Altar underwent major restoration work in Poland and was put back in its place at <a href="/wiki/St._Mary%27s_Basilica,_Krak%C3%B3w" title="St. Mary's Basilica, Kraków">the Basilica</a> ten years later.<sup id="cite_ref-wzks_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wzks-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Nuremberg">Nuremberg</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Veit_Stoss&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Nuremberg">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1496, Stoss returned to Nuremberg with his wife and eight children. He reacquired his citizenship for three <a href="/wiki/Rhenish_guilder" title="Rhenish guilder">gulden</a> and resumed his work there as a sculptor. Between 1500 and 1503 he carved an altar, now lost, for the parish church of <a href="/wiki/Schwaz" title="Schwaz">Schwaz</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tyrol_(state)" title="Tyrol (state)">Tyrol</a> of the "<a href="/wiki/Assumption_of_Mary" title="Assumption of Mary">Assumption of Mary</a>". In 1503, he was arrested for forging the seal and signature of a fraudulent contractor and was sentenced to be branded on both of his cheeks and prohibited from leaving Nuremberg without the explicit permission of the city council. He was pardoned in 1506 by <a href="/wiki/Maximilian_I,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Maximilian I, Holy Roman Emperor">Emperor Maximilian</a> and restored of his civil rights.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>

</div>
<p>Despite the prohibition he went to <a href="/wiki/M%C3%BCnnerstadt" title="Münnerstadt">Münnerstadt</a> in 1504, to paint and gild the altarpiece that <a href="/wiki/Tilman_Riemenschneider" title="Tilman Riemenschneider">Tilman Riemenschneider</a> had left in plain wood ten years earlier, presumably according to his contract (unlike Stoss, his workshop did not include painters and guilders). Leaving wood sculpture unpainted was a new taste at the time, and "perhaps the tastes of the city council were somewhat provincial."<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> He also created the altar for <a href="/wiki/Bamberg_Cathedral" title="Bamberg Cathedral">Bamberg Cathedral</a> and various other sculptures in Nuremberg, including the <i>Annunciation</i> and <i>Tobias and the Angel</i>. In 1506 he was arrested a second time. <a href="/wiki/Maximilian_I,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Maximilian I, Holy Roman Emperor">Emperor Maximilian</a> wrote a letter of pardon, but it was rejected by the council of the <a href="/wiki/Imperial_free_city" class="mw-redirect" title="Imperial free city">Imperial free city</a> Nuremberg as meddling in its internal affairs. He was able to resettle in Nuremberg from 1506, but was shunned by the council and received few large commissions from that time onwards.<sup id="cite_ref-Snyder_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Snyder-1">[1]</a></sup> In 1512, the Emperor asked Stoss to help with the planning of his tomb monument, which was eventually placed in the <a href="/wiki/Hofkirche,_Innsbruck" title="Hofkirche, Innsbruck">Hofkirche</a>, <a href="/wiki/Innsbruck" title="Innsbruck">Innsbruck</a>; it seems Stoss's attempts to cast in <a href="/wiki/Brass" title="Brass">brass</a> were unsuccessful.</p>
<p>During the period 1515–1520, Veit Stoss received a commission for sculptures by <a href="/w/index.php?title=Raffaele_Torrigiani&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Raffaele Torrigiani (page does not exist)">Raffaele Torrigiani</a>, a rich <a href="/wiki/Florence" title="Florence">Florentine</a> merchant. In 1516 he made <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tobias_and_the_Angel_(Veit_Stoss)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Tobias and the Angel (Veit Stoss) (page does not exist)">Tobias and the Angel</a></i> (now in Germanisches Nationalmuseum, Nuremberg), and a statue of Saint <a href="/wiki/Roch" class="mw-redirect" title="Roch">Roch</a> for the <a href="/wiki/Basilica_della_Santissima_Annunziata,_Florence" class="mw-redirect" title="Basilica della Santissima Annunziata, Florence">Basilica of Santissima Annunziata</a> in Florence. This wooden statue represents the saint in a traditional way: in the garb of a pilgrim, lifting his tunic to demonstrate the <a href="/wiki/Plague_(disease)" title="Plague (disease)">plague</a> sore in his thigh. Even <a href="/wiki/Giorgio_Vasari" title="Giorgio Vasari">Giorgio Vasari</a>, who did not think much of artists north of the Alps, praised it in his <i><a href="/wiki/Lives_of_the_Most_Excellent_Painters,_Sculptors,_and_Architects" title="Lives of the Most Excellent Painters, Sculptors, and Architects">Le Vite</a></i> and called it "a miracle in wood", though misattributing it.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>Veit Stoss was buried at St. Johannis cemetery in Nuremberg. His artistic legacy was continued by his son Stanisław.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_popular_culture">In popular culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Veit_Stoss&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: In popular culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Veit Stoss is featured in <a href="/wiki/Judith_Weir" title="Judith Weir">Judith Weir</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Opera" title="Opera">opera</a>, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Black_Spider_(opera)" title="The Black Spider (opera)">The Black Spider</a></i>. He is one of the singing sculptors in Act 3 Scene 2 inside the <a href="/wiki/Wawel_Cathedral" title="Wawel Cathedral">Wawel Cathedral</a>. He is shown chiseling at the tomb of King <a href="/wiki/Casimir_IV_Jagiellon" title="Casimir IV Jagiellon">Casimir IV</a>. There is a Polish book (1913) and film (1961) <i>Historia żółtej ciżemki</i> (<i>The story of a yellow crakow</i>) <sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> about Veit Stoss in Cracow.</p>
