<p>The <b>German Renaissance</b>, part of the <a href="/wiki/Northern_Renaissance" title="Northern Renaissance">Northern Renaissance</a>, was a cultural and artistic movement that spread among <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">German</a> thinkers in the 15th and 16th centuries, which developed from the <a href="/wiki/Italian_Renaissance" title="Italian Renaissance">Italian Renaissance</a>. Many areas of the arts and sciences were influenced, notably by the spread of <a href="/wiki/Renaissance_humanism" title="Renaissance humanism">Renaissance humanism</a> to the various German states and principalities. There were many advances made in the fields of architecture, the arts, and the sciences. Germany produced two developments that were to dominate the 16th century all over Europe: printing and the <a href="/wiki/Protestant_Reformation" class="mw-redirect" title="Protestant Reformation">Protestant Reformation</a>.</p>
<p>One of the most important German humanists was <a href="/wiki/Conrad_Celtes" title="Conrad Celtes">Konrad Celtis</a> (1459–1508). Celtis studied at <a href="/wiki/Cologne" title="Cologne">Cologne</a> and <a href="/wiki/Heidelberg" title="Heidelberg">Heidelberg</a>, and later travelled throughout Italy collecting Latin and Greek manuscripts. Heavily influenced by <a href="/wiki/Tacitus" title="Tacitus">Tacitus</a>, he used the <i><a href="/wiki/Germania_(book)" title="Germania (book)">Germania</a></i> to introduce German history and geography. Eventually he devoted his time to poetry, in which he praised Germany in Latin. Another important figure was <a href="/wiki/Johann_Reuchlin" title="Johann Reuchlin">Johann Reuchlin</a> (1455–1522) who studied in various places in Italy and later taught Greek. He studied the <a href="/wiki/Hebrew_language" title="Hebrew language">Hebrew language</a>, aiming to purify Christianity, but encountering resistance from the church.</p>
<p>The most significant German Renaissance artist is <a href="/wiki/Albrecht_D%C3%BCrer" title="Albrecht Dürer">Albrecht Dürer</a> especially known for his <a href="/wiki/Printmaking" title="Printmaking">printmaking</a> in <a href="/wiki/Woodcut" title="Woodcut">woodcut</a> and <a href="/wiki/Engraving" title="Engraving">engraving</a>, which spread all over Europe, drawings, and painted portraits. Important architecture of this period includes the <a href="/wiki/Landshut" title="Landshut">Landshut</a> Residence, <a href="/wiki/Heidelberg_Castle" title="Heidelberg Castle">Heidelberg Castle</a> and the Town Hall in <a href="/wiki/Augsburg" title="Augsburg">Augsburg</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Background">Background</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=German_Renaissance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Renaissance" title="Renaissance">Renaissance</a> was largely driven by the renewed interest in classical learning, and was also the result of rapid economic development. At the beginning of the 16th century, Germany was one of the most prosperous countries in Europe despite a relatively low level of urbanization compared to Italy or the Netherlands.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources#What_information_to_include" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources"><span title="A more detailed citation is required. (November 2015)">full citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> It benefited from the wealth of certain sectors such as metallurgy, mining, banking and textiles. More importantly, book-printing developed in Germany, and <a href="/wiki/Global_spread_of_the_printing_press" title="Global spread of the printing press">German printers dominated</a> the new book-trade in most other countries until well into the 16th century.</p>

</div>
<p>The concept of the Northern Renaissance or German Renaissance is somewhat confused by the continuation of the use of elaborate Gothic ornament until well into the 16th century, even in works that are undoubtedly Renaissance in their treatment of the human figure and other respects. Classical ornament had little historical resonance in much of Germany, but in other respects Germany was very quick to follow developments, especially in adopting <a href="/wiki/Printing" title="Printing">printing</a> with <a href="/wiki/Movable_type" title="Movable type">movable type</a>, a German invention that remained almost a German monopoly for some decades, and was <a href="/wiki/Global_spread_of_the_printing_press" title="Global spread of the printing press">first brought to most of Europe</a>, including France and Italy, by Germans.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Printmaking" title="Printmaking">Printmaking</a> by <a href="/wiki/Woodcut" title="Woodcut">woodcut</a> and <a href="/wiki/Engraving" title="Engraving">engraving</a> (thought of as a German invention yet originated in China) was already more developed in Germany and the <a href="/wiki/Low_Countries" title="Low Countries">Low Countries</a> than anywhere else, and the Germans took the lead in developing book illustrations, typically of a relatively low artistic standard, but seen all over Europe, with the woodblocks often being lent to printers of editions in other cities or languages. The greatest artist of the German Renaissance, <a href="/wiki/Albrecht_D%C3%BCrer" title="Albrecht Dürer">Albrecht Dürer</a>, began his career as an apprentice to a leading workshop in Nuremberg, that of <a href="/wiki/Michael_Wolgemut" title="Michael Wolgemut">Michael Wolgemut</a>, who had largely abandoned his painting to exploit the new medium. Dürer worked on the most extravagantly illustrated book of the period, the <a href="/wiki/Nuremberg_Chronicle" title="Nuremberg Chronicle">Nuremberg Chronicle</a>, published by his godfather <a href="/wiki/Anton_Koberger" title="Anton Koberger">Anton Koberger</a>, Europe's largest printer-publisher at the time.<sup id="cite_ref-Bartum_2002_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bartum_2002-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>After completing his apprenticeship in 1490, Dürer travelled in Germany for four years, and Italy for a few months, before establishing his own workshop in Nuremberg. He rapidly became famous all over Europe for his energetic and balanced woodcuts and engravings, while also painting. Though retaining a distinctively German style, his work shows strong Italian influence, and is often taken to represent the start of the German Renaissance in visual art, which for the next forty years replaced the Netherlands and France as the area producing the greatest innovation in Northern European art. Dürer supported <a href="/wiki/Martin_Luther" title="Martin Luther">Martin Luther</a> but continued to create <a href="/wiki/Madonna_(art)" title="Madonna (art)">Madonnas</a> and other Catholic imagery, and paint portraits of leaders on both sides of the emerging split of the <a href="/wiki/Protestant_Reformation" class="mw-redirect" title="Protestant Reformation">Protestant Reformation</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Bartum_2002_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bartum_2002-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<p>Dürer died in 1528, before it was clear that the split of the Reformation had become permanent, but his pupils of the following generation were unable to avoid taking sides. Most leading German artists became Protestants, but this deprived them of painting most religious works, previously the mainstay of artists' revenue. <a href="/wiki/Martin_Luther" title="Martin Luther">Martin Luther</a> had objected to much Catholic imagery, but not to imagery itself, and <a href="/wiki/Lucas_Cranach_the_Elder" title="Lucas Cranach the Elder">Lucas Cranach the Elder</a>, a close friend of Luther, had painted a number of "Lutheran altarpieces", mostly showing the <a href="/wiki/Last_Supper" title="Last Supper">Last Supper</a>, some with portraits of the leading Protestant divines as the <a href="/wiki/Twelve_Apostles" class="mw-redirect" title="Twelve Apostles">Twelve Apostles</a>. This phase of Lutheran art was over before 1550, probably under the more fiercely <a href="/wiki/Aniconic" class="mw-redirect" title="Aniconic">aniconic</a> influence of <a href="/wiki/Calvinism" title="Calvinism">Calvinism</a>, and religious works for public display virtually ceased to be produced in Protestant areas. Presumably largely because of this, the development of German art had virtually ceased by about 1550, but in the preceding decades German artists had been very fertile in developing alternative subjects to replace the gap in their order books. Cranach, apart from portraits, developed a format of thin vertical portraits of provocative nudes, given classical or Biblical titles.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Lying somewhat outside these developments is <a href="/wiki/Matthias_Gr%C3%BCnewald" title="Matthias Grünewald">Matthias Grünewald</a>, who left very few works, but whose masterpiece, his <i><a href="/wiki/Isenheim_Altarpiece" title="Isenheim Altarpiece">Isenheim Altarpiece</a></i> (completed 1515), has been widely regarded as the greatest German Renaissance painting since it was restored to critical attention in the 19th century. It is an intensely emotional work that continues the German Gothic tradition of unrestrained gesture and expression, using Renaissance compositional principles, but all in that most Gothic of forms, the multi-winged <a href="/wiki/Triptych" title="Triptych">triptych</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>

</div>
</div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Danube_school" title="Danube school">Danube School</a> is the name of a circle of artists of the first third of the 16th century in <a href="/wiki/Bavaria" title="Bavaria">Bavaria</a> and Austria, including <a href="/wiki/Albrecht_Altdorfer" title="Albrecht Altdorfer">Albrecht Altdorfer</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wolf_Huber" title="Wolf Huber">Wolf Huber</a> and <a href="/wiki/Augustin_Hirschvogel" title="Augustin Hirschvogel">Augustin Hirschvogel</a>. With Altdorfer in the lead, the school produced the first examples of independent <a href="/wiki/Landscape_art" class="mw-redirect" title="Landscape art">landscape art</a> in the West (nearly 1,000 years after China), in both paintings and prints.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Their religious paintings had an <a href="/wiki/Expressionist" class="mw-redirect" title="Expressionist">expressionist</a> style somewhat similar to Grünewald's. Dürer's pupils <a href="/wiki/Hans_Burgkmair" title="Hans Burgkmair">Hans Burgkmair</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hans_Baldung_Grien" class="mw-redirect" title="Hans Baldung Grien">Hans Baldung Grien</a> worked largely in prints, with Baldung developing the topical subject matter of <a href="/wiki/Witch" class="mw-redirect" title="Witch">witches</a> in a number of enigmatic prints.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Hans_Holbein_the_Elder" title="Hans Holbein the Elder">Hans Holbein the Elder</a> and his brother Sigismund Holbein painted religious works in the late Gothic style. Hans the Elder was a pioneer and leader in the transformation of German art from the Gothic to the Renaissance style. His son, <a href="/wiki/Hans_Holbein_the_Younger" title="Hans Holbein the Younger">Hans Holbein the Younger</a> was an important painter of portraits and a few religious works, working mainly in England and Switzerland. Holbein's well known series of small woodcuts on the <i><a href="/wiki/Dance_of_Death" class="mw-redirect" title="Dance of Death">Dance of Death</a></i> relate to the works of the <a href="/wiki/Little_Masters" title="Little Masters">Little Masters</a>, a group of printmakers who specialized in very small and highly detailed engravings for bourgeois collectors, including many erotic subjects.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>The outstanding achievements of the first half of the 16th century were followed by several decades with a remarkable absence of noteworthy German art, other than accomplished portraits that never rival the achievement of Holbein or Dürer. The next significant German artists worked in the rather artificial style of <a href="/wiki/Northern_Mannerism" title="Northern Mannerism">Northern Mannerism</a>, which they had to learn in Italy or Flanders. <a href="/wiki/Hans_von_Aachen" title="Hans von Aachen">Hans von Aachen</a> and the Netherlandish <a href="/wiki/Bartholomeus_Spranger" title="Bartholomeus Spranger">Bartholomeus Spranger</a> were the leading painters at the Imperial courts in <a href="/wiki/Vienna" title="Vienna">Vienna</a> and Prague, and the productive Netherlandish <a href="/wiki/Sadeler_family" title="Sadeler family">Sadeler family</a> of engravers spread out across Germany, among other counties.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>In Catholic parts of South Germany the Gothic tradition of wood carving continued to flourish until the end of the 18th century, adapting to changes in style through the centuries. <a href="/wiki/Veit_Stoss" title="Veit Stoss">Veit Stoss</a> (d. 1533), <a href="/wiki/Tilman_Riemenschneider" title="Tilman Riemenschneider">Tilman Riemenschneider</a> (d.1531) and <a href="/wiki/Peter_Vischer_the_Elder" title="Peter Vischer the Elder">Peter Vischer the Elder</a> (d. 1529) were Dürer's contemporaries, and their long careers covered the transition between the Gothic and Renaissance periods, although their ornament often remained Gothic even after their compositions began to reflect Renaissance principles.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Architecture">Architecture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=German_Renaissance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Architecture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p><a href="/wiki/Renaissance_Architecture" class="mw-redirect" title="Renaissance Architecture">Renaissance Architecture</a> in Germany was inspired first by German philosophers and artists such as <a href="/wiki/Albrecht_D%C3%BCrer" title="Albrecht Dürer">Albrecht Dürer</a> and <a href="/wiki/Johannes_Reuchlin" class="mw-redirect" title="Johannes Reuchlin">Johannes Reuchlin</a> who visited Italy. Important early examples of this period are especially the <a href="/wiki/Landshut_Residence" title="Landshut Residence">Landshut Residence</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Heidelberg_Castle" title="Heidelberg Castle">Castle</a> in <a href="/wiki/Heidelberg" title="Heidelberg">Heidelberg</a>, <a href="/wiki/Schloss_Johannisburg" title="Schloss Johannisburg">Johannisburg Palace</a> in <a href="/wiki/Aschaffenburg" title="Aschaffenburg">Aschaffenburg</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Augsburg_Town_Hall" title="Augsburg Town Hall">City Hall</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fuggerh%C3%A4user" title="Fuggerhäuser">Fugger Houses</a> in <a href="/wiki/Augsburg" title="Augsburg">Augsburg</a> and <a href="/wiki/St._Michael%27s_Church,_Munich" title="St. Michael's Church, Munich">St. Michael</a> in <a href="/wiki/Munich" title="Munich">Munich</a>, the largest Renaissance church north of the Alps.</p>
<p>A particular form of Renaissance architecture in Germany is the <a href="/wiki/Weser_Renaissance" title="Weser Renaissance">Weser Renaissance</a>, with prominent examples such as the <a href="/wiki/Town_Hall_of_Bremen" class="mw-redirect" title="Town Hall of Bremen">City Hall</a> of <a href="/wiki/Bremen" title="Bremen">Bremen</a> and the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Helmstedt" title="University of Helmstedt">Juleum</a> in <a href="/wiki/Helmstedt" title="Helmstedt">Helmstedt</a>.</p>
<p>In July 1567 the city council of <a href="/wiki/Cologne" title="Cologne">Cologne</a> approved a design in the Renaissance style by Wilhelm Vernukken for a two storied loggia for <a href="/wiki/Cologne_City_Hall" title="Cologne City Hall">Cologne City Hall</a>. <a href="/wiki/Michaelskirche_(M%C3%BCnchen)" class="mw-redirect" title="Michaelskirche (München)">St Michael</a> in <a href="/wiki/Munich" title="Munich">Munich</a> is the largest Renaissance church north of the Alps. It was built by <a href="/wiki/William_V,_Duke_of_Bavaria" title="William V, Duke of Bavaria">Duke William V</a> of <a href="/wiki/Bavaria" title="Bavaria">Bavaria</a> between 1583 and 1597 as a spiritual center for the <a href="/wiki/Counter_Reformation" class="mw-redirect" title="Counter Reformation">Counter Reformation</a> and was inspired by the Church of <a href="/wiki/Il_Ges%C3%B9" class="mw-redirect" title="Il Gesù">il Gesù</a> in Rome. The architect is unknown. Many examples of <a href="/wiki/List_of_Brick_Renaissance_buildings" title="List of Brick Renaissance buildings">Brick Renaissance buildings</a> can be found in <a href="/wiki/Hanseatic_League" title="Hanseatic League">Hanseatic</a> old towns, such as <a href="/wiki/Stralsund" title="Stralsund">Stralsund</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wismar" title="Wismar">Wismar</a>, <a href="/wiki/L%C3%BCbeck" title="Lübeck">Lübeck</a>, <a href="/wiki/L%C3%BCneburg" title="Lüneburg">Lüneburg</a>, <a href="/wiki/Friedrichstadt" title="Friedrichstadt">Friedrichstadt</a> and <a href="/wiki/Stade" title="Stade">Stade</a>. Notable German Renaissance architects include <a href="/wiki/Friedrich_Sustris" title="Friedrich Sustris">Friedrich Sustris</a>, <a href="/wiki/Benedikt_Rejt" title="Benedikt Rejt">Benedikt Rejt</a>, <a href="/wiki/Abraham_van_den_Blocke" title="Abraham van den Blocke">Abraham van den Blocke</a>, <a href="/wiki/Elias_Holl" title="Elias Holl">Elias Holl</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hans_Krumpper" title="Hans Krumpper">Hans Krumpper</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Influential_people">Influential people</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=German_Renaissance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Influential people">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Johannes_Gutenberg_.28c._1398.E2.80.931468.29">Johannes Gutenberg (c. 1398–1468)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=German_Renaissance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Johannes Gutenberg (c. 1398–1468)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Born Johannes Gensfleisch zur Laden,<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Johannes_Gutenberg" title="Johannes Gutenberg">Johannes Gutenberg</a> is widely considered the most influential person within the German Renaissance. As a free thinker, humanist, and inventor, Gutenberg also grew up within the Renaissance, but influenced it greatly as well. His best-known invention is the <a href="/wiki/Printing_press" title="Printing press">printing press</a> in 1440. Gutenberg's press allowed the humanists, reformists, and others to circulate their ideas. He is also known as the creator of the <a href="/wiki/Gutenberg_Bible" title="Gutenberg Bible">Gutenberg Bible</a>, a crucial work that marked the start of the "<a href="/wiki/Printing_press#Printing_revolution" title="Printing press">Gutenberg Revolution</a>" and the age of the printed book in the <a href="/wiki/Western_world" title="Western world">Western world</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Johann_Reuchlin_.281455.E2.80.931522.29">Johann Reuchlin (1455–1522)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=German_Renaissance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Johann Reuchlin (1455–1522)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Johann_Reuchlin" title="Johann Reuchlin">Johann Reuchlin</a> was the most important aspect of world culture teaching within Germany at this time. He was a scholar of both Greek and Hebrew. Graduating, then going on to teach at Basel, he was considered extremely intelligent. Yet after leaving Basel, he had to start copying manuscripts and apprenticing within areas of law. However, he is most known for his work within Hebrew studies. Unlike some other "thinkers" of this time, Reuchlin submerged himself into this, even creating a guide to preaching within the Hebrew faith. The book, titled <i>De Arte Predicandi</i> (1503), is possibly one of his best-known works from this period.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Albrecht_D.C3.BCrer_.281471.E2.80.931528.29">Albrecht Dürer (1471–1528)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=German_Renaissance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Albrecht Dürer (1471–1528)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Albrecht_D%C3%BCrer" title="Albrecht Dürer">Albrecht Dürer</a> was at the time, and remains, the most famous artist of the German Renaissance. He was famous across Europe, and greatly admired in Italy, where his work was mainly known through his <a href="/wiki/Old_master_print" title="Old master print">prints</a>. He successfully integrated an elaborate Northern style with Renaissance harmony and monumentality. Among his best known works are <i><a href="/wiki/Melencolia_I" title="Melencolia I">Melencolia I</a></i>, the <i>Four Horsemen</i> from his <a href="/wiki/Woodcut" title="Woodcut">woodcut</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Apocalypse_(D%C3%BCrer)" title="Apocalypse (Dürer)">Apocalypse</a></i> series, and <i>Death, the Knight, and the Devil</i>. Other significant artists were <a href="/wiki/Lucas_Cranach_the_Elder" title="Lucas Cranach the Elder">Lucas Cranach the Elder</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Danube_School" class="mw-redirect" title="Danube School">Danube School</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Little_Masters" title="Little Masters">Little Masters</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Martin_Luther_.281483.E2.80.931546.29">Martin Luther (1483–1546)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=German_Renaissance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Martin Luther (1483–1546)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Martin_Luther" title="Martin Luther">Martin Luther</a><sup id="cite_ref-Plass_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Plass-11">[11]</a></sup> initiated the <a href="/wiki/Protestant_Reformation" class="mw-redirect" title="Protestant Reformation">Protestant Reformation</a> through the criticisms of church practices such as selling indulgences, which he published in his <a href="/wiki/The_Ninety-Five_Theses" class="mw-redirect" title="The Ninety-Five Theses"><i>Ninety-Five Theses</i></a> of 1517. Luther also translated the <a href="/wiki/Luther_Bible" title="Luther Bible">Bible</a> into German, making the Christian scriptures more accessible to the general population and inspiring the standardization of the German language.</p>
