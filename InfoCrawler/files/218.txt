<p><b>Ypres</b> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="/iː/: 'ee' in 'fleece'">iː</span><span title="'p' in 'pie'">p</span><span title="'r' in 'rye'">r</span><span title="/ə/: 'a' in 'about'">ə</span></span>/</a></span></span>; <small>French pronunciation:&#160;​</small><span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/French" title="Help:IPA/French">[ipʁ]</a></span>; <a href="/wiki/Dutch_language" title="Dutch language">Dutch</a>: <span lang="nl" xml:lang="nl"><b>Ieper</b></span>, <small>pronounced</small> <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Dutch" title="Help:IPA/Dutch">[ˈipər]</a></span>) is a <a href="/wiki/Belgium" title="Belgium">Belgian</a> <a href="/wiki/Municipalities_in_Belgium" class="mw-redirect" title="Municipalities in Belgium">municipality</a> located in the <a href="/wiki/Flemish_Region" title="Flemish Region">Flemish</a> <a href="/wiki/Provinces_of_Belgium" title="Provinces of Belgium">province</a> of <a href="/wiki/West_Flanders" title="West Flanders">West Flanders</a>. Though <i>Ieper</i> is the official name, the city's French name, <i>Ypres</i>, is most commonly used in English. The municipality comprises the city of Ypres and the villages of <a href="/wiki/Boezinge" title="Boezinge">Boezinge</a>, Brielen, Dikkebus, <a href="/wiki/Elverdinge" title="Elverdinge">Elverdinge</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hollebeke" title="Hollebeke">Hollebeke</a>, Sint-Jan, Vlamertinge, Voormezele, <a href="/wiki/Zillebeke" title="Zillebeke">Zillebeke</a>, and Zuidschote. Together, they are home to about 34,900 inhabitants.</p>
<p>During the First World War, Ypres was the centre of <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Ypres_(disambiguation)" class="mw-redirect" title="Battle of Ypres (disambiguation)">Battles of Ypres</a> between <a href="/wiki/German_Empire" title="German Empire">German</a> and <a href="/wiki/Allies_of_World_War_I" title="Allies of World War I">Allied</a> forces. During the war it was nicknamed "Wipers", as its name was difficult for English-speaking British Imperial forces troops to pronounce.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Origins_to_First_World_War">Origins to First World War</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ypres&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Origins to First World War">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft">

<p>Ypres is an ancient town, known to have been raided by the <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Rome" title="Ancient Rome">Romans</a> in the first century BC. It is first mentioned by name in 1066 and is probably named after the river <a href="/wiki/Ieperlee" title="Ieperlee">Ieperlee</a> on the banks of which it was founded.<sup id="cite_ref-HistOfY_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HistOfY-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>During the <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">Middle Ages</a>, Ypres was a prosperous <a href="/wiki/Flemish" title="Flemish">Flemish</a> city with a population of 40,000 in 1200 AD,<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> renowned for its <a href="/wiki/Linen" title="Linen">linen</a> trade with England, which was mentioned in the <i><a href="/wiki/Canterbury_Tales" class="mw-redirect" title="Canterbury Tales">Canterbury Tales</a></i>.</p>
<p>As the third largest city in the <a href="/wiki/County_of_Flanders" title="County of Flanders">County of Flanders</a> (after <a href="/wiki/Ghent" title="Ghent">Ghent</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bruges" title="Bruges">Bruges</a>) Ypres played an important role in the history of the textile industry.<sup id="cite_ref-HistOfY_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HistOfY-2">[2]</a></sup> Textiles from Ypres could be found in the markets of <a href="/wiki/Novgorod" class="mw-redirect" title="Novgorod">Novgorod</a> in Kievan Rus' in the early 12th century. In 1241, a major fire ruined much of the old city. The powerful city was involved in important treaties and battles, including the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_the_Golden_Spurs" title="Battle of the Golden Spurs">Battle of the Golden Spurs</a>, the Battle at Mons-en-Pévèle, the <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Melun" title="Treaty of Melun">Peace of Melun</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Cassel_(1328)" title="Battle of Cassel (1328)">Battle of Cassel</a>.</p>
<p>The famous <a href="/wiki/Cloth_Hall,_Ypres" class="mw-redirect" title="Cloth Hall, Ypres">Cloth Hall</a> was built in the thirteenth century. Also during this time cats, then the symbol of the devil and witchcraft, were thrown off Cloth Hall, possibly because of the belief that this would get rid of evil demons. Today, this act is commemorated with a <a href="/wiki/Triennial" title="Triennial">triennial</a> <a href="/wiki/Kattenstoet" title="Kattenstoet">Cat Parade</a> through town.</p>
<p>During the <a href="/wiki/Norwich_Crusade" class="mw-redirect" title="Norwich Crusade">Norwich Crusade</a>, led by the English bishop <a href="/wiki/Henry_le_Despenser" title="Henry le Despenser">Henry le Despenser</a>, <a href="/wiki/Siege_of_Ypres_(1383)" title="Siege of Ypres (1383)">Ypres was besieged</a> from May to August 1383, until French relief forces arrived. After the destruction of <a href="/wiki/Th%C3%A9rouanne" title="Thérouanne">Thérouanne</a>, Ypres became the seat of the new <a href="/wiki/Diocese_of_Ypres" title="Diocese of Ypres">Diocese of Ypres</a> in 1561, and <a href="/wiki/St_Martin%27s_Cathedral,_Ypres" title="St Martin's Cathedral, Ypres">Saint Martin's Church</a> was elevated to cathedral.</p>
<p>On 25 March 1678 <a href="/wiki/Siege_of_Ypres_(1678)" title="Siege of Ypres (1678)">Ypres was conquered</a> by the forces of <a href="/wiki/Louis_XIV_of_France" title="Louis XIV of France">Louis XIV of France</a>. It remained French under the <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Nijmegen" class="mw-redirect" title="Treaty of Nijmegen">treaty of Nijmegen</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Vauban" class="mw-redirect" title="Vauban">Vauban</a> constructed his typical fortifications that can still be seen today. In 1697, after the <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Ryswick" title="Treaty of Ryswick">Treaty of Ryswick</a>, Ypres was returned to the <a href="/wiki/Spanish_Netherlands" title="Spanish Netherlands">Spanish Crown</a>.</p>
<p>During the <a href="/wiki/War_of_the_Spanish_Succession" title="War of the Spanish Succession">War of the Spanish Succession</a>, the <a href="/wiki/John_Churchill,_1st_Duke_of_Marlborough" title="John Churchill, 1st Duke of Marlborough">Duke of Marlborough</a> in 1709 intended to capture Ypres, at the time a major French fortress, but changed his mind owing to the long time and effort it had taken him to capture <a href="/wiki/Tournai" title="Tournai">Tournai</a> and apprehension of disease spreading in his army in the poorly drained land around Ypres (see <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Malplaquet" title="Battle of Malplaquet">Battle of Malplaquet</a>). In 1713 it was handed over to the Habsburgs, and became part of the <a href="/wiki/Austrian_Netherlands" title="Austrian Netherlands">Austrian Netherlands</a>.</p>
<p>In 1782 the <a href="/wiki/Habsburg_Austria" class="mw-redirect" title="Habsburg Austria">Habsburg Austrian</a> <a href="/wiki/Joseph_II,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Joseph II, Holy Roman Emperor">Emperor Joseph II</a> ordered parts of the walls torn down. This destruction, which was only partly repaired, made it easier for the French to capture the city in the <a href="/wiki/Siege_of_Ypres_(1794)" title="Siege of Ypres (1794)">1794 Siege of Ypres</a> during the <a href="/wiki/War_of_the_First_Coalition" title="War of the First Coalition">War of the First Coalition</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1850 the <a href="/wiki/Ypresian" title="Ypresian">Ypresian</a> Age of the <a href="/wiki/Eocene" title="Eocene">Eocene</a> Epoch was named on the basis of geology in the region by <a href="/wiki/Belgium" title="Belgium">Belgian</a> geologist <a href="/wiki/Andr%C3%A9_Hubert_Dumont" class="mw-redirect" title="André Hubert Dumont">André Hubert Dumont</a>.</p>
<p>Ypres had long been fortified to keep out invaders. Parts of the early ramparts, dating from 1385, still survive near the Rijselpoort (Lille Gate). Over time, the earthworks were replaced by sturdier masonry and earth structures and a partial <a href="/wiki/Moat" title="Moat">moat</a>. Ypres was further fortified in the 17th and 18th centuries while under the occupation of the <a href="/wiki/Habsburgs" class="mw-redirect" title="Habsburgs">Habsburgs</a> and the French. Major works were completed at the end of the 17th century by the French military engineer <a href="/wiki/S%C3%A9bastien_Le_Prestre_de_Vauban" title="Sébastien Le Prestre de Vauban">Sébastien Le Prestre de Vauban</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="First_World_War">First World War</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ypres&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: First World War">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

</div>
</div>
<p>Ypres occupied a strategic position during the First World War because it stood in the path of Germany's planned sweep across the rest of Belgium and into France from the north (the <a href="/wiki/Schlieffen_Plan" title="Schlieffen Plan">Schlieffen Plan</a>). The <a href="/wiki/Neutrality_(international_relations)" class="mw-redirect" title="Neutrality (international relations)">neutrality</a> of Belgium was guaranteed by Britain; Germany's invasion of Belgium brought the <a href="/wiki/British_Empire" title="British Empire">British Empire</a> into the war. The German army surrounded the city on three sides, bombarding it throughout much of the war. To counterattack, British, French, and allied forces made costly advances from the <a href="/wiki/Ypres_Salient" title="Ypres Salient">Ypres Salient</a> into the German lines on the surrounding hills.</p>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/First_Battle_of_Ypres" title="First Battle of Ypres">First Battle of Ypres</a> (19 October to 22 November 1914), the Allies captured the town from the Germans. The Germans had used <a href="/wiki/Tear_gas" title="Tear gas">tear gas</a> at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Bolimov" class="mw-redirect" title="Battle of Bolimov">Battle of Bolimov</a> on 3&#160;January 1915. Their use of <a href="/wiki/Poison_gas" class="mw-redirect" title="Poison gas">poison gas</a> for the first time on 22 April 1915 marked the beginning of the <a href="/wiki/Second_Battle_of_Ypres" title="Second Battle of Ypres">Second Battle of Ypres</a>, which continued until 25 May 1915. They captured high ground east of the town. The first gas attack occurred against Canadian, British, and French soldiers, including both metropolitan French soldiers as well as <a href="/wiki/Senegal" title="Senegal">Senegalese</a> and <a href="/wiki/Algeria" title="Algeria">Algerian</a> <a href="/wiki/Tirailleurs" class="mw-redirect" title="Tirailleurs">tirailleurs</a> (light infantry) from French Africa. The gas used was <a href="/wiki/Chlorine" title="Chlorine">chlorine</a>. <a href="/wiki/Mustard_gas" class="mw-redirect" title="Mustard gas">Mustard gas</a>, also called <b>Yperite</b> from the name of this town, was also used for the first time near Ypres, in the autumn of 1917.</p>

<p>Of the battles, the largest, best-known, and most costly in human suffering was the Third Battle of Ypres (31 July to 6 November 1917, also known as the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Passchendaele" title="Battle of Passchendaele">Battle of Passchendaele</a>), in which the British, Canadian, <a href="/wiki/ANZAC" class="mw-redirect" title="ANZAC">ANZAC</a>, and French forces recaptured the <a href="/wiki/Passchendaele_Ridge" class="mw-redirect" title="Passchendaele Ridge">Passchendaele Ridge</a> east of the city at a terrible cost of lives. After months of fighting, this battle resulted in nearly half a million casualties to all sides, and only a few miles of ground won by Allied forces. During the course of the war the town was all but obliterated by the artillery fire.</p>
<p>English-speaking soldiers in that war often referred to Ieper/Ypres by the deliberate mispronunciation <i>Wipers</i>. British soldiers even published a wartime newspaper called the <i><a href="/wiki/Wipers_Times" class="mw-redirect" title="Wipers Times">Wipers Times</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> The same style of deliberate mispronunciation was applied to other Flemish place names in the Ypres area for the benefit of British troops, such as Whyteshaete becoming <i>White Sheet</i> and Ploegsteert becoming <i>Plug Street</i>.</p>
<p>Ypres was one of the sites that hosted an unofficial <a href="/wiki/Christmas_Truce" class="mw-redirect" title="Christmas Truce">Christmas Truce</a> in 1914 between German and British soldiers.</p>
<p>During World War Two, the British Expeditionary Force (B.E.F.) would fight the Germans in a delaying action at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_the_Ypres%E2%80%93Comines_Canal" title="Battle of the Ypres–Comines Canal">Ypres-Comines Canal</a>, one of the actions that allowed the Allied retreat to Dunkirk.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="War_memory_and_memorial">War memory and memorial</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ypres&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: War memory and memorial">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Historian <a href="/wiki/Mark_Connelly" title="Mark Connelly">Mark Connelly</a> states that in the 1920s, British veterans set up the Ypres League and made the city the symbol of all that they believed Britain was fighting for and gave it a holy aura in their minds. The Ypres League sought to transform the horrors of trench warfare into a spiritual quest in which British and imperial troops were purified by their sacrifice. In 1920 Lieutenant-Colonel Beckles Willson's guide book, <i>The Holy Ground of British Arms</i> captured the mood of the Ypres League:</p>
<dl>
<dd>There is not a single half-acre in Ypres that is not sacred. There is not a single stone which has not sheltered scores of loyal young hearts, whose one impulse and desire was to fight and, if need be, to die for England. Their blood has drenched its cloisters and its cellars, but if never a drop had been spilt, if never a life had been lost in defence of Ypres still would Ypres have been hallowed, if only for the hopes and the courage it has inspired and the scenes of valour and sacrifice it has witnessed.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></dd>
</dl>
<p>Ypres became a pilgrimage destination for Britons to imagine and share the sufferings of their men and gain a spiritual benefit.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the 100th anniversary period more attempts are being made to preserve the First World War heritage in and around Ypres.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ypres_today">Ypres today</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ypres&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Ypres today">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>After the war the town was rebuilt using money paid by Germany in reparations, with the main square, including the Cloth Hall and town hall, being rebuilt as close to the original designs as possible (the rest of the rebuilt town is more modern in appearance). The Cloth Hall today is home to <a href="/wiki/In_Flanders_Fields_Museum" title="In Flanders Fields Museum">In Flanders Fields Museum</a>, dedicated to Ypres's role in the First World War.</p>
<p>Today, Ypres is a small city in the very western part of Belgium, the so-called <i>Westhoek</i>. Ypres these days has the title of "city of peace" and maintains a close friendship with another town on which war had a profound impact: <a href="/wiki/Hiroshima" title="Hiroshima">Hiroshima</a>. Both towns witnessed warfare at its worst: Ypres was one of the first places where <a href="/wiki/Chemical_warfare" title="Chemical warfare">chemical warfare</a> was <a href="/wiki/Second_Battle_of_Ypres" title="Second Battle of Ypres">employed</a>, while Hiroshima suffered the debut of <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_warfare" title="Nuclear warfare">nuclear warfare</a>. The city governments of Ypres and Hiroshima advocate that cities should never be targets again and campaign for the abolition of nuclear weapons. Ypres hosts the international campaign secretariat of <a href="/wiki/Mayors_for_Peace" title="Mayors for Peace">Mayors for Peace</a>, an international Mayoral organization mobilizing cities and citizens worldwide to abolish and eliminate nuclear weapons by the year 2020.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sights">Sights</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ypres&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Sights">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Town_centre">Town centre</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ypres&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Town centre">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>The imposing <a href="/wiki/Cloth_Hall,_Ypres" class="mw-redirect" title="Cloth Hall, Ypres">Cloth Hall</a> was built in the 13th century and was one of the largest commercial buildings of the Middle Ages. The structure which stands today is the exact copy of the original medieval building, rebuilt after the war. The <a href="/wiki/Belfries_of_Belgium_and_France" title="Belfries of Belgium and France">belfry</a> that surmounts the hall houses a 49-bell <a href="/wiki/Carillon" title="Carillon">carillon</a>. The whole complex was designated a <a href="/wiki/World_Heritage_Site" title="World Heritage Site">World Heritage Site</a> by UNESCO in 1999.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Gothic_architecture" title="Gothic architecture">Gothic</a>-style <a href="/wiki/Saint_Martin%27s_Cathedral" class="mw-redirect" title="Saint Martin's Cathedral">Saint Martin's Cathedral</a>, originally built in 1221, was also completely reconstructed after the war, but now with a higher spire. It houses the tombs of <a href="/wiki/Jansenius" class="mw-redirect" title="Jansenius">Jansenius</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bishop_of_Ypres" class="mw-redirect" title="Bishop of Ypres">bishop of Ypres</a> and father of the religious movement known as <a href="/wiki/Jansenism" title="Jansenism">Jansenism</a>, and of <a href="/wiki/Robert_III_of_Flanders" class="mw-redirect" title="Robert III of Flanders">Robert of Bethune</a>, nicknamed "The Lion of Flanders", who was <a href="/wiki/Count_of_Nevers" class="mw-redirect" title="Count of Nevers">Count of Nevers</a> (1273–1322) and <a href="/wiki/Count_of_Flanders" title="Count of Flanders">Count of Flanders</a> (1305–1322).</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Menin_Gate">Menin Gate</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ypres&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Menin Gate">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>The <a href="/wiki/Menin_Gate" title="Menin Gate">Menin Gate Memorial to the Missing</a><sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> Ypres commemorates those soldiers of the British Commonwealth – with the exception of Newfoundland and New Zealand – who fell in the <a href="/wiki/Ypres_Salient" title="Ypres Salient">Ypres Salient</a> during the First World War before 16 August 1917, who have no known grave. Those who died from that date – and all from New Zealand and Newfoundland – are commemorated elsewhere.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> The memorial now bears the names of more than 54,000 officers and men whose graves are not known. The memorial, designed by Sir <a href="/wiki/Reginald_Blomfield" title="Reginald Blomfield">Reginald Blomfield</a> with sculpture by Sir <a href="/wiki/William_Reid_Dick" title="William Reid Dick">William Reid Dick</a>, was unveiled by <a href="/wiki/Herbert_Plumer,_1st_Viscount_Plumer" title="Herbert Plumer, 1st Viscount Plumer">Lord Plumer</a> on 24 July 1927. It was built and is maintained by <a href="/wiki/The_Commonwealth_War_Graves_Commission" class="mw-redirect" title="The Commonwealth War Graves Commission">The Commonwealth War Graves Commission</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> It commemorates those of all Commonwealth nations (except New Zealand) who died in the Salient, in the case of United Kingdom casualties before 16 August 1917. Those United Kingdom and New Zealand servicemen who died after that date are named on the memorial at <a href="/wiki/Tyne_Cot" title="Tyne Cot">Tyne Cot</a>, a site which marks the furthest point reached by Commonwealth forces in Belgium until nearly the end of the war. Other New Zealand casualties are commemorated on memorials at <a href="/wiki/Buttes_New_British_Cemetery" title="Buttes New British Cemetery">Buttes New British Cemetery</a> and <a href="/wiki/Messines_Ridge_(New_Zealand)_Memorial" title="Messines Ridge (New Zealand) Memorial">Messines Ridge British Cemetery</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>The memorial's location is especially poignant, as it lies on the eastward route from the town, which <a href="/wiki/Entente_Cordiale" title="Entente Cordiale">Entente</a> soldiers would have taken towards the fighting – many never to return. Every evening since 1928 (except for a period during the Second World War when Ypres was occupied by Germany), at precisely eight o'clock, traffic around the imposing arches of the Menin Gate Memorial has been stopped while the <i><a href="/wiki/Last_Post" title="Last Post">Last Post</a></i> is sounded beneath the gate by the local fire brigade. This tribute is given in honour of the memory of <a href="/wiki/British_Empire" title="British Empire">British Empire</a> soldiers who fought and died there. The Menin Gate in Ypres records only the soldiers for whom there is no known grave. As graves are identified, the names of those buried in them are removed from the Menin Gate.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>The ceremony was prohibited by occupying German forces during the Second World War, but it was resumed on the very evening of liberation – 6 September 1944 – notwithstanding the heavy fighting that still went on in other parts of the town. The lions that marked the original gate were given to Australia by the people of Belgium and can be found at the <a href="/wiki/Australian_War_Memorial" title="Australian War Memorial">Australian War Memorial</a> in <a href="/wiki/Canberra" title="Canberra">Canberra</a>. The Last Post ceremony was hosted during the German occupation of Belgium in WWII, at Brookwood Military Cemetery in England.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="War_graves">War graves</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ypres&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: War graves">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>War graves, both of the Allied side and the Central Powers, cover the landscape around Ypres. The largest number of dead are at <a href="/wiki/Langemark_German_war_cemetery" title="Langemark German war cemetery">Langemark German war cemetery</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tyne_Cot" title="Tyne Cot">Tyne Cot Commonwealth war cemetery</a>. The countryside around Ypres is featured in the famous poem by <a href="/wiki/John_McCrae" title="John McCrae">John McCrae</a>, <i><a href="/wiki/In_Flanders_Fields" title="In Flanders Fields">In Flanders Fields</a></i>.</p>

<p><a href="/wiki/Saint_George%27s_Memorial_Church,_Ypres" title="Saint George's Memorial Church, Ypres">Saint George's Memorial Church</a> commemorates the British and Commonwealth soldiers who died in the five battles fought for Ypres during First World War.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Events">Events</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ypres&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Events">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Kattenstoet_(2).JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5c/Kattenstoet_%282%29.JPG/220px-Kattenstoet_%282%29.JPG" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5c/Kattenstoet_%282%29.JPG/330px-Kattenstoet_%282%29.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5c/Kattenstoet_%282%29.JPG/440px-Kattenstoet_%282%29.JPG 2x" data-file-width="1024" data-file-height="768" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Kattenstoet_(2).JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Kattenstoet" title="Kattenstoet">Cat Parade</a></div>

<ul>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Kattenstoet" title="Kattenstoet">Cat Parade</a> ("Kattenstoet") takes place every three years on the second Sunday of May. It involves the throwing of stuffed toy cats from the belfry and a colourful parade of cats and witches. The next Cat Parade takes place on 13 May 2018.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></li>
<li>Ypres is also the home of the <a href="/wiki/Belgium_Ypres_Westhoek_Rally" title="Belgium Ypres Westhoek Rally">Belgium Ypres Westhoek Rally</a> since its creation in 1965. It is organized by the Auto Club Targa Florio. Some of the drivers to have taken part are among the best-known names in rallying, such as <a href="/wiki/Juha_Kankkunen" title="Juha Kankkunen">Juha Kankkunen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bruno_Thiry" title="Bruno Thiry">Bruno Thiry</a>, <a href="/wiki/Henri_Toivonen" title="Henri Toivonen">Henri Toivonen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Colin_McRae" title="Colin McRae">Colin McRae</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jimmy_McRae" title="Jimmy McRae">Jimmy McRae</a>, <a href="/wiki/Marc_Duez" title="Marc Duez">Marc Duez</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fran%C3%A7ois_Duval" title="François Duval">François Duval</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Freddy_Loix" title="Freddy Loix">Freddy Loix</a> among others.</li>
<li>Ypres holds an annual canoe polo tournament in which teams come from all over Europe to play.</li>
<li>On 9 July 2014, the 101st Tour de France started stage 5 in Ypres.</li>
<li>During the last weekend of August each year, Ypres hosts the <i>Ieperfest</i>, one of the biggest European festivals in the hardcore punk subculture.</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Economy">Economy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ypres&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Economy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Though Ypres is a historic city, and generates significant income from tourism, it also has a number of industrial areas. The biggest one is along the <a href="/wiki/Ieperlee" title="Ieperlee">Ieperlee</a> canal, which hosts room for around 120 companies and a wind farm in the north of Ypres.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>The office area known as <a href="/w/index.php?title=Ieper_Business_Park&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Ieper Business Park (page does not exist)">Ieper Business Park</a> is connected to the industrial area. That office area started as the site of speech recognition company <a href="/wiki/Lernout_%26_Hauspie" title="Lernout &amp; Hauspie">Lernout &amp; Hauspie</a>, and was named "Flanders Language Valley" (mimicking <a href="/wiki/Silicon_Valley" title="Silicon Valley">Silicon Valley</a>), until the company went bankrupt. Since then, the office area had many difficult years, where a big share of the offices were unused. However, those years are mostly over, and currently, the area offers about 1000 employees a job.</p>
<p>Then there are also various other, smaller industrial areas like the area around <a href="/wiki/Picanol" title="Picanol">Picanol</a> in the south of Ypres.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Transport">Transport</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ypres&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Transport">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Ieper_railway_station" title="Ieper railway station">Ieper railway station</a> run by <a href="/wiki/NMBS" class="mw-redirect" title="NMBS">NMBS</a> has frequent trains to <a href="/wiki/Kortrijk_railway_station" title="Kortrijk railway station">Kortrijk</a>.</p>
<p>It can also be accessed from Brussels, linking to Eurostar, and takes about 75 minutes with two stops.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
