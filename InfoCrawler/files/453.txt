<p>The <b>Battle of Nuremberg</b> was a five-day battle between the forces of the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> <a href="/wiki/7th_Army_(United_States)" class="mw-redirect" title="7th Army (United States)">7th Army</a> on one side, and <a href="/wiki/Nazi_Germany" title="Nazi Germany">Nazi Germany</a> and <a href="/wiki/Russian_Liberation_Army" title="Russian Liberation Army">Russian Liberation Army</a> volunteers on the other during <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>. The battle saw some of the fiercest <a href="/wiki/Urban_combat" class="mw-redirect" title="Urban combat">urban combat</a> during the war and it took four days for the United States to capture the city. The battle was a blow to Nazi Germany as Nuremberg was a center of the Nazi regime. Many rallies took place in the city and to lose the city to the Americans took a heavy toll on already low German morale.<sup id="cite_ref-NB_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NB-1">[1]</a></sup> Even though American forces heavily outnumbered the German forces,<sup id="cite_ref-troops_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-troops-2">[2]</a></sup> it wasn't until 20 April, that the 7th Army took the city center. The battle devastated the city.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Background">Background</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Battle_of_Nuremberg_(1945)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>, <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>, <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a> and <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a> jointly invaded Germany from the west on 8 February 1945. The German Army took heavy losses as the Allied armies crossed the <a href="/wiki/Rhine_river" class="mw-redirect" title="Rhine river">Rhine</a> and surrounded the Ruhr area as the Soviet and Polish armies pushed from the east. By April the US and Soviet armies were closing in on each other, constricting an already narrow gap of German-controlled territory running from <a href="/wiki/Berlin" title="Berlin">Berlin</a> to <a href="/wiki/Munich" title="Munich">Munich</a> and including Nuremberg. As the <a href="/wiki/US_12th_Army_Group" class="mw-redirect" title="US 12th Army Group">12th Army Group</a> continued to push east towards Berlin, the <a href="/wiki/US_6th_Army_Group" class="mw-redirect" title="US 6th Army Group">6th Army Group</a> received orders to push into southern Germany and into Austria.<sup id="cite_ref-Bed_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bed-4">[4]</a></sup> Despite the heavier resistance from the German Army in the south as compared to the north, the US 7th Army broke out of its bridgehead on the Rhine south of <a href="/wiki/Frankfurt" title="Frankfurt">Frankfurt</a> on 28 March. After fierce fighting, the 7th Army captured <a href="/wiki/Aschaffenburg" title="Aschaffenburg">Aschaffenburg</a> in Bavaria on 3 April and <a href="/wiki/Heilbronn" title="Heilbronn">Heilbronn</a> in Württemberg on 12 April, which left Nuremberg wide open to American attack. On 12 April the German High Command ordered the unconditional defense of all cities and Hitler placed <a href="/w/index.php?title=Reich_Defense_Commissioner&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Reich Defense Commissioner (page does not exist)">Reich Defense Commissioner</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gauleiter" title="Gauleiter">Gauleiter</a> of <a href="/wiki/Gau_Franconia" title="Gau Franconia">Franconia</a> <a href="/wiki/Karl_Holz_(Nazi)" title="Karl Holz (Nazi)">Karl Holz</a> in charge of the German Army around Nuremberg.<sup id="cite_ref-NB_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NB-1">[1]</a></sup> On 15 April the 7th Army advanced towards Nuremberg, rapidly capturing <a href="/wiki/Bamberg" title="Bamberg">Bamberg</a> in the process.<sup id="cite_ref-Bed_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bed-4">[4]</a></sup> As the 7th Army neared Nuremberg, Holz ordered setting up anti-tank barriers as well as anti-aircraft guns around the old city. Holz's forces were heavily outnumbered, but he still believed that "the Americans would break sooner or later".<sup id="cite_ref-NB_1-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NB-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="The_battle">The battle</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Battle_of_Nuremberg_(1945)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: The battle">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>By 16 April, the 7th Army had begun its assault on Nuremberg—not from the west as Holz expected, but from the east and northeast. By the end of the day, the Americans had captured the outskirts of Erlenstegen and Buch. Arthur Schoeddert, a constable of anti-aircraft artillery, failed to execute Hitler's orders to blow up electricity, gas and water plants in the city. By 17 April, the 7th Army captured the marshaling yard and the surrounding area as well as the Veilhofstrasse and Woehrd neighborhoods. By evening, the airport to the north was captured and US artillery began to shell the old city. American troops met fierce resistance around the old city on 18 April, which destroyed and damaged many buildings around the old city, including the historic city castle. On 18 April, as American artillery continued to shell the old city, U.S. troops reached the old city via the Burgschmietstrasse. On 20 April, the 3rd Infantry Division under the command of Major General <a href="/wiki/John_W._O%27Daniel" title="John W. O'Daniel">John W. O'Daniel</a> and the 45th Infantry Division under Major General <a href="/wiki/Robert_T._Frederick" title="Robert T. Frederick">Robert T. Frederick</a> laid siege to the old city. German resistance was so great that American heavy artillery and air support was deployed. Holz ordered his men to continue to fight. Holz himself was trapped in the police station in the old city, but continued to resist. After American troops gave him four chances for a peaceful surrender, he was killed while American troops overran the building. After Holz's death, second-in-command Colonel Wolf realized that the city could no longer be held. At 11:00 he ordered all German troops in the area to surrender. On the evening of 20 April, the American flag was hoisted at Adolf Hitler Platz, formally ending the battle.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
