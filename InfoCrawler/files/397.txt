<p>The <b>Prince Bishopric of Bamberg</b> was established in 1007, to further expand the spread of <a href="/wiki/Christianity" title="Christianity">Christianity</a> in <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a>. The ecclesiastical state was a member of the <a href="/wiki/Holy_Roman_Empire" title="Holy Roman Empire">Holy Roman Empire</a> from about 1245 until it was subsumed to the <a href="/wiki/Electorate_of_Bavaria" title="Electorate of Bavaria">Electorate of Bavaria</a> in 1802.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="State">State</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Prince-Bishopric_of_Bamberg&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: State">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Bishops of Bamberg received the <a href="/wiki/Princes_of_the_Holy_Roman_Empire" title="Princes of the Holy Roman Empire">princely</a> title by Emperor <a href="/wiki/Frederick_II,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Frederick II, Holy Roman Emperor">Frederick II of Hohenstaufen</a> before his deposition by <a href="/wiki/Pope_Innocent_IV" title="Pope Innocent IV">Pope Innocent IV</a> in 1245, whereby the diocese became an <a href="/wiki/Imperial_state" class="mw-redirect" title="Imperial state">Imperial state</a>.</p>
<p>During the 18th century, it was often held in conjunction with the neighboring <a href="/wiki/Bishopric_of_W%C3%BCrzburg" title="Bishopric of Würzburg">Bishopric of Würzburg</a>. Bamberg was bordered, among others, by Würzburg to the west, by the Margraviate of <a href="/wiki/Brandenburg-Ansbach" class="mw-redirect" title="Brandenburg-Ansbach">Brandenburg-Ansbach</a> and the Free City of <a href="/wiki/Nuremberg" title="Nuremberg">Nuremberg</a> to the south, by the Margraviate of <a href="/wiki/Brandenburg-Bayreuth" class="mw-redirect" title="Brandenburg-Bayreuth">Brandenburg-Bayreuth</a> to the east and by the Duchy of <a href="/wiki/Saxe-Coburg" title="Saxe-Coburg">Saxe-Coburg</a> to the north.</p>

<p>The prince-bishopric also ruled over large possessions within the <a href="/wiki/Duchy_of_Carinthia" title="Duchy of Carinthia">Duchy of Carinthia</a>, including the strategically important towns of <a href="/wiki/Villach" title="Villach">Villach</a>, <a href="/wiki/Feldkirchen_in_K%C3%A4rnten" title="Feldkirchen in Kärnten">Feldkirchen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wolfsberg,_Carinthia" title="Wolfsberg, Carinthia">Wolfsberg</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tarvisio" title="Tarvisio">Tarvisio</a> at the transalpine road to <a href="/wiki/Venice" title="Venice">Venice</a>, as well as <a href="/wiki/Kirchdorf_an_der_Krems" title="Kirchdorf an der Krems">Kirchdorf an der Krems</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Archduchy_of_Austria" title="Archduchy of Austria">Archduchy of Austria</a>. The <a href="/wiki/House_of_Habsburg" title="House of Habsburg">Habsburg</a> Empress <a href="/wiki/Maria_Theresa_of_Austria" class="mw-redirect" title="Maria Theresa of Austria">Maria Theresa</a> purchased these territories in 1759.</p>
<p>In the course of the <i><a href="/wiki/German_Mediatization" class="mw-redirect" title="German Mediatization">German Mediatization</a></i> of 1802/3 which saw the suppression of virtually all the ecclesiastical principalities, Bamberg was annexed to Bavaria. The former prince-bishopric then had an area of 3,580 km² and a population of 207,000.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Prince-Bishopric_of_Bamberg&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Beginning on 1 November 1007, a <a href="/wiki/Synod" title="Synod">synod</a> was held in the city of <a href="/wiki/Frankfurt_am_Main" class="mw-redirect" title="Frankfurt am Main">Frankfurt am Main</a>. Eight archbishops and twenty-seven bishops were present, led by Archbishop <a href="/wiki/Willigis" title="Willigis">Willigis</a> of <a href="/wiki/Electorate_of_Mainz" title="Electorate of Mainz">Mainz</a>, as well as the King of the Romans, <a href="/wiki/Henry_II,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Henry II, Holy Roman Emperor">Heinrich II</a> (Henry II). The king intended to create a new diocese that would aid in the final conquest of paganism in the area around Bamberg. But the territory of the <a href="/wiki/Wends" title="Wends">Wends</a> on the upper <a href="/wiki/Main_(river)" title="Main (river)">Main</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Wiesent_(Regnitz)" title="Wiesent (Regnitz)">Wiesent</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Aisch" title="Aisch">Aisch</a> had belonged to the <a href="/wiki/Diocese_of_W%C3%BCrzburg" class="mw-redirect" title="Diocese of Würzburg">Diocese of Würzburg</a> since the organization of the Middle German bishoprics by <a href="/wiki/St._Boniface" class="mw-redirect" title="St. Boniface">St. Boniface</a>, so that no new diocese could be erected without the consent of the occupant of that see. Bishop Henry I of Würzburg was willing to go along with parting with some of his territory, as the king promised to have Würzburg raised to an <a href="/wiki/Archbishopric" class="mw-redirect" title="Archbishopric">archbishopric</a> and to give him an equivalent in <a href="/wiki/Meiningen" title="Meiningen">Meiningen</a>. The consent of <a href="/wiki/Pope_John_XVII" title="Pope John XVII">Pope John XVII</a> was obtained for this arrangement, but the elevation of Würzburg to an archbishopric proved impracticable also due to Willigis' reservations, and Bishop Henry I at first withdrew his consent.</p>

<p>Nevertheless, after several further concessions, King Heinrich II obtained the consent for the foundation of the diocese of Bamberg from parts of the dioceses of Würzburg and - later - the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic_Diocese_of_Eichst%C3%A4tt" title="Roman Catholic Diocese of Eichstätt">Diocese of Eichstätt</a>. Bamberg was made directly subordinate to Rome. It was also decided that Eberhard, the king's <a href="/wiki/Archchancellor" title="Archchancellor">chancellor</a>, would be ordained by Archbishop Willigis of Mainz, to be the head of the new border area diocese. The new diocese had expensive gifts at the synod confirmed by documents, in order to place it on a solid foundation.</p>
<p>Henry wanted the celebrated monkish rigour and studiousness of the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic_Diocese_of_Hildesheim" title="Roman Catholic Diocese of Hildesheim">Hildesheim</a> cathedral chapter - Henry himself was educated there - linked together with the churches under his control, including his favourite bishopric of Bamberg. The next seven bishops were named by the emperors, after which election by the cathedral chapter became the rule, as in all the German prince-bishoprics. Eberhard's immediate successor, Suidger of Morsleben, became pope in 1046 as <a href="/wiki/Clement_II" class="mw-redirect" title="Clement II">Clement II</a>. He was the only pope to be interred north of the Alps at the <a href="/wiki/Bamberg_Cathedral" title="Bamberg Cathedral">Bamberg Cathedral</a>.</p>
<p>In the thirteenth century the diocese gradually became a territorial principality, and its bishops took secular precedence next after the archbishops; Bishop Henry I of Bilversheim (1242-57) became the first <a href="/wiki/Prince-Bishop" class="mw-redirect" title="Prince-Bishop">Prince-Bishop</a>.</p>
<p>The fortieth bishop, George III of Limburg (1505-22), was inclined toward the <a href="/wiki/Protestant_Reformation" class="mw-redirect" title="Protestant Reformation">Reformation</a>, which caused a violent social outbreak under his successor Weigand (1522-56), and the city suffered severely in the <a href="/wiki/Second_Margrave_War" title="Second Margrave War">Second Margrave War</a> (1552-54), as well as in the <a href="/wiki/Thirty_Years%27_War" title="Thirty Years' War">Thirty Years' War</a>, when it was placed under the jurisdiction of Bernard, the new Duke of <a href="/wiki/Franconia" title="Franconia">Franconia</a>.</p>
<p>At the <a href="/wiki/Peace_of_Westphalia" title="Peace of Westphalia">Peace of Westphalia</a> (1648), the prince-bishops recovered their possessions. During the French Revolution, Bamberg was overrun by the French and, in the course of the <a href="/wiki/German_mediatization" title="German mediatization">German mediatization</a> of 1802/3, it was annexed to <a href="/wiki/Bavaria" title="Bavaria">Bavaria</a>. From 1808 to 1817 the See was vacant; but by the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Bavarian_Concordat&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Bavarian Concordat (page does not exist)">Bavarian Concordat</a> of the latter year it was made an archbishopric, with Würzburg, <a href="/wiki/Diocese_of_Speyer" class="mw-redirect" title="Diocese of Speyer">Speyer</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Diocese_of_Eichst%C3%A4tt" class="mw-redirect" title="Diocese of Eichstätt">Eichstädt</a> as suffragan sees.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Patronage">Patronage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Prince-Bishopric_of_Bamberg&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Patronage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The prince bishops operated courts like minor royalty and employed artists, in particular musicians. Kapellmeisters and organists attached to the court included several minor South German masters such as <a href="/wiki/Heinrich_Pfendner" title="Heinrich Pfendner">Heinrich Pfendner</a>, <a href="/wiki/Johann_Baal" title="Johann Baal">Johann Baal</a>, <a href="/wiki/Georg_Arnold" title="Georg Arnold">Georg Arnold</a> and <a href="/wiki/Georg_Mengel" title="Georg Mengel">Georg Mengel</a>.</p>
