<p><b>Quarrington</b> is a village and former <a href="/wiki/Civil_parishes_in_England" class="mw-redirect" title="Civil parishes in England">civil parish</a>, now part of the civil parish of <a href="/wiki/Sleaford" title="Sleaford">Sleaford</a>, in the <a href="/wiki/North_Kesteven" title="North Kesteven">North Kesteven</a> <a href="/wiki/Non-metropolitan_district" title="Non-metropolitan district">district</a> of <a href="/wiki/Lincolnshire" title="Lincolnshire">Lincolnshire</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Non-metropolitan_county" title="Non-metropolitan county">non-metropolitan county</a> in the <a href="/wiki/East_Midlands" title="East Midlands">East Midlands</a> of England. The old village and its church lie approximately 1.2 miles (2&#160;km) south-west from the centre of Sleaford, the nearest <a href="/wiki/Market_town" title="Market town">market town</a>, but suburban housing developments at <b>New Quarrington</b> and <b>Quarrington Hill</b> effectively link the two settlements. Bypassed by the <a href="/wiki/A151_road" title="A151 road">A15</a>, it is connected to <a href="/wiki/Lincoln,_England" title="Lincoln, England">Lincoln</a> and <a href="/wiki/Peterborough" title="Peterborough">Peterborough</a>, as well as <a href="/wiki/Newark-on-Trent" title="Newark-on-Trent">Newark</a> and <a href="/wiki/King%27s_Lynn" title="King's Lynn">King's Lynn</a> (via the <a href="/wiki/A17_road_(England)" title="A17 road (England)">A17</a>). At the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_Census_2011" title="United Kingdom Census 2011">2011 Census</a>, Quarrington and Mareham ward, which incorporates most of the settlement, had an estimated population of 7,046.</p>
<p>Quarrington was a rural community during the early and middle <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Saxons" title="Anglo-Saxons">Anglo-Saxon</a> period while <a href="/wiki/Watermill" title="Watermill">mills</a> along the <a href="/wiki/River_Slea" title="River Slea">River Slea</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">Middle Ages</a> gave the village its alternative name of Millthorpe. The <a href="/wiki/Bishop_of_Lincoln" title="Bishop of Lincoln">Bishop of Lincoln</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ramsey_Abbey" title="Ramsey Abbey">Ramsey Abbey</a> held manors in Quarrington after the <a href="/wiki/Norman_conquest_of_England" title="Norman conquest of England">Norman Conquest</a>, but the Carre family of Sleaford were the principal land-owners between 1559 and 1683, when its estate passed by marriage to the <a href="/wiki/Marquess_of_Bristol" title="Marquess of Bristol">Marquesses of Bristol</a>. Although the growth of <a href="/wiki/Victorian_era" title="Victorian era">Victorian</a> Sleaford saw the town encroach into the parish's boundaries, the old village remained a small cluster of farm buildings and cottages for much of its history. The sale of most of the surrounding farmland by successive Marquesses of Bristol in the late 20th century led to the rapid development of residential estates on Quarrington Hill and in New Quarrington which have engulfed the original settlement. Low crime rates, affordable housing, high standards of living and access to good schools have attracted home-buyers to the area, contributing to a sharp rise in the population.</p>
<p>The medieval <a href="/wiki/St_Botolph%27s_Church,_Quarrington" title="St Botolph's Church, Quarrington">St Botolph's Church</a>, a grade II* <a href="/wiki/Listed_building" title="Listed building">listed building</a>, lies at the heart of the old village and remains a hub for the <a href="/wiki/Church_of_England" title="Church of England">Anglican</a> community. The village's primary school serves local children, who continue their secondary education in one of Sleaford's three secondary schools. The nearest railway station on the <a href="/wiki/Poacher_Line" title="Poacher Line">Grantham to Skegness</a> and <a href="/wiki/Peterborough_to_Lincoln_Line" class="mw-redirect" title="Peterborough to Lincoln Line">Peterborough to Lincoln</a> lines is in Sleaford. In the 19th century, the most common employment was in agriculture and more than half of the village's population were farm labourers. By 2011, most residents were employed in the wholesale and retail trades, public administration and defence, human health and social work, and manufacturing.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Quarrington,_Lincolnshire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early">Early</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Quarrington,_Lincolnshire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Early">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Scattered <a href="/wiki/Paleolithic" title="Paleolithic">Palaeolithic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bronze_Age" title="Bronze Age">Bronze Age</a> materials have been discovered in and around Quarrington but while nearby Old Sleaford is known to have been settled in the <a href="/wiki/Iron_Age" title="Iron Age">Iron Age</a> and occupied by the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Britain" title="Roman Britain">Romans</a>, there is little evidence for sustained pre-Saxon settlement at Quarrington.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Between 1992 and 1995, archaeologists evaluating 34 trenches across 13 hectares of land around the village uncovered 56 ditches or gullies, a number of <a href="/wiki/Posthole" title="Posthole">postholes</a>, a large collection of pottery <a href="/wiki/Sherd" title="Sherd">sherds</a> and "extremely rare" evidence of metalworking from the 6th–7th centuries. The site has been dated to the 5th–9th centuries, representing an early and middle <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Saxons" title="Anglo-Saxons">Anglo-Saxon</a> settlement.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Although noted for its metalworking and its size, the archaeologists concluded that it "displayed all the signs of a typical rural community", reflecting how "the vast majority" of the Anglo-Saxon population lived.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Analysis of animal bones revealed that sheep-farming increasingly replaced pig-rearing at the site during this period.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> A small early Anglo-Saxon cemetery containing <a href="/wiki/Inhumation" class="mw-redirect" title="Inhumation">inhumations</a> was uncovered in the parish in 2000.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Medieval">Medieval</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Quarrington,_Lincolnshire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Medieval">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Ramsey_Abbey" title="Ramsey Abbey">Ramsey Abbey</a> was granted the manor of Quarrington by <a href="/w/index.php?title=Jol_of_Lincoln&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Jol of Lincoln (page does not exist)">Jol of Lincoln</a>, a monk, in <i>c</i>. 1051.<sup id="cite_ref-:28_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:28-6">[6]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> The <i><a href="/wiki/Domesday_Book" title="Domesday Book">Domesday Book</a></i> of 1086 recorded the abbey's manor consisted of one <a href="/wiki/Carucate" title="Carucate">carucate</a> and six <a href="/wiki/Bovate" class="mw-redirect" title="Bovate">bovates</a> and had two churches. Local historians Christine Mahany and David Roffe concluded that one of the churches was probably at Old Sleaford, where the abbey held a manor as <a href="/wiki/Soke_(legal)" title="Soke (legal)">sokeland</a> of Quarrington.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> A <a href="/wiki/Thegn" title="Thegn">thegn</a>, Bardi, held another manor in Quarrington before the <a href="/wiki/Norman_conquest_of_England" title="Norman conquest of England">conquest</a>, but it had been granted to the <a href="/wiki/Bishop_of_Lincoln" title="Bishop of Lincoln">Bishop of Lincoln</a> by 1086 and consisted of nine carucates and two and a half bovates plus 60 acres of meadow and two mills.<sup id="cite_ref-tr426_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-tr426-9">[9]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[n 1]</a></sup> A separate village, Millthorpe was also recorded, but the archaeologists Fiona Walker and Tom Lane suggest this may have been an alternative name for Quarrington.<sup id="cite_ref-:27_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:27-11">[10]</a></sup> Amongst the bishop's tenants was Hugh de St Vedasto or Vedeto, who held a <a href="/wiki/Knight%27s_fee" title="Knight's fee">knight's fee</a> in c. 1200–10. His family were prominent tenants in the village; Amicia, wife of Hugh de St Vedasto, died in possession of lands and tenements there in 1253, and Alexander and William de St Vedasto are named in connection in other documents.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[11]</a></sup> Henry Selvein, a knight, held Quarrington of the abbey and in <i>c</i>. 1166 granted the lands to <a href="/wiki/Haverholme_Priory" title="Haverholme Priory">Haverholme Priory</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-:28_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:28-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>Quarrington's medieval name was recorded in the <a href="/wiki/Domesday_Book" title="Domesday Book">Domesday Book</a> as <i>Corninctune</i> or <i>Cornunctone</i>, from the <a href="/wiki/Old_English" title="Old English">Old English</a> <i>cweorn</i> ("mill") and <i>tun</i> ("homestead"), meaning "miller's homestead",<sup id="cite_ref-:27_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:27-11">[10]</a></sup> reflecting the importance of the watermills which were built along the River Slea. Bardi had owned 10 mills in Sleaford and Quarrington before <i>Domesday</i>, and the 11 or 12 in existence by 1086 represents the largest cluster of mills in Lincolnshire.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[12]</a></sup> Mahany and Roffe suggest that Quarrington was a specialised part of his compact estate, geared in particular to milling.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[13]</a></sup> Excavations have revealed medieval pits and pottery in the village, with ditches reflecting a predominantly agricultural use of the land.<sup id="cite_ref-:26_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:26-15">[14]</a></sup> In the Lay Subsidy of 1334, Quarrington and Millthorpe were valued at £4 10s. 4½d., slightly below average for its <a href="/wiki/Wapentake" class="mw-redirect" title="Wapentake">wapentake</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-:26_15-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:26-15">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_and_later_modern">Early and later modern</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Quarrington,_Lincolnshire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Early and later modern">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Bishop of Lincoln alienated his lands at Quarrington to the Crown in 1547; they passed to <a href="/wiki/Edward_Seymour,_1st_Duke_of_Somerset" title="Edward Seymour, 1st Duke of Somerset">Edward Seymour, 1st Duke of Somerset</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lord_Protector" title="Lord Protector">Lord Protector</a>, but reverted to the Crown on his <a href="/wiki/Bill_of_attainder" title="Bill of attainder">attainder</a> for treason in 1549. <a href="/wiki/Mary_I_of_England" title="Mary I of England">Mary I</a> granted them to <a href="/wiki/Edward_Clinton,_1st_Earl_of_Lincoln" title="Edward Clinton, 1st Earl of Lincoln">Edward Clinton, 9th Baron Clinton and later Earl of Lincoln</a>, who sold them to <a href="/w/index.php?title=Robert_Carre&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Robert Carre (page does not exist)">Robert Carre</a> of Sleaford in 1559. Carre acquired numerous manors, including Old and New Sleaford, during the mid-16th century and they passed through marriage from his male-line descendants to the <a href="/wiki/Marquess_of_Bristol" title="Marquess of Bristol">Earls (later Marquesses) of Bristol</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-:29_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:29-16">[15]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[16]</a></sup> A 1563 diocesan return shows that 17 families lived in the village and 120 people took <a href="/wiki/Eucharist" title="Eucharist">Holy Communion</a>;<sup id="cite_ref-:26_15-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:26-15">[14]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[17]</a></sup> by the early 18th century, the diocesan visitations by <a href="/wiki/Edmund_Gibson" title="Edmund Gibson">Edmund Gibson</a> show the number of families had risen to 35.<sup id="cite_ref-:26_15-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:26-15">[14]</a></sup> The local historian <a href="/wiki/Edward_Trollope" title="Edward Trollope">Edward Trollope</a> recorded few changes in the early modern period, with the exception of a fire that burnt down the rectory in 1760; it was rebuilt in <i>c</i>. 1845.<sup id="cite_ref-:29_16-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:29-16">[15]</a></sup> Two 17th-century buildings still exist: the Bristol Farmhouse<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[18]</a></sup> and the coursed rubble Manor House, which one "widow Timberland" occupied in 1691.<sup id="cite_ref-:29_16-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:29-16">[15]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[19]</a></sup></p>

<p>The town's fields were <a href="/wiki/Enclosure(legal)" class="mw-redirect" title="Enclosure(legal)">enclosed</a> in 1796 and a map of the village was drawn up at the same time, showing the settlement along Town Road and Townside Road with Rector Field and Earl Field to the north and north-east respectively.<sup id="cite_ref-:29_16-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:29-16">[15]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[20]</a></sup> At the time, more than 210 acres of land were allotted to the rectory by Lord Bristol in place of the <a href="/wiki/Tithe" title="Tithe">tithes</a> it had previously been entitled to.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[21]</a></sup> During the mid and late-19th century, the population of the old village of Quarrington declined (from 98 in 1851 to 72 in 1871). Aside from the rectory and church, the village included two large farms and a cluster of labourers cottages around Town Road.<sup id="cite_ref-:3_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:3-23">[22]</a></sup> By 1872, the parish spanned 1,620 acres and the village contained 63 houses. The same year, Trollope wrote that "the appearance of this small village, lying around its well cared for church, is very pleasing".<sup id="cite_ref-:29_16-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:29-16">[15]</a></sup> As Sleaford expanded, houses were built along London and Station Roads, pushing the town inside the Quarrington parish boundaries in what became New Quarrington.<sup id="cite_ref-:3_23-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:3-23">[22]</a></sup> Sanitation in the poorest parts of Sleaford worsened during the 19th century and a <a href="/wiki/Local_Board_of_Health" class="mw-redirect" title="Local Board of Health">Local Board of Health</a> was charged with improving living conditions. In 1879, Lord Bristol sold land on Quarrington Hill to the board, who built a pumping station to transport clean fenland water east into the town.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>Most of the land remained in possession of the Marquesses of Bristol throughout the 20th century, but from the 1970s the indebted <a href="/wiki/Victor_Hervey,_6th_Marquess_of_Bristol" title="Victor Hervey, 6th Marquess of Bristol">6th Marquess</a> and his son, the <a href="/wiki/John_Hervey,_7th_Marquess_of_Bristol" title="John Hervey, 7th Marquess of Bristol">7th Marquess</a>, sold much of their farmland around Sleaford and Quarrington. In 1989, the Bristol Estates office in Sleaford closed. The result was a boom in house-building, especially in the fields around Quarrington. In the 1980s, hundreds of houses were constructed in Southfields, and developments on Quarrington Hill followed in the late 1990s.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[24]</a></sup> Low house prices and crime rates, and good educational facilities in Sleaford made the new homes attractive.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[25]</a></sup> As the local historian Simon Pawley wrote, "Quarrington&#160;... began to look more like a suburb of Sleaford than a village in its own right".<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[26]</a></sup> In July 2015 planning permission for a further 200 homes between Northfield Road and the A15 was granted by <a href="/wiki/North_Kesteven_District_Council" title="North Kesteven District Council">North Kesteven District Council</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[27]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geography">Geography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Quarrington,_Lincolnshire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Geography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Topography">Topography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Quarrington,_Lincolnshire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Topography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Quarrington_Hill_-_geograph.org.uk_-_283449.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a8/Quarrington_Hill_-_geograph.org.uk_-_283449.jpg/220px-Quarrington_Hill_-_geograph.org.uk_-_283449.jpg" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a8/Quarrington_Hill_-_geograph.org.uk_-_283449.jpg/330px-Quarrington_Hill_-_geograph.org.uk_-_283449.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a8/Quarrington_Hill_-_geograph.org.uk_-_283449.jpg/440px-Quarrington_Hill_-_geograph.org.uk_-_283449.jpg 2x" data-file-width="640" data-file-height="427" /></a>

<p>Quarrington is a settlement south west of Sleaford, a <a href="/wiki/Market_town" title="Market town">market town</a> in the <a href="/wiki/North_Kesteven" title="North Kesteven">North Kesteven</a> district of <a href="/wiki/Lincolnshire" title="Lincolnshire">Lincolnshire</a>. Quarrington proper is clustered around Town Road and between Grantham Road (the B1517) and London Road. To the north of Grantham Road is Quarrington Hill and to the north and east of the old village is New Quarrington; all the settlements are linked by contiguous housing developments to form a suburb of Sleaford.<sup id="cite_ref-:23_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:23-29">[28]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[29]</a></sup> Lincolnshire County Council's <i>State of the Environment Report</i> (1994) found that roughly three-quarters of Lincolnshire is low-lying, with much of it near sea-level;<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[30]</a></sup> Quarrington lies between approximately 15 and 25 meters above sea level, close to <a href="/wiki/Lincoln_Cliff" title="Lincoln Cliff">Lincoln Cliff</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Lincolnshire_limestone" title="Lincolnshire limestone">limestone</a> <a href="/wiki/Escarpment" title="Escarpment">scarp</a> running north–south through <a href="/wiki/Lindsey,_Lincolnshire" title="Lindsey, Lincolnshire">Lindsey</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kesteven" title="Kesteven">Kesteven</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-:2_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:2-32">[31]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-:24_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:24-33">[32]</a></sup> The bedrock under most of Quarrington is Cornbrash limestone belonging to the <a href="/wiki/Great_Oolite_Group" title="Great Oolite Group">Great Oolite Group</a> of <a href="/wiki/Jurassic" title="Jurassic">Jurassic</a> rocks formed 168−165 million years ago.<sup id="cite_ref-:25_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:25-34">[33]</a></sup> The soil belongs to the Quarrington series, a type of brown, calcareous sand.<sup id="cite_ref-:2_32-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:2-32">[31]</a></sup> The county's agricultural land is generally of "very good" quality; as a result, intensive <a href="/wiki/Arable_land" title="Arable land">arable</a> and vegetable farming is predominant while pastoral farming declined in the 20th century.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">[34]</a></sup> Quarrington and Sleaford are on the edge of <a href="/wiki/The_Fens" title="The Fens">the Fens</a>, a low-lying region of the East of England which, before drainage from the 17th to the 20th centuries, was marshy and liable to flooding. Draining has revealed nutrient-rich soils and enabled 88% of the land to be cultivated, especially for arable farming, and most of it is graded amongst the most productive farmland in the country.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">[35]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">[36]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Climate">Climate</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Quarrington,_Lincolnshire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Climate">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>According to the <a href="/wiki/K%C3%B6ppen_classification" class="mw-redirect" title="Köppen classification">Köppen classification</a>, the <a href="/wiki/British_Isles" title="British Isles">British Isles</a> experience a maritime climate characterised by relatively cool summers and mild winters. Lincolnshire's position on the east of the Isles allows for a sunnier and warmer climate relative to the national average, and it is one of the driest counties in the United Kingdom.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">[37]</a></sup> Although it may vary depending on altitude and proximity to the coast, the mean average temperature for the East of England is approximately 9&#160;°C to 10.5&#160;°C; the highest temperature recorded in the region was 37.3&#160;°C at <a href="/wiki/Cavendish,_Suffolk" title="Cavendish, Suffolk">Cavendish</a> on 10 August 2003. On average, the region experiences 30 days of rainfall in winter and 25 in summer, with 15 days of thunder and 6–8 days of hail per year; on 25 August 2001, hail the size of golf balls were reported in Sleaford and other parts of central Lincolnshire. Wind tends to affect the north and west of the country more than the East, and Lincolnshire tends to receive no more than 2 days of gale per year (where gale is a gust of wind at &gt;34&#160;knots, sustained for at least 10 minutes). Despite this, tornadoes form more often in the East of England than elsewhere in the country; Sleaford experienced tornadoes in 2006 and 2012, both of which caused damage to property.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">[38]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">[39]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">[40]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Government_and_politics">Government and politics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Quarrington,_Lincolnshire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Government and politics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Before 1832, Quarrington was in the <a href="/wiki/Lincolnshire_(UK_Parliament_constituency)" title="Lincolnshire (UK Parliament constituency)">Lincolnshire parliamentary constituency</a>, which encompassed all the county except for four boroughs. In the 1818 election, 49 of the 2,000 people living in New and Old Sleaford and Quarrington qualified to vote. In 1832, the <a href="/wiki/Reform_Act_1832" title="Reform Act 1832">Reform Act</a> widened the franchise and divided Lincolnshire. Quarrington was in the <a href="/wiki/South_Lincolnshire_(UK_Parliament_constituency)" title="South Lincolnshire (UK Parliament constituency)">South Lincolnshire constituency</a> that elected two members to parliament.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">[41]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-:9_43-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:9-43">[42]</a></sup> The franchise was widened by the reforms so that roughly 15% (202) of males in Sleaford and Quarrington could vote in 1868.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">[43]</a></sup> The constituency was abolished in 1885 and Quarrington was in the new <a href="/w/index.php?title=North_Kesteven_(UK_Parliament_constituency)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="North Kesteven (UK Parliament constituency) (page does not exist)">North Kesteven constituency</a>. It merged with the <a href="/wiki/Grantham_(UK_Parliament_constituency)" title="Grantham (UK Parliament constituency)">Grantham seat</a> in 1918.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">[44]</a></sup> In 1997, Quarrington was reorganised into <a href="/wiki/Sleaford_and_North_Hykeham_(UK_Parliament_constituency)" title="Sleaford and North Hykeham (UK Parliament constituency)">Sleaford and North Hykeham</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">[45]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Member_of_Parliament#United_Kingdom" class="mw-redirect" title="Member of Parliament">member</a> returned in <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_general_election,_2010" title="United Kingdom general election, 2010">2010</a> for Sleaford and North Hykeham was the <a href="/wiki/Conservative_Party_(UK)" title="Conservative Party (UK)">Conservative</a> candidate <a href="/wiki/Stephen_Phillips_(politician)" title="Stephen Phillips (politician)">Stephen Phillips</a>, who replaced <a href="/wiki/Douglas_Hogg" title="Douglas Hogg">Douglas Hogg</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">[46]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">[47]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Lincolnshire_(European_Parliament_constituency)" title="Lincolnshire (European Parliament constituency)">Lincolnshire</a> elected a Member of the <a href="/wiki/European_Parliament" title="European Parliament">European Parliament</a> from 1974 until 1994,<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">[48]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">[49]</a></sup> and then became part of the <a href="/wiki/Lincolnshire_and_Humberside_South_(European_Parliament_constituency)" title="Lincolnshire and Humberside South (European Parliament constituency)">Lincolnshire and Humberside South</a> constituency until 1999;<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">[50]</a></sup> since then, it has elected members as part of the <a href="/wiki/East_Midlands_(European_Parliament_constituency)" title="East Midlands (European Parliament constituency)">East Midlands constituency</a>; from 1999, there were six members for the East Midlands, but the number was reduced to five in 2009.<sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">[51]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">[52]</a></sup></p>
<p>The ancient parish of Quarrington lay within <a href="/wiki/Kesteven" title="Kesteven">Kesteven</a>'s <a href="/w/index.php?title=Aswardhurn&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Aswardhurn (page does not exist)">Aswardhurn</a> <a href="/wiki/Wapentake" class="mw-redirect" title="Wapentake">wapentake</a>. It was incorporated into Sleaford <a href="/wiki/Poor_Law_Union" class="mw-redirect" title="Poor Law Union">Poor Law Union</a> in 1851. The <a href="/w/index.php?title=Public_Health_Act_1872&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Public Health Act 1872 (page does not exist)">Public Health Act 1872</a> established <a href="/wiki/Urban_sanitary_district" class="mw-redirect" title="Urban sanitary district">urban sanitary districts</a> (USD) and Quarrington became part of the Sleaford USD, which in turn was reorganised into Sleaford Urban District (UD) by the <a href="/wiki/Local_Government_Act_1894" title="Local Government Act 1894">Local Government Act 1894</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-:1_54-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-54">[53]</a></sup> Sleaford UD was abolished by the <a href="/wiki/Local_Government_Act_1972" title="Local Government Act 1972">Local Government Act 1972</a> and, by statutory instrument, Sleaford civil parish became its <a href="/wiki/Successor_parish" title="Successor parish">successor</a>, thus merging Quarrington, New Sleaford, Old Sleaford and Holdingham civil parishes.<sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">[54]</a></sup> Subsequently, it has been served by Sleaford Town Council,<sup id="cite_ref-56" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-56">[55]</a></sup> North Kesteven District Council and <a href="/wiki/Lincolnshire_County_Council" title="Lincolnshire County Council">Lincolnshire County Council</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-57" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-57">[56]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Economy">Economy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Quarrington,_Lincolnshire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Economy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1831, more than 60% of Quarrington's adult males worked in agriculture and more than a quarter were employed in retail and handicraft industries;<sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58">[57]</a></sup> more than half were labourers and slightly more than a quarter were characterised as "middle sorts".<sup id="cite_ref-59" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-59">[58]</a></sup> Sleaford had a weekly market throughout the 19th century and a livestock market on Northgate from 1874 until 1984.<sup id="cite_ref-:23_29-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:23-29">[28]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-60" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-60">[59]</a></sup> At the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_Census_2011" title="United Kingdom Census 2011">2011 Census</a>, the largest <a href="/wiki/Social_class" title="Social class">socio-economic grouping</a> in the census's Quarrington and Mareham ward is workers in lower-tier managerial or administrative roles (25.9%), followed by intermediate (14.7%), semi-routine (14.6%) and higher managerial (10.9%) occupations; no other group comprised 10% or more. In terms of industry, the most common, based on those working in the sector, are wholesale and retail trade (including automotive repairs) at 15.5%, public administration and defence (15.4%), human health and social work (14.3%) and manufacturing (10.0%), with no other groups representing 10% or more. The largest group of working-age persons by economic activity are those in full-time employment, who make up 47.5% of this section of the population while 15.4% are part-time employees and 7.7% are self-employed; 13.7% of the working-age population are retired. 2.9% were unemployed, with approximately 38.0% of those in long-term unemployment and roughly 27.5% aged 16 to 24.<sup id="cite_ref-61" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-61">[60]</a></sup> An unemployment survey of Lincolnshire in 2014 found that the county experienced a decline in unemployment (based on <a href="/wiki/Jobseekers_Allowance" class="mw-redirect" title="Jobseekers Allowance">Jobseekers Allowance</a> claimants) by 29% over the preceding 12 months, while the county's unemployment rate was marginally below the national average.<sup id="cite_ref-62" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-62">[61]</a></sup></p>

<p>At the 2011 Census, Sleaford Quarrington and Mareham ward had an estimated total population of 7,046, which accounts for roughly 6.5% of the total population of North Kesteven.<sup id="cite_ref-64" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-64">[63]</a></sup> After Quarrington civil parish was abolished in 1974, the population statistics for the new Sleaford parish which absorbed it were divided into four wards, which did not necessarily correspond to the former boundaries of Quarrington CP.<sup id="cite_ref-65" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-65">[n 2]</a></sup> The town's population (including Quarrington) grew by 39% between 1991 and 2001, the fastest growth rate of any town in Lincolnshire;<sup id="cite_ref-:12_66-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:12-66">[64]</a></sup> this has been attributed in part to the developments around Quarrington since the 1980s<sup id="cite_ref-67" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-67">[65]</a></sup> and a joint planning strategy report found that "This growth has largely been the result of people moving to the area attracted by the quality of life, low crime rates, relatively low house prices and good-quality education."<sup id="cite_ref-:22_68-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:22-68">[66]</a></sup> The district population is predicted to rise by a further 29% between 2008 and 2033, compared with a national average of 18%;<sup id="cite_ref-:12_66-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:12-66">[64]</a></sup> in 2013, county councillors approved plans to build 4,500 new homes.<sup id="cite_ref-:11_69-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:11-69">[67]</a></sup></p>
<p>The 2011 Census revealed that approximately 94.3% of Quarrington and Mareham ward's resident population were White British; the second largest ethnic group was White (other) at 3.0%, then Asian (including Asian British) at 0.7%, followed by White Irish at approximately 0.6%, Black, Afro-Caribbean and Black British at 0.2% and Arab at 0.1%; no other ethnic group represented 1% or more of the population. 88.0% of residents were born in England and 4.9% in other parts of the United Kingdom; 4.0% were from EU countries, with 2.0% coming from EU member states which joined after March 2001.<sup id="cite_ref-www.neighbourhood.statistics.gov.uk_LeadDatasetList_70-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-www.neighbourhood.statistics.gov.uk_LeadDatasetList-70">[68]</a></sup> Most people in the Quarrington and Mareham ward identified as <a href="/wiki/Christianity" title="Christianity">Christian</a>. 72.5% of the residents stated they were Christian and 26.5% registered as "no religion" or chose not to state any; no other group constituted more than 1% of the population.<sup id="cite_ref-www.neighbourhood.statistics.gov.uk_LeadDatasetList_70-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-www.neighbourhood.statistics.gov.uk_LeadDatasetList-70">[68]</a></sup></p>
<p>Between December 2013 and November 2014, 1,289 criminal acts were reported in the policing neighbourhood of Sleaford Town (which includes Quarrington), of which 43.9% were classed as anti-social behaviour, making it the largest portion of reported crimes.<sup id="cite_ref-71" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-71">[69]</a></sup> In 2010, recorded crime levels were amongst the lowest in the country and, for the year ending June 2014, the crime rate in the North Kesteven district is the lowest in Lincolnshire at 24.38 crimes per thousand residents.<sup id="cite_ref-:22_68-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:22-68">[66]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-72" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-72">[70]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Religion">Religion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Quarrington,_Lincolnshire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Religion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In the 17th century, the <a href="/wiki/Rector_(ecclesiastical)" title="Rector (ecclesiastical)">rectory</a> of Quarrington and the <a href="/wiki/Vicar" title="Vicar">vicarage</a> of Old Sleaford combined to form the <a href="/wiki/Ecclesiastical_parish" class="mw-redirect" title="Ecclesiastical parish">ecclesiastical parish</a> of Quarrington with Old Sleaford.<sup id="cite_ref-Trollope_Sleaford_pp._184.E2.80.93185_73-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Trollope_Sleaford_pp._184.E2.80.93185-73">[71]</a></sup> The parish boundaries of Quarrington with Old Sleaford were last altered in 1928.<sup id="cite_ref-74" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-74">[72]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-75" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-75">[73]</a></sup> The rectory is in the <a href="/wiki/Diocese_of_Lincoln" title="Diocese of Lincoln">Diocese</a> and Archdeaconry of Lincoln and has been in the <a href="/wiki/Deanery_of_Lafford" title="Deanery of Lafford">Lafford Rural Deanery</a> since 1968;<sup id="cite_ref-76" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-76">[74]</a></sup> the current rector of Quarrington is the Rev Sandra Benham<sup id="cite_ref-77" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-77">[75]</a></sup> and the patrons are alternatively the Bishop of Lincoln and <a href="/wiki/Tollemache_baronets" title="Tollemache baronets">Sir Lyonel Tollemache, 7th Baronet</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-:4_78-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:4-78">[76]</a></sup></p>
<p>The grade II* listed <a href="/wiki/St_Botolph%27s_Church,_Quarrington" title="St Botolph's Church, Quarrington">St Botolph's Church</a> serves Quarrington's <a href="/wiki/Church_of_England" title="Church of England">Anglican</a> community. Its 13th-century north arcade is the oldest part of the church. The tower and spire date to the following century and St Botolph's listing reflects the "excellent" 14th century <a href="/wiki/Tracery" title="Tracery">tracery</a> in two of its windows. The <a href="/wiki/Chancel" title="Chancel">chancel</a> was rebuilt when Charles Kirk restored the church in 1862−3 and an extension was completed in 2001.<sup id="cite_ref-79" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-79">[77]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-80" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-80">[78]</a></sup> The church had 120 sittings at the Census of Religious Worship in 1851; there were 20 morning attendees, 40 who came to the church in the afternoon and 20 <a href="/wiki/Sunday_school" title="Sunday school">Sunday scholars</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-81" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-81">[79]</a></sup> As of 2015, family services are held on the first Sunday of the month at 11:00am and Holy Communion is carried out every second, third and fourth Sunday at 11:00am and every Wednesday at 10:00am.<sup id="cite_ref-:4_78-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:4-78">[76]</a></sup> In the early 1900s a second church was designed to be built on donated land in the parish but closer to Sleaford. Disruption during <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a>, parish boundary changes in 1928 and rising costs delayed the plans. Instead, a church hall was built in 1932 on Grantham Road and is now used as a community centre. The current rectory was constructed c. 2000 and a curate's house of a similar age was being rented by the Church of England in 2009.<sup id="cite_ref-82" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-82">[80]</a></sup></p>

<p>Holdingham roundabout connects the <a href="/wiki/A17_road_(England)" title="A17 road (England)">A17</a> to the <a href="/wiki/A15_road_(England)" title="A15 road (England)">A15 road</a> from <a href="/wiki/Peterborough" title="Peterborough">Peterborough</a> to <a href="/wiki/Scawby" title="Scawby">Scawby</a>. The A15 passed through Quarrington and Sleaford until 1993 when its bypass was completed.<sup id="cite_ref-83" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-83">[81]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-84" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-84">[82]</a></sup> The main route through the village is Grantham Road (B1517) which connects the A15 to Southgate in Sleaford. London Road forks from it after the railway crossing in Sleaford and continues along the eastern edge of Quarrington to Silk Willougby and the A15.<sup id="cite_ref-:14_85-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:14-85">[83]</a></sup></p>
<p>The nearest railway stations are at <a href="/wiki/Sleaford_railway_station" title="Sleaford railway station">Sleaford</a> and <a href="/wiki/Rauceby_railway_station" title="Rauceby railway station">Rauceby</a>. The railways arrived in the 19th century when plans to build the Boston, Sleaford and Midland Counties Railway were sanctioned by <a href="/wiki/Act_of_Parliament" title="Act of Parliament">Act of Parliament</a> 1853. The line from Grantham opened in 1857; <a href="/wiki/Boston" title="Boston">Boston</a> was connected in 1859, <a href="/wiki/Bourne,_Lincolnshire" title="Bourne, Lincolnshire">Bourne</a> in 1871 and <a href="/wiki/Ruskington" title="Ruskington">Ruskington</a> on <a href="/wiki/Great_Northern_and_Great_Eastern_Joint_Railway" title="Great Northern and Great Eastern Joint Railway">Great Northern and Great Eastern Joint Railway</a> in 1882.<sup id="cite_ref-:20_86-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:20-86">[84]</a></sup> As of 2015, Sleaford is a stop on the <a href="/wiki/Peterborough_to_Lincoln_Line" class="mw-redirect" title="Peterborough to Lincoln Line">Peterborough to Lincoln Line</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Poacher_Line" title="Poacher Line">Poacher Line</a>, from Grantham to Skegness;<sup id="cite_ref-87" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-87">[85]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-88" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-88">[86]</a></sup> Rauceby is a stop on the Poacher line only.<sup id="cite_ref-89" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-89">[87]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Grantham_railway_station" title="Grantham railway station">Grantham</a>, roughly 14 miles (23&#160;km) away by road and two stops from Sleaford (and one from Rauceby) on the Poacher Line, is a major stop on the <a href="/wiki/East_Coast_Main_Line" title="East Coast Main Line">East Coast Main Line</a>. Trains from Grantham to London <a href="/wiki/London_King%27s_Cross_railway_station" title="London King's Cross railway station">King's Cross</a> take approximately 1 hour 15 minutes.<sup id="cite_ref-90" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-90">[88]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-91" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-91">[89]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Education">Education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Quarrington,_Lincolnshire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The 1833 <a href="/wiki/House_of_Commons_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="House of Commons of the United Kingdom">House of Commons</a> enquiry into education in England found that Quarrington had no schools<sup id="cite_ref-92" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-92">[90]</a></sup> and still had none in the 1860s when New Quarrington was emerging. The lack of school and an accessible church became a cause for concern. The Marquess of Bristol gave an acre of land on the western edge of New Quarrington, between London and Grantham Roads, on which to build a school.<sup id="cite_ref-93" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-93">[91]</a></sup> Sleaford architect Charles Kirk constructed the school and master's house at his own expense in 1867 for "to provide a place where the adults and children only of the labouring and other poorer classes of Quarrington and Old Sleaford may be instructed in the catechism and doctrine and worship of the Church of England".<sup id="cite_ref-94" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-94">[92]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-:0_95-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-95">[93]</a></sup> The school operated along the <a href="/wiki/National_school_(England_and_Wales)" title="National school (England and Wales)">National Society's</a> recommended lines, with two teachers and 65 pupils on roll in 1870. The school could accommodate c. 100 children and had an attached chancel.<sup id="cite_ref-96" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-96">[94]</a></sup> The school was extended in 1898, the 1960s and 1980s.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_95-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-95">[93]</a></sup> The successor, St Botolph's Church of England School, moved out of the old premises into new accommodation in 2002.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_95-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-95">[93]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-97" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-97">[95]</a></sup> As of 2015, the school is a <a href="/wiki/Voluntary_controlled_school" title="Voluntary controlled school">voluntary controlled</a> mixed <a href="/wiki/Primary_school" title="Primary school">primary school</a> admitting juniors and infants with 394 pupils on roll, and was rated "good" by <a href="/wiki/Ofsted" title="Ofsted">Ofsted</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-98" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-98">[96]</a></sup></p>
<p>Sleaford is served by three <a href="/wiki/Secondary_schools" class="mw-redirect" title="Secondary schools">secondary schools</a> with sixth forms: <a href="/wiki/Carre%27s_Grammar_School" title="Carre's Grammar School">Carre's Grammar School</a>, a boys' <a href="/wiki/Grammar_school" title="Grammar school">grammar school</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kesteven_and_Sleaford_High_School" title="Kesteven and Sleaford High School">Kesteven and Sleaford High School</a>, a girls' grammar school, and <a href="/wiki/St_George%27s_Academy" title="St George's Academy">St George's Academy</a>, a mixed <a href="/wiki/Comprehensive_school" title="Comprehensive school">comprehensive school</a>. The grammar schools are selective and pupils are required to pass the <a href="/wiki/Eleven_plus_exam" class="mw-redirect" title="Eleven plus exam">Eleven plus exam</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-99" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-99">[97]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-100" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-100">[98]</a></sup> St George's is not selective.<sup id="cite_ref-101" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-101">[99]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Mixed-sex_education" title="Mixed-sex education">co-educational</a> <a href="/wiki/Sleaford_Joint_Sixth_Form" title="Sleaford Joint Sixth Form">Joint Sixth Form</a> consortium between Carre's and St George's allows pupils to choose subjects taught at either school.<sup id="cite_ref-102" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-102">[100]</a></sup></p>
