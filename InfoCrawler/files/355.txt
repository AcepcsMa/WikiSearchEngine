			<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Imperial Diet (Holy Roman Empire)</h1>
									<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
									<div id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
								<div id="contentSub"></div>
												<div id="jump-to-nav" class="mw-jump">

<p>The <b>Imperial Diet</b> (<a href="/wiki/Latin_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Latin language">Latin</a>: <span lang="la" xml:lang="la"><i>Dieta Imperii</i> or <i>Comitium Imperiale</i></span>; <a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a>: <span lang="de" xml:lang="de"><i>Reichstag</i></span>) was the deliberative and legislative body of the <a href="/wiki/Holy_Roman_Empire" title="Holy Roman Empire">Holy Roman Empire</a>. Its members were the <a href="/wiki/Imperial_Estate" title="Imperial Estate">Imperial Estates</a>, divided into three colleges. The <a href="/wiki/Diet_(assembly)" title="Diet (assembly)">diet</a> as a permanent, regularized institution evolved from the <i><a href="/wiki/Hoftag" title="Hoftag">Hoftage</a></i> (court assemblies) of the <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">Middle Ages</a>. From 1663 until the end of the empire in 1806, it was in <a href="/wiki/Perpetual_Diet_of_Regensburg" title="Perpetual Diet of Regensburg">permanent session</a> at <a href="/wiki/Regensburg" title="Regensburg">Regensburg</a>.</p>
<p>The Imperial Estates had, according to <a href="/wiki/Feudalism" title="Feudalism">feudal law</a>, no authority above them besides the <a href="/wiki/Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Holy Roman Emperor">Holy Roman Emperor</a> (or emperor-elect) himself. The holding of an Imperial Estate entitled one to a vote in the diet. Thus, an individual member might have multiple votes and votes in different colleges. In general, members did not attend the permanent diet at Regensburg, but sent representatives instead. The late imperial diet was in effect a permanent meeting of ambassadors between the Estates.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Imperial_Diet_(Holy_Roman_Empire)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The precise role and function of the Imperial Diet changed over the centuries, as did the Empire itself, in that the estates and separate territories gained more and more control of their own affairs at the expense of imperial power. Initially, there was neither a fixed time nor location for the Diet. It started as a convention of the <a href="/wiki/Duke" title="Duke">dukes</a> of the old <a href="/wiki/Germanic_peoples" title="Germanic peoples">Germanic</a> tribes that formed the <a href="/wiki/Francia" title="Francia">Frankish kingdom</a> when important decisions had to be made, and was probably based on the old Germanic law whereby each leader relied on the support of his leading men. For example, already under Emperor <a href="/wiki/Charlemagne" title="Charlemagne">Charlemagne</a> during the <a href="/wiki/Saxon_Wars" title="Saxon Wars">Saxon Wars</a>, the Diet, according to the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Frankish_Annals" title="Royal Frankish Annals">Royal Frankish Annals</a>, met at <a href="/wiki/Paderborn" title="Paderborn">Paderborn</a> in 777 officially determined laws concerning the subdued <a href="/wiki/Duchy_of_Saxony" title="Duchy of Saxony">Saxons</a> and other tribes. In 803, the Frankish emperor issued the final version of the <a href="/wiki/Lex_Saxonum" title="Lex Saxonum">Lex Saxonum</a>.</p>
<p>At the Diet of 919 in <a href="/wiki/Fritzlar" title="Fritzlar">Fritzlar</a> the dukes elected the first <a href="/wiki/List_of_German_monarchs" title="List of German monarchs">King of the Germans</a>, who was a Saxon, <a href="/wiki/Henry_the_Fowler" title="Henry the Fowler">Henry the Fowler</a>, thus overcoming the longstanding rivalry between Franks and Saxons and laying the foundation for the German realm. After the conquest of <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Italy_(medieval)" class="mw-redirect" title="Kingdom of Italy (medieval)">Italy</a>, the 1158 <a href="/wiki/Diet_of_Roncaglia" title="Diet of Roncaglia">Diet of Roncaglia</a> finalized four laws that would significantly alter the (never formally written) <a href="/wiki/Constitution" title="Constitution">constitution</a> of the Empire, marking the beginning of the steady decline of the central power in favour of the local dukes. The <a href="/wiki/Golden_Bull_of_1356" title="Golden Bull of 1356">Golden Bull of 1356</a> cemented the concept of "territorial rule" (<i>Landesherrschaft</i>), the largely independent rule of the dukes over their respective territories, and also limited the number of electors to seven. The Pope, contrary to modern myth, was never involved in the electoral process but only in the process of ratification and coronation of whomever the Prince-Electors chose.</p>

<p>However, until the late 15th century, the Diet was not actually formalized as an institution. Instead, the dukes and other princes would irregularly convene at the court of the Emperor; these assemblies were usually referred to as <i><a href="/wiki/Hoftag" title="Hoftag">Hoftage</a></i> (from German <i>Hof</i> "court"). Only beginning in 1489 was the Diet called the <i>Reichstag</i>, and it was formally divided into several <i>collegia</i> ("colleges"). Initially, the two colleges were that of the <a href="/wiki/Prince-elector" title="Prince-elector">prince-electors</a> and that of the other dukes and princes. Later, the <a href="/wiki/Free_imperial_city" title="Free imperial city">imperial cities</a>, that is, cities that had <a href="/wiki/Imperial_immediacy" title="Imperial immediacy">Imperial immediacy</a> and were oligarchic republics independent of a local ruler that were subject only to the Emperor himself, managed to be accepted as a third party.</p>
<p>Several attempts to reform the Empire and end its slow disintegration, notably starting with the <a href="/wiki/Diet_of_1495" class="mw-redirect" title="Diet of 1495">Diet of 1495</a>, did not have much effect. In contrast, this process was only hastened with the <a href="/wiki/Peace_of_Westphalia" title="Peace of Westphalia">Peace of Westphalia</a> of 1648, which formally bound the Emperor to accept all decisions made by the Diet, in effect depriving him of his few remaining powers. From then to its end in 1806, the Empire was not much more than a collection of largely independent states.</p>
<p>Probably the most famous Diets were those held in <a href="/wiki/Diet_of_Worms" title="Diet of Worms">Worms</a> in 1495, where the <a href="/wiki/Imperial_Reform" title="Imperial Reform">Imperial Reform</a> was enacted, and <a href="/wiki/Diet_of_Worms" title="Diet of Worms">1521</a>, where <a href="/wiki/Martin_Luther" title="Martin Luther">Martin Luther</a> was banned (see <a href="/wiki/Edict_of_Worms" class="mw-redirect" title="Edict of Worms">Edict of Worms</a>), the Diets of <a href="/wiki/Speyer" title="Speyer">Speyer</a> <a href="/wiki/Diet_of_Speyer_(1526)" title="Diet of Speyer (1526)">1526</a> and <a href="/wiki/Diet_of_Speyer_(1529)" title="Diet of Speyer (1529)">1529</a> (see <a href="/wiki/Protestation_at_Speyer" title="Protestation at Speyer">Protestation at Speyer</a>), and several in <a href="/wiki/Nuremberg" title="Nuremberg">Nuremberg</a> (<a href="/wiki/Diet_of_Nuremberg" title="Diet of Nuremberg">Diet of Nuremberg</a>). Only with the introduction of the <a href="/wiki/Perpetual_Diet_of_Regensburg" title="Perpetual Diet of Regensburg">Perpetual Diet of Regensburg</a> in 1663 did the Diet permanently convene in a fixed location.</p>
<p>The Imperial Diet of Constance opened on 27 April 1507;<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> it recognized the unity of the Holy Roman Empire and founded the Imperial Chamber, the empire’s supreme court.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Participants">Participants</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Imperial_Diet_(Holy_Roman_Empire)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Participants">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Since 1489, the Diet comprised three colleges:</p>

<p>The <a href="/wiki/Electoral_college" title="Electoral college">Electoral college</a> (<i>Kurfürstenrat</i>), led by the <a href="/wiki/Electorate_of_Mainz" title="Electorate of Mainz">Prince-Archbishop of Mainz</a> in his capacity as <a href="/wiki/Archchancellor" title="Archchancellor">Archchancellor</a> of <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Germany" title="Kingdom of Germany">Germany</a>. The seven Prince-electors were designated by the Golden Bull of 1356:</p>
<ul>
<li>three ecclesiastical <a href="/wiki/Prince-Bishop" class="mw-redirect" title="Prince-Bishop">Prince-Bishops</a>,
<ul>
<li>the Prince-Archbishop of Mainz as Archchancellor of Germany</li>
<li>the <a href="/wiki/Electorate_of_Cologne" title="Electorate of Cologne">Prince-Archbishop of Cologne</a> as Archchancellor of <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Italy" title="Kingdom of Italy">Italy</a></li>
<li>the <a href="/wiki/Electorate_of_Trier" title="Electorate of Trier">Prince-Archbishop of Trier</a> as Archchancellor of <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Burgundy" title="Kingdom of Burgundy">Burgundy</a></li>
</ul>
</li>
<li>four secular Princes,
<ul>
<li>the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Bohemia" title="Kingdom of Bohemia">King of Bohemia</a> as <a href="/wiki/Cup-bearer" title="Cup-bearer">Archcupbearer</a></li>
<li>the <a href="/wiki/Electorate_of_the_Palatinate" class="mw-redirect" title="Electorate of the Palatinate">Elector of the Palatinate</a> as <a href="/wiki/Steward_(office)" title="Steward (office)">Archsteward</a> (<i>Erztruchsess</i>)</li>
<li>the <a href="/wiki/Electorate_of_Saxony" title="Electorate of Saxony">Elector of Saxony</a> as <a href="/wiki/Marshal" title="Marshal">Archmarshal</a></li>
<li>the <a href="/wiki/Margraviate_of_Brandenburg" title="Margraviate of Brandenburg">Margrave of Brandenburg</a> as <a href="/wiki/Chamberlain_(office)" title="Chamberlain (office)">Archchamberlain</a></li>
</ul>
</li>
</ul>
<p>The number increased to eight, when in 1623 the <a href="/wiki/Duchy_of_Bavaria" title="Duchy of Bavaria">Duke of Bavaria</a> took over the electoral dignity of the Count Palatine, who himself received a separate vote in the electoral college according to the 1648 <a href="/wiki/Peace_of_Westphalia" title="Peace of Westphalia">Peace of Westphalia</a> (<i>Causa Palatina</i>), including the high office of an <a href="/wiki/Treasurer" title="Treasurer">Archtreasurer</a>. In 1692 the <a href="/wiki/Electorate_of_Brunswick-L%C3%BCneburg" title="Electorate of Brunswick-Lüneburg">Elector of Brunswick-Lüneburg</a> (Hannover) became the ninth Prince-elector as Archbannerbearer during the <a href="/wiki/Nine_Years%27_War" title="Nine Years' War">Nine Years' War</a>.</p>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/War_of_the_Bavarian_Succession" title="War of the Bavarian Succession">War of the Bavarian Succession</a>, the electoral dignities of the Palatinate and <a href="/wiki/Electorate_of_Bavaria" title="Electorate of Bavaria">Bavaria</a> were merged, approved by the 1779 <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Teschen" title="Treaty of Teschen">Treaty of Teschen</a>. The <a href="/wiki/German_Mediatisation" class="mw-redirect" title="German Mediatisation">German Mediatisation</a> of 1803 entailed the dissolution of the Cologne and Trier Prince-archbishoprics, the Prince-Archbishop of Mainz and German Archchancellor received—as compensation for his lost territory occupied by <a href="/wiki/French_First_Republic" title="French First Republic">Revolutionary France</a>—the newly established <a href="/wiki/Principality_of_Regensburg" title="Principality of Regensburg">Principality of Regensburg</a>. In turn, four secular princes were elevated to prince-electors:</p>
<ul>
<li>the <a href="/wiki/Electorate_of_Salzburg" title="Electorate of Salzburg">Duke of Salzburg</a></li>
<li>the <a href="/wiki/Electorate_of_W%C3%BCrttemberg" title="Electorate of Württemberg">Duke of Württemberg</a></li>
<li>the <a href="/wiki/Electorate_of_Baden" title="Electorate of Baden">Margrave of Baden</a></li>
<li>the <a href="/wiki/Electorate_of_Hesse" title="Electorate of Hesse">Landgrave of Hesse-Kassel</a></li>
</ul>
<p>These changes however had little effect, as with the abdication of <a href="/wiki/Francis_II,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Francis II, Holy Roman Emperor">Francis II</a> as Holy Roman Emperor the Empire was dissolved only three years later.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Princes">Princes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Imperial_Diet_(Holy_Roman_Empire)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Princes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The college of <a href="/wiki/Princes_of_the_Holy_Roman_Empire" title="Princes of the Holy Roman Empire">Imperial Princes</a> (<i>Reichsfürstenrat</i> or <i>Fürstenbank</i>) incorporated the <a href="/wiki/Imperial_Count" title="Imperial Count">Imperial Counts</a> as well as <a href="/wiki/Imperial_immediacy" title="Imperial immediacy">immediate</a> lords, <a href="/wiki/Prince-Bishop" class="mw-redirect" title="Prince-Bishop">Prince-Bishops</a> and <a href="/wiki/List_of_Imperial_abbeys" title="List of Imperial abbeys">Imperial abbots</a>. Strong in members, though often discordant, the second college tried to preserve its interests against the dominance of the Prince-electors.</p>
<p>The House of Princes was again subdivided into an ecclesiastical and a secular bench. Remarkably, the ecclesiastical bench was headed by the—secular—<a href="/wiki/Archduchy_of_Austria" title="Archduchy of Austria">Archduke of Austria</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Burgundian_Netherlands" title="Burgundian Netherlands">Burgundian</a> duke of the <a href="/wiki/Habsburg_Netherlands" title="Habsburg Netherlands">Habsburg Netherlands</a> (held by <a href="/wiki/Habsburg_Spain" title="Habsburg Spain">Habsburg Spain</a> from 1556). As the Austrian <a href="/wiki/House_of_Habsburg" title="House of Habsburg">House of Habsburg</a> had failed to assume the leadership of the secular bench, they received the guidance over the ecclesiastical princes instead. The first ecclesiastical prince was the <a href="/wiki/Archbishopric_of_Salzburg" title="Archbishopric of Salzburg">Archbishop of Salzburg</a> as <i><a href="/wiki/Primas_Germaniae" title="Primas Germaniae">Primas Germaniae</a></i>; the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic_Archdiocese_of_Besan%C3%A7on" title="Roman Catholic Archdiocese of Besançon">Prince-Archbishop of Besançon</a>, though officially a member until the 1678 <a href="/wiki/Treaties_of_Nijmegen" title="Treaties of Nijmegen">Treaty of Nijmegen</a>, did not attend the Diet's meetings.</p>
<p>The ecclesiastical bench also comprised the <a href="/wiki/Grand_Master_(order)" title="Grand Master (order)">Grand Master</a> and <i>Deutschmeister</i> of the <a href="/wiki/Teutonic_Knights" class="mw-redirect" title="Teutonic Knights">Teutonic Knights</a>, as well as the <a href="/wiki/Prior" title="Prior">Grand Prior</a> of the Monastic State of the <a href="/wiki/Knights_Hospitaller" title="Knights Hospitaller">Knights Hospitaller</a> at <a href="/wiki/Heitersheim" title="Heitersheim">Heitersheim</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Bishopric_of_L%C3%BCbeck" title="Bishopric of Lübeck">Prince-Bishopric of Lübeck</a> remained an ecclesiastical member even after it had turned <a href="/wiki/Protestant_Reformation" class="mw-redirect" title="Protestant Reformation">Protestant</a>, ruled by <a href="/wiki/Diocesan_administrator" title="Diocesan administrator">diocesan administrators</a> from the <a href="/wiki/Duke_of_Holstein-Gottorp" title="Duke of Holstein-Gottorp">House of Holstein-Gottorp</a> from 1586. The <a href="/wiki/Prince-Bishopric_of_Osnabr%C3%BCck" title="Prince-Bishopric of Osnabrück">Prince-Bishopric of Osnabrück</a>, according to the 1648 Peace of Westphalia was under alternating rule of a <a href="/wiki/Catholic_Church" title="Catholic Church">Catholic</a> bishop and a Lutheran bishop from the House of Brunswick-Lüneburg.</p>
<p>Each member of the Princes' College held either a single vote (<i>Virilstimme</i>) or a collective vote (<i>Kuriatstimme</i>). Due to the Princes, their single vote from 1582 strictly depended on their immediate fiefs; this principle led to an accumulation of votes, when one ruler held several territories in <a href="/wiki/Personal_union" title="Personal union">personal union</a>. Counts and Lords only were entitled to collective votes, they therefore formed separate colleges like the <a href="/wiki/Wetterau_Association_of_Imperial_Counts" title="Wetterau Association of Imperial Counts">Wetterau Association of Imperial Counts</a> and mergers within the <a href="/wiki/Swabian_Circle" class="mw-redirect" title="Swabian Circle">Swabian</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Franconian_Circle" title="Franconian Circle">Franconian</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Lower_Rhenish%E2%80%93Westphalian_Circle" title="Lower Rhenish–Westphalian Circle">Lower Rhenish–Westphalian Circles</a>. Likewise, on the ecclesiastical bench, the Imperial abbots joined a Swabian or <a href="/wiki/Rhine" title="Rhine">Rhenish</a> college.</p>
<p>In the German Mediatisation of 1803, numerous ecclesiastical territories were annexed by secular estates. A reform of the Princes' college was however not carried out until the Empire's dissolution in 1806.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cities">Cities</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Imperial_Diet_(Holy_Roman_Empire)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Cities">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The college of <a href="/wiki/Free_imperial_city" title="Free imperial city">Imperial Cities</a> (<i>Reichsstädtekollegium</i>) evolved from 1489 onwards, it contributed greatly to the development of the Imperial Diets as a political institution. Nevertheless, the collective vote of the cities initially was of inferior importance until a 1582 <a href="/wiki/Recess_(Holy_Roman_Empire)" title="Recess (Holy Roman Empire)">Recess</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Diet_of_Augsburg" title="Diet of Augsburg">Augsburg Diet</a>. The college was led by the city council of the actual venue; with the implementation of the Perpetual Diet in 1663, the chair passed to <a href="/wiki/Regensburg" title="Regensburg">Regensburg</a>.</p>
<p>The Imperial cities also divided into a Swabian and Rhenish bench. The Swabian cities were led by <a href="/wiki/Nuremberg" title="Nuremberg">Nuremberg</a>, <a href="/wiki/Augsburg" title="Augsburg">Augsburg</a> and Regensburg, the Rhenish cities by <a href="/wiki/Cologne" title="Cologne">Cologne</a>, <a href="/wiki/Aachen" title="Aachen">Aachen</a> and <a href="/wiki/Frankfurt" title="Frankfurt">Frankfurt</a>.</p>
<p>For a complete list of members of the Imperial Diet from 1792, near the end of the Empire, see <a href="/wiki/List_of_Reichstag_participants_(1792)" class="mw-redirect" title="List of Reichstag participants (1792)">List of Reichstag participants (1792)</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Religious_bodies">Religious bodies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Imperial_Diet_(Holy_Roman_Empire)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Religious bodies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>After the <a href="/wiki/Peace_of_Westphalia" title="Peace of Westphalia">Peace of Westphalia</a>, religious matters could no longer be decided by a majority vote of the colleges. Instead, the Reichstag would separate into Catholic and Protestant bodies, which would discuss the matter separately and then negotiate an agreement with each other.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> The Catholic body, or <i>corpus catholicum</i>, was headed by the Archbishop-Elector of <a href="/wiki/Electorate_of_Mainz" title="Electorate of Mainz">Mainz</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-kalipke_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kalipke-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Protestant body, or <i><a href="/wiki/Corpus_evangelicorum" class="mw-redirect" title="Corpus evangelicorum">corpus evangelicorum</a></i>, was headed by the Elector of <a href="/wiki/Electorate_of_Saxony" title="Electorate of Saxony">Saxony</a>. At meetings of the Protestant body, Saxony would introduce each topic of discussion, after which <a href="/wiki/Brandenburg-Prussia" title="Brandenburg-Prussia">Brandenburg-Prussia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Electorate_of_Brunswick-Luneburg" class="mw-redirect" title="Electorate of Brunswick-Luneburg">Hanover</a> would speak, followed by the remaining states in order of size. When all the states had spoken, Saxony would weigh the votes and announce a consensus.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Augustus_II_the_Strong" title="Augustus II the Strong">Frederick Augustus I, Elector of Saxony</a> converted to Catholicism in 1697 in order to become King of Poland, but the Electorate itself remained officially Protestant and retained the directorship of the Protestant body. When <a href="/wiki/Augustus_III_of_Poland" title="Augustus III of Poland">the Elector's son</a> also converted to Catholicism, Prussia and Hanover attempted to take over the directorship in 1717–1720, but without success. The Electors of Saxony would head the Protestant body until the end of the Holy Roman Empire.<sup id="cite_ref-kalipke_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kalipke-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Collection_of_records">Collection of records</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Imperial_Diet_(Holy_Roman_Empire)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Collection of records">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>After the formation of the new <a href="/wiki/German_Empire" title="German Empire">German Empire</a> in 1871, the Historical Commission of the <a href="/wiki/Bavarian_Academy_of_Sciences" class="mw-redirect" title="Bavarian Academy of Sciences">Bavarian Academy of Sciences</a> started to collect imperial records (<i>Reichsakten</i>) and imperial diet records (<i>Reichstagsakten</i>). In 1893 the commission published the first volume. At present the years 1524–1527 and years up to 1544 are being collected and researched. A volume dealing with the 1532 Diet of Regensburg, including the peace negotiations with the <a href="/wiki/Protestants" class="mw-redirect" title="Protestants">Protestants</a> in <a href="/wiki/Schweinfurt" title="Schweinfurt">Schweinfurt</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nuremberg" title="Nuremberg">Nuremberg</a>, by <a href="/w/index.php?title=Rosemarie_Aulinger&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Rosemarie Aulinger (page does not exist)">Rosemarie Aulinger</a> of <a href="/wiki/Vienna" title="Vienna">Vienna</a> was published in 1992.</p>
