			<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Imperial immediacy</h1>
									<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
									<div id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
								<div id="contentSub"></div>
												<div id="jump-to-nav" class="mw-jump">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>
				</div>
				<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Marchtal_Immediate_and_Exempt_1748.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b4/Marchtal_Immediate_and_Exempt_1748.jpg/220px-Marchtal_Immediate_and_Exempt_1748.jpg" width="220" height="59" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b4/Marchtal_Immediate_and_Exempt_1748.jpg/330px-Marchtal_Immediate_and_Exempt_1748.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b4/Marchtal_Immediate_and_Exempt_1748.jpg/440px-Marchtal_Immediate_and_Exempt_1748.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2026" data-file-height="544" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Marchtal_Immediate_and_Exempt_1748.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Document signed by the <a href="/wiki/Marchtal_Abbey" title="Marchtal Abbey">Abbot of Marchtal</a>, "immediate and exempt"</div>
</div>
</div>
<p><b>Imperial immediacy</b> (<a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a>: <span lang="de" xml:lang="de"><i>Reichsfreiheit</i></span> or <i><span lang="de" xml:lang="de">Reichsunmittelbarkeit</span></i>) was a privileged constitutional and political status rooted in German feudal law under which the <a href="/wiki/Imperial_state" class="mw-redirect" title="Imperial state">Imperial estates</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Holy_Roman_Empire" title="Holy Roman Empire">Holy Roman Empire</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Free_imperial_city" title="Free imperial city">Imperial cities</a>, prince-bishoprics and secular principalities, and individuals such as the <a href="/wiki/Imperial_knights" class="mw-redirect" title="Imperial knights">Imperial knights</a>, were declared free from the authority of any local lord and placed under the direct ("immediate") authority of the Emperor, and later of the institutions of the Empire such as the <a href="/wiki/Imperial_Diet_(Holy_Roman_Empire)" title="Imperial Diet (Holy Roman Empire)">Diet</a> (<i><span lang="de" xml:lang="de">Reichstag</span></i>), the <a href="/wiki/Reichskammergericht" title="Reichskammergericht">Imperial Chamber of Justice</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Aulic_Council" title="Aulic Council">Aulic Council</a>.</p>
<p>The granting of immediacy began in the Early Middle Ages, and for the immediate bishops, abbots and cities, then the main beneficiaries of that status, immediacy could be exacting and often meant being subjected to the fiscal, military and hospitality demands of their overlord, the Emperor. However, with the gradual exit of the Emperor from the centre stage from the mid-13th century onwards, holders of imperial immediacy eventually found themselves vested with considerable rights and powers previously exercised by the emperor.</p>
<p>As confirmed by the <a href="/wiki/Peace_of_Westphalia" title="Peace of Westphalia">Peace of Westphalia</a> in 1648, the possession of imperial immediacy came with a particular form of territorial authority known as territorial superiority (<i><span lang="de" xml:lang="de">Landeshoheit</span></i> or <i><span lang="la" xml:lang="la">superioritas territorialis</span></i> in German and Latin documents of the time).<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> In today's terms, it would be understood as a limited form of sovereignty.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Gradations">Gradations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Imperial_immediacy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Gradations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Frontispice_page_of_treaty_between_France_and_Li%C3%A8ge.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bd/Frontispice_page_of_treaty_between_France_and_Li%C3%A8ge.jpg/170px-Frontispice_page_of_treaty_between_France_and_Li%C3%A8ge.jpg" width="170" height="239" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bd/Frontispice_page_of_treaty_between_France_and_Li%C3%A8ge.jpg/255px-Frontispice_page_of_treaty_between_France_and_Li%C3%A8ge.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bd/Frontispice_page_of_treaty_between_France_and_Li%C3%A8ge.jpg/340px-Frontispice_page_of_treaty_between_France_and_Li%C3%A8ge.jpg 2x" data-file-width="504" data-file-height="708" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Frontispice_page_of_treaty_between_France_and_Li%C3%A8ge.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The <a href="/wiki/Prince-Bishopric_of_Li%C3%A8ge" title="Prince-Bishopric of Liège">Prince-Bishop of Liège</a>, member of the Imperial estates, enjoyed Imperial immediacy and therefore could negotiate and sign international treaties on his own, as long as they were not directed against the Emperor and the Empire.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Several immediate estates held the privilege of attending meetings of the <i><span lang="de" xml:lang="de">Reichstag</span></i> in person, including an individual vote (<i><span lang="la" xml:lang="la">votum virile</span></i>):</p>
<ul>
<li>the seven <a href="/wiki/Prince-elector" title="Prince-elector">Prince-electors</a> designated by the <a href="/wiki/Golden_Bull_of_1356" title="Golden Bull of 1356">Golden Bull of 1356</a></li>
<li>the other <a href="/wiki/Princes_of_the_Holy_Roman_Empire" title="Princes of the Holy Roman Empire">Princes of the Holy Roman Empire</a>
<ul>
<li>secular: <a href="/wiki/Duke" title="Duke">Dukes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Margrave" title="Margrave">Margraves</a>, <a href="/wiki/Landgrave" title="Landgrave">Landgraves</a> et al.</li>
<li>ecclesiastical: <a href="/wiki/Prince-Bishop" class="mw-redirect" title="Prince-Bishop">Prince-Bishops</a>, Prince-Abbots and <a href="/wiki/Prince-Provost" title="Prince-Provost">Prince-Provosts</a>.</li>
</ul>
</li>
</ul>
<p>They formed the <a href="/wiki/Imperial_State" class="mw-redirect" title="Imperial State">Imperial Estates</a>, together with roughly 100 immediate counts, 40 <a href="/wiki/Imperial_prelate" class="mw-redirect" title="Imperial prelate">Imperial prelates</a> (abbots and abbesses) and 50 Imperial Cities who only enjoyed a collective vote (<i><span lang="la" xml:lang="la">votum curiatum</span></i>).</p>
<p>Further immediate estates not represented in the <i><span lang="de" xml:lang="de">Reichstag</span></i> were the <a href="/wiki/Imperial_Knight" title="Imperial Knight">Imperial Knights</a> as well as several abbeys and minor localities, the remains of those territories which in the <a href="/wiki/High_Middle_Ages" title="High Middle Ages">High Middle Ages</a> had been under the direct authority of the <a href="/wiki/Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Holy Roman Emperor">Emperor</a> and since then had mostly been given in pledge to the princes.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Advantages_and_disadvantages">Advantages and disadvantages</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Imperial_immediacy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Advantages and disadvantages">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Additional advantages might include the rights to collect <a href="/wiki/Taxes" class="mw-redirect" title="Taxes">taxes</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fee" title="Fee">tolls</a>, to hold a <a href="/wiki/Market_(place)" title="Market (place)">market</a>, to <a href="/wiki/Mint_coins" class="mw-redirect" title="Mint coins">mint coins</a>, to <a href="/wiki/Bear_arms" class="mw-redirect" title="Bear arms">bear arms</a>, and to conduct <a href="/wiki/Legal_proceedings" class="mw-redirect" title="Legal proceedings">legal proceedings</a>. The last of these might include the so-called <i><span lang="de" xml:lang="de"><a href="/wiki/Blood_court" class="mw-redirect" title="Blood court">Blutgericht</a></span></i> ("blood justice") through which capital punishment could be administered. These rights varied according to the legal patents granted by the emperor.</p>
<p>Disadvantages might include direct intervention by imperial commissions, as happened in several of the south-western cities after the <a href="/wiki/Schmalkaldic_War" title="Schmalkaldic War">Schmalkaldic War</a>, and the potential restriction or outright loss of previously held legal patents. Immediate rights might be lost if the Emperor and/or the Imperial Diet could not defend them against external aggression, as occurred in the <a href="/wiki/First_Coalition" class="mw-redirect" title="First Coalition">French Revolutionary wars</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Second_Coalition" class="mw-redirect" title="Second Coalition">Napoleonic Wars</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Lun%C3%A9ville" title="Treaty of Lunéville">Treaty of Lunéville</a> in 1801 required the emperor to renounce all claims to the portions of the Holy Roman Empire west of the <a href="/wiki/Rhine" title="Rhine">Rhine</a>. At the last meeting of the Imperial Diet (<a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a>: <span lang="de" xml:lang="de"><i>Reichsdeputationshauptschluss</i></span>) in 1802–03, also called the <a href="/wiki/German_Mediatisation" class="mw-redirect" title="German Mediatisation">German Mediatisation</a>, most of the free imperial cities and the ecclesiastic states lost their imperial immediacy and were absorbed by several dynastic states.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Problems_in_understanding_the_Empire">Problems in understanding the Empire</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Imperial_immediacy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Problems in understanding the Empire">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Map_of_the_Holy_Roman_Empire,_1789_en.png" class="image"><img alt="map of the Holy Roman Empire (central Europe) in 1789 showing the several hundred states, in different colours" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cc/Map_of_the_Holy_Roman_Empire%2C_1789_en.png/220px-Map_of_the_Holy_Roman_Empire%2C_1789_en.png" width="220" height="179" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cc/Map_of_the_Holy_Roman_Empire%2C_1789_en.png/330px-Map_of_the_Holy_Roman_Empire%2C_1789_en.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cc/Map_of_the_Holy_Roman_Empire%2C_1789_en.png/440px-Map_of_the_Holy_Roman_Empire%2C_1789_en.png 2x" data-file-width="2650" data-file-height="2160" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Map_of_the_Holy_Roman_Empire,_1789_en.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The Holy Roman Empire in 1789. Each of these states (different colours) on the map had a specific set of legal rights that governed its social, economic, and juridical relationships between the state and the emperor, and among the states themselves.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The practical application of the rights of immediacy was complex; this makes the history of the Holy Roman Empire particularly difficult to understand, especially for modern historians. Even such contemporaries as <a href="/wiki/Johann_Wolfgang_von_Goethe" title="Johann Wolfgang von Goethe">Goethe</a> and <a href="/wiki/Johann_Gottlieb_Fichte" title="Johann Gottlieb Fichte">Fichte</a> called the Empire a monstrosity. Voltaire wrote of the Empire as something neither Holy nor Roman, nor an Empire, and in comparison to the <a href="/wiki/British_Empire" title="British Empire">British Empire</a>, saw its German counterpart as an abysmal failure that reached its pinnacle of success in the early Middle Ages and declined thereafter.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Prussian historian <a href="/wiki/Heinrich_von_Treitschke" title="Heinrich von Treitschke">Heinrich von Treitschke</a> described it in the 19th century as having become "a chaotic mess of rotted imperial forms and unfinished territories". For nearly a century after the publication of <a href="/wiki/James_Bryce,_1st_Viscount_Bryce" title="James Bryce, 1st Viscount Bryce">James Bryce</a>'s monumental work <i>The Holy Roman Empire</i> (1864), this view prevailed among most <a href="/wiki/English-speaking" class="mw-redirect" title="English-speaking">English-speaking</a> historians of the <a href="/wiki/Early_Modern_period" class="mw-redirect" title="Early Modern period">Early Modern period</a>, and contributed to the development of the <span lang="de" xml:lang="de"><a href="/wiki/Sonderweg" title="Sonderweg">Sonderweg</a></span> theory of the German past.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>A <a href="/wiki/Historical_revisionism" title="Historical revisionism">revisionist</a> view popular in Germany but increasingly adopted elsewhere argued that "though not powerful politically or militarily, [the Empire] was extraordinarily diverse and free by the standards of Europe at the time". Pointing out that people like Goethe meant "monster" as a compliment in modern understanding,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (December 2016)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup> <i><a href="/wiki/The_Economist" title="The Economist">The Economist</a></i> has called the Empire "a great place to live ... a union with which its subjects identified, whose loss distressed them greatly" and praised its cultural and religious diversity, saying that it "allowed a degree of liberty and diversity that was unimaginable in the neighbouring kingdoms" and that "ordinary folk, including women, had far more rights to property than in France or Spain".<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
