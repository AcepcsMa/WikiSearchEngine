<p><b>Amortization</b> (or <b>amortisation</b>; <small>see <a href="/wiki/Spelling_differences" class="mw-redirect" title="Spelling differences">spelling differences</a></small>) is paying off an amount owed over time by making planned, incremental payments of <a href="/wiki/Principal_(finance)" class="mw-redirect" title="Principal (finance)">principal</a> and <a href="/wiki/Interest" title="Interest">interest</a>. To amortize a loan means "to kill it off".<sup id="cite_ref-Merriam-Webster_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Merriam-Webster-1">[1]</a></sup> In accounting, amortization refers to charging or writing off an <a href="/wiki/Intangible_asset" title="Intangible asset">intangible asset</a>'s cost as an <a href="/wiki/Operational_expense" class="mw-redirect" title="Operational expense">operational expense</a> over its estimated useful life to reduce a company's taxable income.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Merriam-Webster_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Merriam-Webster-1">[1]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Amortization&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The word comes from Middle English <i>amortisen</i> to kill, alienate in <a href="/wiki/Mortmain" title="Mortmain">mortmain</a>, from Anglo-French <i>amorteser</i>, alteration of <i>amortir</i>, from <a href="/wiki/Vulgar_Latin" title="Vulgar Latin">Vulgar Latin</a> <i>admortire</i> "to kill", from <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> <i>ad-</i> and <i>mort-</i>, "death".</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Applications_of_amortization">Applications of amortization</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Amortization&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Applications of amortization">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul>
<li>When used in the context of a home purchase, amortization is the process by which loan principal decreases over the life of a loan, typically an <a href="/wiki/Amortizing_loan" title="Amortizing loan">amortizing loan</a>. As each mortgage payment is made, part of the payment is applied as interest on the loan, and the remainder of the payment is applied towards reducing the principal. An <a href="/wiki/Amortization_schedule" title="Amortization schedule">amortization schedule</a>, a table detailing each periodic payment on a loan, shows the amounts of principal and interest and demonstrates how a loan's principal amount decreases over time. An amortization schedule can be generated by an <a href="/wiki/Amortization_calculator" title="Amortization calculator">amortization calculator</a>. <a href="/wiki/Negative_amortization" title="Negative amortization">Negative amortization</a> is an amortization schedule where the loan amount actually increases through not paying the full interest.</li>
<li>In business, <a href="/wiki/Amortization_(business)" title="Amortization (business)">amortization</a> allocates a lump sum amount to different time periods, particularly for loans and other forms of finance, including related <a href="/wiki/Interest" title="Interest">interest</a> or other finance charges. Amortization is also applied to capital expenditures of certain assets under accounting rules, particularly <a href="/wiki/Intangible_assets" class="mw-redirect" title="Intangible assets">intangible assets</a>, in a manner analogous to <a href="/wiki/Depreciation" title="Depreciation">depreciation</a>.</li>
<li>In <a href="/wiki/Tax_law" title="Tax law">tax law</a> in the United States, <a href="/wiki/Amortization_(tax_law)" title="Amortization (tax law)">amortization</a> refers to the cost recovery system for <a href="/wiki/Intangible_property" title="Intangible property">intangible property</a>.</li>
<li>In <a href="/wiki/Computer_science" title="Computer science">computer science</a>, <a href="/wiki/Amortized_analysis" title="Amortized analysis">amortized analysis</a> is a method of <a href="/wiki/Analysis_of_algorithms" title="Analysis of algorithms">analyzing</a> the execution cost of <a href="/wiki/Algorithms" class="mw-redirect" title="Algorithms">algorithms</a> over a sequence of operations.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></li>
<li>In the context of <a href="/wiki/Zoning" title="Zoning">zoning regulations</a>, amortization refers to the time period a non-conforming property has to conform to a new zoning classification before the non-conforming use becomes prohibited. For example, if the city rezones property from industrial to residential and sets an amortization period of one year, all property within the rezoned boundary must move from industrial use to residential use within one year.</li>
</ul>
