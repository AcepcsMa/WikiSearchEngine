			<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Listed building</h1>
									<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
									<div id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
								<div id="contentSub"></div>
												<div id="jump-to-nav" class="mw-jump">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>

The <a href="/wiki/Forth_Bridge" title="Forth Bridge">Forth Bridge</a>, designed by <a href="/wiki/Sir_Benjamin_Baker" class="mw-redirect" title="Sir Benjamin Baker">Sir Benjamin Baker</a> and <a href="/wiki/John_Fowler_(engineer)" class="mw-redirect" title="John Fowler (engineer)">Sir John Fowler</a>, which opened in 1890, and is now owned by <a href="/wiki/Network_Rail" title="Network Rail">Network Rail</a>, is designated as a Category A listed building by <a href="/wiki/Historic_Scotland" title="Historic Scotland">Historic Scotland</a>.</div>

<p>A <b>listed building</b> or <b>listed structure</b>, in the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>, is one that has been placed on the Statutory List of Buildings of Special Architectural or Historic Interest.</p>
<p>The statutory bodies maintaining the list are <a href="/wiki/Historic_England" title="Historic England">Historic England</a> in <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a>; <a href="/wiki/Cadw" title="Cadw">Cadw</a> (The Historic Environment Service of the Welsh Government) in <a href="/wiki/Wales" title="Wales">Wales</a>; <a href="/wiki/Historic_Scotland" title="Historic Scotland">Historic Scotland</a> in <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a>; and the <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland_Environment_Agency" title="Northern Ireland Environment Agency">Northern Ireland Environment Agency</a> (NIEA) in <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland" title="Northern Ireland">Northern Ireland</a>.</p>
<p>The term has also been used in the <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Ireland" title="Republic of Ireland">Republic of Ireland</a>, where buildings are surveyed for the National Inventory of Architectural Heritage in accordance with the country's obligations under the <a href="/wiki/Convention_for_the_protection_of_the_architectural_heritage_of_Europe" class="mw-redirect" title="Convention for the protection of the architectural heritage of Europe">Granada Convention</a>. However, the preferred term in Ireland is <i>protected structure</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-inventory_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-inventory-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>A listed building may not be demolished, extended, or altered without special permission from the local planning authority, which typically consults the relevant central government agency, particularly for significant alterations to the more notable listed buildings. In England and Wales, a national <a href="/wiki/Amenity_society" title="Amenity society">amenity society</a> must be notified of any work to a listed building which involves any element of demolition.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Exemption from secular listed building control is provided for some buildings in current use for worship, but only in cases where the relevant religious organisation operates its own equivalent permissions procedure. Owners of listed buildings are, in some circumstances, compelled to repair and maintain them and can face criminal prosecution if they fail to do so or if they perform unauthorised alterations. When alterations are permitted, or when listed buildings are repaired or maintained, the owners are often compelled to use specific materials or techniques.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Although most structures appearing on the lists are buildings, other structures such as <a href="/wiki/Bridge" title="Bridge">bridges</a>, <a href="/wiki/Monument" title="Monument">monuments</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sculpture" title="Sculpture">sculptures</a>, <a href="/wiki/War_memorial" title="War memorial">war memorials</a>, and even <a href="/wiki/Milestone" title="Milestone">milestones and mileposts</a> and The Beatles' <a href="/wiki/Abbey_Road,_London" title="Abbey Road, London">Abbey Road pedestrian crossing</a><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> are also listed. Ancient, military, and uninhabited structures, such as <a href="/wiki/Stonehenge" title="Stonehenge">Stonehenge</a>, are sometimes instead classified as <a href="/wiki/Scheduled_monument" title="Scheduled monument">scheduled monuments</a> and protected by much older legislation whilst cultural landscapes such as parks and gardens are currently "listed" on a non-statutory basis. Slightly different systems operate in each area of the United Kingdom, though the basic principles of the listing remain the same.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Background">Background</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Listed_building&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Although a limited number of 'ancient monuments' were given protection under the <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Monuments_Protection_Act_1882" title="Ancient Monuments Protection Act 1882">Ancient Monuments Protection Act 1882</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> there was reluctance to restrict the owners of occupied buildings in what they could do to their property. It was the damage to buildings caused by <a href="/wiki/The_Blitz" title="The Blitz">German bombing</a> during <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a> that prompted the first listing of buildings that were deemed to be of particular architectural merit.<sup id="cite_ref-victorian_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-victorian-6">[6]</a></sup> Three hundred members of the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Institute_of_British_Architects" title="Royal Institute of British Architects">Royal Institute of British Architects</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Society_for_the_Protection_of_Ancient_Buildings" title="Society for the Protection of Ancient Buildings">Society for the Protection of Ancient Buildings</a> were dispatched to prepare the list under the supervision of the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Inspectorate_of_Ancient_Monuments&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Inspectorate of Ancient Monuments (page does not exist)">Inspectorate of Ancient Monuments</a>, with funding from the Treasury.<sup id="cite_ref-independent_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-independent-7">[7]</a></sup> The listings were used as a means of determining whether a particular building should be rebuilt if it was damaged by bombing,<sup id="cite_ref-victorian_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-victorian-6">[6]</a></sup> with varying degrees of success.<sup id="cite_ref-independent_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-independent-7">[7]</a></sup> In Scotland the process slightly predated the war with <a href="/wiki/John_Crichton-Stuart,_4th_Marquess_of_Bute" title="John Crichton-Stuart, 4th Marquess of Bute">the Marquess of Bute</a> (in his connections to the <a href="/wiki/National_Trust_for_Scotland" title="National Trust for Scotland">National Trust for Scotland</a>) commissioning <a href="/wiki/Ian_Gordon_Lindsay" title="Ian Gordon Lindsay">the architect Ian Lindsay</a> in September 1936 to survey 103 towns and villages based on an Amsterdam model using three categories (A, B and C).<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>The basis of the current more comprehensive listing process was developed from the wartime system and was enacted by a provision in the <a href="/wiki/Town_and_Country_Planning_Act_1947" title="Town and Country Planning Act 1947">Town and Country Planning Act 1947</a> covering England and Wales, and the Town and Country Planning (Scotland) Act 1947 covering Scotland. Listing was first introduced into Northern Ireland under the Planning (Northern Ireland) Order 1972. The listing process has since developed slightly differently in each part of the UK.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Heritage_protection">Heritage protection</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Listed_building&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Heritage protection">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the UK, the process of protecting the built historic environment (i.e. getting a <a href="/wiki/Heritage_asset" title="Heritage asset">heritage asset</a> legally protected) is called ‘designation’. To complicate things, several different terms are used because the processes use separate legislation: buildings are ‘listed’; ancient monuments are ‘scheduled’, wrecks are ‘protected’, and battlefields, gardens and parks are ‘registered’. A heritage asset is a part of the historic environment that is valued because of its historic, archaeological, architectural or artistic interest.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>Only some of these are judged to be important enough to have extra legal protection through designation. However, buildings that are not formally listed but still judged as being of heritage interest are still regarded as being a <a href="/wiki/Material_consideration" title="Material consideration">material consideration</a> in the planning process.<sup id="cite_ref-PPS5_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PPS5-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>As a very rough guide, listed buildings are structures considered of special architectural and historical importance whereas <a href="/wiki/Ancient_monument" title="Ancient monument">ancient monuments</a> are of 'national importance' containing evidential values and can on many occasions also relate to below ground and/or unoccupied sites and buildings.<sup id="cite_ref-LBCA1990_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-LBCA1990-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="What_can_be_listed">What can be listed</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Listed_building&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: What can be listed">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Almost anything can be listed – it does not have to be a building. Buildings and structures of special historic interest come in a wide variety of forms and types, ranging from telephone boxes and road signs, to castles. Historic England has created twenty broad categories of structures, and published selection guides for each one to aid with assessing buildings and structures. These include historical overviews and describe the special considerations for listing each category.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-wilts_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wilts-13">[13]</a></sup> Both Historic Scotland and Cadw produce guidance for owners.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Procedure_for_listing_or_delisting">Procedure for listing or delisting</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Listed_building&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Procedure for listing or delisting">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In England, to have a building considered for listing or delisting, the process is to apply to the secretary of state; this can be done by submitting an application form online to <a href="/wiki/Historic_England" title="Historic England">Historic England</a>. The applicant does not need to be the owner of the building to apply for it to be listed.<sup id="cite_ref-wilts_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wilts-13">[13]</a></sup> Full information including application form guidance notes are on the Historic England website. Historic England assesses buildings put forward for listing or delisting and provides advice to the Secretary of State on the architectural and historic interest. The Secretary of State, who may seek additional advice from others, then decides whether or not to list or delist the building.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="England_and_Wales">England and Wales</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Listed_building&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: England and Wales">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">For lists of buildings, see <a href="/wiki/Listed_buildings_in_England" title="Listed buildings in England">Listed buildings in England</a> and <a href="/wiki/Listed_buildings_in_Wales" title="Listed buildings in Wales">Listed buildings in Wales</a>.</div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_legislation_relevant_to_listing">The legislation relevant to listing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Listed_building&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: The legislation relevant to listing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In England and Wales the authority for listing is granted to the Secretary of State by the <a href="/wiki/Planning_(Listed_Buildings_and_Conservation_Areas)_Act_1990" title="Planning (Listed Buildings and Conservation Areas) Act 1990">Planning (Listed Buildings and Conservation Areas) Act 1990</a>. Listed buildings in danger of decay are listed on the Historic England '<a href="/wiki/Heritage_at_Risk" title="Heritage at Risk">Heritage at Risk</a>' Register.</p>
<p>In 1980 there was public outcry at the sudden destruction of the <a href="/wiki/Art_deco" class="mw-redirect" title="Art deco">art deco</a> <a href="/wiki/Firestone_tyre_factory_(London)" class="mw-redirect" title="Firestone tyre factory (London)">Firestone Tyre Factory</a> (<a href="/wiki/Wallis,_Gilbert_and_Partners" title="Wallis, Gilbert and Partners">Wallis, Gilbert and Partners</a>, 1928–29), which was demolished over the August bank holiday weekend by its owners <a href="/wiki/Trafalgar_House_(company)" title="Trafalgar House (company)">Trafalgar House</a> who had been told that it was likely to be 'spot-listed' a few days later,<sup id="cite_ref-Firestone_destruction_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Firestone_destruction-14">[14]</a></sup> and the Government undertook to review arrangements for listing buildings.<sup id="cite_ref-1980_revisions_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1980_revisions-15">[15]</a></sup> After the Firestone demolition, the <a href="/wiki/Secretary_of_State_for_the_Environment" title="Secretary of State for the Environment">Secretary of State for the Environment</a> <a href="/wiki/Michael_Heseltine" title="Michael Heseltine">Michael Heseltine</a> also initiated a complete re-survey of buildings to ensure that everything that merited preservation was on the lists.<sup id="cite_ref-Re-survey_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Re-survey-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>In England, the <a href="/wiki/Department_for_Culture,_Media_and_Sport" class="mw-redirect" title="Department for Culture, Media and Sport">Department for Culture, Media and Sport</a> (DCMS) works with <a href="/wiki/Historic_England" title="Historic England">Historic England</a> (an agency of the DCMS), and other government departments, e.g. <a href="/wiki/Department_for_Communities_and_Local_Government" title="Department for Communities and Local Government">Department for Communities and Local Government</a> (DCLG) and the <a href="/wiki/Department_for_the_Environment,_Food_and_Rural_Affairs" class="mw-redirect" title="Department for the Environment, Food and Rural Affairs">Department for the Environment, Food and Rural Affairs</a> (DEFRA) to deliver the government policy on the protection to historic buildings and other heritage assets. The decision about whether or not to list a building is made by the Secretary of State, although the process is administered in England by <a href="/wiki/Historic_England" title="Historic England">Historic England</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-dcmsWhatDo_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dcmsWhatDo-17">[17]</a></sup> In Wales (where it is a devolved issue) it is administered by <a href="/wiki/Cadw" title="Cadw">Cadw</a> on behalf of the <a href="/wiki/National_Assembly_for_Wales" title="National Assembly for Wales">National Assembly for Wales</a><sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> and in Scotland it is administered by <a href="/wiki/Historic_Scotland" title="Historic Scotland">Historic Scotland</a> on behalf of the Scottish Ministers.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Heritage_protection_reform_legislation_in_England">Heritage protection reform legislation in England</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Listed_building&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Heritage protection reform legislation in England">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>There have been several attempts to simplify the heritage planning process for listed buildings in England, which has still (at the time of writing in May 2011) to reach a conclusion.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>The review process was started in 2000 by <a href="/wiki/Alan_Howarth,_Baron_Howarth_of_Newport" title="Alan Howarth, Baron Howarth of Newport">Alan Howarth</a>, then minister at the <a href="/wiki/Department_for_Culture,_Media_and_Sport" class="mw-redirect" title="Department for Culture, Media and Sport">Department for Culture, Media and Sport</a> (DCMS). The outcome was the paper 'The Power of Place' in 2000<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> followed by the subsequent policy document 'The Historic Environment: A Force for Our Future' published by the DCMS and the <a href="/wiki/Department_of_the_Environment,_Transport_and_the_Regions" class="mw-redirect" title="Department of the Environment, Transport and the Regions">Department of the Environment, Transport and the Regions</a> (DTLR) in December 2001.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> The launch of the Government’s Heritage Protection Reform (HPR) report in July 2003 by the DCMS entitled: 'Protecting our historic environment: Making the system work better',<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> asked questions about how the current designation systems could be improved. The HPR decision report 'Review of Heritage Protection: The Way Forward' green paper published in June 2004 by the DCMS committed the UK government and <a href="/wiki/English_Heritage" title="English Heritage">English Heritage</a> to a process of reform including a review of the criteria used for listing buildings.</p>
<p>The Government also began a process of consultation on changes to Planning Policy Guidance 15 (<a href="/wiki/PPG_15" class="mw-redirect" title="PPG 15">PPG 15</a>) relating to the principles of selection for listing buildings in England. After several years of consultation with heritage groups, charities, planning authorities and English Heritage, this eventually resulted in the publication of Planning Publication Statement 5 'Planning for the Historic Environment' in March 2010 by the <a href="/wiki/DCLG" class="mw-redirect" title="DCLG">DCLG</a>. This replaced PPG15 and sets out the government's national policies on the conservation of the historic environment for the England.<sup id="cite_ref-PPS5_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PPS5-10">[10]</a></sup> PPS5 is supported by a Practice Guide, endorsed by the DCLG, the DCMS, and English Heritage,<sup id="cite_ref-PPS5_10-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PPS5-10">[10]</a></sup> which describes how to apply the policies stated in PPS5.</p>
<p>The government's White Paper 'Heritage Protection for the 21st Century' published on 8 March 2007 offered a commitment to sharing the understanding of the historic environment and more openness in the process of designation.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2008, a draft Heritage Protection Bill<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> was subject to pre-legislative scrutiny before its passage through UK Parliament. The legislation was abandoned despite strong cross-party support, to make room in the parliamentary legislative programme for measures to deal with the credit crunch,<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> though it may be revived in future. The proposal was that the existing registers of buildings, parks and gardens, archaeology and battlefields, maritime wrecks, and <a href="/wiki/World_Heritage_Site" title="World Heritage Site">World Heritage Sites</a> be merged into a single online register that will "explain what is special and why". English Heritage would become directly responsible for identifying historic assets in England and there would be wider consultation with the public and asset owners, and new rights of appeal. There would have been streamlined systems for granting consent for work on historic assets.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Categories_of_listed_building">Categories of listed building</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Listed_building&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Categories of listed building">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>There are three types of listed status for buildings in England and Wales:<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li><span id="Grade_I"></span> <b>Grade I</b>: buildings of exceptional interest.</li>
<li><span id="Grade_II.2A"></span> <b>Grade II*</b>: particularly important buildings of more than special interest.</li>
<li><span id="Grade_II"></span> <b>Grade II</b>: buildings that are of special interest, warranting every effort to preserve them.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<p>There was formerly a non-statutory Grade III, which was abolished in 1970.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> Additionally, Grades A, B and C were used mainly for <a href="/wiki/Church_of_England" title="Church of England">Anglican</a> churches in use – these correspond approximately to Grades I, II* and II. These grades were used mainly before 1977, although a few buildings are still listed using these grades.</p>
<p>Listed buildings account for about 2% of English building stock.<sup id="cite_ref-HAR2010report_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HAR2010report-31">[31]</a></sup> In March 2010, there were about 374,000 list entries<sup id="cite_ref-dcmsWhatDo_17-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dcmsWhatDo-17">[17]</a></sup> of which 92% were Grade II, 5.5% were Grade II*, and 2.5% were Grade I.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> Places of worship play an important role in the UK’s architectural heritage. England alone has 14,500 listed places of worship (4,000 Grade I, 4,500 Grade II* and 6,000 Grade II). In fact, 45% of all Grade I listed buildings are places of worship.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<p>There are estimated to be about 500,000 actual buildings listed, as listing entries can apply to more than one building.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Statutory_criteria_for_listing">Statutory criteria for listing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Listed_building&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Statutory criteria for listing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The criteria for listing include architectural interest, historic interest and close historical associations with significant people or events. Buildings not individually noteworthy may still be listed if they form part of a group that is—for example, all the buildings in a square. This is called 'group value'. Sometimes large areas comprising many buildings may not justify listing but receive the looser protection of designation as a <a href="/wiki/Conservation_area_(United_Kingdom)" title="Conservation area (United Kingdom)">conservation area</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-listingprinciples_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-listingprinciples-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
<p>The specific criteria include:</p>
<ul>
<li><b>Age and rarity:</b> The older a building is, the more likely it is to be listed. All buildings erected before 1700 that "contain a significant proportion of their original fabric" will be listed. Most buildings built between 1700 and 1840 are listed. After 1840 more selection is exercised and "particularly careful selection" is applied after 1945. Buildings less than 30 years old are rarely listed unless they are of outstanding quality and under threat.</li>
<li><b>Aesthetic merits:</b> i.e. the appearance of a building. However, buildings that have little visual appeal may be listed on grounds of representing particular aspects of social or economic history.</li>
<li><b>Selectivity:</b> where a large number of buildings of a similar type survive, the policy is only to list the most representative or significant examples.</li>
<li><b>National interest:</b> significant or distinctive regional buildings; e.g. those that represent a nationally important but localised industry.</li>
</ul>
<p>The state of repair of a building is <i>not</i> deemed to be a relevant consideration for listing.<sup id="cite_ref-listingprinciples_34-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-listingprinciples-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
<p>Additionally:</p>
<ul>
<li>Any buildings or structures constructed before 1 July 1948 that fall within the <a href="/wiki/Curtilage" title="Curtilage">curtilage</a> of a listed building are treated as part of the listed building.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup></li>
<li>The effect of a proposed development on the setting of a listed building is a material consideration in determining a planning application. Setting is defined as "the surroundings in which a heritage is experienced".<sup id="cite_ref-PPS5_10-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PPS5-10">[10]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<p>Although the decision to list a building may be made on the basis of the architectural or historic interest of one small part of the building, the listing protection nevertheless applies to the whole building. Listing applies not just to the exterior fabric of the building itself, but also to the interior, fixtures, fittings, and objects within the curtilage of the building even if they are not fixed.<sup id="cite_ref-1990Act_36-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1990Act-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
<p>De-listing is possible but rare in practice. One example is the 30 November 2001 de-listing of <a href="/wiki/North_Corporation_Primary_School" title="North Corporation Primary School">North Corporation Primary School</a>, <a href="/wiki/Liverpool" title="Liverpool">Liverpool</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Emergency_listing">Emergency listing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Listed_building&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Emergency listing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In an emergency, the local planning authority can serve a temporary listed "building preservation notice", if a building is in danger of demolition or alteration in such a way that might affect its historic character.<sup id="cite_ref-1990Act_36-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1990Act-36">[36]</a></sup> This remains in force for 6 months until the Secretary of State decides whether or not to formally list the building.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Certificates_of_immunity">Certificates of immunity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Listed_building&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Certificates of immunity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Until the passing of the Enterprise and Regulatory Reform Act in 2013 an application for a Certificate of Immunity from Listing could only be made If planning permission was being sought or had been obtained in England. However, the changes brought about by the Act means that now anyone can ask the Secretary of State to issue a <a href="/wiki/Certificate_of_Immunity" class="mw-redirect" title="Certificate of Immunity">Certificate of Immunity</a> (CoI) in respect of a particular building at any time.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Altering_a_listed_building">Altering a listed building</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Listed_building&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Altering a listed building">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In England and Wales, the management of listed buildings is the responsibility of local planning authorities and the Department for Communities and Local Government (i.e., not DCMS, which originally listed the building). There is a general principle that listed buildings are put to 'appropriate and viable use' and recognition that this may involve the re-use and modification of the building.<sup id="cite_ref-PPS5_10-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PPS5-10">[10]</a></sup> However, listed buildings cannot be modified without first obtaining Listed Building Consent through the relevant local planning authority.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup></p>
<p>In Wales, applications are made using a form obtained from the relevant local authority.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> There is no provision for consent to be granted in outline. When a local authority is disposed to grant listed building consent, it must first notify the National Assembly (<i>i.e.</i> <a href="/wiki/Cadw" title="Cadw">Cadw</a>) of the application. If the planning authority decides to refuse consent, it may do so without any reference to Cadw.</p>
<p>In Scotland, applications are made on a form obtained from Historic Scotland. After consulting the local planning authority, the owner, where possible, and an independent third party, <a href="/wiki/Historic_Scotland" title="Historic Scotland">Historic Scotland</a> makes a recommendation on behalf of the Scottish Ministers.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup></p>
<p>Carrying out unauthorised works to a listed building is a criminal offence and owners can be prosecuted. A planning authority can also insist that all work undertaken without consent be reversed at the owner’s expense.</p>

(January 2017)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup> <a href="/wiki/Gothic_Revival" class="mw-redirect" title="Gothic Revival">Gothic</a>-designed cemetery and crematorium which contains 65 structures of Grade II or II*, mainly sepulchral monuments but also boundary structures and <a href="/wiki/Mausoleum" title="Mausoleum">mausolea</a>. <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Locally_listed_buildings">Locally listed buildings</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Listed_building&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Locally listed buildings">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Many councils, for example, <a href="/wiki/Birmingham_City_Council" title="Birmingham City Council">Birmingham City Council</a> and <a href="/wiki/Crawley" title="Crawley">Crawley Borough Council</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-Crawley_41-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Crawley-41">[41]</a></sup> maintain a list of <i><b>locally listed buildings</b></i> as separate to the statutory list (and in addition to it). There is no statutory protection of a building or object on the local list but many receive a degree of protection from loss through being in a Conservation Area and/or through planning policy. Councils hope that owners will recognise the merits of their properties and keep them unaltered if at all possible.</p>
<p>These grades are used by Birmingham:</p>
<dl>
<dt>Grade A</dt>
<dd>This is of statutory list quality. To be the subject of notification to Historic England and/or the serving of a Building Preservation Notice if imminently threatened.</dd>
<dt>Grade B</dt>
<dd>Important in the citywide architectural or local street scene context, warranting positive efforts to ensure retention.</dd>
<dt>Grade C</dt>
<dd>Of significance in the local historical/vernacular context, including industrial archaeological features, and worthy of retention.</dd>
</dl>
<p>Crawley Borough Council judges buildings on five criteria: historic interest, architectural interest, group and townscape value, intactness and communal value. As of November 2010, there were <a href="/wiki/Locally_listed_buildings_in_Crawley" title="Locally listed buildings in Crawley">59 buildings on its local list</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Crawley_41-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Crawley-41">[41]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Northern_Ireland">Northern Ireland</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Listed_building&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Northern Ireland">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">For lists of buildings, see <a href="/wiki/Listed_buildings_in_Northern_Ireland" title="Listed buildings in Northern Ireland">Listed buildings in Northern Ireland</a>.</div>

The Grade-A-listed <a href="/wiki/Mussenden_Temple" title="Mussenden Temple">Mussenden Temple</a>, <a href="/wiki/County_Londonderry" title="County Londonderry">County Londonderry</a><sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup></div>

<p>Listing began later in Northern Ireland than in the rest of the UK: the first provision for listing was contained in the Planning (Northern Ireland) Order 1972; and the current legislative basis for listing is the Planning (Northern Ireland) Order 1991.<sup id="cite_ref-criteria_43-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-criteria-43">[43]</a></sup> Under Article 42 of the Order, the relevant Department of the <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland_Executive" title="Northern Ireland Executive">Northern Ireland Executive</a> is required to compile lists of buildings of "special architectural or historic interest". Since 2016, the responsibility for the listing process rests with the Historic Environment Division of the <a href="/wiki/Department_for_Communities" title="Department for Communities">Department for Communities</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> which took over the built heritage functions of the <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland_Environment_Agency" title="Northern Ireland Environment Agency">Northern Ireland Environment Agency</a> (formerly the Environment and Heritage Service) following the break up of the Department of the Environment.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup></p>
<p>Following the introduction of listing, an initial survey of Northern Ireland's building stock was begun in 1974.<sup id="cite_ref-second_46-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-second-46">[46]</a></sup> By the time of the completion of this First Survey in 1994, the listing process had developed considerably, and it was therefore decided to embark upon a Second Survey, which is still ongoing, to update and cross-check the original information. Information gathered during this survey, relating to both listed and unlisted buildings, is entered into the publicly accessible Northern Ireland Buildings Database.<sup id="cite_ref-second_46-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-second-46">[46]</a></sup></p>
<p>A range of listing criteria, which aim to define architectural and historic interest, are used to determine whether or not to list a building.<sup id="cite_ref-criteria_43-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-criteria-43">[43]</a></sup> Listed building consent must be obtained from local authorities before any alteration to a listed structure.<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> There are about 8,500 listed buildings in Northern Ireland,<sup id="cite_ref-DfC_48-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DfC-48">[48]</a></sup> divided into four grades, defined as follows:</p>
<ul>
<li><span id="Grade_A"></span> <b>Grade A</b>: "buildings of greatest importance to Northern Ireland including both outstanding architectural set-pieces and the least altered examples of each representative style, period and type."<sup id="cite_ref-criteria_43-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-criteria-43">[43]</a></sup></li>
<li><span id="Grade_B.2B"></span> <b>Grade B+</b>: "high quality buildings that because of exceptional features, interiors or environmental qualities are clearly above the general standard set by grade B1 buildings. Also buildings which might have merited Grade A status but for detracting features such as an incomplete design, lower quality additions or alterations."<sup id="cite_ref-criteria_43-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-criteria-43">[43]</a></sup></li>
<li><span id="Grade_B"></span> <b>Grade B1</b>: "good examples of a particular period or style. A degree of alteration or imperfection of design may be acceptable. Generally B1 is chosen for buildings that qualify for listing by virtue of a relatively wide selection of attributes. Usually these will include interior features or where one or more features are of exceptional quality and/or interest."<sup id="cite_ref-criteria_43-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-criteria-43">[43]</a></sup></li>
<li><b>Grade B2</b>: "special buildings which meet the test of the legislation. A degree of alteration or imperfection of design may be acceptable. B2 is chosen for buildings that qualify for listing by virtue of only a few attributes. An example would be a building sited within a conservation area where the quality of its architectural appearance or interior raises it appreciably above the general standard of buildings within the conservation area."<sup id="cite_ref-criteria_43-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-criteria-43">[43]</a></sup></li>
</ul>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Scotland">Scotland</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Listed_building&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Scotland">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>In Scotland, listing was begun by a provision in the Town and Country Planning (Scotland) Act 1947, and the current legislative basis for listing is the Planning (Listed Buildings and Conservation Areas) (Scotland) Act 1997.<sup id="cite_ref-shep_56-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-shep-56">[56]</a></sup> As with other matters regarding planning, conservation is a power devolved to the <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Parliament" title="Scottish Parliament">Scottish Parliament</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Government" title="Scottish Government">Scottish Government</a>. The authority for listing rests with <a href="/wiki/Historic_Environment_Scotland" title="Historic Environment Scotland">Historic Environment Scotland</a> (formerly <a href="/wiki/Historic_Scotland" title="Historic Scotland">Historic Scotland</a>), an <a href="/wiki/Executive_agency" title="Executive agency">executive agency</a> of the Scottish Government, which inherited this role from the Scottish Development Department in 1991. Listed building consent must be obtained from local authorities before any alteration to a listed structure.<sup id="cite_ref-shep_56-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-shep-56">[56]</a></sup></p>
<p>The scheme for classifying buildings is:</p>
<ul>
<li><span id="Category_A"></span><b>Category A</b>: "buildings of national or international importance, either architectural or historic, or fine little-altered examples of some particular period, style or building type."<sup id="cite_ref-HSlisting_57-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HSlisting-57">[57]</a></sup></li>
<li><span id="Category_B"></span><b>Category B</b>: "buildings of regional or more than local importance, or major examples of some particular period, style or building type which may have been altered."<sup id="cite_ref-HSlisting_57-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HSlisting-57">[57]</a></sup></li>
<li><span id="Category_C"></span><b>Category C</b>: "buildings of local importance, lesser examples of any period, style, or building type, as originally constructed or moderately altered; and simple traditional buildings which group well with others in categories A and B."<sup id="cite_ref-HSlisting_57-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HSlisting-57">[57]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<p>There are about 47,400&#160;listed buildings in Scotland. Of these, around 8&#160;percent (some 3,800) are Category A, and 50&#160;percent are Category B, with the rest listed at Category C.<sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Records_of_listed_buildings">Records of listed buildings</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Listed_building&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: Records of listed buildings">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Although the 2008 draft legislation was abandoned, English Heritage published a single list of all designated <a href="/wiki/Heritage_asset" title="Heritage asset">heritage assets</a> within England in 2011.<sup id="cite_ref-72" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-72">[72]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/National_Heritage_List_for_England" title="National Heritage List for England">National Heritage List for England</a> is an online searchable database which includes 400,000 (most but not all) of England’s listed buildings, scheduled monuments, registered parks and gardens, protected historic wrecks and registered battlefields in one place. The legislative frameworks for each type of historic asset remains unchanged (2011).<sup id="cite_ref-NHL_73-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NHL-73">[73]</a></sup></p>
<p>In Scotland, the national dataset of listed buildings and other heritage assets can be searched online via Historic Scotland,<sup id="cite_ref-74" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-74">[74]</a></sup> or through the map database Pastmap.<sup id="cite_ref-75" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-75">[75]</a></sup></p>
<p>To find a listed building in Wales, it is necessary to contact the appropriate local authority or <a href="/wiki/Cadw" title="Cadw">Cadw</a>. Also British Listed Buildings (website)<sup id="cite_ref-76" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-76">[76]</a></sup> has sections on England, Wales and Scotland. It can be searched either by browsing for listed buildings by country, county and parish/locality, or by keyword search or via the online map. Not all buildings have photographs, as it is run on a volunteer basis.</p>
<p>The Northern Ireland Buildings Database contains details of all listed buildings in Northern Ireland.<sup id="cite_ref-77" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-77">[77]</a></sup></p>
<p>A photographic library of English listed buildings was started in 1999 as a snapshot of buildings listed at the turn of the millennium. This is not an up-to-date record of all listed buildings in England – the listing status and descriptions are only correct as at February 2001.<sup id="cite_ref-78" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-78">[78]</a></sup> The photographs were taken between 1999 and 2008. It is maintained by the Historic England archive at the <a href="/wiki/Images_of_England" title="Images of England">Images of England</a> project website. The National Heritage List for England contains the up-to-date list of listed buildings.<sup id="cite_ref-NHL_73-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NHL-73">[73]</a></sup></p>
<p>Listed buildings in danger of being lost through damage or decay in England started to be recorded by survey in 1991.<sup id="cite_ref-79" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-79">[79]</a></sup> This was extended in 1998 with the publication of Historic England's Buildings at Risk Register which surveyed Grade I and Grade II* buildings. In 2008 this survey was renamed <a href="/wiki/Heritage_at_Risk" title="Heritage at Risk">Heritage at Risk</a> and extended to include all listed buildings, scheduled monuments, registered parks and gardens, registered battlefields, protected wreck sites and conservation areas.<sup id="cite_ref-80" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-80">[80]</a></sup> The register is compiled by survey using information from local authorities, official and voluntary heritage groups and the general public. It is possible to search this list online.<sup id="cite_ref-81" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-81">[81]</a></sup></p>
<p>In Scotland, a <a href="/wiki/Buildings_at_Risk_Register_for_Scotland" title="Buildings at Risk Register for Scotland">buildings at risk register</a> was started in 1990 by <a href="/wiki/Historic_Scotland" title="Historic Scotland">Historic Scotland</a> in response to similar concerns at the number of listed buildings that were vacant and in disrepair. <a href="/wiki/RCAHMS" class="mw-redirect" title="RCAHMS">RCAHMS</a> maintain the register on behalf of Historic Scotland,<sup id="cite_ref-82" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-82">[82]</a></sup> and provide information on properties of architectural or historic merit throughout the country that are considered to be at risk.</p>
<p>In Wales, at risk registers of listed buildings are compiled by local planning authorities, and Cadw produced a report in 2009.<sup id="cite_ref-83" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-83">[83]</a></sup> The Royal Commission on the Ancient and Historical Monuments of Wales's (RCAHMW) Emergency Buildings Recording team is responsible for surveying historic buildings threatened with destruction, substantial alteration, or serious decay.</p>
