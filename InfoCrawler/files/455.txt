<p>The <b>42nd Infantry Division</b> (42ID) ("Rainbow"<sup id="cite_ref-SUD_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SUD-1">[1]</a></sup>) is a <a href="/wiki/Division_(military)" title="Division (military)">division</a> of the United States <a href="/wiki/Army_National_Guard" title="Army National Guard">Army National Guard</a>. The 42nd Infantry Division has served in <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a>, <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a> and the <a href="/wiki/War_on_Terror" title="War on Terror">Global War on Terrorism</a> (GWOT). The division is currently headquartered at the Glenmore Road Armory in <a href="/wiki/Troy,_New_York" title="Troy, New York">Troy</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_York_(state)" title="New York (state)">New York</a>.</p>
<p>The division headquarters is a unit of the <a href="/wiki/New_York_Army_National_Guard" title="New York Army National Guard">New York Army National Guard</a>. The division currently includes Army National Guard units from fourteen different states, including Connecticut, Maine, Maryland, Massachusetts, New Hampshire, New Jersey, New York, Rhode Island, and Vermont. As of 2007<sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=42nd_Infantry_Division_(United_States)&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, 67 percent of 42ID soldiers are located in New York and New Jersey.<sup id="cite_ref-army_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-army-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Rainbow_Division">Rainbow Division</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=42nd_Infantry_Division_(United_States)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Rainbow Division">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The 42ID came to be known as the "Rainbow Division". When the United States declared war on Germany in 1917, it federalized National Guard divisions to quickly build up an Army. In addition, <a href="/wiki/Douglas_MacArthur" title="Douglas MacArthur">Douglas MacArthur</a>, then a <a href="/wiki/Major_(United_States)" title="Major (United States)">major</a>, suggested to <a href="/wiki/William_A._Mann" class="mw-redirect" title="William A. Mann">William A. Mann</a>, the head of the <a href="/wiki/National_Guard_Bureau" title="National Guard Bureau">Militia Bureau</a>, that he form another division from the non-divisional units of several states. Secretary of War <a href="/wiki/Newton_D._Baker" title="Newton D. Baker">Newton D. Baker</a> approved the proposal, and recalled <a href="/wiki/Douglas_MacArthur" title="Douglas MacArthur">Douglas MacArthur</a> saying that such an organization would "stretch over the whole country like a rainbow."<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> The division was created using units from 26 states and the <a href="/wiki/District_of_Columbia" class="mw-redirect" title="District of Columbia">District of Columbia</a>. The name stuck, and MacArthur was promoted to <a href="/wiki/Colonel_(United_States)" title="Colonel (United States)">colonel</a> as the division chief of staff.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>

<p>The 42nd Division adopted a <a href="/wiki/Shoulder_patch" class="mw-redirect" title="Shoulder patch">shoulder patch</a> and unit crests acknowledging the nickname. The original version of the patch symbolized a half arc rainbow and contained thin bands in multiple colors. During the latter part of World War I and post war occupation duty in Germany, Rainbow Division soldiers modified the patch to a quarter arc, removing half the symbol to memorialize the half of the division's soldiers who became casualties (killed or wounded) during the war. They also reduced the number of colors to just red, gold and blue bordered in green, in order to standardize the design and make the patch easier to reproduce.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>Description: The 4th quadrant of a rainbow with three bands of color: red, gold and blue, each 3/8 inch (.95&#160;cm) in width, outer radius 2 inches (5.08&#160;cm); all within a 1/8 inch (.32&#160;cm) Army green border.</p>
<p>Background: The shoulder sleeve insignia was originally authorized by telegram on 29 Oct 1918. It was officially authorized for wear on 27 May 1922. It was reauthorized for wear when the division was reactivated for World War II. On 8 September 1947, it was authorized for the post-World War II 42nd Infantry Division when it was reactivated as a National Guard unit.</p>

<p>The 42nd Division was activated in August 1917, four months after the <a href="/wiki/American_entry_into_World_War_I" title="American entry into World War I">American entry</a> into <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a>, drawing men from 26 states and the District of Columbia. It was composed of the 83rd Infantry Brigade (<a href="/wiki/69th_Infantry_Regiment_(New_York)" title="69th Infantry Regiment (New York)">165th</a> and <a href="/wiki/166th_Infantry_Regiment_(United_States)" title="166th Infantry Regiment (United States)">166th Infantry Regiments</a>) and the 84th Infantry Brigade (<a href="/wiki/167th_Infantry_Regiment_(United_States)" title="167th Infantry Regiment (United States)">167th</a> and <a href="/wiki/168th_Infantry_Regiment" class="mw-redirect" title="168th Infantry Regiment">168th Infantry Regiments</a>), together with supporting units such as artillery, engineers, signals and so on.</p>
<p>The 42nd went overseas to the <a href="/wiki/Western_Front_(World_War_I)" title="Western Front (World War I)">Western Front</a> of Belgium and France in November 1917, one of the first divisions of the <a href="/wiki/American_Expeditionary_Forces" title="American Expeditionary Forces">American Expeditionary Force</a> (AEF) to do so. The AEF was commanded by <a href="/wiki/General_(United_States)" title="General (United States)">General</a> <a href="/wiki/John_J._Pershing" title="John J. Pershing">John Joseph Pershing</a>. Upon arrival there the 42nd Division began intensive training with the British and French armies in learning the basics of <a href="/wiki/Trench_warfare" title="Trench warfare">trench warfare</a> which had, for the past three years, dominated strategy on the Western Front, with neither side advancing much further than they had in 1914. The following year, the division took part in four major operations: the <a href="/wiki/United_States_campaigns_in_World_War_I#Champagne-Marne.2C_15.E2.80.9318_July_1918" title="United States campaigns in World War I">Champagne-Marne</a>, the <a href="/wiki/United_States_campaigns_in_World_War_I#Aisne-Marne.2C_18_July_.E2.80.93_6_August_1918" title="United States campaigns in World War I">Aisne-Marne</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Saint-Mihiel" title="Battle of Saint-Mihiel">Battle of Saint-Mihiel</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Meuse-Argonne_Offensive" title="Meuse-Argonne Offensive">Meuse-Argonne Offensive</a>. In total, it saw 264 days of combat. While in France, the division was placed under French control for a time, commanded by various French commanders, including <a href="/wiki/Henri_Gouraud_(French_Army_officer)" class="mw-redirect" title="Henri Gouraud (French Army officer)">Henri Gouraud</a> and <a href="/wiki/Georges_de_Bazelaire" title="Georges de Bazelaire">Georges de Bazelaire</a>, of the French <a href="/w/index.php?title=VII_Army_Corps&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="VII Army Corps (page does not exist)">VII Army Corps</a>.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Combat_chronicle">Combat chronicle</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=42nd_Infantry_Division_(United_States)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Combat chronicle">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>When formed and activated for World War II, the 42ID was a unique unit, as it was a reconstitution of the Rainbow Division from World War I. Except for the division headquarters, none of its earlier elements had reformed in the interwar period, so the Army Ground Forces filled its new units with personnel from every state. From the division standup at Camp Gruber until the division stood down in Austria, at every formal assembly, the division displayed not only the National and Divisional Colors, but all 48 state colors (State Flags). To emphasize the 42ID lineage from the 42ID of World War I, Maj. Gen. Harry J. Collins activated the unit on 14 July, the eve of the twenty-fifth anniversary of the Champagne-Marne campaign in France.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Dachau-002edit.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c3/Dachau-002edit.jpg/220px-Dachau-002edit.jpg" width="220" height="272" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c3/Dachau-002edit.jpg/330px-Dachau-002edit.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c3/Dachau-002edit.jpg/440px-Dachau-002edit.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2048" data-file-height="2528" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">

Plaque at <a href="/wiki/Dachau_concentration_camp" title="Dachau concentration camp">Dachau concentration camp</a> commemorating the liberation of the camp by the Rainbow Division.</div>

<p>Following training at <a href="/wiki/Camp_Gruber" title="Camp Gruber">Camp Gruber</a>, OK and the journey to Europe, the three infantry regiments (222nd, 232nd, &amp; 242nd) and a detachment of the 42ID Headquarters arrived in France at <a href="/wiki/Marseille" title="Marseille">Marseille</a>, 8–9 December 1944, and were formed into Task Force (TF) Linden, under <a href="/wiki/Henning_Linden" title="Henning Linden">Henning Linden</a>, the Assistant Division Commander (ADC). TF Linden was task organized to VI Corps under 7th Army. TF Linden entered combat in the vicinity of <a href="/wiki/Strasbourg" title="Strasbourg">Strasbourg</a>, relieving elements of the <a href="/wiki/U.S._36th_Infantry_Division" class="mw-redirect" title="U.S. 36th Infantry Division">36ID</a> on 24 December 1944.</p>
<p>In January 1945, defending a 31-mile sector along the <a href="/wiki/Rhine" title="Rhine">Rhine</a>, north and south of <a href="/wiki/Strasbourg" title="Strasbourg">Strasbourg</a>, TF Linden repulsed a number of enemy counterattacks, at Hatten and other locations, during the German "<a href="/wiki/Operation_Northwind" class="mw-redirect" title="Operation Northwind">Operation Northwind</a>" offensive. At the headquarters of the First Battalion, 242IR, Private First Class <a href="/wiki/Vito_R._Bertoldo" title="Vito R. Bertoldo">Vito R. Bertoldo</a> was waging his 48-hour defense of the Command Post, for which he received the Medal of Honor. When the battalion CP was attacked by a German tank with its 88-mm. gun and machine gun fire, Bertoldo remained at his post and with his own machine gun killed the occupants of the tank when they tried to remove mines which were blocking their advance.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> On 24 and 25 January 1945, in the Bois D’Ohlungen, and the vicinity of <a href="/wiki/Schweighouse-sur-Moder" title="Schweighouse-sur-Moder">Schweighouse-sur-Moder</a> and Neubourg, the <a href="/wiki/222nd_Infantry_Regiment_(United_States)" title="222nd Infantry Regiment (United States)">222nd Infantry Regiment</a> held a position covering a front of 7,500 yards, three times the normal frontage for a regiment in defense. After a two-hour artillery bombardment, the 222nd Infantry Regiment was repeatedly attacked by elements of the German <a href="/wiki/7th_Parachute_Division_(Germany)" title="7th Parachute Division (Germany)">7th Parachute</a>, 47th Volks Grenadier Division, and the 25th Panzer Grenadier Division. During the ensuing struggle, one company of the 222nd IR was surrounded, but withdrew from their position and infiltrated back through the Germans to the regimental lines only after exhausting all but 35 rounds of ammunition. For 24 hours, the battle raged, but the Germans were never able to break through the 222nd IR lines. For this action the 222nd Infantry Regiment was awarded the Presidential Unit Citation (2001). After these enemy attacks, TF Linden returned to reserve of the 7th Army and trained with the remainder of the 42ID which had arrived in the meantime.</p>
<p>On 14 February 1945, the 42ID as a whole entered combat, taking up defensive positions near <a href="/wiki/Haguenau" title="Haguenau">Haguenau</a> in the Hardt Forest. After a month of extensive patrolling and active defense, the 42ID went on the offensive. On the night of February 27 Germans laid down a heavy concentration of artillery and mortar fire and under this the elements of the 6th Mountain Division were withdrawn and replaced by the 221st Volksgrenadier Regiment.</p>
<p>In the brief period this unit had been in the line, it had come to respect the Rainbow and fear its patrols and raids. "Is your Division a part of Roosevelt's SS?" asked one German when captured. The remark was passed along and men kidded each other about being in the Rainbow SS.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> The 42ID attacked through the Hardt Forest, broke through the <a href="/wiki/Siegfried_Line" title="Siegfried Line">Siegfried Line</a>, 15–21 March 1945, cleared <a href="/wiki/Dahn" title="Dahn">Dahn</a> and <a href="/wiki/Busenberg" title="Busenberg">Busenberg</a>, and mopped up in that general area, while the 3rd Army created and expanded bridgeheads across the Rhine. Moving across the Rhine, 31 March 1945, the 42ID captured <a href="/wiki/Wertheim_am_Main" title="Wertheim am Main">Wertheim am Main</a>, 1 April 1945, and <a href="/wiki/W%C3%BCrzburg" title="Würzburg">Würzburg</a>, 2–6 April 1945, after a fierce battle. <a href="/wiki/Schweinfurt" title="Schweinfurt">Schweinfurt</a> fell next after hand-to-hand engagements, 9–12 April 1945. <a href="/wiki/F%C3%BCrth" title="Fürth">Fürth</a>, near <a href="/wiki/N%C3%BCrnberg" class="mw-redirect" title="Nürnberg">Nürnberg</a>, put up fanatical resistance, but was taken, 18–19 April 1945, by the 42ID.</p>
<p>On 25 April, the 42ID captured <a href="/wiki/Donauw%C3%B6rth" title="Donauwörth">Donauwörth</a> on the Danube, and, on 29 April 1945, liberated some 30,000 inmates at <a href="/wiki/Dachau_concentration_camp" title="Dachau concentration camp">Dachau</a>, a Nazi concentration camp. The 42ID passed through <a href="/wiki/Munich" title="Munich">Munich</a>, 30 April 1945, and ended its campaign by crossing the Austrian border north of <a href="/wiki/Salzburg" title="Salzburg">Salzburg</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cold_War">Cold War</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=42nd_Infantry_Division_(United_States)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Cold War">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>On 13 October 1945, the War Department published a postwar policy statement for the entire Army.<sup id="cite_ref-Evolution_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Evolution-12">[12]</a></sup> After the policy statement was published, the Army Staff prepared a postwar National Guard troop basis, which included twenty-four divisions, including the 42nd Infantry Division. Most soldiers considered the 42nd, initially organized with state troops in 1917, as a Guard formation. During the process, New York successfully petitioned the War Department for the 42nd Infantry Division. After the state governors formally notified the <a href="/wiki/National_Guard_Bureau" title="National Guard Bureau">National Guard Bureau</a> that they accepted the new troop allotments, the bureau authorized reorganization of the units with 100 percent of their officers and 80 percent of their enlisted personnel. By September 1947, the 42nd Division headquarters, along with all the other new Guard divisional headquarters, had received federal recognition.</p>
<p>In April 1963, the 42nd Division was reorganized under the <a href="/wiki/Reorganization_Objective_Army_Division" class="mw-redirect" title="Reorganization Objective Army Division">Reorganization Objective Army Division</a> structure.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> From 1967 to 1969, the division was briefly part of the Selected Reserve Force, designed to reinforce the active army in <a href="/wiki/Vietnam" title="Vietnam">Vietnam</a>. In a 1968 reorganization, the division was split between the New York Army National Guard and the <a href="/wiki/Pennsylvania_Army_National_Guard" title="Pennsylvania Army National Guard">Pennsylvania Army National Guard</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> In 1973-74, the division was converted back into an all-New York organization.</p>
<p>The 42nd Infantry Division absorbed the units of the <a href="/wiki/26th_Infantry_Division_(United_States)" title="26th Infantry Division (United States)">26th Infantry Division</a> and the <a href="/wiki/U.S._50th_Armored_Division" class="mw-redirect" title="U.S. 50th Armored Division">50th Armored Division</a> of the Massachusetts and New Jersey Army National Guard, respectively, in post-Cold War restructuring. All three divisions were severely understrength, so the assets of the three were combined into one. The 50th Brigade, created from the assets of the disbanding <a href="/wiki/50th_Armored_Division_(United_States)" title="50th Armored Division (United States)">50th Armored Division</a>, was initially assigned to the 42nd Infantry Division as an armored brigade, but was transformed to an infantry brigade combat team (BCT) in the very first years of the 21st century as part of Army modularity.</p>
<p>In the 1970s, the division headquarters was located at the armory at 125 West 14th Street in Manhattan. It was later relocated to the Glenmore Armory in Troy, New York and remains there to this day.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="War_on_terrorism">War on terrorism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=42nd_Infantry_Division_(United_States)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: War on terrorism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Since the <a href="/wiki/11_September_attacks" class="mw-redirect" title="11 September attacks">11 September attacks</a>, the 42ID has been extensively involved in the war on terrorism, in both homeland security (HLS) and expeditionary operations. The 42ID's 1-101st Cavalry led the <a href="/wiki/New_York_Army_National_Guard" title="New York Army National Guard">New York Army National Guard</a>'s efforts and provided security at Ground Zero during the rescue and then recovery efforts there. 42ID units from the <a href="/wiki/New_Jersey_Army_National_Guard" title="New Jersey Army National Guard">New Jersey Army National Guard</a> provided security at all the major river crossings into New York City and <a href="/wiki/Newark_International_Airport" class="mw-redirect" title="Newark International Airport">Newark International Airport</a> in the months following 11 September 2001.</p>
<p>The first major overseas effort of the 42ID was the deployment of elements of the 50th BCT/42ID to <a href="/wiki/Guantanamo_Bay_Naval_Base" title="Guantanamo Bay Naval Base">Guantanamo Bay Naval Base</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cuba" title="Cuba">Cuba</a>. The 2–102nd Armor Battalion deployed as ILO MP's and served with the <a href="/wiki/Joint_Detention_Operation_Group" class="mw-redirect" title="Joint Detention Operation Group">Joint Detention Operation Group</a> in the detention facility. The 2nd Battalion,113th Infantry deployed to Guantanamo Bay as well and provided security for the Joint Task Force at <a href="/wiki/Camp_Delta" class="mw-redirect" title="Camp Delta">Camp Delta</a>. While there elements of the 2nd Battalion, 102nd Armor supported the first military tribunals held at the Guantanamo Bay Detention Facility. Elements have also deployed to the <a href="/wiki/Horn_of_Africa" title="Horn of Africa">Horn of Africa</a> and <a href="/wiki/Djibouti" title="Djibouti">Djibouti</a>. New Jersey's 3/112 Field Artillery and 5/117 Cavalry deployed as an ILO Military Police Company with <a href="/wiki/89th_Military_Police_Brigade_(United_States)" title="89th Military Police Brigade (United States)">89th MP Brigade</a>/759 MP Battalion; served in <a href="/wiki/Sadr_City" title="Sadr City">Sadr City</a>, and eventually attached to the First Cavalry Division. Stationed out of Camp Cuervo (Al Rustimayah) in <a href="/wiki/Baghdad" title="Baghdad">Baghdad</a>, platoons also worked with U.S. Marines in <a href="/wiki/Fallujah" title="Fallujah">Fallujah</a>. On 4 June 2004, SSgt Carvill and Spc Duffy were killed and the following day the unit lost Sgt Dotz and Sgt Timoteo.</p>

<p>The 2/108th Infantry deployed to Iraq in 2004. In 2004-05, the 1/69 Infantry served in Iraq, eventually providing security on the <a href="/wiki/Baghdad_International_Airport" title="Baghdad International Airport">Baghdad International Airport</a> (BIAP) Road. The 42nd Combat Aviation Brigade also deployed to Iraq during this period.</p>
<p>In 2004, the division headquarters and division troops of the 42nd Infantry Division deployed as part of <a href="/wiki/Operation_Iraqi_Freedom" class="mw-redirect" title="Operation Iraqi Freedom">Operation Iraqi Freedom</a> (OIF) III, relieving the 1st Infantry Division (1ID). The division controlled the north-central Iraq area of operations. Serving as the command and control (C2) of Task Force Liberty, the 42ID took over responsibility for the area known as Multi-National Division North Central (MND-NC) including the provinces of <a href="/w/index.php?title=Salah_Ah_Din&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Salah Ah Din (page does not exist)">Salah Ah Din</a>, <a href="/wiki/Diyala_Province" class="mw-redirect" title="Diyala Province">Diyala</a>, <a href="/w/index.php?title=At_Tamamim&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="At Tamamim (page does not exist)">At Tamamim</a> (<a href="/wiki/Kirkuk" title="Kirkuk">Kirkuk</a>) and <a href="/w/index.php?title=As_Sulymaniah&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="As Sulymaniah (page does not exist)">As Sulymaniah</a> from the 1st Infantry Division on 14 February 2005. The 42ID directed the operations of: 1st BCT, <a href="/wiki/3rd_Infantry_Division_(United_States)" title="3rd Infantry Division (United States)">3ID</a>; 3rd BCT, <a href="/wiki/3rd_Infantry_Division_(United_States)" title="3rd Infantry Division (United States)">3ID</a>; the 278th RCT; 3rd- 133rd Field Artillery, 56th BCT (Texas Army National Guard); and the <a href="/wiki/116th_Cavalry_Brigade_Combat_Team" title="116th Cavalry Brigade Combat Team">116th Cavalry Brigade Combat Team</a> (<a href="/wiki/Idaho_Army_National_Guard" title="Idaho Army National Guard">Idaho</a>, Oregon, and <a href="/wiki/Montana_Army_National_Guard" title="Montana Army National Guard">Montana Army National Guard</a>). Soldiers conducted combat actions and raids, seized weapons caches, destroyed improvised explosive devices (IEDs), trained Iraqi army forces, and worked on reconstruction to ensure free elections. 1-69th Infantry of the 42ID manned the checkpoint where Italian <a href="/wiki/SISMI" title="SISMI">SISMI</a> officer <a href="/wiki/Nicola_Calipari" title="Nicola Calipari">Nicola Calipari</a> was shot and killed.</p>
<p>Maj. Gen. Joseph J. Taluto, commanding general of the division during its deployment, commended the many contributions of the 42ID led “Task Force Liberty.” Vice Chief of Staff of the Army (VCSA), Gen. Richard Cody, saluted members of the 42ID at the unit's homecoming ceremony.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The division's Headquarters and Headquarters Company was awarded the Army Meritorious Unit Commendation for its service in Iraq.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2008, 26 company-sized elements of the <a href="/wiki/50th_Infantry_Brigade_Combat_Team_(United_States)" title="50th Infantry Brigade Combat Team (United States)">50th Infantry Brigade Combat Team</a> (IBCT), headquartered at <a href="/wiki/Fort_Dix" title="Fort Dix">Fort Dix</a>, New Jersey, deployed to Iraq bringing the total number of NJ National Guard soldiers sent to Iraq and <a href="/wiki/Afghanistan" title="Afghanistan">Afghanistan</a> to over 3,200. These elements of the 50th IBCT were mobilized for one year, including stateside training and “boots on the ground” in theater. Premobilization training began in 2007 and took place in New Jersey, with further OIF specific preparation conducted at other Army installations out-of-state. Originally slated to deploy to Iraq in 2010, these elements deployed earlier as a result of changes needed to comply with new Department of Defense (DoD) policies. Earlier, in 2007, the DoD had reduced the amount of time units spend overseas in a combat theater, which in turn shifted mobilization schedules and required earlier deployments than anticipated. Elements of the 50th IBCT had deployed in support of Operation Iraqi Freedom (OIF) previously in 2004.</p>
<p>In 2008, the 27th Infantry Brigade Combat Team (IBCT), headquartered in <a href="/wiki/Syracuse,_New_York" title="Syracuse, New York">Syracuse, New York</a>, was mobilized and deployed to Afghanistan to train <a href="/wiki/Afghan_National_Army" title="Afghan National Army">Afghan National Army</a> (ANA) and police forces. Initial personnel from the 27th IBCT deployed in late 2007, with the majority of the approximately 1,700 service members deployed by mid-2008.</p>
<p>In conjunction around February 2008, soldiers of the 86th Infantry Brigade Combat Team were beginning to receive notification of their upcoming deployment. The Brigade Commander at the time was Colonel William F. Roy. In 2009, the brigade did a rotation at JRTC in Fort Polk, LA. In December 2009, the brigade was officially mobilized and to report to <a href="/wiki/Camp_Atterbury" title="Camp Atterbury">Camp Atterbury</a>, IN. While in Indiana, the brigade trained and prepped for their future deployment to Afghanistan. After receiving numerous replacements and volunteer soldiers, the brigade was sent back to JRTC for one more rotation before they left the country.</p>
<p>The majority of the brigade landed in Afghanistan in early March. The brigade headquarters was on <a href="/wiki/Bagram_Airfield" title="Bagram Airfield">Bagram Airfield</a> in RC-East. The brigade was tasked with numerous missions across eastern Afghanistan. The missions included partnering with the <a href="/wiki/Afghan_National_Security_Forces" title="Afghan National Security Forces">Afghan National Security Forces</a> (ANSF), assisting in the Government of the <a href="/wiki/Afghanistan" title="Afghanistan">Islamic Republic of Afghanistan</a>, and securing over 30,000 soldiers on Bagram Airfield while ensuring the base was continuing its daily operations. The brigade left Afghanistan in early December returning to Camp Atterbury, IN. The brigade was released from federal service and returned to the northeast to continue their respective state missions. Several component units of the brigade were awarded the Meritorious Unit Commendation for their service from 8 March 2010 – 4 December 2010 while deployed in support of <a href="/wiki/Operation_Enduring_Freedom" title="Operation Enduring Freedom">Operation Enduring Freedom</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Deaths_of_Esposito_and_Allen">Deaths of Esposito and Allen</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=42nd_Infantry_Division_(United_States)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Deaths of Esposito and Allen">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Deaths_of_Phillip_Esposito_and_Louis_Allen" title="Deaths of Phillip Esposito and Louis Allen">Deaths of Phillip Esposito and Louis Allen</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Captain_(United_States_uniformed_services)" class="mw-redirect" title="Captain (United States uniformed services)">Captain</a> Phillip Esposito and <a href="/wiki/First_Lieutenant" class="mw-redirect" title="First Lieutenant">First Lieutenant</a> Louis Allen were killed on June 7, 2005, at <a href="/wiki/Forward_Operating_Base_Danger" title="Forward Operating Base Danger">Forward Operating Base Danger</a> in <a href="/wiki/Tikrit" title="Tikrit">Tikrit</a>, <a href="/wiki/Iraq" title="Iraq">Iraq</a> by a <a href="/wiki/M18A1_Claymore_mine" class="mw-redirect" title="M18A1 Claymore mine">M18A1 Claymore mine</a> placed in the window of Esposito's office. Esposito was commander, Headquarters and Headquarters Company, 42ID. Allen had recently arrived in Iraq to serve as Esposito's executive officer, or second in command.</p>
<p>Military investigators determined that the mine was deliberately placed and detonated with the intention of killing Esposito and Allen. <a href="/wiki/Staff_Sergeant" class="mw-redirect" title="Staff Sergeant">Staff Sergeant</a> Alberto B. Martinez from the officers' unit was charged in the killing but was acquitted in a court martial trial at <a href="/wiki/Fort_Bragg_(North_Carolina)" class="mw-redirect" title="Fort Bragg (North Carolina)">Fort Bragg</a>, <a href="/wiki/North_Carolina" title="North Carolina">North Carolina</a>, on December 4, 2008. The case was one of only two publicly announced alleged <a href="/wiki/Fragging" title="Fragging">fragging</a> incidents among American forces during the <a href="/wiki/Iraq_war" class="mw-redirect" title="Iraq war">Iraq war</a>.</p>
<p>Subsequently, Siobhan Esposito and Barbara Allen, the widows of the officers, have continued to pursue justice for their husbands' deaths, pushing for the military to strictly enforce regulations that prohibit threats against superiors and require soldiers to report violations of "good order and discipline." <sup id="cite_ref-zucJustice_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-zucJustice-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Homeland_security">Homeland security</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=42nd_Infantry_Division_(United_States)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Homeland security">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>During the Cold War and through the present, the 42ID and its soldiers have been regularly called upon for homeland security missions including disaster relief (such as <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Katrina" title="Hurricane Katrina">Hurricane Katrina</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Floyd" title="Hurricane Floyd">Hurricane Floyd</a>), emergency preparedness (such as Y2K missions), airport security, critical infrastructure protection, border security, bridge and tunnel security, as well as rail/train station security.</p>
<p>Several first responders to the 11 September 2001 attacks were members of the 42ID, and led much of the military support to the relief and recovery efforts. The 42ID was part of the relief team for the duration of the effort at Ground Zero in New York City. The 42ID has also been actively engaged in missions supporting <a href="/wiki/Operation_Noble_Eagle" title="Operation Noble Eagle">Operation Noble Eagle</a>.</p>
<p>In October 2005, elements of the 42nd ID/50th Brigade were activated for Operation Hurricane Katrina relief in the city of New Orleans. The 2–102nd Armor and the 1–114th Infantry were called to active duty and the combined unit shipped to Louisiana to provide security for FEMA. The 50th Brigade arrived at <a href="/w/index.php?title=Belle_Chase_Naval_Air_Station&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Belle Chase Naval Air Station (page does not exist)">Belle Chase Naval Air Station</a> and from there went to the <a href="/wiki/New_Orleans_Convention_Center" class="mw-redirect" title="New Orleans Convention Center">New Orleans Convention Center</a>. From there, the elements of the 42nd ID sent teams to various parts of the city on various missions of security ranging from roving patrol to security escort for the New Orleans Fire Department and other relief agencies</p>
