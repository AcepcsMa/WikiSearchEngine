<p>See <a href="/wiki/Standard_German_phonology" title="Standard German phonology">Standard German phonology</a> for a more thorough look at the sounds of German. For information on how to convert spelling to pronunciation, see <a href="/wiki/German_orthography#Grapheme-to-phoneme_correspondences" title="German orthography">German orthography §&#160;Grapheme-to-phoneme correspondences</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Notes">Notes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Help:IPA/Standard_German&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Notes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="reflist" style="list-style-type: decimal;">
<div class="mw-references-wrap mw-references-columns">
<ol class="references">
<li id="cite_note-fortislenis-1"><span class="mw-cite-backlink">^ <a href="#cite_ref-fortislenis_1-0"><sup><i><b>a</b></i></sup></a> <a href="#cite_ref-fortislenis_1-1"><sup><i><b>b</b></i></sup></a> <a href="#cite_ref-fortislenis_1-2"><sup><i><b>c</b></i></sup></a> <a href="#cite_ref-fortislenis_1-3"><sup><i><b>d</b></i></sup></a> <a href="#cite_ref-fortislenis_1-4"><sup><i><b>e</b></i></sup></a> <a href="#cite_ref-fortislenis_1-5"><sup><i><b>f</b></i></sup></a> <a href="#cite_ref-fortislenis_1-6"><sup><i><b>g</b></i></sup></a></span> <span class="reference-text">In Austrian Standard German and Swiss Standard German, the lenis obstruents <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/b, d, ɡ, z, dʒ, ʒ/</span> are voiceless <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[b̥, d̥, ɡ̊, z̥, d̥ʒ̊, ʒ̊]</span> and are distinguished from <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/p, t, k, s, tʃ, ʃ/</span> only by articulatory strength (<span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/v/</span> is really voiced). The distinction is also retained word-finally. In German Standard German, voiceless <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[b̥, d̥, ɡ̊, z̥, d̥ʒ̊, ʒ̊]</span> as well as <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[v̥]</span> occur allophonically after fortis obstruents and, for <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/b, d, ɡ/</span>, often also word-initially. See <a href="/wiki/Fortis_and_lenis" title="Fortis and lenis">fortis and lenis</a>.</span></li>
<li id="cite_note-devoicing-2"><span class="mw-cite-backlink">^ <a href="#cite_ref-devoicing_2-0"><sup><i><b>a</b></i></sup></a> <a href="#cite_ref-devoicing_2-1"><sup><i><b>b</b></i></sup></a> <a href="#cite_ref-devoicing_2-2"><sup><i><b>c</b></i></sup></a></span> <span class="reference-text">In German Standard German, voiced stops <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/b, d, ɡ/</span> are devoiced to <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[p, t, k]</span> at the end of a syllable.</span></li>
<li id="cite_note-r-allophones-3"><span class="mw-cite-backlink">^ <a href="#cite_ref-r-allophones_3-0"><sup><i><b>a</b></i></sup></a> <a href="#cite_ref-r-allophones_3-1"><sup><i><b>b</b></i></sup></a> <a href="#cite_ref-r-allophones_3-2"><sup><i><b>c</b></i></sup></a></span> <span class="reference-text">Pronunciation of <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/r/</span> in German varies according to region and speaker. While older prescriptive pronunciation dictionaries allowed only <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[r]</span>, that pronunciation is now found mainly in Switzerland, <a href="/wiki/Bavaria" title="Bavaria">Bavaria</a> and Austria. In other regions, the uvular pronunciation prevails, mainly as a fricative/approximant <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[ʁ]</span>. In many regions except for most parts of Switzerland, the <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/r/</span> in the <a href="/wiki/Syllable_coda" class="mw-redirect" title="Syllable coda">syllable coda</a> is vocalized to <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[ɐ̯]</span> after long vowels or after all vowels, and <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/ər/</span> is pronounced as <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[ɐ]</span></span></li>
<li id="cite_note-4"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-4">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">Initial vowels are usually preceded by <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[<a href="/wiki/Glottal_stop" title="Glottal stop">ʔ</a>]</span>, except in <a href="/wiki/Swiss_Standard_German" title="Swiss Standard German">Swiss Standard German</a>.</span></li>
<li id="cite_note-postalveolarlenis-5"><span class="mw-cite-backlink">^ <a href="#cite_ref-postalveolarlenis_5-0"><sup><i><b>a</b></i></sup></a> <a href="#cite_ref-postalveolarlenis_5-1"><sup><i><b>b</b></i></sup></a></span> <span class="reference-text">Many speakers lack the lenis <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/ʒ/</span> and replace it with its fortis counterpart <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/ʃ/</span> (<a href="#CITEREFHall2003">Hall 2003</a>, p.&#160;42). The same applies to the corresponding lenis <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/dʒ/</span>, which also tends to be replaced with its fortis counterpart <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/tʃ/</span>. According to the prescriptive standard, such pronunciations are not correct.</span></li>
<li id="cite_note-openvowels-6"><span class="mw-cite-backlink">^ <a href="#cite_ref-openvowels_6-0"><sup><i><b>a</b></i></sup></a> <a href="#cite_ref-openvowels_6-1"><sup><i><b>b</b></i></sup></a> <a href="#cite_ref-openvowels_6-2"><sup><i><b>c</b></i></sup></a></span> <span class="reference-text">The Austrian and Swiss pronunciation of <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/a/</span> and <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/aː/</span> is <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[ɑ]</span> and <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[ɑː]</span> (<a href="#CITEREFMoosm.C3.BCllerSchmidBrandst.C3.A4tter2015">Moosmüller, Schmid &amp; Brandstätter 2015</a>). In some northern German dialects influenced by Low German there may be <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[æ~a]</span> for /a/ but <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[ɑː]</span> for <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/aː/</span> thus also having a difference in vowel quality not just length. (see e.g. <a href="#CITEREFWierzbickaRynkowska1992">Wierzbicka &amp; Rynkowska 1992</a>, pp.&#160;412–415).</span></li>
<li id="cite_note-7"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-7">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">In <a href="/wiki/Northern_Germany" title="Northern Germany">Northern Germany</a>, <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/ɛː/</span> often merges with <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/eː/</span> to <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[<a href="/wiki/Close-mid_front_unrounded_vowel" title="Close-mid front unrounded vowel">eː</a>]</span>.</span></li>
<li id="cite_note-nasalV-8"><span class="mw-cite-backlink">^ <a href="#cite_ref-nasalV_8-0"><sup><i><b>a</b></i></sup></a> <a href="#cite_ref-nasalV_8-1"><sup><i><b>b</b></i></sup></a> <a href="#cite_ref-nasalV_8-2"><sup><i><b>c</b></i></sup></a> <a href="#cite_ref-nasalV_8-3"><sup><i><b>d</b></i></sup></a> <a href="#cite_ref-nasalV_8-4"><sup><i><b>e</b></i></sup></a> <a href="#cite_ref-nasalV_8-5"><sup><i><b>f</b></i></sup></a> <a href="#cite_ref-nasalV_8-6"><sup><i><b>g</b></i></sup></a> <a href="#cite_ref-nasalV_8-7"><sup><i><b>h</b></i></sup></a></span> <span class="reference-text">The nasal vowels occur in French loans. They are long <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[ãː, ɛ̃ː, õː, œ̃ː]</span> when stressed and short <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[ã, ɛ̃, õ, œ̃]</span> when unstressed. In colloquial speech they may be replaced with <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[aŋ, ɛŋ, ɔŋ, œŋ]</span> irrespective of length, and the <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[ŋ]</span> in these sequences may optionally be <a href="/wiki/Assimilation_(linguistics)" class="mw-redirect" title="Assimilation (linguistics)">assimilated</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Place_of_articulation" title="Place of articulation">place of articulation</a> of a following consonant, e.g. <i>Ensemble</i> <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[aŋˈsaŋbl̩]</span> or <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[anˈsambl̩]</span> for <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[ãˈsãːbl̩]</span> (<a href="#CITEREFMangold2005">Mangold 2005</a>, p.&#160;65).</span></li>
<li id="cite_note-middiphthongs-9"><span class="mw-cite-backlink">^ <a href="#cite_ref-middiphthongs_9-0"><sup><i><b>a</b></i></sup></a> <a href="#cite_ref-middiphthongs_9-1"><sup><i><b>b</b></i></sup></a></span> <span class="reference-text">The diphthongs <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/ɛɪ, ɔʊ/</span> occur only in loanwords (mostly from English), such as <i>okay</i>. Depending on the speaker and the region, they may be monophthongized to <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[eː, oː]</span> (or <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[e, o]</span> in an unstressed syllable-final position). Thus, the aforementioned word <i>okay</i> can be pronounced as either <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[ɔʊˈkɛɪ]</span> or <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[oˈkeː]</span>.</span></li>
<li id="cite_note-10"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-10">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text"><span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[œːɐ̯]</span> or <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[øːr]</span> is the German rendering of the English <span class="smallcaps"><span style="font-variant: small-caps; text-transform: lowercase;">NURSE</span></span> vowel <span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ɜːr/: 'ur' in 'fur'">ɜːr</span></span>/</a></span></span>. It also appears in certain French surnames, e.g. <i>Vass<b>eur</b></i> (<a href="#CITEREFKrech_et_al.2009">Krech et al. 2009</a>, pp.&#160;64, 142).</span></li>
<li id="cite_note-shorttense-11"><span class="mw-cite-backlink">^ <a href="#cite_ref-shorttense_11-0"><sup><i><b>a</b></i></sup></a> <a href="#cite_ref-shorttense_11-1"><sup><i><b>b</b></i></sup></a> <a href="#cite_ref-shorttense_11-2"><sup><i><b>c</b></i></sup></a> <a href="#cite_ref-shorttense_11-3"><sup><i><b>d</b></i></sup></a> <a href="#cite_ref-shorttense_11-4"><sup><i><b>e</b></i></sup></a> <a href="#cite_ref-shorttense_11-5"><sup><i><b>f</b></i></sup></a> <a href="#cite_ref-shorttense_11-6"><sup><i><b>g</b></i></sup></a></span> <span class="reference-text"><span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[a, e, i, o, ø, u, y]</span>, the short versions of the long vowels <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[aː, eː, iː, oː, øː, uː, yː]</span>, are used at the end of unstressed syllables before the accented syllable and occur mainly in loanwords. In native words, the accent is generally on the first syllable, and syllables before the accent other than prepositional prefixes are rare but occasionally occur, e.g. in <i>jedoch</i> <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[jeˈdɔx]</span>, <i>soeben</i> <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[zoˈʔeːbn̩]</span>, <i>vielleicht</i> <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[fiˈlaɪçt]</span> etc. In casual speech short <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[e, i, o, ø, u, y]</span> preceding a phonemic consonant (i.e., not a <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[ʔ]</span>) may be replaced with <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[ɛ, ɪ, ɔ, œ, ʊ, ʏ]</span>, e.g. <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[jɛˈdɔx]</span>, <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[fɪˈlaɪçt]</span> (<a href="#CITEREFMangold2005">Mangold 2005</a>, p.&#160;65).</span></li>
