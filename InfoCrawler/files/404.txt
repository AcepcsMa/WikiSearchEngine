<p><b>St. Egidien</b> on Egidienplatz is the former <a href="/wiki/Order_of_St._Benedict" class="mw-redirect" title="Order of St. Benedict">Benedictine</a> <a href="/wiki/Abbey" title="Abbey">Abbey</a> of <a href="/wiki/Saint_Giles" title="Saint Giles">Saint Giles</a> (<i>Egidienskirche</i>), now a church in the former free imperial city of <a href="/wiki/Nuremberg" title="Nuremberg">Nuremberg</a>, southern <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a>. It is considered a significant contribution to the baroque church architecture of <a href="/wiki/Middle_Franconia" title="Middle Franconia">Middle Franconia</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-HLB:N.C3.BCrnberg_Entwicklung_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HLB:N.C3.BCrnberg_Entwicklung-1">[1]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=St._Egidien,_Nuremberg&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:St_Egidien_-_N%C3%BCrnberg_022.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dd/St_Egidien_-_N%C3%BCrnberg_022.JPG/220px-St_Egidien_-_N%C3%BCrnberg_022.JPG" width="220" height="330" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dd/St_Egidien_-_N%C3%BCrnberg_022.JPG/330px-St_Egidien_-_N%C3%BCrnberg_022.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dd/St_Egidien_-_N%C3%BCrnberg_022.JPG/440px-St_Egidien_-_N%C3%BCrnberg_022.JPG 2x" data-file-width="4000" data-file-height="6000" /></a>

<p>The first church building was probably built in the years 1120/1130 on the site of the second, northern Nuremberg royal court. The royal courts administered royal possessions, agriculture and forestry. Thus, it had the status of a royal church.</p>
<p>Around the year 1140 <a href="/wiki/Conrad_III_of_Germany" title="Conrad III of Germany">Emperor Conrad III</a> and his wife Gertrud raised the foundation to the rank of a benedictine abbey and endowed it generously. They made Carus, Abbot of the <a href="/wiki/Scots_Monastery,_Regensburg" title="Scots Monastery, Regensburg">Scots Monastery, Regensburg</a>, their royal chaplain and the first Abbot of St Egidien. The monastery was rich immediately and subordinate in secular terms only to the <a href="/wiki/Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Holy Roman Emperor">Holy Roman Emperor</a>. The first monks came from the <a href="/wiki/Scots_Monastery,_Regensburg" title="Scots Monastery, Regensburg">Scots Monastery, Regensburg</a> and <a href="/wiki/St._James%27s_Abbey,_W%C3%BCrzburg" title="St. James's Abbey, W√ºrzburg">St. James's Abbey, W√ºrzburg</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>It was a three-aisled basilica in the Romanesque style.</p>
<p>In 1418, the monastery was impoverished and in debt. The altar vessels were mortgaged, and the <a href="/wiki/Scots_Monastery,_Regensburg" title="Scots Monastery, Regensburg">Scots Monastery, Regensburg</a> no longer guaranteed the debt. The abbey was taken over by German Benedictines from Reichenbach After the take-over the monastery was partially rebuilt, and the church and chapels were renovated. The Irish monks had to come to terms with the new regime or leave the abbey.</p>
<p>At the Reformation in 1525 the monastery was dissolved, and the monastic estates transferred to the city authorities. After the <a href="/wiki/Peace_of_Augsburg" title="Peace of Augsburg">Peace of Augsburg</a> there were two unsuccessful attempts to recover the former monastic estates for the Benedictine order, firstly in 1578 by the Scottish Bishop <a href="/wiki/John_Lesley" title="John Lesley">John Lesley</a> on behalf of <a href="/wiki/Mary,_Queen_of_Scots" title="Mary, Queen of Scots">Mary, Queen of Scots</a>, and from 1629 to 1631 by a Commission for the <a href="/wiki/Prince-Bishopric_of_Bamberg" title="Prince-Bishopric of Bamberg">Prince-Bishopric of Bamberg</a> to implement a <a href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic" class="mw-redirect" title="Roman Catholic">Roman Catholic</a> Restitution Edict. On 6 and 7 July 1696 a fire destroyed the monastery and church.</p>
<p>The church was rebuilt in the baroque style. The foundation stone was laid on 14 October 1711. The architects were Johann Trost and Gottlieb Trost. It was the largest construction project in Nuremberg in the 18th century. The stucco decorations were done by Donato Polli. The frescos were painted by Daniel Preisler and Johann Martin Schuster.</p>
<p>The church was badly damaged during the <a href="/wiki/Second_World_War" class="mw-redirect" title="Second World War">Second World War</a> in an air raid on 2 January 1945. The roof on the nave, crossing, transepts and choir collapsed and the outer walls were badly damaged. It was rebuilt between 1946 and 1959<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> by the Nuremberg architect Rudolf Gr√∂schel in an economic interpretative way, as the costs to reconstruct the baroque interior with its ornate detail was unaffordable at the time.</p>
