			<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">St Botolph's Church, Quarrington</h1>

<p><b>St Botolph's Church</b> is an <a href="/wiki/Church_of_England" title="Church of England">Anglican</a> place of worship in the village of <a href="/wiki/Quarrington,_Lincolnshire" title="Quarrington, Lincolnshire">Quarrington</a>, part of the <a href="/wiki/Civil_parish" title="Civil parish">civil parish</a> of <a href="/wiki/Sleaford" title="Sleaford">Sleaford</a> in <a href="/wiki/Lincolnshire" title="Lincolnshire">Lincolnshire</a>, England. The area has been settled since at least the <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Saxon_England" class="mw-redirect" title="Anglo-Saxon England">Anglo-Saxon period</a>, and a church existed at Quarrington by the time <i><a href="/wiki/Domesday_Book" title="Domesday Book">Domesday</a></i> was compiled in 1086, when it formed part of <a href="/wiki/Ramsey_Abbey" title="Ramsey Abbey">Ramsey Abbey</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Fief" title="Fief">fee</a>. It was granted to <a href="/wiki/Haverholme_Priory" title="Haverholme Priory">Haverholme Priory</a> about 1165, and the Abbey claimed the <a href="/wiki/Advowson" title="Advowson">right to present the rector</a> in the 13th century. This right was claimed by the <a href="/wiki/Bishop_of_Lincoln" title="Bishop of Lincoln">Bishop of Lincoln</a> during the early 16th century, and then passed to <a href="/w/index.php?title=Robert_Carre&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Robert Carre (page does not exist)">Robert Carre</a> and his descendants after Carre acquired a manor at Quarrington. With capacity for 124 people, the church serves the <a href="/wiki/Ecclesiastic_parish" class="mw-redirect" title="Ecclesiastic parish">ecclesiastic parish</a> of Quarrington with Old Sleaford and, as of 2009, had an average congregation of 50.</p>
<p>Recognised for its age and <a href="/wiki/Tracery" title="Tracery">tracery</a>, the church has been designated a grade II* <a href="/wiki/Listed_building" title="Listed building">listed building</a>. It has a tower and spire with a <a href="/wiki/Nave" title="Nave">nave</a> and north aisle ending at a <a href="/wiki/Chancel" title="Chancel">chancel</a> at the east end. The oldest parts of the building date to the 13th century, although substantial rebuilding took place over the following century. Renovations followed and the local architect Charles Kirk the Younger carried out <a href="/wiki/Victorian_restoration" title="Victorian restoration">restoration</a> work in 1862–63, when he added the chancel in his parents' memory. The high interior's three bays of <a href="/wiki/Arcade_(architecture)" title="Arcade (architecture)">arcading</a> correspond to the three windows in the nave's south wall and the north aisle wall; those on the south wall are unusual for the hexagons and <a href="/wiki/Trefoil" title="Trefoil">trefoils</a> in the <a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/reticulated" class="extiw" title="wikt:reticulated">reticulated</a> designs.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=St_Botolph%27s_Church,_Quarrington&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span id="Location,_services_and_facilities"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Location.2C_services_and_facilities">Location, services and facilities</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=St_Botolph%27s_Church,_Quarrington&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Location, services and facilities">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>St Botolph's Church is the <a href="/wiki/Parish_church" title="Parish church">parish church</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Benefice" title="Benefice">benefice</a> of <a href="/wiki/Quarrington,_Lincolnshire" title="Quarrington, Lincolnshire">Quarrington</a> with Old Sleaford, which encompasses most of the village of Quarrington in the English <a href="/wiki/Non-metropolitan_county" title="Non-metropolitan county">non-metropolitan county</a> of <a href="/wiki/Lincolnshire" title="Lincolnshire">Lincolnshire</a>. The benefice is a <a href="/wiki/Rectory" class="mw-redirect" title="Rectory">rectory</a> and falls within the <a href="/wiki/Deanery_of_Lafford" title="Deanery of Lafford">deanery of Lafford</a> and the archdeaconry and <a href="/wiki/Diocese_of_Lincoln" title="Diocese of Lincoln">diocese of Lincoln</a>;<sup id="cite_ref-:4_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:4-1">[1]</a></sup> the priest-in-charge is the Reverend Mark Stephen Thomson, who took over from the Reverend Sandra Rhys Benham in 2016.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Dedicated to <a href="/wiki/Botwulf_of_Thorney" title="Botwulf of Thorney">St Botolph</a>, the church is on Town Road.<sup id="cite_ref-:4_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:4-1">[1]</a></sup> The rectory was constructed in about 2000 and has a study area used as the parish office.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Along with 20 spaces in the choir stalls, the <a href="/wiki/Nave" title="Nave">nave</a> and aisle pews can comfortably seat 124 adults and "149 at a pinch"; as of 2009, the average congregation size for the main Sunday service was 50, about half of whom were retired and 1 to 3 were under 16. In 2004 a log cabin-style building was constructed by Pinelog Ltd using funds from the Parochial Church Council. It serves as a robing space for choristers and can be used as a meeting room for up to 30 people. Equipped with disabled toilet facilities, the building also serves as a Sunday School.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Architecture_and_fittings">Architecture and fittings</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=St_Botolph%27s_Church,_Quarrington&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Architecture and fittings">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>St Botolph's Church consists of a west tower adjoining a nave with a north aisle; at the end of the nave is a chancel with a chapel on the north side.<sup id="cite_ref-nhle_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nhle-6">[6]</a></sup> Owing to its age, the "excellent" tracery and a "very good" 14th century door, the church has been listed at grade II* on the <a href="/wiki/Listed_building" title="Listed building">Statutory List of Buildings of Special Architectural or Historic Interest</a> in the United Kingdom since 1949,<sup id="cite_ref-nhle_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nhle-6">[6]</a></sup> recognising it as "particularly important&#160;... of more than special interest".<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Incorporating a window and two-light openings for the bell, the tower and its spire have been dated to the mid-14th century, although its pinnacles were replaced in 1887.<sup id="cite_ref-nhle_6-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nhle-6">[6]</a></sup> The antiquarian <a href="/wiki/Edward_Trollope" title="Edward Trollope">Edward Trollope</a> did not like the spire's design, saying that it "looks as if it had slipped down".<sup id="cite_ref-t430_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-t430-8">[8]</a></sup> Built in a <a href="/wiki/English_Gothic_architecture#Decorated_Gothic" title="English Gothic architecture">Decorated Gothic</a> style, the tower joins to the nave with a triple-chamferred arch.<sup id="cite_ref-pevsner_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pevsner-9">[9]</a></sup> The nave has been heavily restored and contains elements from a range of periods. The architectural historian Sir <a href="/wiki/Nikolaus_Pevsner" title="Nikolaus Pevsner">Nikolaus Pevsner</a> refers to the south wall as "puzzling" because of the tracery—it is unclear what is "Victorian fancy" and what is "correct restoration". The wall itself is 14th century (Pevsner suggests about 1300) and incorporates a priest's doorway with mouldings, capitals and a bust of a man; the windows to its east have been restored, but follow a 14th-century reticulated style, although unusually they feature hexagons with pointed trefoils: "the oddest patterns", as Pevsner puts it.<sup id="cite_ref-nhle_6-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nhle-6">[6]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-pevsner_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pevsner-9">[9]</a></sup> The north aisle is from the 13th century, although a 12th-century doorway sits between its late-14th-century windows.<sup id="cite_ref-nhle_6-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nhle-6">[6]</a></sup> At the eastern end of the nave is the chancel, which has a polygonal (quinquangular) apse and was constructed between 1862 and 1863 by Charles Kirk the younger in memory of his parents, the elder <a href="/wiki/Charles_Kirk_(senior)" title="Charles Kirk (senior)">Charles Kirk</a> and his wife Elizabeth. Its windows were made by <a href="/wiki/Ward_and_Hughes" title="Ward and Hughes">Ward and Hughes</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-t430_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-t430-8">[8]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-pevsner_9-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pevsner-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>Both Pevsner and <a href="/wiki/Historic_England" title="Historic England">Historic England</a> regard the interior as high relative to the Church's small size.<sup id="cite_ref-nhle_6-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nhle-6">[6]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-pevsner_9-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pevsner-9">[9]</a></sup> The nave has arcading across three bays with chamfered arches and capitals; the arcades on the north side are <a href="/wiki/English_Gothic_architecture#Early_English" title="English Gothic architecture">Early English</a> and, dating to the 13th century, are the earliest visible parts of the church. Historic England suggest that this nave was likely added to an earlier, now-lost nave.<sup id="cite_ref-nhle_6-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nhle-6">[6]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-pevsner_9-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pevsner-9">[9]</a></sup> The westernmost arch is wider and shorter than the rest, giving what Trollope called "a very awkward appearance".<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> The chancel arch follows a 13th-century style, although it was built with the 1862–63 work. Inside the chancel, shafts with floral capitals adorn the walls, while the tiled flooring and painted roof give it a "rich" appearance. The nave includes 19th and 20th century windows by H. Hughes (1877) and <a href="/wiki/Burlison_and_Grylls" title="Burlison and Grylls">Burlison and Grylls</a>, while another (dated 1917) commemorates the Barrett family. <a href="/wiki/Morris_%26_Co." title="Morris &amp; Co.">Morris and Co.</a> worked on a window in the north aisle in 1935.<sup id="cite_ref-nhle_6-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nhle-6">[6]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-pevsner_9-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pevsner-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>The church's fixtures include a 19th-century pulpit and choir stalls, and a chancel screen in a Decorated style; a font from the 14th century has foliage designs on the bowl, but lacks a base.<sup id="cite_ref-nhle_6-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nhle-6">[6]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> The earliest record of an organ at St Botolph's is from 1867, when one was opened for the chancel by Bevington; it has since been moved to <a href="/wiki/Pointon" title="Pointon">Pointon</a>. In 1915, the church paid for an organ bought from N. E. Snow to be fixed by Cousans at the cost of £130. The present organ, however, was built in 1929 by J. J. Binns and has two manuals and a pedalboard.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>Some of the memorials in St Botolph's date to the 18th and 19th century,<sup id="cite_ref-nhle_6-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nhle-6">[6]</a></sup> although a plaque commemorating one Thomas Appleby dates to 1683 and several other 17th century tablets were noted by <a href="/wiki/Gervase_Holles" title="Gervase Holles">Gervase Holles</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> A tablet to Romaine Hervey (d. 1837) by J. J. Saunders is inside the church;<sup id="cite_ref-pevsner_9-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pevsner-9">[9]</a></sup> elsewhere in the grounds are tombstones belonging to the Sharpe and Kirk families and markers to the Shannons, including the artist <a href="/wiki/Charles_Haslewood_Shannon" title="Charles Haslewood Shannon">Charles Haslewood Shannon</a> (d. 1937),<sup id="cite_ref-nhle_6-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nhle-6">[6]</a></sup> whose father, Rev. Frederick William Shannon, was rector of Quarrington and Old Sleaford from 1861 to 1910.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> The churchyard contains the <a href="/wiki/Commonwealth_War_Graves_Commission" title="Commonwealth War Graves Commission">Commonwealth war graves</a> of four British Army soldiers of the <a href="/wiki/First_World_War" class="mw-redirect" title="First World War">First World War</a> and four <a href="/wiki/Royal_Air_Force" title="Royal Air Force">Royal Air Force</a> airmen of the <a href="/wiki/Second_World_War" class="mw-redirect" title="Second World War">Second World War</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=St_Botolph%27s_Church,_Quarrington&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span id="Background,_origins_and_advowson"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Background.2C_origins_and_advowson">Background, origins and advowson</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=St_Botolph%27s_Church,_Quarrington&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Background, origins and advowson">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Ramsey_Abbey" title="Ramsey Abbey">Ramsey Abbey</a> possessed a manor at Quarrington from about 1051 which, by the time <i>Domesday</i> was compiled, included two churches.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> The antiquarian James Creasey suggested that the missing church was <a href="/w/index.php?title=All_Saints%27_Church,_Old_Sleaford&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="All Saints' Church, Old Sleaford (page does not exist)">All Saints' in Old Sleaford</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> where the Abbey held a manor as <a href="/wiki/Soke_(legal)" title="Soke (legal)">sokeland</a> of Quarrington,<sup id="cite_ref-MR11-14_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MR11-14-19">[19]</a></sup> while Trollope thought it had been lost, buried "probably in a farm yard now occupied by Mrs. Cubley".<sup id="cite_ref-Trollope428_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Trollope428-20">[20]</a></sup> In 1909, two amateur local historians, H. Greenval and F. Cenlices, reckoned that both churches were lost and had stood on land marked by stone crosses near Tellgate on the Sleaford–Folkingham road and on Stump Cross Hill.<sup id="cite_ref-WL3_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WL3-21">[21]</a></sup> But in 1979, the local historians Christine Mahany and David Roffe reassessed the <i>Domesday</i> evidence and, after analysing the manorial structure, documentary evidence and the history of the <a href="/wiki/Advowson" title="Advowson">advowson</a> of All Saints', concluded that it was the second church in the Abbey's manor of Quarrington, and that the other church was St Botolph's or a predecessor.<sup id="cite_ref-WL3_21-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WL3-21">[21]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-MR11-14_19-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MR11-14-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>Henry Selvein, a knight, held Quarrington of the Abbey and in about 1165 granted it to <a href="/wiki/Haverholme_Priory" title="Haverholme Priory">Haverholme Priory</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> who presented Alexander de Brauncewell as rector in 1218.<sup id="cite_ref-Trollope429_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Trollope429-23">[23]</a></sup> The priory are known to have presented rectors in 1248 and 1269 as well.<sup id="cite_ref-Trollope429_23-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Trollope429-23">[23]</a></sup> The Bishop of Lincoln, who had held a manor at Quarrington since <i>Domesday</i>, claimed the right to present its rector in the early 16th century. Bishop <a href="/wiki/Henry_Holbeach" title="Henry Holbeach">Holbeach</a> alienated the manor to the Crown in 1547 and it was eventually purchased by the Sleaford merchant <a href="/w/index.php?title=Robert_Carre&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Robert Carre (page does not exist)">Robert Carre</a>, but the Bishop still tried to present the rector; Carre protested and the dispute was settled when Lord Chief Justice <a href="/wiki/Edward_Coke" title="Edward Coke">Coke</a> ruled that Carre would present in future.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Construction_and_later_history">Construction and later history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=St_Botolph%27s_Church,_Quarrington&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Construction and later history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A slender chancel arch existed until the mid-19th century and might have been pre-<a href="/wiki/Norman_conquest_of_England" title="Norman conquest of England">Conquest</a>, but the earliest visible extant part of the church is the 13th century north <a href="/wiki/Arcade_(architecture)" title="Arcade (architecture)">arcade</a>, which may have been added to an earlier, now-lost, nave. The spire and tower date to the middle of the next century, roughly when the nave was rebuilt. Many of the windows are reticulated in a fashion popular during the early 14th century.<sup id="cite_ref-nhle_6-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nhle-6">[6]</a></sup> Several medieval bequests are known: Olivia, wife of John Rossen of Quarrington left 12 pence to the rector and church each in 1412; a donation of wool was made by another parishioner, Joan, wife of William Ward, around the same time. Later, 8 pence was left to the churchwardens by an unknown resident.<sup id="cite_ref-Trollope428_20-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Trollope428-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the latter half of the 16th century, the living of Old Sleaford became "extremely poor" and its church probably fell out of use. Some time afterwards, the rector of Quarrington obtained a presentation to Old Sleaford, but, discovering the lack of <a href="/wiki/Tithe" title="Tithe">tithes</a>, he left. Robert Carre convinced him to take in the parishioners of Old Sleaford at Quarrington in return for a yearly payment;<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> as of 2015, the parishes are still combined.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> Amendments to the fabric of the church were made in the early modern period, beginning when the chancel was rebuilt on a smaller scale some time after the <a href="/wiki/Protestant_Reformation" class="mw-redirect" title="Protestant Reformation">Reformation</a> and inset with 12th and 13th century stonework,<sup id="cite_ref-nhle_6-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nhle-6">[6]</a></sup> the whole thing described as "very miserable" by Edward Trollope.<sup id="cite_ref-t430_8-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-t430-8">[8]</a></sup> This was replaced in 1812 by a <a href="/wiki/Georgian_architecture" title="Georgian architecture">Georgian</a>-style chancel, constructed under the guidance of the rector, <a href="/wiki/Charles_James_Blomfield" title="Charles James Blomfield">Charles James Blomfield</a>. The North Aisle was rebuilt in 1848 and a new pulpit, screen and pews were added the following year.<sup id="cite_ref-nhle_6-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nhle-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Victorian period witnessed extensive restoration work at Quarrington. Most of the nave's windows were altered, and the <a href="/wiki/Chancel" title="Chancel">chancel</a> and part of the vestry were rebuilt by Charles Kirk in 1862–63, who also widened the chancel arch. Parts of the tower and spire were remodelled 24 years later.<sup id="cite_ref-nhle_6-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nhle-6">[6]</a></sup> The Census of Religious Worship (1851) reveals that the Church had room for 120 people, attendances of 20 and 40 in the morning and afternoon respectively and 20 Sunday scholars.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> As Sleaford expanded, houses were built along London and Station Roads, pushing the town inside the Quarrington parish boundaries in what became New Quarrington.<sup id="cite_ref-:3_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:3-28">[28]</a></sup> To deal with the growing population, a second church was designed in the early 1900s on donated land in the parish, to be built closer to Sleaford. Disruption during the <a href="/wiki/First_World_War" class="mw-redirect" title="First World War">First World War</a>, parish boundary changes in 1928, and rising costs delayed the plans. Instead a church hall was built in 1932 on Grantham Road and as of 2009 was being used as a community centre. An extension to the Church was added in 2001, providing a kitchen, disabled toilet and other facilities; built in matching stone, it incorporated a stained glass window from the north aisle.<sup id="cite_ref-BeneficeProfile_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BeneficeProfile-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
