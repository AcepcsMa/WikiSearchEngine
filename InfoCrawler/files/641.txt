<p><b>Bamberg</b> (<small>German pronunciation:</small> <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Standard_German" title="Help:IPA/Standard German">[Ààbamb…õ…êÃØk]</a></span>) is a town in <a href="/wiki/Upper_Franconia" title="Upper Franconia">Upper Franconia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a>, on the river <a href="/wiki/Regnitz" title="Regnitz">Regnitz</a> close to its confluence with the river <a href="/wiki/Main_(river)" title="Main (river)">Main</a>. A large part of the town has been a <a href="/wiki/UNESCO_World_Heritage_Site" class="mw-redirect" title="UNESCO World Heritage Site">UNESCO World Heritage Site</a> since 1993.</p>

<p>During the post-<a href="/wiki/Roman_Empire" title="Roman Empire">Roman</a> centuries of Germanic migration and settlement, the region afterwards included in the <a href="/wiki/Diocese_of_Bamberg" class="mw-redirect" title="Diocese of Bamberg">Diocese of Bamberg</a> was inhabited for the most part by <a href="/wiki/Slavs" title="Slavs">Slavs</a>. The town, first mentioned in 902, grew up by the castle <i><span lang="de" xml:lang="de">Babenberch</span></i> which gave its name to the <a href="/wiki/Babenberg" class="mw-redirect" title="Babenberg">Babenberg</a> family. On their extinction it passed to the <a href="/wiki/Saxony" title="Saxony">Saxon</a> house.<sup id="cite_ref-EB1911_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EB1911-3">[3]</a></sup> The area was Christianized chiefly by the monks of the <a href="/wiki/Rule_of_St._Benedict" class="mw-redirect" title="Rule of St. Benedict">Benedictine</a> <a href="/wiki/Fulda_Abbey" class="mw-redirect" title="Fulda Abbey">Fulda Abbey</a>, and the land was under the spiritual authority of the <a href="/wiki/Diocese_of_W%C3%BCrzburg" class="mw-redirect" title="Diocese of W√ºrzburg">Diocese of W√ºrzburg</a>.</p>
<p>In 1007, <a href="/wiki/Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Holy Roman Emperor">Holy Roman Emperor</a> <a href="/wiki/Henry_II,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Henry II, Holy Roman Emperor">Henry II</a> or <i>Heinrich II</i> made Bamberg a family inheritance, the seat of a separate <a href="/wiki/Diocese" title="Diocese">diocese</a>. The emperor's purpose in this was to make the Diocese of W√ºrzburg less unwieldy in size and to give Christianity a firmer footing in the districts of <a href="/wiki/Franconia" title="Franconia">Franconia</a>, east of Bamberg. In 1008, after long negotiations with the Bishops of W√ºrzburg and <a href="/wiki/Diocese_of_Eichst%C3%A4tt" class="mw-redirect" title="Diocese of Eichst√§tt">Eichst√§tt</a>, who were to cede portions of their dioceses, the boundaries of the new diocese were defined, and <a href="/wiki/Pope_John_XVIII" title="Pope John XVIII">Pope John XVIII</a> granted the papal confirmation in the same year. Henry II ordered the building of a new <a href="/wiki/Bamberg_Cathedral" title="Bamberg Cathedral">cathedral</a>, which was consecrated 6 May 1012. The church was enriched with gifts from the <a href="/wiki/Pope" title="Pope">pope</a>, and Henry had it dedicated in honor of him. In 1017 Henry also founded <a href="/wiki/Michaelsberg_Abbey,_Bamberg" title="Michaelsberg Abbey, Bamberg">Michaelsberg Abbey</a> on the <i><span lang="de" xml:lang="de">Michaelsberg</span></i> ("Mount St. Michael"), near Bamberg, a Benedictine abbey for the training of the clergy. The emperor and his wife <a href="/wiki/Cunigunde_of_Luxemburg" class="mw-redirect" title="Cunigunde of Luxemburg">Kunigunde</a> gave large temporal possessions to the new diocese, and it received many privileges out of which grew the secular power of the bishop. Pope Benedict VIII visited Bamberg in 1020<sup id="cite_ref-BAM1_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BAM1-4">[4]</a></sup> to meet Henry II for discussions concerning the Holy Roman Empire. While he was here he placed the diocese in direct dependence on the <a href="/wiki/Holy_See" title="Holy See">Holy See</a>. He also personally consecrated some of Bamberg's churches. For a short time Bamberg was the centre of the <a href="/wiki/Holy_Roman_Empire" title="Holy Roman Empire">Holy Roman Empire</a>. Henry and Kunigunde were both buried in the cathedral.</p>

<p>From the middle of the 13th century onward the <a href="/wiki/Bishop" title="Bishop">bishops</a> were princes of the Empire<sup id="cite_ref-EB1911_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EB1911-3">[3]</a></sup> and ruled Bamberg, overseeing the construction of monumental buildings. In 1248 and 1260 the see obtained large portions of the estates of the <a href="/wiki/Duke_of_Merania" class="mw-redirect" title="Duke of Merania">Counts of Meran</a>, partly through purchase and partly through the appropriation of extinguished fiefs. The old <a href="/wiki/Bishopric_of_Bamberg" class="mw-redirect" title="Bishopric of Bamberg">Bishopric of Bamberg</a> was composed of an unbroken territory extending from <a href="/wiki/Schl%C3%BCsselfeld" title="Schl√ºsselfeld">Schl√ºsselfeld</a> in a northeasterly direction to the <a href="/wiki/Franconian_Forest" title="Franconian Forest">Franconian Forest</a>, and possessed in addition estates in the Duchies of <a href="/wiki/Duchy_of_Carinthia" title="Duchy of Carinthia">Carinthia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Salzburg" title="Salzburg">Salzburg</a>, in the <a href="/wiki/Nordgau_(Bavaria)" class="mw-redirect" title="Nordgau (Bavaria)">Nordgau</a> (the present <a href="/wiki/Upper_Palatinate" title="Upper Palatinate">Upper Palatinate</a>), in <a href="/wiki/Thuringia" title="Thuringia">Thuringia</a>, and on the <a href="/wiki/Danube" title="Danube">Danube</a>. By the changes resulting from the <a href="/wiki/Protestant_Reformation" class="mw-redirect" title="Protestant Reformation">Reformation</a>, the territory of this see was reduced nearly one half in extent. Since 1279 the <a href="/wiki/Coat_of_arms_of_the_city_of_Bamberg" title="Coat of arms of the city of Bamberg">coat of arms of the city of Bamberg</a> is known in form of a seal.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Bamberg_witch_trials" title="Bamberg witch trials">witch trials</a> of the 17th century claimed about one thousand victims in Bamberg, reaching a climax between 1626 and 1631, under the rule of <a href="/wiki/Prince-Bishop" class="mw-redirect" title="Prince-Bishop">Prince-Bishop</a> Johann Georg II Fuchs von Dornheim.<sup id="cite_ref-SZ_2012_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SZ_2012-5">[5]</a></sup> The famous <i><a href="/wiki/Drudenhaus_(prison)" title="Drudenhaus (prison)">Drudenhaus</a></i> (witch prison), built in 1627, is no longer standing today; however, detailed accounts of some cases, such as that of <a href="/wiki/Johannes_Junius" title="Johannes Junius">Johannes Junius</a>, remain.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1647, the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Bamberg" title="University of Bamberg">University of Bamberg</a> was founded as <i><span lang="la" xml:lang="la">Academia Bambergensis</span></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Bambrzy" title="Bambrzy">Bambrzy</a> (<i><span lang="de" xml:lang="de">Posen Bambergers</span></i>) are German <a href="/wiki/Poles" title="Poles">Poles</a> who are descended from settlers from the Bamberg area who settled in villages around <a href="/wiki/Pozna%C5%84" title="Pozna≈Ñ">Pozna≈Ñ</a> in the years 1719‚Äì1753.</p>
<p>In 1759, the possessions and jurisdictions of the diocese situated in <a href="/wiki/Austria" title="Austria">Austria</a> were sold to that state. When the secularization of church lands took place (1802) the diocese covered 3,305&#160;km<sup>2</sup> (1,276&#160;sq&#160;mi) and had a population of 207,000. Bamberg thus lost its independence in 1802, becoming part of <a href="/wiki/Electorate_of_Bavaria" title="Electorate of Bavaria">Bavaria</a> in 1803.</p>
<p>Bamberg was first connected to the German rail system in 1844, which has been an important part of its infrastructure ever since. After a communist uprising took control over Bavaria in the years following <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a>, the state government fled to Bamberg and stayed there for almost two years before the Bavarian capital of <a href="/wiki/Munich" title="Munich">Munich</a> was retaken by <i><a href="/wiki/Freikorps" title="Freikorps">Freikorps</a></i> units (see <a href="/wiki/Bavarian_Soviet_Republic" title="Bavarian Soviet Republic">Bavarian Soviet Republic</a>). The first republican constitution of Bavaria was passed in Bamberg, becoming known as the <i>Bamberger Verfassung</i> (Bamberg Constitution).</p>
<p>In February 1926 Bamberg served as the venue for the <a href="/wiki/Bamberg_Conference" title="Bamberg Conference">Bamberg Conference</a>, convened by <a href="/wiki/Adolf_Hitler" title="Adolf Hitler">Adolf Hitler</a> in his attempt to foster unity and to stifle dissent within the then-young <a href="/wiki/Nazi_party" class="mw-redirect" title="Nazi party">Nazi party</a>. Bamberg was chosen for its location in Upper Franconia, reasonably close to the residences of the members of the dissident northern Nazi faction but still within Bavaria.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1973, the town celebrated the 1,000th anniversary of its founding.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geography">Geography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bamberg&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Geography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Bamberg is located in <a href="/wiki/Franconia" title="Franconia">Franconia</a>, 63&#160;km (39&#160;mi) north of <a href="/wiki/Nuremberg" title="Nuremberg">Nuremberg</a> by railway and 101&#160;km (63&#160;mi) east of <a href="/wiki/W%C3%BCrzburg" title="W√ºrzburg">W√ºrzburg</a>, also by rail. It is situated on the Regnitz river, 3&#160;km (1.9&#160;mi) before it flows into the <a href="/wiki/Main_(river)" title="Main (river)">Main</a> river.</p>
<p>Its geography is shaped by the Regnitz and by the foothills of the <a href="/wiki/Steigerwald" title="Steigerwald">Steigerwald</a>, part of the German uplands. From northeast to southwest, the town is divided into first the Regnitz plain, then one large and several small islands formed by two arms of the Regnitz (<i><span lang="de" xml:lang="de">Inselstadt</span></i>), and finally the part of town on the hills, the "Hill Town" (<i><span lang="de" xml:lang="de">Bergstadt</span></i>).</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_seven_hills_of_Bamberg">The seven hills of Bamberg</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bamberg&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: The seven hills of Bamberg">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Bamberg extends over seven hills, each crowned by a beautiful church. This has led to Bamberg being called the "Franconian Rome" ‚Äî although a running joke among Bamberg's tour guides is to refer to Rome instead as the "Italian Bamberg". The hills are Cathedral Hill, Michaelsberg, Kaulberg/Obere Pfarre, Stefansberg, Jakobsberg, Altenburger Hill and Abtsberg.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Climate">Climate</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bamberg&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Climate">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Climate in this area has mild differences between highs and lows, and there is adequate rainfall year-round. The <a href="/wiki/K%C3%B6ppen_Climate_Classification" class="mw-redirect" title="K√∂ppen Climate Classification">K√∂ppen Climate Classification</a> subtype for this climate is "<a href="/wiki/K%C3%B6ppen_climate_classification#GROUP_C:_Mild_Temperate.2Fmesothermal_climates" title="K√∂ppen climate classification">Cfb</a>" (Marine West Coast Climate/<a href="/wiki/Oceanic_climate" title="Oceanic climate">Oceanic climate</a>), with a certain continental influence as indicated by average winter nighttime temperatures well below zero.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>

<p>The old town of Bamberg is listed as a <a href="/wiki/UNESCO" title="UNESCO">UNESCO</a> <a href="/wiki/World_Heritage_Sites" class="mw-redirect" title="World Heritage Sites">World Heritage Site</a>, primarily because of its authentic medieval appearance. The town established a documentation centre in 2005 to support World Heritage activities. Some of the main sights are:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Bamberg_Cathedral" title="Bamberg Cathedral">Bamberg Cathedral</a> (1237), with the tombs of Emperor Henry II and <a href="/wiki/Pope_Clement_II" title="Pope Clement II">Pope Clement II</a></li>
<li><i><span lang="de" xml:lang="de">Alte Hofhaltung</span></i>, residence of the bishops in the 16th and 17th centuries</li>
<li><i><span lang="de" xml:lang="de">Neue Residenz</span></i>, residence of the bishops after the 17th century</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Bamberg_State_Library" title="Bamberg State Library">Bamberg State Library</a> in the New Residence</li>
<li>Old town hall (1386), built in the middle of the Regnitz river, accessible by two bridges</li>
<li><i><span lang="de" xml:lang="de">Klein-Venedig</span></i> ("Little Venice"), a colony of fishermen's houses from the 19th century along one bank of the river Regnitz</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Michaelsberg_Abbey,_Bamberg" title="Michaelsberg Abbey, Bamberg">Michaelsberg Abbey</a>, built in the 12th century on one of Bamberg's "Seven Hills"</li>
<li><span lang="de" xml:lang="de"><a href="/wiki/Altenburg_(Bamberg)" title="Altenburg (Bamberg)">Altenburg</a></span>, castle, former residence of the bishops</li>
</ul>
<dl>
<dt>Cathedral</dt>
</dl>
<p><a href="/wiki/Bamberg_Cathedral" title="Bamberg Cathedral">Bamberg Cathedral</a> is a late <a href="/wiki/Romanesque_architecture" title="Romanesque architecture">Romanesque</a> building with four towers. It was founded in 1004 by Emperor Henry II, finished in 1012<sup id="cite_ref-EB1911_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EB1911-3">[3]</a></sup> and consecrated on 6 May 1012. It was later partially destroyed by fire in 1081. The new cathedral, built by Saint <a href="/wiki/Otto_of_Bamberg" title="Otto of Bamberg">Otto of Bamberg</a>, was consecrated in 1111 and in the 13th century received its present late-Romanesque form.</p>
<p>The cathedral is 94&#160;m (308&#160;ft) long, 28&#160;m (92&#160;ft) wide, 26&#160;m (85&#160;ft) high, and the four towers are each about 81&#160;m (266&#160;ft) high. It contains many historic works of art, such as the marble tomb of the founder and his wife, considered one of the greatest works of the sculptor <a href="/wiki/Tilman_Riemenschneider" title="Tilman Riemenschneider">Tilman Riemenschneider</a>, and carved between 1499 and 1513. Another treasure of the cathedral is an <a href="/wiki/Equestrian_statue" title="Equestrian statue">equestrian statue</a> known as the <a href="/wiki/Bamberg_Horseman" title="Bamberg Horseman">Bamberg Horseman</a> (<i><span lang="de" xml:lang="de">Der Bamberger Reiter</span></i>).<sup id="cite_ref-EB1911_3-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EB1911-3">[3]</a></sup> This statue, possibly depicting the emperor <a href="/wiki/Conrad_III_of_Germany" title="Conrad III of Germany">Conrad III</a>, most likely dates to the second quarter of the 13th century. The statue also serves as a symbol of the town of Bamberg.</p>
<dl>
<dt><span lang="de" xml:lang="de">Neue Residenz</span></dt>
</dl>
<p>The <i><span lang="de" xml:lang="de">Neue Residenz</span></i> (New Residence) (1698‚Äì1704) was initially occupied by the prince-bishops, and from 1864 to 1867 by the deposed King <a href="/wiki/Otto_of_Greece" title="Otto of Greece">Otto of Greece</a>. Its <i><span lang="de" xml:lang="de">Rosengarten</span></i> (Rose Garden) overlooks the town. It has over 4500 roses.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<dl>
<dt><span lang="de" xml:lang="de">Altenburg</span></dt>
</dl>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:AltenburgEingang.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7b/AltenburgEingang.jpg/220px-AltenburgEingang.jpg" width="220" height="303" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7b/AltenburgEingang.jpg/330px-AltenburgEingang.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7b/AltenburgEingang.jpg/440px-AltenburgEingang.jpg 2x" data-file-width="547" data-file-height="754" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:AltenburgEingang.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Bamberg <a href="/wiki/Altenburg_(Bamberg)" title="Altenburg (Bamberg)">Altenburg</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The <span lang="de" xml:lang="de"><a href="/wiki/Altenburg_(Bamberg)" title="Altenburg (Bamberg)">Altenburg</a></span> is located on the highest of Bamberg's seven hills. It was mentioned for the first time in 1109.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Between 1251 and 1553 it was the residence of Bamberg's bishops. Destroyed in 1553 by <a href="/wiki/Albert_Alcibiades,_Margrave_of_Brandenburg-Kulmbach" title="Albert Alcibiades, Margrave of Brandenburg-Kulmbach">Albert Alcibiades, Margrave of Brandenburg-Kulmbach</a>, it was used, after scanty repairs, only as a prison, and increasingly decayed.</p>
<p>In 1801, A. F. Marcus bought the castle and completely repaired it. His friend, the famous German writer <a href="/wiki/E.T.A._Hoffmann" class="mw-redirect" title="E.T.A. Hoffmann">E.T.A. Hoffmann</a>, who was very impressed by the building, lived there for a while. The next owner, Anton von Greifenstein, in 1818 founded an association to save the castle. This society still maintains the whole property today. The Altenburg today houses a restaurant.</p>
<dl>
<dt>Other sights</dt>
</dl>
<p>Other churches are the <i><span lang="de" xml:lang="de">Jakobskirche</span></i>, an 11th century Romanesque <a href="/wiki/Basilica" title="Basilica">basilica</a>; the <i><span lang="de" xml:lang="de">St. Martinskirche</span></i>; the <i><span lang="de" xml:lang="de">Marienkirche</span></i> or <i><span lang="de" xml:lang="de">Obere Pfarrkirche</span></i> (1320‚Äì1387), which has now been restored to its original pure <a href="/wiki/Gothic_architecture" title="Gothic architecture">Gothic</a> style. The <i><span lang="de" xml:lang="de"><a href="/wiki/Michaelskirche,_Bamberg" class="mw-redirect" title="Michaelskirche, Bamberg">Michaelskirche</a></span></i>, 12th century Romanesque (restored), on the Michaelsberg, was formerly the church of the <a href="/wiki/Rule_of_St._Benedict" class="mw-redirect" title="Rule of St. Benedict">Benedictine</a> <a href="/wiki/Michaelsberg_Abbey,_Bamberg" title="Michaelsberg Abbey, Bamberg">Michaelsberg Abbey</a> secularized in 1803 and now contains the <i><span lang="de" xml:lang="de">B√ºrgerspital</span></i>, or <a href="/wiki/Almshouse" title="Almshouse">almshouse</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Museum" title="Museum">museum</a> and municipal art collections.</p>
<p>Of the bridges connecting the sections of the lower town the <i><span lang="de" xml:lang="de">Obere Br√ºcke</span></i> was completed in 1455. Halfway across this, on an island, is the <i><span lang="de" xml:lang="de">Rathaus</span></i> or town hall (rebuilt 1744-1756). The lyceum, formerly a <a href="/wiki/Society_of_Jesus" title="Society of Jesus">Jesuit</a> college, contains a natural history museum. The old palace (<i><span lang="de" xml:lang="de">Alte Hofhaltung</span></i>) was built in 1591 on the site of an old residence of the counts of <a href="/wiki/Babenberg" class="mw-redirect" title="Babenberg">Babenberg</a>. Monuments include the Maximilian fountain (1880), with statues of <a href="/wiki/Maximilian_I_Joseph_of_Bavaria" title="Maximilian I Joseph of Bavaria">King Maximilian I of Bavaria</a>, the emperor Henry II and his wife, <a href="/wiki/Conrad_III_of_Germany" title="Conrad III of Germany">Conrad III</a> and <a href="/wiki/Otto_of_Bamberg" title="Otto of Bamberg">Saint Otto</a>, bishop of Bamberg.<sup id="cite_ref-EB1911_3-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EB1911-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>There are also underground tunnels beneath the town. These were originally constructed as mines which supplied sandstone which could be used for construction or as an abrasive cleaner. Mining came to an end in 1920 but a 7.5-mile (12.1&#160;km) tunnel network remained. The tunnels were used as an <a href="/wiki/Air_raid_shelter" title="Air raid shelter">air raid shelter</a> during World War II. A part of the network can be visited on a guided tour.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Beer">Beer</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bamberg&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Beer">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Bamberg is known for its smoked <a href="/wiki/Rauchbier" class="mw-redirect" title="Rauchbier">Rauchbier</a> and is home to nine breweries, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Brauerei_F%C3%A4ssla&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Brauerei F√§ssla (page does not exist)">Brauerei F√§ssla</a>, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Brauerei_Greifenklau&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Brauerei Greifenklau (page does not exist)">Brauerei Greifenklau</a>, Brauerei Heller-Trum (<a href="/wiki/Schlenkerla" title="Schlenkerla">Schlenkerla</a>), <a href="/wiki/Brauerei_Kaiserdom" title="Brauerei Kaiserdom">Brauerei Kaiserdom</a>, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Brauerei_Keesmann&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Brauerei Keesmann (page does not exist)">Keesmann Br√§u</a>, <a href="/wiki/Klosterbr%C3%A4u_Bamberg" title="Klosterbr√§u Bamberg">Klosterbr√§u</a>, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Brauerei_Mahr&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Brauerei Mahr (page does not exist)">Mahrs Br√§u</a> and <a href="/w/index.php?title=Brauerei_Spezial&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Brauerei Spezial (page does not exist)">Brauerei Spezial</a>, and one <a href="/wiki/Brewpub" class="mw-redirect" title="Brewpub">brewpub</a>, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Ambr%C3%A4usianum_Bamberg&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Ambr√§usianum Bamberg (page does not exist)">Ambr√§usianum</a>. Every August there is a five-day <i><span lang="de" xml:lang="de">Sandkerwa</span></i>, a <a href="/wiki/Kirmess" class="mw-redirect" title="Kirmess">kirmess</a> celebrated with beers.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Education">Education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bamberg&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/University_of_Bamberg" title="University of Bamberg">University of Bamberg</a>, named Otto-Friedrich University, offers higher education in the areas of <a href="/wiki/Social_science" title="Social science">social science</a>, <a href="/wiki/Business_studies" title="Business studies">business studies</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Humanities" title="Humanities">humanities</a>, and is attended by more than 13,000 students. The University of Applied Sciences Bamberg offers higher education in the areas of <a href="/wiki/Public_health" title="Public health">public health</a>. Bamberg is also home to eight secondary schools (<a href="/wiki/Gymnasium_(school)" title="Gymnasium (school)">gymnasiums</a>):</p>

<p>There are also numerous other institutes for primary, secondary, technical, vocational and adult education.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Infrastructure">Infrastructure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bamberg&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Infrastructure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Transport">Transport</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bamberg&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Transport">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Railway">Railway</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bamberg&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Railway">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/InterCityExpress" class="mw-redirect" title="InterCityExpress">InterCityExpress</a> main line #28 (<a href="/wiki/Munich" title="Munich">Munich</a> - <a href="/wiki/Nuremberg" title="Nuremberg">Nuremberg</a> - <a href="/wiki/Leipzig" title="Leipzig">Leipzig</a> - <a href="/wiki/Berlin" title="Berlin">Berlin</a> / <a href="/wiki/Hamburg" title="Hamburg">Hamburg</a>) runs through <a href="/wiki/Bamberg_station" title="Bamberg station">Bamberg station</a> on the <a href="/wiki/Nuremberg%E2%80%93Bamberg_railway" title="Nuremberg‚ÄìBamberg railway">Nuremberg‚ÄìBamberg</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Bamberg%E2%80%93Hof_railway" title="Bamberg‚ÄìHof railway">Bamberg‚ÄìHof lines</a>. It takes less than two hours to Munich on the train and about four hours to reach Berlin. But the <a href="/wiki/Nuremberg%E2%80%93Erfurt_high-speed_railway" title="Nuremberg‚ÄìErfurt high-speed railway">Nuremberg‚ÄìErfurt high-speed railway</a> is currently being constructed through the Thuringian mountains and should shorten the journey time considerably.</p>
<p>East-west connections are poorer. Bamberg is connected to other towns in eastern Upper Franconia such as <a href="/wiki/Bayreuth" title="Bayreuth">Bayreuth</a>, <a href="/wiki/Coburg" title="Coburg">Coburg</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Kronach" title="Kronach">Kronach</a> via the <a href="/wiki/Bamberg%E2%80%93Hof_railway" title="Bamberg‚ÄìHof railway">Bamberg‚ÄìHof line</a> with trains usually running at least every hour. Connections on the <a href="/wiki/W%C3%BCrzburg%E2%80%93Bamberg_railway" title="W√ºrzburg‚ÄìBamberg railway">W√ºrzburg‚ÄìBamberg line</a> to the west are hourly regional trains to W√ºrzburg, which is fully connected to the ICE network. Tourists arriving at <a href="/wiki/Frankfurt_International_Airport" class="mw-redirect" title="Frankfurt International Airport">Frankfurt International Airport</a> can take advantage of the new direct connection from Frankfurt main station.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Motorways">Motorways</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bamberg&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Motorways">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Bamberg is not near any of the major (i.e. single-digit) <a href="/wiki/Autobahns" class="mw-redirect" title="Autobahns">autobahns</a>. But it is nevertheless well connected to the network in all directions: the <a href="/wiki/Bundesautobahn_70" title="Bundesautobahn 70">A70</a> from <a href="/wiki/Schweinfurt" title="Schweinfurt">Schweinfurt</a> (connecting to the <a href="/wiki/Bundesautobahn_7" title="Bundesautobahn 7">A7</a> there) to Bayreuth (connecting to the <a href="/wiki/Bundesautobahn_9" title="Bundesautobahn 9">A9</a>) runs along the northern edge of the town. The <a href="/wiki/Bundesautobahn_73" title="Bundesautobahn 73">A73</a> on the eastern side of town connects Bamberg to Nuremberg (connecting to the <a href="/wiki/Bundesautobahn_9" title="Bundesautobahn 9">A9</a>) and <a href="/wiki/Thuringia" title="Thuringia">Thuringia</a>, ending at <a href="/wiki/Suhl" title="Suhl">Suhl</a>.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Air_transport">Air transport</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bamberg&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Air transport">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Bamberg is served by <a href="/w/index.php?title=Bamberg-Breitenau_Airfield&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Bamberg-Breitenau Airfield (page does not exist)">Bamberg-Breitenau Airfield</a>. Mostly public aircraft operate there. It used to be a military airport. (IATA-Code: ZCD, ICAO-Code: EDQA) It is also possible to charter public flights to and from this airport.</p>
<p>Most international tourists who travel by plane arrive at <a href="/wiki/Frankfurt_International_Airport" class="mw-redirect" title="Frankfurt International Airport">Frankfurt International Airport</a> or <a href="/wiki/Munich_Airport" title="Munich Airport">Munich Airport</a>. The nearest major airport is <a href="/wiki/Nuremberg_Airport" title="Nuremberg Airport">Nuremberg Airport</a> which can be reached within half an hour by car or one hour by train and subway.</p>

<p>Both the <a href="/wiki/Rhine-Main-Danube_Canal" class="mw-redirect" title="Rhine-Main-Danube Canal">Rhine-Main-Danube Canal</a> and its predecessor, the <a href="/wiki/Ludwig_Canal" title="Ludwig Canal">Ludwig Canal</a>, begin near Bamberg. The Ludwig Canal was opened in 1846 but closed in 1950 after damage during the <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">second world war</a>. With the completion of the Rhine-Main-Danube Canal in 1992, uninterrupted water transport was again made possible between the <a href="/wiki/North_Sea" title="North Sea">North Sea</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Black_Sea" title="Black Sea">Black Sea</a>.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Local_public_transport">Local public transport</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bamberg&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Local public transport">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Local public transport within Bamberg relies exclusively on buses. More than 20 routes connect the outlying quarters and some villages in the vicinity to the central bus station. In addition, there are several "Night Lines" (the last of these, though, tend to run around midnight) and some <a href="/wiki/Park-and-ride" class="mw-redirect" title="Park-and-ride">park-and-ride</a> lines from parking lots on the periphery to the town centre.<br />
A short-lived tram system existed in the 1920s.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Military_bases">Military bases</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bamberg&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Military bases">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Bamberg was an important base for the Bavarian, German and then American military stationed at <a href="/wiki/Warner_Barracks_in_Bamberg,_Germany" class="mw-redirect" title="Warner Barracks in Bamberg, Germany">Warner Barracks</a>. Warner Barracks was closed in the fall of 2014, with the last battalion leaving being the 54th Engineer Battalion and returned to the German government. In 2016, a large part of the facility was taken over by the <a href="/wiki/German_Federal_Police" class="mw-redirect" title="German Federal Police">German Federal Police</a> for training purposes. Muna Kasserne was a small base occupied by the 504th Maintenance Company, 71st Maintenance Bn. It was part of Warner Barracks although located separately.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Governance">Governance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bamberg&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Governance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Bamberg is an urban district, or <i>kreisfreie Stadt</i>. Its town council (<i>Stadtrat</i>) and its Mayor (<i>Oberb√ºrgermeister</i>) are elected every six years, though not in the same year. Thus, the last municipal election for the town council was in 2014, for the Mayor in 2012. As an exception to the six-year term, the term starting in 2012 will take eight years to synchronize the elections with those in the rest of Bavaria.</p>
<p>As of the elections of 16 March 2014, the 44 member strong town council comprises 12 <a href="/wiki/Christian_Social_Union_of_Bavaria" class="mw-redirect" title="Christian Social Union of Bavaria">CSU</a> councillors, 10 <a href="/wiki/Social_Democratic_Party_of_Germany" title="Social Democratic Party of Germany">SPD</a> councillors, 8 <a href="/wiki/Alliance_%2790/The_Greens" class="mw-redirect" title="Alliance '90/The Greens">Green</a> councillors, 4 councillors of the <i>Bamberger B√ºrger-Block</i> and 4 of the <i>Freie W√§hler</i> (Free Voters), both local political movements. These five parties achieved the number of councillors necessary to form a <a href="/wiki/Parliamentary_group" title="Parliamentary group">parliamentary group</a>. In addition, there are 3 councillors of the <i>Bamberger Unabh√§ngige B√ºrger</i> and the 1 councillor each of the <i>Bamberger Realisten</i>, the <a href="/wiki/Free_Democratic_Party_(Germany)" title="Free Democratic Party (Germany)">FDP</a> and the <i>Bamberger Linke Liste</i>.</p>
<p>The previous council, elected on 2 March 2008, was composed of 15 <a href="/wiki/Christian_Social_Union_of_Bavaria" class="mw-redirect" title="Christian Social Union of Bavaria">CSU</a> councillors, 10 <a href="/wiki/Social_Democratic_Party_of_Germany" title="Social Democratic Party of Germany">SPD</a> councillors, 7 <a href="/wiki/Alliance_%2790/The_Greens" class="mw-redirect" title="Alliance '90/The Greens">Green</a> councillors, 5 councillors of the Bamberger B√ºrger-Block and 3 of the Freie W√§hler (Free Voters), both local political movements. These five parties achieved the number of councillors necessary to form a <a href="/wiki/Parliamentary_group" title="Parliamentary group">parliamentary group</a>. In addition, there were 2 councillors of the <i>Bamberger Realisten</i> and one of the <a href="/wiki/Free_Democratic_Party_(Germany)" title="Free Democratic Party (Germany)">FDP</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Republicans_(Germany)" class="mw-redirect" title="Republicans (Germany)">Republikaner</a>, making them ineligible for caucus status.</p>
