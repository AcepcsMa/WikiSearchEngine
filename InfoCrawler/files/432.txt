<p><b>Helene Bertha Amalie</b> "<b>Leni</b>" <b>Riefenstahl</b> (<small>German:</small> <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Standard_German" title="Help:IPA/Standard German">[ˈʁiːfn̩ʃtaːl]</a></span>; 22 August 1902 – 8 September 2003) was a <a href="/wiki/Germans" title="Germans">German</a> film director, producer, screenwriter, editor, photographer, actress and dancer.</p>
<p>Born in 1902 Leni Riefenstahl grew up in <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a> with her brother Heinz (1905–1944), who was killed on the <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Front_(World_War_II)" title="Eastern Front (World War II)">Eastern Front</a> in <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>. A talented swimmer and artist, she also became interested in dancing during her childhood, taking dancing lessons and performing across <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>.</p>
<p>After seeing a promotional poster for the 1924 film <i><a href="/wiki/Mountain_of_Destiny" title="Mountain of Destiny">Der Berg des Schicksals</a></i> ("The Mountain of Destiny"), Riefenstahl was inspired to move into acting. Between 1925 and 1929, she starred in five successful motion pictures. In 1932, Riefenstahl decided to try directing with her own film called <i><a href="/wiki/Das_Blaue_Licht" class="mw-redirect" title="Das Blaue Licht">Das Blaue Licht</a></i> ("The Blue Light").</p>
<p>In the 1930s, she directed <i><a href="/wiki/Triumph_des_Willens" class="mw-redirect" title="Triumph des Willens">Triumph des Willens</a></i> ("Triumph of the Will") and <i><a href="/wiki/Olympia_(1938_film)" title="Olympia (1938 film)">Olympia</a></i>, resulting in worldwide attention and acclaim. Both movies are widely considered two of the most effective, and technically innovative, <a href="/wiki/Propaganda_film" title="Propaganda film">propaganda films</a> ever made. Her involvement in <i>Triumph des Willens</i>, however, significantly damaged her career and reputation after the war. The exact nature of her relationship with <a href="/wiki/Nazi_Party" title="Nazi Party">Nazi Party</a> leader <a href="/wiki/Adolf_Hitler" title="Adolf Hitler">Adolf Hitler</a> remains a matter of debate. However, Hitler was in close collaboration with Riefenstahl during the production of at least three important Nazi films, and a closer friendship is claimed to have existed.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDowning201223_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDowning201223-2">[2]</a></sup> When in 2000 <a href="/wiki/Jodie_Foster" title="Jodie Foster">Jodie Foster</a> was planning a biographical drama on Riefenstahl, war-crime documenters warned against a revisionist view that glorified the director. They stated that publicly Riefenstahl seemed "quite infatuated" with Hitler and was in fact the last surviving member of his "inner circle".<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Others go further, arguing that Riefenstahl's visions were essential to the success of the Holocaust.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> After the war, Riefenstahl was arrested, but classified as being a "<a href="/wiki/Fellow_traveler" class="mw-redirect" title="Fellow traveler">fellow traveler</a>" or "Nazi sympathiser" only and was not associated with <a href="/wiki/War_crimes" class="mw-redirect" title="War crimes">war crimes</a>. Throughout her life, she denied having known about the <a href="/wiki/Holocaust" class="mw-redirect" title="Holocaust">Holocaust</a>. Besides directing, Riefenstahl released an autobiography and wrote several books on the <a href="/wiki/Nuba" class="mw-redirect" title="Nuba">Nuba</a> people.</p>
<p>Riefenstahl died of cancer on 8 September 2003 at the age of 101 and was buried at <a href="/wiki/Munich_Waldfriedhof" title="Munich Waldfriedhof">Munich Waldfriedhof</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Leni_Riefenstahl&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Helene Bertha Amalie Riefenstahl was born in Germany on 22 August 1902.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEJohnson_2014_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJohnson_2014-5">[5]</a></sup> Her father, Alfred Theodor Paul Riefenstahl,<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> owned a successful heating and ventilation company and wanted his daughter to follow him into the business world.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEUniversity_of_Washington_2008_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEUniversity_of_Washington_2008-7">[7]</a></sup> Since Riefenstahl was the only child for several years, Alfred wanted her to carry on the family name and secure the family fortune.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEUniversity_of_Washington_2008_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEUniversity_of_Washington_2008-7">[7]</a></sup> However, her mother, Bertha Ida (Scherlach), who had been a part-time seamstress before her marriage, had faith in Riefenstahl and believed that her daughter's future was in show business.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTETrimborn_2007_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTETrimborn_2007-8">[8]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEUniversity_of_Washington_2008_7-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEUniversity_of_Washington_2008-7">[7]</a></sup> Riefenstahl had a younger brother, Heinz, who was killed at the age of 39 on the <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Front_(World_War_II)" title="Eastern Front (World War II)">Eastern Front</a> in <a href="/wiki/Nazi_Germany" title="Nazi Germany">Nazi Germany</a>'s war against the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet Union</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERother2003112_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERother2003112-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>Riefenstahl fell in love with the arts in her childhood.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESalkeld20112_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESalkeld20112-10">[10]</a></sup> She began to paint and write poetry at the age of four.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESalkeld20112_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESalkeld20112-10">[10]</a></sup> She was also athletic, and at the age of twelve joined a gymnastics and swimming club.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEUniversity_of_Washington_2008_7-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEUniversity_of_Washington_2008-7">[7]</a></sup> Her mother was confident her daughter would grow up to be successful in the field of art and therefore gave her full support, unlike Riefenstahl's father, who was not interested in his daughter's artistic inclinations.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEUniversity_of_Washington_2008_7-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEUniversity_of_Washington_2008-7">[7]</a></sup> In 1918, when she was 16, Riefenstahl attended a presentation of <a href="/wiki/Snow_White" title="Snow White">Snow White</a> which interested her deeply; it led her to want to be a dancer.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEUniversity_of_Washington_2008_7-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEUniversity_of_Washington_2008-7">[7]</a></sup> Her father instead wanted to provide his daughter with an education that could lead to a more dignified occupation. His wife, however, continued to support her daughter's passion.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEUniversity_of_Washington_2008_7-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEUniversity_of_Washington_2008-7">[7]</a></sup> Without her father's knowledge, she enrolled Riefenstahl in dance and ballet classes at the Grimm-Reiter Dance School in Berlin, where she quickly became a star pupil.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEUniversity_of_Washington_2008_7-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEUniversity_of_Washington_2008-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Dancing_and_acting_careers">Dancing and acting careers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Leni_Riefenstahl&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Dancing and acting careers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Riefenstahl attended dancing academies and became well known for her self-styled interpretive dancing skills, traveling across Europe with <a href="/wiki/Max_Reinhardt" title="Max Reinhardt">Max Reinhardt</a> in a show funded by Jewish producer <a href="/wiki/Harry_R._Sokal" title="Harry R. Sokal">Harry Sokal</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFalcon_2003_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFalcon_2003-11">[11]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEInfield197614.E2.80.9316_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEInfield197614.E2.80.9316-12">[12]</a></sup> Riefenstahl often made almost 700 <i><a href="/wiki/Reichmark" class="mw-redirect" title="Reichmark">Reichmarks</a></i> for each performance and was so dedicated to dancing that she gave filmmaking no thought.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEInfield197614.E2.80.9316_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEInfield197614.E2.80.9316-12">[12]</a></sup> She began to suffer a series of foot injuries that led to knee surgery that threatened her dancing career.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEUniversity_of_Washington_2008_7-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEUniversity_of_Washington_2008-7">[7]</a></sup> It was while going to a doctor's appointment that she first saw a poster for the 1924 film <i><a href="/wiki/Mountain_of_Destiny" title="Mountain of Destiny">Der Berg des Schicksals</a></i> ("The Mountain of Destiny").<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELangford201220_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELangford201220-13">[13]</a></sup> She became inspired to go into movie making, and began visiting the cinema to see films and also attended film shows.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEUniversity_of_Washington_2008_7-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEUniversity_of_Washington_2008-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>On one of her adventures, Riefenstahl met Luis Trenker, who was an actor from <i>Der Berg des Schicksals</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELangford201220_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELangford201220-13">[13]</a></sup> At a meeting arranged by her friend Gunther Rahn, she met <a href="/wiki/Arnold_Fanck" title="Arnold Fanck">Arnold Fanck</a>, the director of <i>Der Berg des Schicksals</i> and a pioneer of the <a href="/wiki/Mountain_film" title="Mountain film">mountain film</a> genre.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELangford201220_13-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELangford201220-13">[13]</a></sup> Fanck was working on a film in Berlin. After Riefenstahl told him how much she admired his work, she also convinced him of her acting skill.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELangford201220_13-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELangford201220-13">[13]</a></sup> She persuaded him to feature her in one of his movies.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELangford201220_13-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELangford201220-13">[13]</a></sup> Riefenstahl later received a package from Fanck containing the script of the 1926 film <i>Der Heilige Berg</i> ("The Holy Mountain").<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELangford201220_13-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELangford201220-13">[13]</a></sup> She made a series of films for Fanck, where she learned from him acting and film editing techniques.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELangford201220_13-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELangford201220-13">[13]</a></sup> One of Fanck's films that brought Riefenstahl into the limelight was <i><a href="/wiki/The_White_Hell_of_Pitz_Palu" title="The White Hell of Pitz Palu">Die Weisse Hölle vom Piz Palü</a></i> ("The White Hell of <a href="/wiki/Piz_Pal%C3%BC" title="Piz Palü">Piz Palü</a>") of 1929, co-directed by <a href="/wiki/G._W._Pabst" title="G. W. Pabst">G. W. Pabst</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELangford201220_13-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELangford201220-13">[13]</a></sup> Her fame spread to countries outside Germany.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELangford201220_13-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELangford201220-13">[13]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEUniversity_of_Washington_2008_7-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEUniversity_of_Washington_2008-7">[7]</a></sup></p>

<p>Riefenstahl produced and directed her own work called <i><a href="/wiki/The_Blue_Light_(1932_film)" title="The Blue Light (1932 film)">Das Blaue Licht</a></i> ("The Blue Light") in 1932, co-written by <a href="/wiki/Carl_Mayer" title="Carl Mayer">Carl Mayer</a> and <a href="/wiki/B%C3%A9la_Bal%C3%A1zs" title="Béla Balázs">Béla Balázs</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELangford201277_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELangford201277-14">[14]</a></sup> This film won the Silver Medal at the <a href="/wiki/Venice_Film_Festival" title="Venice Film Festival">Venice Film Festival</a>, but was not universally well-received, for which Riefenstahl blamed the critics, many of whom were Jewish.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEJames_2007_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJames_2007-15">[15]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHinton200019_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHinton200019-16">[16]</a></sup> Upon its 1938 re-release, the names of Balázs and Sokal, both Jewish, were removed from the credits; some reports claim this was at Riefenstahl's behest.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEJames_2007_15-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJames_2007-15">[15]</a></sup> In the film, Riefenstahl played an innocent peasant girl who is hated by the villagers because they think she is diabolic and cast out.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELangford201220_13-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELangford201220-13">[13]</a></sup> She is protected by a glowing mountain grotto.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELangford201220_13-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELangford201220-13">[13]</a></sup> According to herself, Riefenstahl received invitations to travel to Hollywood to create films, but she refused them in favour of remaining in Germany with a boyfriend.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEM.C3.BCller_1999_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEM.C3.BCller_1999-17">[17]</a></sup> The film attracted the attention of Hitler, who believed she epitomized the perfect German female.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHinton200019_16-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHinton200019-16">[16]</a></sup> He saw talent in Riefenstahl and arranged a meeting.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHinton200019_16-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHinton200019-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1933, Riefenstahl appeared in the U.S.-German co-productions of the <a href="/wiki/Arnold_Fanck" title="Arnold Fanck">Arnold Fanck</a>-directed, German-language <i><a href="/wiki/SOS_Eisberg" class="mw-redirect" title="SOS Eisberg">SOS Eisberg</a></i> and the <a href="/wiki/Tay_Garnett" title="Tay Garnett">Tay Garnett</a>-directed, English-language <i>SOS Iceberg</i>. The movies were filmed simultaneously in English and German and produced and distributed by <a href="/wiki/Universal_Studios" class="mw-redirect" title="Universal Studios">Universal Studios</a>. Her role as an actress in <i>SOS Iceberg</i> was her only English language role in film.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESalkeld200114.E2.80.9316_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESalkeld200114.E2.80.9316-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Directing_career">Directing career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Leni_Riefenstahl&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Directing career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Propaganda_films">Propaganda films</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Leni_Riefenstahl&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Propaganda films">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>Riefenstahl heard <a href="/wiki/Nazi_Party" title="Nazi Party">Nazi Party</a> (NSDAP) leader <a href="/wiki/Adolf_Hitler" title="Adolf Hitler">Adolf Hitler</a> speak at a rally in 1932 and was mesmerized by his talent as a public speaker.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDowning201223_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDowning201223-2">[2]</a></sup> Describing the experience in her memoir, Riefenstahl wrote, "I had an almost apocalyptic vision that I was never able to forget. It seemed as if the Earth's surface were spreading out in front of me, like a hemisphere that suddenly splits apart in the middle, spewing out an enormous jet of water, so powerful that it touched the sky and shook the earth".<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDowning201223_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDowning201223-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Riefenstahl eventually came to the notice of Hitler who was immediately captivated by her work. She is described as fitting in with Hitler’s ideal of Aryan womanhood, a feature he had noted when he saw her starring performance in <i>Das Blaue Licht.</i><sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> After meeting Hitler, Riefenstahl was offered the opportunity to direct <i><a href="/wiki/Der_Sieg_des_Glaubens" class="mw-redirect" title="Der Sieg des Glaubens">Der Sieg des Glaubens</a></i> ("The Victory of Faith"), an hour-long propaganda film about the fifth <a href="/wiki/Nuremberg_Rally" title="Nuremberg Rally">Nuremberg Rally</a> in 1933.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDowning201223_2-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDowning201223-2">[2]</a></sup> The opportunity that was offered was a huge surprise to Riefenstahl. Hitler had ordered Goebbels Propaganda Ministry to give the film commission to Riefenstahl, but the Ministry had never informed her.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-20">[20]</a></sup> Riefenstahl agreed to direct the movie even though she was only given a few days before the rally to prepare.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_20-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-20">[20]</a></sup> She and Hitler got on well, forming a friendly relationship.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDowning201223_2-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDowning201223-2">[2]</a></sup> The propaganda film was funded entirely by the NSDAP.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDowning201223_2-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDowning201223-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>During the filming of <i>Victory of Faith,</i> Hitler had stood side by side with the leader of the <a href="/wiki/Sturmabteilung" title="Sturmabteilung">Sturmabteilung</a> (SA) <a href="/wiki/Ernst_R%C3%B6hm" title="Ernst Röhm">Ernst Röhm</a>, a man with whom he clearly had a close working relationship. Rohm was ruthlessly murdered on Hitler's orders a short time later during the purge of the SA referred to as the <a href="/wiki/Night_of_the_Long_Knives" title="Night of the Long Knives">Night of the Long Knives</a>. It has gone on record that, immediately following the killings, Hitler subsequently ordered all copies of the film to be destroyed, although Riefenstahl disputes that this ever happened.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>

<p>Still Impressed with Riefenstahl's work, Hitler asked her to film <i><a href="/wiki/Triumph_des_Willens" class="mw-redirect" title="Triumph des Willens">Triumph des Willens</a></i> ("Triumph of the Will"), a new propaganda film about the 1934 party rally in Nuremberg.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHinton200020_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHinton200020-22">[22]</a></sup> More than one million Germans participated in the rally.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEThe_History_Place_2001_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEThe_History_Place_2001-23">[23]</a></sup> Initially, according to Riefenstahl, she resisted and did not want to create further Nazi Party films, instead wanting to direct a feature film based on Hitler's favourite opera, <a href="/wiki/Eugen_d%27Albert" title="Eugen d'Albert">Eugen d'Albert</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Tiefland_(opera)" title="Tiefland (opera)">Tiefland</a></i> ("Lowlands").<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHinton200020_22-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHinton200020-22">[22]</a></sup> Riefenstahl received private funding for the production of <i>Tiefland</i>, but the filming in Spain was derailed and the project was cancelled.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHinton200020_22-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHinton200020-22">[22]</a></sup> Hitler was able to convince her to film <i>Triumph des Willens</i> on the condition that she would not be required to make further films for the party, according to Riefenstahl.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHinton200021.E2.80.9322_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHinton200021.E2.80.9322-24">[24]</a></sup> The motion picture was generally recognized as an epic, innovative work of <a href="/wiki/Propaganda" title="Propaganda">propaganda</a> filmmaking.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHinton200021.E2.80.9322_24-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHinton200021.E2.80.9322-24">[24]</a></sup> The film took Riefenstahl's career to a new level and gave her further international recognition.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHinton200021_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHinton200021-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<p>In interviews for the 1993 documentary <i><a href="/wiki/The_Wonderful,_Horrible_Life_of_Leni_Riefenstahl" class="mw-redirect" title="The Wonderful, Horrible Life of Leni Riefenstahl">The Wonderful, Horrible Life of Leni Riefenstahl</a></i>, Riefenstahl adamantly denied any deliberate attempt to create <a href="/wiki/Nazi_propaganda" class="mw-redirect" title="Nazi propaganda">Nazi propaganda</a> and said she was disgusted that <i>Triumph des Willens</i> was used in such a way.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEM.C3.BCller_1999_17-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEM.C3.BCller_1999-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>Despite allegedly vowing not to make any more films about the Nazi Party, Riefenstahl made the 28-minute <i><a href="/wiki/Tag_der_Freiheit:_Unsere_Wehrmacht" title="Tag der Freiheit: Unsere Wehrmacht">Tag der Freiheit: Unsere Wehrmacht</a></i> ("Day of Freedom: Our Armed Forces") about the <a href="/wiki/German_Army_(Wehrmacht)" title="German Army (Wehrmacht)">German Army</a> in 1935.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERother2003238_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERother2003238-26">[26]</a></sup> Like <i>Der Sieg des Glaubens</i> and <i>Triumph des Willens</i>, this was filmed at the annual Nazi Party rally at Nuremberg.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERother2003238_26-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERother2003238-26">[26]</a></sup> Riefenstahl said this film was a sub-set of <i>Der Sieg des Glaubens</i>, added to mollify the German Army which felt it was not represented well in <i>Triumph des Willens</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAitken2013760_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAitken2013760-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p>Hitler invited Riefenstahl to film the <a href="/wiki/1936_Summer_Olympics" title="1936 Summer Olympics">1936 Summer Olympics</a> scheduled to be held in Berlin, a film which Riefenstahl claimed had been commissioned by the <a href="/wiki/International_Olympic_Committee" title="International Olympic Committee">International Olympic Committee</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTETomlinson201274.E2.80.9376_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTETomlinson201274.E2.80.9376-28">[28]</a></sup> She visited Greece to take footage of the route of the <a href="/wiki/1936_Summer_Olympics_torch_relay" title="1936 Summer Olympics torch relay">inaugural torch relay</a> and the games' original site at <a href="/wiki/Olympia,_Greece" title="Olympia, Greece">Olympia</a>, where she was aided by Greek photographer <a href="/wiki/Nelly%27s" title="Nelly's">Nelly's</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTETomlinson201274.E2.80.9376_28-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTETomlinson201274.E2.80.9376-28">[28]</a></sup> This material became <i><a href="/wiki/Olympia_(1938_film)" title="Olympia (1938 film)">Olympia</a></i>, a hugely successful film which has since been widely noted for its technical and aesthetic achievements.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTETomlinson201274.E2.80.9376_28-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTETomlinson201274.E2.80.9376-28">[28]</a></sup> She was one of the first filmmakers to use <a href="/wiki/Tracking_shot" title="Tracking shot">tracking shots</a> in a documentary,<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAndrew1999183.E2.80.93184_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAndrew1999183.E2.80.93184-29">[29]</a></sup> placing a camera on rails to follow the athletes' movement. The film is also noted for its slow motion shots.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAndrew1999183.E2.80.93184_29-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAndrew1999183.E2.80.93184-29">[29]</a></sup> Riefenstahl played with the idea of slow motion, underwater diving shots, extremely high and low shooting angles, panoramic aerial shots, and tracking system shots for allowing fast action. Many of these shots were relatively unheard of at the time, but Leni’s use and augmentation of them set a standard, and is the reason why they are still used to this day.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> Riefenstahl's work on <i>Olympia</i> has been cited as a major influence in modern sports photography.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTETomlinson201274.E2.80.9376_28-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTETomlinson201274.E2.80.9376-28">[28]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAndrew1999183.E2.80.93184_29-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAndrew1999183.E2.80.93184-29">[29]</a></sup> Riefenstahl filmed competitors of all races, including African-American <a href="/wiki/Jesse_Owens" title="Jesse Owens">Jesse Owens</a> in what later became famous footage.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEEdmondson200772_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEEdmondson200772-31">[31]</a></sup></p>

Riefenstahl in conversation with Propaganda Minister <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Goebbels" title="Joseph Goebbels">Joseph Goebbels</a>, 1937</div>

<p><i>Olympia</i> premiered for Hitler's 49th birthday in 1938. Its international debut led Riefenstahl to embark on an American publicity tour in an attempt to secure commercial release.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEF-R_Publishing_Corporation_2007_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEF-R_Publishing_Corporation_2007-32">[32]</a></sup> In February 1937, Riefenstahl enthusiastically told a reporter for the <i><a href="/wiki/Detroit_News" class="mw-redirect" title="Detroit News">Detroit News</a></i>, "To me, Hitler is the greatest man who ever lived. He truly is without fault, so simple and at the same time possessed of masculine strength".<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERollyson_2007_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERollyson_2007-33">[33]</a></sup> She arrived in New York City on 4 November 1938, five days before <i><a href="/wiki/Kristallnacht" title="Kristallnacht">Kristallnacht</a></i> (the "Night of the Broken Glass").<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGraham_1993_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGraham_1993-34">[34]</a></sup> When news of the event reached the United States,<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGraham_1993_34-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGraham_1993-34">[34]</a></sup> Riefenstahl publicly defended Hitler.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGraham_1993_34-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGraham_1993-34">[34]</a></sup> On 18 November, she was received by <a href="/wiki/Henry_Ford" title="Henry Ford">Henry Ford</a> in Detroit. <i>Olympia</i> was shown at the Chicago Engineers Club two days later.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGraham_1993_34-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGraham_1993-34">[34]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Avery_Brundage" title="Avery Brundage">Avery Brundage</a>, President of the International Olympic Committee, praised the film and held Riefenstahl in the highest regard.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBernstein2013111_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBernstein2013111-35">[35]</a></sup> She negotiated with <a href="/wiki/Louis_B._Mayer" title="Louis B. Mayer">Louis B. Mayer</a>, and on 8 December, <a href="/wiki/Walt_Disney" title="Walt Disney">Walt Disney</a> brought her on a three-hour tour showing her the ongoing production of <i><a href="/wiki/Fantasia_(1940_film)" title="Fantasia (1940 film)">Fantasia</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGraham_1993_34-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGraham_1993-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
<p>From the <i><a href="/wiki/Goebbels_Diaries" title="Goebbels Diaries">Goebbels Diaries</a></i>, researchers learned that Riefenstahl had been friendly with <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Goebbels" title="Joseph Goebbels">Joseph Goebbels</a> and his wife <a href="/wiki/Magda_Goebbels" title="Magda Goebbels">Magda</a>, attending the opera with them and going to his parties.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERollyson_2007_33-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERollyson_2007-33">[33]</a></sup> Riefenstahl maintained that Goebbels was upset when she rejected his advances and was jealous of her influence on Hitler, seeing her as an internal threat.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEJames_2007_15-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJames_2007-15">[15]</a></sup> She therefore insisted his diary entries could not be trusted.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEJames_2007_15-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJames_2007-15">[15]</a></sup> By later accounts, Goebbels thought highly of Riefenstahl's filmmaking but was angered with what he saw as her overspending on the Nazi-provided filmmaking budgets.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEJames_2007_15-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJames_2007-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="World_War_II">World War II</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Leni_Riefenstahl&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: World War II">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>When <a href="/wiki/Invasion_of_Poland" title="Invasion of Poland">Germany invaded Poland</a> on 1 September 1939, Riefenstahl was photographed in Poland wearing a military uniform and a pistol on her belt in the company of German soldiers; she had gone to Poland as a war correspondent.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERobert_2013_36-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERobert_2013-36">[36]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEJames_2007_15-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJames_2007-15">[15]</a></sup> On 12 September, she was in the town of <a href="/wiki/Ko%C5%84skie" title="Końskie">Końskie</a> when 30 civilians were executed in retaliation for an alleged attack on German soldiers.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHarris_2007_37-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHarris_2007-37">[37]</a></sup> According to her <a href="/wiki/Memoir" title="Memoir">memoir</a>, Riefenstahl tried to intervene but a furious German soldier held her at gunpoint and threatened to shoot her on the spot.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEJames_2007_15-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJames_2007-15">[15]</a></sup> She claimed she did not realize the victims were Jews.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEJames_2007_15-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJames_2007-15">[15]</a></sup> Closeup photographs of a distraught Riefenstahl survive from that day.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEJames_2007_15-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJames_2007-15">[15]</a></sup> Nevertheless, by 5 October 1939, Riefenstahl was back in <a href="/wiki/History_of_Poland_(1939%E2%80%9345)" class="mw-redirect" title="History of Poland (1939–45)">occupied Poland</a> filming Hitler's <a href="/wiki/Invasion_of_Poland_(1939)#Aftermath" class="mw-redirect" title="Invasion of Poland (1939)">victory parade in Warsaw</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHarris_2007_37-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHarris_2007-37">[37]</a></sup> Afterwards, she left Poland and chose not to make any more Nazi-related movies.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMoore_2003_38-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMoore_2003-38">[38]</a></sup></p>

<p>On 14 June 1940, the day Paris was declared an <a href="/wiki/Open_city" title="Open city">open city</a> by the French and occupied by German troops, Riefenstahl wrote to Hitler in a <a href="/wiki/Telegram" class="mw-redirect" title="Telegram">telegram</a>, "With indescribable joy, deeply moved and filled with burning gratitude, we share with you, my Führer, your and Germany's greatest victory, the entry of German troops into Paris. You exceed anything human imagination has the power to conceive, achieving deeds without parallel in the history of mankind. How can we ever thank you?"<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHarris_2007_37-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHarris_2007-37">[37]</a></sup> She later explained, "Everyone thought the war was over, and in that spirit I sent the cable to Hitler".<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERiding_2003_39-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERiding_2003-39">[39]</a></sup> Riefenstahl was friends with Hitler for 12 years and reports vary as to whether she ever had an intimate relationship with him.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMathews_40-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMathews-40">[40]</a></sup> Her relationship with Hitler severely declined in 1944 after her brother died on the Russian Front.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMoore_2003_38-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMoore_2003-38">[38]</a></sup></p>
<p>After the Nuremberg rallies trilogy and <i>Olympia</i>, Riefenstahl began work on the movie she had tried and failed to direct once before, namely <i>Tiefland</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKenrick2006197_41-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKenrick2006197-41">[41]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTETrimborn_2007_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTETrimborn_2007-8">[8]</a></sup> On Hitler's direct order, the <a href="/wiki/Government_of_Nazi_Germany" title="Government of Nazi Germany">German government</a> paid her seven million <i>Reichsmarks</i> in compensation.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> From 23 September until 13 November 1940, she filmed in <a href="/wiki/Kr%C3%BCn" title="Krün">Krün</a> near <a href="/wiki/Mittenwald" title="Mittenwald">Mittenwald</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKenrick2006197_41-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKenrick2006197-41">[41]</a></sup> The extras playing Spanish women and farmers were drawn from gypsies detained in a camp at <a href="/wiki/Salzburg" title="Salzburg">Salzburg-Maxglan</a> who were forced to work with her.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKenrick2006197_41-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKenrick2006197-41">[41]</a></sup> Filming at the <a href="/wiki/Babelsberg_Studios" class="mw-redirect" title="Babelsberg Studios">Babelsberg Studios</a> near Berlin began 18 months later in April 1942.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKenrick2006197_41-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKenrick2006197-41">[41]</a></sup> This time <a href="/wiki/Sinti" title="Sinti">Sinti</a> and <a href="/wiki/Romani_people" title="Romani people">Roma</a> people from the <a href="/wiki/Marzahn" title="Marzahn">Marzahn</a> detention camp near Berlin were compelled to work as extras.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKenrick2006197_41-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKenrick2006197-41">[41]</a></sup> Almost to the end of her life, despite overwhelming evidence that the concentration camp occupants had been forced to work on the movie unpaid, Riefenstahl continued to maintain all the film extras survived and that she had met several of them after the war.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTETrimborn2008206.E2.80.93208_43-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTETrimborn2008206.E2.80.93208-43">[43]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMathews_40-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMathews-40">[40]</a></sup> Riefenstahl sued filmmaker Nina Gladitz, who said Riefenstahl personally chose the extras at their holding camp; Gladitz had found one of the Gypsy survivors and matched his memory with stills of the movie for a documentary Gladitz was filming.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTETaylor_2007_44-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTETaylor_2007-44">[44]</a></sup> The German court ruled largely in favour of Gladitz, declaring that Riefenstahl had known the extras were from a concentration camp, but they also agreed that Riefenstahl had not been informed the Gypsies would be sent to <a href="/wiki/Auschwitz" class="mw-redirect" title="Auschwitz">Auschwitz</a> after filming was completed.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTETaylor_2007_44-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTETaylor_2007-44">[44]</a></sup></p>

<p>This issue came up again in 2002, when Riefenstahl was one hundred years old and she was taken to court by a Roma group for denying the Nazis had exterminated gypsies.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEConnolly_2002_45-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEConnolly_2002-45">[45]</a></sup> Riefenstahl apologized and said, "I regret that Sinti and Roma [people] had to suffer during the period of National Socialism. It is known today that many of them were murdered in concentration camps".<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEConnolly_2002_45-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEConnolly_2002-45">[45]</a></sup></p>
<p>In October 1944 the production of <i>Tiefland</i> moved to <a href="/wiki/Barrandov_Studios" title="Barrandov Studios">Barrandov Studios</a> in Prague for interior filming.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEUniversity_of_Washington_2008_7-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEUniversity_of_Washington_2008-7">[7]</a></sup> Lavish sets made these shots some of the most costly of the film.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEUniversity_of_Washington_2008_7-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEUniversity_of_Washington_2008-7">[7]</a></sup> The film was not edited and released until almost ten years later.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEUniversity_of_Washington_2008_7-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEUniversity_of_Washington_2008-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>The last time Riefenstahl saw Hitler was when she married Peter Jacob on 21 March 1944.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERiding_2003_39-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERiding_2003-39">[39]</a></sup> Riefenstahl and Jacob divorced in 1946.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHeck-Rabi1984126_46-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHeck-Rabi1984126-46">[46]</a></sup> As Germany's military situation became impossible by early 1945, Riefenstahl left Berlin and was <a href="/wiki/Hitchhiking" title="Hitchhiking">hitchhiking</a> with a group of men, trying to reach her mother, when she was taken into custody by American troops.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEUniversity_of_Washington_2008_7-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEUniversity_of_Washington_2008-7">[7]</a></sup> She walked out of a holding camp, beginning a series of escapes and arrests across the chaotic landscape.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEUniversity_of_Washington_2008_7-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEUniversity_of_Washington_2008-7">[7]</a></sup> At last making it back home on a bicycle, she found that American troops had seized her house.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEUniversity_of_Washington_2008_7-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEUniversity_of_Washington_2008-7">[7]</a></sup> She was surprised by how kindly they treated her.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEUniversity_of_Washington_2008_7-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEUniversity_of_Washington_2008-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Thwarted_film_projects">Thwarted film projects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Leni_Riefenstahl&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Thwarted film projects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Most of Riefenstahl's unfinished projects were lost towards the end of the war.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEUniversity_of_Washington_2008_7-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEUniversity_of_Washington_2008-7">[7]</a></sup> The French government confiscated all of her editing equipment, along with the production reels of <i>Tiefland</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEUniversity_of_Washington_2008_7-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEUniversity_of_Washington_2008-7">[7]</a></sup> After years of legal wrangling, these were returned to her, but the French government had reportedly damaged some of the film stock whilst trying to develop and edit it, with a few key scenes being missing (although Riefenstahl was surprised to find the original negatives for <i>Olympia</i> in the same shipment).<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEUniversity_of_Washington_2008_7-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEUniversity_of_Washington_2008-7">[7]</a></sup> She edited and dubbed the remaining material and <i>Tiefland</i> premiered on 11 February 1954 in <a href="/wiki/Stuttgart" title="Stuttgart">Stuttgart</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEUniversity_of_Washington_2008_7-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEUniversity_of_Washington_2008-7">[7]</a></sup> However, it was denied entry into the <a href="/wiki/Cannes_Film_Festival" title="Cannes Film Festival">Cannes Film Festival</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEUniversity_of_Washington_2008_7-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEUniversity_of_Washington_2008-7">[7]</a></sup> Although Riefenstahl lived for almost another half century, <i>Tiefland</i> was her last feature film.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBBC_News_2005_47-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBBC_News_2005-47">[47]</a></sup></p>

</div>
<p>Riefenstahl tried many times to make more films during the 1950s and 1960s, but was met with resistance, public protests and sharp criticism.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEUniversity_of_Washington_2008_7-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEUniversity_of_Washington_2008-7">[7]</a></sup> Many of her filmmaking peers in Hollywood had fled Nazi Germany and were unsympathetic to her.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEUniversity_of_Washington_2008_7-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEUniversity_of_Washington_2008-7">[7]</a></sup> Although both film professionals and investors were willing to support her work, most of the projects she attempted were stopped owing to ever-renewed and highly negative publicity about her past work for the Third Reich.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEUniversity_of_Washington_2008_7-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEUniversity_of_Washington_2008-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1954, <a href="/wiki/Jean_Cocteau" title="Jean Cocteau">Jean Cocteau</a>, who greatly admired the film, insisted on <i>Tiefland</i> being shown at the Cannes Film Festival, which he was running that year.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFalcon_2003_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFalcon_2003-11">[11]</a></sup> In 1960, Riefenstahl attempted to prevent filmmaker <a href="/wiki/Erwin_Leiser" title="Erwin Leiser">Erwin Leiser</a> from juxtaposing scenes from <i>Triumph des Willens</i> with footage from concentration camps in his film <i>Mein Kampf</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFalcon_2003_11-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFalcon_2003-11">[11]</a></sup> Riefenstahl had high hopes for a collaboration with Cocteau called <i>Friedrich und Voltaire</i> ("Friedrich and Voltaire"), wherein Cocteau was to play two roles.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHinton2000135_48-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHinton2000135-48">[48]</a></sup> They thought the film might symbolize the love-hate relationship between Germany and France.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHinton2000135_48-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHinton2000135-48">[48]</a></sup> Cocteau's illness and 1963 death put an end to the project.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHinton2000135_48-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHinton2000135-48">[48]</a></sup> A musical remake of <i>Das Blaue Licht</i> ("The Blue Light") with an English production company also fell apart.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHinton2000136_49-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHinton2000136-49">[49]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the 1960s, Riefenstahl became interested in Africa from <a href="/wiki/Ernest_Hemingway" title="Ernest Hemingway">Ernest Hemingway</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Green_Hills_of_Africa" title="Green Hills of Africa">Green Hills of Africa</a></i> and from the photographs of <a href="/wiki/George_Rodger" title="George Rodger">George Rodger</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERhielO.27SickeyPages2008244_50-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERhielO.27SickeyPages2008244-50">[50]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMoore_2003_38-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMoore_2003-38">[38]</a></sup> She visited Kenya for the first time in 1956 and later Sudan, where she photographed <a href="/wiki/Nuba_peoples" title="Nuba peoples">Nuba</a> tribes with whom she sporadically lived, learning about their culture so she could photograph them more easily.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERhielO.27SickeyPages2008244_50-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERhielO.27SickeyPages2008244-50">[50]</a></sup> Even though her film project about modern slavery entitled <i>Die Schwarze Fracht</i> ("The Black Cargo") was never completed, Riefenstahl was able to sell the stills from the expedition to magazines in various parts of the world.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERhielO.27SickeyPages2008244_50-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERhielO.27SickeyPages2008244-50">[50]</a></sup> While scouting shooting locations, she almost died from injuries received in a truck accident.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEUniversity_of_Washington_2008_7-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEUniversity_of_Washington_2008-7">[7]</a></sup> After waking up from a coma in a <a href="/wiki/Nairobi" title="Nairobi">Nairobi</a> hospital, she finished writing the script, but was soon thoroughly thwarted by uncooperative locals, the <a href="/wiki/Suez_Crisis" title="Suez Crisis">Suez Canal crisis</a> and bad weather.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEUniversity_of_Washington_2008_7-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEUniversity_of_Washington_2008-7">[7]</a></sup> In the end, the film project was called off.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEUniversity_of_Washington_2008_7-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEUniversity_of_Washington_2008-7">[7]</a></sup> Even so, Riefenstahl was granted <a href="/wiki/Sudanese_people" class="mw-redirect" title="Sudanese people">Sudanese</a> citizenship for her services to the country, becoming the first foreigner to receive a Sudanese passport.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESalkeld2011253_51-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESalkeld2011253-51">[51]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Post-war_life">Post-war life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Leni_Riefenstahl&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Post-war life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Detention_and_trials">Detention and trials</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Leni_Riefenstahl&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Detention and trials">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Novelist and sports writer <a href="/wiki/Budd_Schulberg" title="Budd Schulberg">Budd Schulberg</a>, assigned by the <a href="/wiki/U.S._Navy" class="mw-redirect" title="U.S. Navy">U.S. Navy</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Office_of_Strategic_Services" title="Office of Strategic Services">OSS</a> for intelligence work while attached to <a href="/wiki/John_Ford" title="John Ford">John Ford</a>'s documentary unit, was ordered to arrest Riefenstahl at her chalet in Kitzbühel, ostensibly to have her identify Nazi war criminals in German film footage captured by the Allied troops shortly after the war.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESalkeld2011Chapter_17.2C_page_1-3_52-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESalkeld2011Chapter_17.2C_page_1-3-52">[52]</a></sup> Riefenstahl claimed she was not aware of the nature of the internment camps.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKennicott_2005_53-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKennicott_2005-53">[53]</a></sup> According to Schulberg, "She gave me the usual song and dance. She said, 'Of course, you know, I'm really so misunderstood. I'm not political'".<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKennicott_2005_53-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKennicott_2005-53">[53]</a></sup></p>
<p>Riefenstahl claimed she was fascinated by the Nazis, but also politically naive, remaining ignorant about war crimes.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEThe_Scotsman_2012_54-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEThe_Scotsman_2012-54">[54]</a></sup> Throughout 1945 to 1948, she was held by various Allied-controlled prison camps across Germany.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEThe_Scotsman_2012_54-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEThe_Scotsman_2012-54">[54]</a></sup> She was also under house arrest for a period of time.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEThe_Scotsman_2012_54-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEThe_Scotsman_2012-54">[54]</a></sup> She was tried four times by postwar authorities for <a href="/wiki/Denazification" title="Denazification">denazification</a> and eventually found to be a "fellow traveler" (<i><a href="/wiki/Mitl%C3%A4ufer" title="Mitläufer">Mitläufer</a></i>) who sympathised with the Nazis.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEThe_Scotsman_2012_54-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEThe_Scotsman_2012-54">[54]</a></sup></p>
<p>Riefenstahl said that her biggest regret in life was meeting Hitler, declaring, "It was the biggest catastrophe of my life. Until the day I die people will keep saying, 'Leni is a Nazi', and I'll keep saying, 'But what did she do?'"<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEThe_Scotsman_2012_54-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEThe_Scotsman_2012-54">[54]</a></sup> Even though she went on to win up to 50 libel cases, details about her relation to the Nazi party generally remain unclear.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEThe_Scotsman_2012_54-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEThe_Scotsman_2012-54">[54]</a></sup></p>
<p>Shortly before she died, Riefenstahl voiced her final words on the subject of her connection to Adolf Hitler in a BBC interview: "<i>I was one of millions who thought Hitler had all the answers. We saw only the good things; we didn’t know bad things were to come."</i><sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Books_and_final_film">Books and final film</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Leni_Riefenstahl&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Books and final film">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Riefenstahl began a lifelong companionship with her cameraman Horst Kettner, who was 40 years her junior and assisted her with the photographs; they were together from the time she was 60 and he was 20.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECorliss_2002_56-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECorliss_2002-56">[56]</a></sup></p>
<p>Riefenstahl's books with photographs of the Nuba tribes were published in 1974 and republished in 1976 as <i><a href="/wiki/The_Last_of_the_Nuba" title="The Last of the Nuba">Die Nuba</a></i> (translated as "The Last of the Nuba") and <i><a href="/wiki/The_People_of_Kau" title="The People of Kau">Die Nuba von Kau</a></i> ("The Nuba People of Kau"). While heralded by many as outstanding colour photographs, they were harshly criticized by <a href="/wiki/Susan_Sontag" title="Susan Sontag">Susan Sontag</a>, who claimed in a review that they were further evidence of Riefenstahl's "fascist aesthetics".<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESuntag_2007_57-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESuntag_2007-57">[57]</a></sup> The Art Director's Club of Germany awarded Riefenstahl a gold medal for the best photographic achievement of 1975.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESuntag_2007_57-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESuntag_2007-57">[57]</a></sup> She also sold some of the pictures to German magazines.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEUniversity_of_Washington_2008_7-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEUniversity_of_Washington_2008-7">[7]</a></sup> She photographed the <a href="/wiki/1972_Summer_Olympics" title="1972 Summer Olympics">1972 Olympic Games</a> in Munich, and rock star <a href="/wiki/Mick_Jagger" title="Mick Jagger">Mick Jagger</a> along with his wife <a href="/wiki/Bianca_Jagger" title="Bianca Jagger">Bianca</a> for <i><a href="/wiki/The_Sunday_Times" title="The Sunday Times">The Sunday Times</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFalcon_2003_11-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFalcon_2003-11">[11]</a></sup> Years later, Riefenstahl photographed <a href="/wiki/Las_Vegas_Strip" title="Las Vegas Strip">Las Vegas</a> entertainers <a href="/wiki/Siegfried_%26_Roy" title="Siegfried &amp; Roy">Siegfried &amp; Roy</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELeni_Riefenstahl_2001_58-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELeni_Riefenstahl_2001-58">[58]</a></sup> She was guest of honour at the <a href="/wiki/1976_Summer_Olympics" title="1976 Summer Olympics">1976 Olympic Games</a> in <a href="/wiki/Montreal" title="Montreal">Montreal</a>, Canada.<sup id="cite_ref-59" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1978, Riefenstahl published a book of her sub-aquatic photographs called <i><a href="/wiki/Koralleng%C3%A4rten" class="mw-redirect" title="Korallengärten">Korallengärten</a></i> ("Coral Gardens"), followed by the 1990 book <i><a href="/wiki/Wunder_unter_Wasser" class="mw-redirect" title="Wunder unter Wasser">Wunder unter Wasser</a></i> ("Wonder under Water").<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBBC_News_2002_60-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBBC_News_2002-60">[60]</a></sup> In her 90s, Riefenstahl was still photographing marine life and gained the distinction of being one of the world's oldest scuba divers.<sup id="cite_ref-61" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup> On 22 August 2002, her 100th birthday, she released the film <i><a href="/wiki/Impressionen_unter_Wasser" title="Impressionen unter Wasser">Impressionen unter Wasser</a></i> ("Underwater Impressions"), an idealized documentary of life in the oceans and her first film in over 25 years.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHarris_2007_37-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHarris_2007-37">[37]</a></sup> Riefenstahl was a member of <a href="/wiki/Greenpeace" title="Greenpeace">Greenpeace</a> for eight years.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESteinberg_2003_62-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESteinberg_2003-62">[62]</a></sup></p>
<p>Riefenstahl survived a <a href="/wiki/Helicopter" title="Helicopter">helicopter</a> crash in Sudan in 2000 while trying to learn the fates of her Nuba friends during the <a href="/wiki/Second_Sudanese_Civil_War" title="Second Sudanese Civil War">Second Sudanese Civil War</a> and was airlifted to a Munich hospital where she received treatment for two broken ribs.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBBC_News_2001_63-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBBC_News_2001-63">[63]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMoore_2003_38-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMoore_2003-38">[38]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Death">Death</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Leni_Riefenstahl&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Death">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>Riefenstahl celebrated her 101st birthday on 22 August 2003 at a hotel in <a href="/wiki/Feldafing" title="Feldafing">Feldafing</a>, on <a href="/wiki/Lake_Starnberg" title="Lake Starnberg">Lake Starnberg</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bavaria" title="Bavaria">Bavaria</a>, near her home. The day after her birthday celebration, she became ill.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDavis_2003_64-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDavis_2003-64">[64]</a></sup></p>
<p>Riefenstahl had been suffering from cancer for some time, and her health rapidly deteriorated during the last weeks of her life.<sup id="cite_ref-65" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup> Kettner said in an interview in 2002, "Ms. Riefenstahl is in great pain and she has become very weak and is taking painkillers".<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFantz_2002_66-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFantz_2002-66">[66]</a></sup> Leni Riefenstahl died in her sleep at around 10:00 pm on 8 September 2003 at her home in <a href="/wiki/P%C3%B6cking" title="Pöcking">Pöcking</a>, Germany.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERhielO.27SickeyPages2008245_67-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERhielO.27SickeyPages2008245-67">[67]</a></sup> After her death, there was a varied response in the obituary pages of leading publications, although most recognized her technical breakthroughs in film making.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMoore_2003_38-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMoore_2003-38">[38]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reception">Reception</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Leni_Riefenstahl&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Reception">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Film scholar <a href="/wiki/Mark_Cousins_(film_critic)" title="Mark Cousins (film critic)">Mark Cousins</a> notes in his book <i><a href="/wiki/Mark_Cousins_(film_critic)#Career" title="Mark Cousins (film critic)">The Story of Film</a></i> that, "Next to <a href="/wiki/Orson_Welles" title="Orson Welles">Orson Welles</a> and <a href="/wiki/Alfred_Hitchcock" title="Alfred Hitchcock">Alfred Hitchcock</a>, Leni Riefenstahl was the most technically talented Western film maker of her era".<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWorld_Press_Review_2014_68-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWorld_Press_Review_2014-68">[68]</a></sup></p>
<p>Reviewer Gary Morris called Riefenstahl, "An artist of unparalleled gifts, a woman in an industry dominated by men, one of the great formalists of the cinema on a par with <a href="/wiki/Sergei_Eisenstein" title="Sergei Eisenstein">Eisenstein</a> or <a href="/wiki/Orson_Welles" title="Orson Welles">Welles</a>".<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMorris_1999_69-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMorris_1999-69">[69]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tleft">

<p>Film critic Hal Erickson of the <i><a href="/wiki/New_York_Times" class="mw-redirect" title="New York Times">New York Times</a></i> states that the "<a href="/wiki/Jewish_Question" class="mw-redirect" title="Jewish Question">Jewish Question</a>" is mainly unmentioned in <i>Triumph des Willens</i>; "filmmaker Leni Riefenstahl prefers to concentrate on cheering crowds, precision marching, military bands, and Hitler's climactic speech, all orchestrated, choreographed and illuminated on a scale that makes Griffith and DeMille look like poverty-row directors".<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEErickson_70-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEErickson-70">[70]</a></sup></p>
<p>Charles Moore of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Daily_Telegraph" title="The Daily Telegraph">The Daily Telegraph</a></i> wrote, "She was perhaps the most talented female cinema director of the 20th century; her celebration of Nazi Germany in film ensured that she was certainly the most infamous".<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMoore_2003_38-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMoore_2003-38">[38]</a></sup></p>
<p>Film journalist Sandra Smith from <i><a href="/wiki/The_Independent" title="The Independent">The Independent</a></i> remarked, "Opinions will be divided between those who see her as a young, talented and ambitious woman caught up in the tide of events which she did not fully understand, and those who believe her to be a cold and opportunist propagandist and a Nazi by association."<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESmith_2003_71-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESmith_2003-71">[71]</a></sup></p>
<p>Critic Judith Thurman said in <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_Yorker" title="The New Yorker">The New Yorker</a></i> that, "Riefenstahl's genius has rarely been questioned, even by critics who despise the service to which she lent it. Riefenstahl was a consummate stylist obsessed with bodies in motion, particularly those of dancers and athletes. Riefenstahl relies heavily for her transitions on portentous cutaways to clouds, mist, statuary, foliage, and rooftops. Her reaction shots have a tedious sameness: shining, ecstatic faces—nearly all young and Aryan, except for Hitler's".<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEThurman_2007_72-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEThurman_2007-72">[72]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Pauline_Kael" title="Pauline Kael">Pauline Kael</a>, also a film reviewer employed for <i>The New Yorker</i>, called <i>Triumph des Willens</i> and <i>Olympia</i>, "the two greatest films ever directed by a woman".<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECorliss_2002_56-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECorliss_2002-56">[56]</a></sup></p>
<p>Writer Richard Corliss wrote in <i><a href="/wiki/Time_(magazine)" title="Time (magazine)">Time</a></i> that he was "impressed by Riefenstahl's standing as a total auteur: producer, writer, director, editor and, in the fiction films, actress. The issues her films and her career raise are as complex and they are important, and her vilifiers tend to reduce the argument to one of a director's complicity in atrocity or her criminal ignorance".<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECorliss_2002_56-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECorliss_2002-56">[56]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Film_biographies">Film biographies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Leni_Riefenstahl&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Film biographies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1993, Riefenstahl was the subject of the award-winning German documentary film <i><a href="/wiki/The_Wonderful_Horrible_Life_of_Leni_Riefenstahl" title="The Wonderful Horrible Life of Leni Riefenstahl">The Wonderful, Horrible Life of Leni Riefenstahl</a></i>, directed by Ray Müller.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGale20073276_73-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGale20073276-73">[73]</a></sup> Riefenstahl appeared in the film and answered several questions and detailed the production of her films.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGale20073276_73-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGale20073276-73">[73]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEEbert_Ebert_74-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEEbert_Ebert-74">[74]</a></sup> The biofilm was nominated for seven <a href="/wiki/Emmy_Award" title="Emmy Award">Emmy Awards</a>, winning in one category.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGale20073276_73-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGale20073276-73">[73]</a></sup> Riefenstahl, who for some time had been working on her memoirs, decided to cooperate in the production of this documentary to tell her life story about the struggles she had gone through in her personal life, her film-making career and what people thought of her.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEEbert_Ebert_74-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEEbert_Ebert-74">[74]</a></sup> She was also the subject of Müller's 2000 documentary film <i><a href="/wiki/Leni_Riefenstahl:_Her_Dream_of_Africa" title="Leni Riefenstahl: Her Dream of Africa">Leni Riefenstahl: Her Dream of Africa</a></i>, about her return to Sudan to visit the Nuba people.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBBC_News_2001_63-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBBC_News_2001-63">[63]</a></sup></p>
<p>In April 2007, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Guardian" title="The Guardian">The Guardian</a></i> reported that British screenwriter Rupert Walters was writing a movie based on Riefenstahl's life which would star actress <a href="/wiki/Jodie_Foster" title="Jodie Foster">Jodie Foster</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHarris_2007_37-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHarris_2007-37">[37]</a></sup> The project did not receive Riefenstahl's approval prior to her death, as Riefenstahl asked for a veto on any scenes to which she did not agree.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHarris_2007_37-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHarris_2007-37">[37]</a></sup> Riefenstahl also wanted <a href="/wiki/Sharon_Stone" title="Sharon Stone">Sharon Stone</a> to play her rather than Foster, which ultimately resulted in the cancellation of the project.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHarris_2007_37-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHarris_2007-37">[37]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2011, director <a href="/wiki/Steven_Soderbergh" title="Steven Soderbergh">Steven Soderbergh</a> revealed that he had also been working on a biopic of Riefenstahl for about six months.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEJagernauth_2011_75-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJagernauth_2011-75">[75]</a></sup> He eventually abandoned the project over concerns of its commercial prospects.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEJagernauth_2011_75-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJagernauth_2011-75">[75]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_popular_culture">In popular culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Leni_Riefenstahl&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: In popular culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Riefenstahl's filming merits are discussed between characters in the 2009 <a href="/wiki/Quentin_Tarantino" title="Quentin Tarantino">Quentin Tarantino</a> film <i><a href="/wiki/Inglourious_Basterds" title="Inglourious Basterds">Inglourious Basterds</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWhipp_2009_76-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWhipp_2009-76">[76]</a></sup></p>
<p>Riefenstahl was portrayed by <a href="/wiki/Zdena_Studenkov%C3%A1" title="Zdena Studenková">Zdena Studenková</a> in <i>Leni</i>, a 2014 Slovak drama play about her fictional participation in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Tonight_Show_Starring_Johnny_Carson" title="The Tonight Show Starring Johnny Carson">The Tonight Show Starring Johnny Carson</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESlovensk.C3.A9_N.C3.A1rodn.C3.A9_Divadlo_2014_77-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESlovensk.C3.A9_N.C3.A1rodn.C3.A9_Divadlo_2014-77">[77]</a></sup></p>
<p>Riefenstahl was portrayed by Dutch actress <a href="/wiki/Carice_van_Houten" title="Carice van Houten">Carice van Houten</a> in <i><a href="/wiki/Race_(2016_film)" title="Race (2016 film)">Race</a></i>, a <a href="/wiki/Sports_film" title="Sports film">sports</a> <a href="/wiki/Drama_film" class="mw-redirect" title="Drama film">drama film</a> directed by <a href="/wiki/Stephen_Hopkins_(director)" title="Stephen Hopkins (director)">Stephen Hopkins</a> about <a href="/wiki/Jesse_Owens" title="Jesse Owens">Jesse Owens</a>. It was released in North America on February 19, 2016.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKit_2014_78-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKit_2014-78">[78]</a></sup> To make her sympathetic portrayal acceptable to an American audience, the film dramatizes her quarrels with Goebbels over her direction of the film, <i>Olympia</i>, especially about filming the African American star who is proving to be a politically embarrassing refutation of Nazi Germany's claims of Aryan athletic supremacy.</p>
<p>Riefenstahl was referred to in the series finale of the television show <a href="/wiki/Weeds_(tv_series)" class="mw-redirect" title="Weeds (tv series)"><i>Weeds</i></a> when Nancy questions Andy for naming his daughter after a Nazi to which he replied "she was a pioneer in film-making, I don't believe in holding grudges."<sup id="cite_ref-79" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-79">[79]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the 2016 short film <i>Leni. Leni.</i>, based on the play by Tom McNab and directed by Adrian Vitoria, <a href="/wiki/Hildegard_Neil" class="mw-redirect" title="Hildegard Neil">Hildegard Neil</a> portrays Riefenstahl<sup id="cite_ref-80" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-80">[80]</a></sup> preparing to give an interview in 1993. In her dressing room she is "visited" by herself as a young woman portrayed by Valeria Kozhevnikova at three stages/turning points in her life: as a dancer (1924), an actress (1929) and a director (1940).</p>
