<p><b>Howard Hathaway Aiken</b> (March 8, 1900 – March 14, 1973) was an American <a href="/wiki/Physicist" title="Physicist">physicist</a> and a pioneer in <a href="/wiki/Computing" title="Computing">computing</a>, being the original conceptual designer behind <a href="/wiki/IBM" title="IBM">IBM</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Harvard_Mark_I" title="Harvard Mark I">Harvard Mark I</a> computer.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biography">Biography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Howard_H._Aiken&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Biography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Aiken studied at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Wisconsin%E2%80%93Madison" title="University of Wisconsin–Madison">University of Wisconsin–Madison</a> and later obtained his PhD in physics at <a href="/wiki/Harvard_University" title="Harvard University">Harvard University</a> in 1939.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> During this time, he encountered <a href="/wiki/Differential_equation" title="Differential equation">differential equations</a> that he could only solve numerically. He envisioned an electro-mechanical computing device that could do much of the tedious work for him. This computer was originally called the ASCC (Automatic Sequence Controlled Calculator) and later renamed <a href="/wiki/Harvard_Mark_I" title="Harvard Mark I">Harvard Mark I</a>. With engineering, construction, and funding from <a href="/wiki/IBM" title="IBM">IBM</a>, the machine was completed and installed at Harvard in February, 1944.<sup id="cite_ref-Cohen_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cohen-5">[5]</a></sup> Richard Milton Bloch, Robert Campbell and <a href="/wiki/Grace_Hopper" title="Grace Hopper">Grace Hopper</a> joined the project later as programmers.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> In 1947, Aiken completed his work on the <a href="/wiki/Harvard_Mark_II" title="Harvard Mark II">Harvard Mark II</a> computer. He continued his work on the <a href="/wiki/Harvard_Mark_III" title="Harvard Mark III">Mark III</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Harvard_Mark_IV" title="Harvard Mark IV">Harvard Mark IV</a>. The Mark III used some electronic components and the Mark IV was all-electronic. The Mark III and Mark IV used <a href="/wiki/Drum_memory" title="Drum memory">magnetic drum</a> memory and the Mark IV also had <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_core_memory" class="mw-redirect" title="Magnetic core memory">magnetic core memory</a>.</p>
<p>Aiken was inspired by <a href="/wiki/Charles_Babbage" title="Charles Babbage">Charles Babbage</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Difference_engine" title="Difference engine">difference engine</a>.</p>
<p>Aiken accumulated honorary degrees at the University of Wisconsin, Wayne State and Technische Hochschule, Darmstadt. He was elected a Fellow of the <a href="/wiki/American_Academy_of_Arts_and_Sciences" title="American Academy of Arts and Sciences">American Academy of Arts and Sciences</a> in 1947.<sup id="cite_ref-AAAS_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AAAS-7">[7]</a></sup> He received the University of Wisconsin–Madison College of Engineering Engineers Day Award in 1958, the <a href="/wiki/Harry_H._Goode_Memorial_Award" title="Harry H. Goode Memorial Award">Harry H. Goode Memorial Award</a> in 1964, the <a href="/wiki/John_Price_Wetherill_Medal" title="John Price Wetherill Medal">John Price Wetherill Medal</a> in 1964, and the <a href="/wiki/IEEE" class="mw-redirect" title="IEEE">IEEE</a> (Institute of Electrical and Electronics Engineers) <a href="/wiki/Edison_Medal" class="mw-redirect" title="Edison Medal">Edison Medal</a> in 1970 "For a meritorious career of pioneering contributions to the development and application of large-scale digital computers and important contributions to education in the digital computer field."</p>
<p>In addition to his work on the Mark series, another important contribution of Aiken's was the introduction of a master's program for computer science at Harvard in 1947,<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> nearly a decade before the programs began to appear in other universities. This became a starting ground to future computer scientists, many of whom did doctoral dissertations under Aiken.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life">Personal life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Howard_H._Aiken&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Personal life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Howard Aiken was married three times: to Louise Mancill, later to Agnes Montgomery, and lastly to Mary McFarland. He had two children; Rachel Ann by his first wife, Elizabeth (Betsy) by his second.</p>
<p>Howard Aiken was also a Commander in the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Navy_Reserve" title="United States Navy Reserve">United States Navy Reserve</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Cohen_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cohen-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>After he retired at age 60 to <a href="/wiki/Fort_Lauderdale,_Florida" title="Fort Lauderdale, Florida">Fort Lauderdale, Florida</a>, Aiken continued his contributions to technology. He founded Howard Aiken Industries Incorporated, which was a consulting firm that helped failing businesses recover. During his years in Florida, he joined the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Miami" title="University of Miami">University of Miami</a> as a Distinguished Professor of Information. In addition, Aiken became a consultant for companies such as <a href="/wiki/Lockheed_Martin" title="Lockheed Martin">Lockheed Martin</a> and <a href="/wiki/Monsanto" title="Monsanto">Monsanto</a>. On March 14, 1973, Aiken died during a consulting trip to <a href="/wiki/St._Louis,_Missouri" class="mw-redirect" title="St. Louis, Missouri">St. Louis, Missouri</a> .<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> His widow, Mary, died in 2013.</p>
