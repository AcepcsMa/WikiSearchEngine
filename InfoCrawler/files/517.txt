<p><b>Johannes Müller von Königsberg</b> (6 June 1436 – 6 July 1476), better known as <b>Regiomontanus</b>, was a <a href="/wiki/Mathematician" title="Mathematician">mathematician</a> and <a href="/wiki/Astronomer" title="Astronomer">astronomer</a> of the <a href="/wiki/German_Renaissance" title="German Renaissance">German Renaissance</a>, active in <a href="/wiki/Vienna" title="Vienna">Vienna</a>, <a href="/wiki/Buda" title="Buda">Buda</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nuremberg" title="Nuremberg">Nuremberg</a>. His contributions were instrumental in the development of <a href="/wiki/Copernican_heliocentrism" title="Copernican heliocentrism">Copernican heliocentrism</a> in the decades following his death.</p>
<p>Regiomontanus wrote under the latinized name of <i>Ioannes de Monteregio</i> (or <i>Monte Regio</i>; <i>Regio Monte</i>); the adjectival <i>Regiomontanus</i> was first used by <a href="/wiki/Philipp_Melanchthon" class="mw-redirect" title="Philipp Melanchthon">Philipp Melanchthon</a> in 1534. He is named for <a href="/wiki/K%C3%B6nigsberg,_Bavaria" title="Königsberg, Bavaria">Königsberg</a> in <a href="/wiki/Lower_Franconia" title="Lower Franconia">Lower Franconia</a>, not after the larger <a href="/wiki/K%C3%B6nigsberg" title="Königsberg">Königsberg</a> (modern <a href="/wiki/Kaliningrad" title="Kaliningrad">Kaliningrad</a>) in Prussia.</p>

<p>At eleven years of age, Regiomontanus became a student at the university in <a href="/wiki/Leipzig" title="Leipzig">Leipzig</a>, <a href="/wiki/Saxony" title="Saxony">Saxony</a>. In 1451 he continued his studies at <a href="/wiki/University_of_Vienna" title="University of Vienna">Alma Mater Rudolfina</a>, the university in <a href="/wiki/Vienna" title="Vienna">Vienna</a>, Austria. There he became a pupil and friend of <a href="/wiki/Georg_von_Peuerbach" title="Georg von Peuerbach">Georg von Peuerbach</a>. In 1452 he was awarded his “magister artium” (<a href="/wiki/Master_of_Arts" title="Master of Arts">Master of Arts</a>) at the age of 21 in 1457. It is known that he held lectures in <a href="/wiki/Optics" title="Optics">optics</a> and ancient literature.</p>
<p>Regiomontanus continued to work with Peuerbach learning and extending the then known areas of astronomy, mathematics and instrument making until Peuerbach's death in 1461.</p>
<p>In 1460 the papal legate <a href="/wiki/Basilios_Bessarion" title="Basilios Bessarion">Basilios Bessarion</a> came to Vienna on a diplomatic mission. Being a humanist scholar and great fan of the mathematical sciences, Bessarion sought out Peuerbach's company. <a href="/wiki/George_of_Trebizond" title="George of Trebizond">George of Trebizond</a> who was Bessarion's philosophical rival had recently produced a new Latin translation of Ptolemy's <a href="/wiki/Almagest" title="Almagest">Almagest</a> from the Greek, which Bessarion, correctly, regarded as inaccurate and badly translated, so he asked Peuerbach to produce a new one. Peuerbach's Greek was not good enough to do a translation but he knew the <a href="/wiki/Almagest" title="Almagest">Almagest</a> intimately so instead he started work on a modernised, improved abridgement of the work. Bessarion also invited Peuerbach to become part of his household and to accompany him back to Italy when his work in Vienna was finished. Peuerbach accepted the invitation on the condition that Regiomontanus could also accompany them. However Peuerbach fell ill in 1461 and died only having completed the first six books of his abridgement of the Almagest. On his death bed Peuerbach made Regiomontanus promise to finish the book and publish it.</p>
<p>In 1461 Regiomontanus left Vienna with Bessarion and spent the next four years travelling around Northern Italy as a member of Bessarion's household, looking for and copying mathematical and astronomical manuscripts for Bessarion, who possessed the largest private library in Europe at the time. Regiomontanus also made the acquaintance of the leading Italian mathematicians of the age such as <a href="/wiki/Giovanni_Bianchini" title="Giovanni Bianchini">Giovanni Bianchini</a> and <a href="/wiki/Paolo_dal_Pozzo_Toscanelli" title="Paolo dal Pozzo Toscanelli">Paolo dal Pozzo Toscanelli</a> who had also been friends of Peuerbach during his prolonged stay in Italy more than twenty years earlier.</p>
<p>He went to work for <a href="/wiki/J%C3%A1nos_Vit%C3%A9z_(bishop)" class="mw-redirect" title="János Vitéz (bishop)">János Vitéz</a>, archbishop of <a href="/wiki/Esztergom" title="Esztergom">Esztergom</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Dates_and_numbers" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Dates and numbers"><span title="Need the year this event took place (March 2017)">year&#160;needed</span></a></i>]</sup> There he calculated extensive astronomical tables and built astronomical instruments. In 1467 he went to <a href="/wiki/Buda" title="Buda">Buda</a>, and the court of <a href="/wiki/Matthias_Corvinus" title="Matthias Corvinus">Matthias Corvinus</a> of Hungary, for whom he built an astrolabe, and where he collated Greek manuscripts for a handsome salary.<sup id="cite_ref-eb_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-eb-1">[1]</a></sup> The tables that he created while living in Hungary, his <i>Tabulae directionum</i>, were designed for astrology, including finding astrological houses.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1471 Regiomontanus moved to the Free City of <a href="/wiki/Nuremberg" title="Nuremberg">Nuremberg</a>, in <a href="/wiki/Franconia" title="Franconia">Franconia</a>, then one of the Empire's important seats of learning, publication, commerce and art, where he worked with the humanist and merchant <a href="/wiki/Bernhard_Walther" title="Bernhard Walther">Bernhard Walther</a>. Here he founded the world's first scientific printing press, and in 1472 he published the first printed astronomical textbook, the <i>Theoricae novae Planetarum</i> of his teacher Georg von Peurbach.</p>
<p>Regiomontanus and Bernhard Walther observed the <a href="/wiki/Comet_of_1472" title="Comet of 1472">comet of 1472</a>. Regiomontanus tried to estimate its distance from Earth, using the angle of parallax.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[a]</a></sup> According to David A. Seargeant:<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[3]</a></sup></p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>In agreement with the prevailing Aristotelian theory on comets as atmospheric phenomena, he estimated its distance to be at least 8,200 miles (13,120 km) and, from this, estimated the central condensation as 26, and the entire coma as 81 miles (41.6 and 129.6 km respectively) in diameter. These values, of course, fail by orders of magnitude, but he is to be commended for this attempt at determining the physical dimensions of the comet.</p>
</blockquote>
<p>The 1472 comet was visible from Christmas Day 1471 to 1 March 1472 (Julian Calendar), a total of 59 days.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1475, Regiomontanus was called to Rome by <a href="/wiki/Pope_Sixtus_IV" title="Pope Sixtus IV">Pope Sixtus IV</a> on to work on the planned <a href="/wiki/Gregorian_calendar#Background" title="Gregorian calendar">calendar reform</a>. Sixtus promised substantial rewards, including the title of <a href="/wiki/Bishop_of_Regensburg" class="mw-redirect" title="Bishop of Regensburg">bishop of Regensburg</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[5]</a></sup> On his way to Rome, stopping in Venice, he commissioned the publication of his <i>Calendarium</i> with <a href="/wiki/Erhard_Ratdolt" title="Erhard Ratdolt">Erhard Ratdolt</a> (printed in 1476). Regiomontanus reached Rome, but he died there after only a few months, in his 41st year, on 6 July 1476. According to a rumor repeated by <a href="/wiki/Pierre_Gassendi" title="Pierre Gassendi">Gassendi</a> in his Regiomontanus biography, he was poisoned by relatives of <a href="/wiki/George_of_Trebizond" title="George of Trebizond">George of Trebizond</a> whom he had criticized in his writing; it is however considered more likely that he died from the plague.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[6]</a></sup></p>

<p>During his time in Italy he completed Peuerbach's <a href="/wiki/Almagest" title="Almagest">Almagest</a> abridgement, <i>Epytoma in almagesti Ptolemei</i>. In 1464, he completed <i>De Triangulis omnimodis</i>. <i>De Triangulis</i> (On Triangles) was one of the first textbooks presenting the current state of <a href="/wiki/Trigonometry" title="Trigonometry">trigonometry</a> and included lists of questions for review of individual chapters. In it he wrote:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>You who wish to study great and wonderful things, who wonder about the movement of the stars, must read these theorems about triangles. Knowing these ideas will open the door to all of astronomy and to certain geometric problems.</p>
</blockquote>
<p>His work on <a href="/wiki/Arithmetic" title="Arithmetic">arithmetic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Algebra" title="Algebra">algebra</a>, <i>Algorithmus Demonstratus</i>, was among the first containing <a href="/wiki/Symbolic_algebra" class="mw-redirect" title="Symbolic algebra">symbolic algebra</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-nie_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nie-8">[7]</a></sup> In 1465, he built a portable <a href="/wiki/Sundial" title="Sundial">sundial</a> for <a href="/wiki/Pope_Paul_II" title="Pope Paul II">Pope Paul II</a>.</p>
<p>In <i>Epytoma in almagesti Ptolemei</i>, he critiqued the translation of <i>Almagest</i> by <a href="/wiki/George_of_Trebizond" title="George of Trebizond">George of Trebizond</a>, pointing out inaccuracies. Later <a href="/wiki/Nicolaus_Copernicus" title="Nicolaus Copernicus">Nicolaus Copernicus</a> would refer to this book as an influence on his own work.</p>
<p>A prolific author, Regiomontanus was internationally famous in his lifetime. Despite having completed only a quarter of what he had intended to write, he left a substantial body of work. <a href="/wiki/Nicolaus_Copernicus" title="Nicolaus Copernicus">Nicolaus Copernicus</a>' teacher, <a href="/wiki/Domenico_Maria_Novara_da_Ferrara" title="Domenico Maria Novara da Ferrara">Domenico Maria Novara da Ferrara</a>, referred to Regiomontanus as having been his own teacher. There is speculation that Regiomontanus had arrived at a theory of heliocentrism before he died; a manuscript shows particular attention to the <a href="/wiki/Heliocentric" class="mw-redirect" title="Heliocentric">heliocentric</a> theory of the <a href="/wiki/Pythagoreanism" title="Pythagoreanism">Pythagorean</a> <a href="/wiki/Aristarchus_of_Samos" title="Aristarchus of Samos">Aristarchus</a>, mention was also given to the motion of the earth in a letter to a friend.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>Much of the material on <a href="/wiki/Spherical_trigonometry" title="Spherical trigonometry">spherical trigonometry</a> in Regiomontanus' <i>On Triangles</i> was taken directly<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Accuracy_dispute#Disputed_statement" title="Wikipedia:Accuracy dispute"><span title="The material near this tag is possibly inaccurate or nonfactual. (March 2017)">dubious</span></a> <span class="metadata">– <a href="/wiki/Talk:Regiomontanus#Dubious" title="Talk:Regiomontanus">discuss</a></span></i>]</sup> from the twelfth-century work of <a href="/wiki/Jabir_ibn_Aflah" title="Jabir ibn Aflah">Jabir ibn Aflah</a> otherwise known as Geber, as noted in the sixteenth century by <a href="/wiki/Gerolamo_Cardano" title="Gerolamo Cardano">Gerolamo Cardano</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legacy">Legacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Regiomontanus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Legacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Regiomontanus designed his own <a href="/wiki/Astrological_houses#Regiomontanus" class="mw-redirect" title="Astrological houses">astrological house system</a>, which became one of the most popular systems in Europe.<sup id="cite_ref-Lewis2003_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lewis2003-11">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1561, <a href="/wiki/Daniel_Santbech" title="Daniel Santbech">Daniel Santbech</a> compiled a collected edition of the works of Regiomontanus, <i>De triangulis planis et sphaericis libri quinque</i> (first published in 1533) and <i>Compositio tabularum sinum recto</i>, as well as Santbech's own <i>Problematum astronomicorum et geometricorum sectiones septem</i>. It was published in <a href="/wiki/Basel" title="Basel">Basel</a> by <a href="/wiki/Henrich_Petri" class="mw-redirect" title="Henrich Petri">Henrich Petri</a> and <a href="/wiki/Petrus_Perna" class="mw-redirect" title="Petrus Perna">Petrus Perna</a>.</p>
<p>There is an image of him in Hartmann Schedel's 1493 <i><a href="/wiki/Nuremberg_Chronicle" title="Nuremberg Chronicle">Nuremberg Chronicle</a></i>. He is holding an astrolabe.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[b]</a></sup> Yet, although there are thirteen illustrations of comets in the '<i>Chronicle</i> (from 471 to 1472), they are stylized, rather than representing the actual objects. <sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[c]</a></sup></p>
<p>The crater <a href="/wiki/Regiomontanus_(crater)" title="Regiomontanus (crater)">Regiomontanus</a> on the Moon is named after him.</p>
