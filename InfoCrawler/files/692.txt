<p><b>Verona</b> (<small>Italian pronunciation:&#160;</small><span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Italian" title="Help:IPA/Italian">[veÀàroÀêna]</a></span><small class="nowrap metadata">&#160;(<span class="unicode haudio"><span class="fn"><span style="white-space:nowrap"><a href="/wiki/File:It-Verona.ogg" title="About this sound"><img alt="About this sound" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/11px-Loudspeaker.svg.png" width="11" height="11" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/17px-Loudspeaker.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/22px-Loudspeaker.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="20" data-file-height="20" /></a>&#160;</span><a href="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/f/fb/It-Verona.ogg" class="internal" title="It-Verona.ogg">listen</a></span></span>)</small>; <a href="/wiki/Venetian_language" title="Venetian language">Venetian</a>: <i>Verona</i> or <i>Ver√≤na</i>) is a city on the <a href="/wiki/Adige" title="Adige">Adige</a> river in <a href="/wiki/Veneto" title="Veneto">Veneto</a>, <a href="/wiki/Northern_Italy" title="Northern Italy">Italy</a>, with approximately 265,000 inhabitants and one of the seven provincial capitals of the region. It is the second largest city <a href="/wiki/Comune" title="Comune">municipality</a> in the region and the third largest in <a href="/wiki/Northeast_Italy" title="Northeast Italy">northeast Italy</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Metropolitan_area" title="Metropolitan area">metropolitan area</a> of Verona covers an area of 1,426&#160;km<sup>2</sup> (550.58&#160;sq&#160;mi) and has a population of 714,274 inhabitants.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> It is one of the main tourist destinations in northern Italy, owing to its artistic heritage, several annual fairs, shows, and <a href="/wiki/Opera" title="Opera">operas</a>, such as the lyrical season in the <a href="/wiki/Verona_Arena" title="Verona Arena">Arena</a>, the ancient <a href="/wiki/Amphitheater" class="mw-redirect" title="Amphitheater">amphitheater</a> built by the <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Rome" title="Ancient Rome">Romans</a>.</p>
<p>Three of Shakespeare's plays are set in Verona: <i><a href="/wiki/Romeo_and_Juliet" title="Romeo and Juliet">Romeo and Juliet</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Two_Gentlemen_of_Verona" title="The Two Gentlemen of Verona">The Two Gentlemen of Verona</a></i>, and <i><a href="/wiki/The_Taming_of_the_Shrew" title="The Taming of the Shrew">The Taming of the Shrew</a></i>. It is unknown if Shakespeare ever visited Verona or Italy at all, but his plays have lured many visitors to Verona and surrounding cities many times over. The city has been awarded <a href="/wiki/World_Heritage_Site" title="World Heritage Site">World Heritage Site</a> status by <a href="/wiki/UNESCO" title="UNESCO">UNESCO</a> because of its urban structure and architecture.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Verona&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>According to a theory that considers the geographical position of the city, Verona was a key step for those from <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Galicia" title="Eastern Galicia">Eastern Gaul</a> across the Alps to Rome along the Via Claudia Augusta. Verona is short for <i>Versus Romae</i> which means "In the direction of Rome". The -ona suffix, found in many Gallic names, is a simple deformation due to incorrect auditory perception that leads any person to hear the sound of a foreign language as a common sound in their own language, so to pronounce it and write it incorrectly.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>The exclamation <i>Vae Romae</i> if understood in Latin means "Alas Rome". In fact, to express distress or denounce a disgrace ancient Romans used the Latin interjection <i>vae</i>. So, you also explain the famous poem by William Shakespeare "<i>There</i> is <i>no world</i> without <i>Verona walls</i>, But <i>purgatory</i>, <i>torture</i>, <i>hell itself</i>. Hence-<i>banished</i> is <i>banish</i>'d from the <i>world</i>, And <i>world's exile</i> is <i>death</i>.". The writer would express a Roman concept through its character named Romeo, a name that invokes Rome, according to which the city of Verona was a boundary between the Roman world and barbaric one. Verona was a place of passage and to place horses, for those who wanted to go and had walked the Via Claudia Augusta, the Roman road that led over the Alps, or that led to Rome. So the expression <i>Vae Romae</i> "Alas Rome" would indicate spirit of the place, the "<i>genius loci</i>" of the city, expression of a spirit then called Verona. The exclamation <i>Vae Romae</i> would be the expression of the spirit that pervaded the Romans who left the place with a sense of anguish, this expression would later become the name for the place, that is "Alas Roma", which then turned into Verona.</p>
<p>Another theory is that it is connected to the river. "Vera" was a name of the river Adige before the adoption of the current name.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> As in many similar instances in Europe the name of the town is formed with the addition of suffix <i>-ona</i> which means settlement over.</p>
<p>The city was sometimes archaically known as "Welsch-Bern" in <a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Verona&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/History_of_Verona" title="History of Verona">History of Verona</a> and <a href="/wiki/Timeline_of_Verona" title="Timeline of Verona">Timeline of Verona</a></div>
<p>The precise details of Verona's early history remain a mystery. One theory is it was a city of the <a href="/wiki/Euganei" title="Euganei">Euganei</a>, who were obliged to give it up to the <a href="/wiki/Cenomani_(Cisalpine_Gaul)" title="Cenomani (Cisalpine Gaul)">Cenomani</a> (550 BC). With the conquest of the Valley of the <a href="/wiki/Po_(river)" title="Po (river)">Po</a> the Veronese territory became Roman (about 300 BC). Verona became a Roman <i>colonia</i> in 89 BC, and then a <i>municipium</i> in 49 BC when its citizens were ascribed to the Roman tribe <i>Poblilia</i> or <i>Publicia</i>.</p>
<p>The city became important because it was at the intersection of several roads. <a href="/wiki/Stilicho" title="Stilicho">Stilicho</a> defeated <a href="/wiki/Alaric_I" title="Alaric I">Alaric</a> and his <a href="/wiki/Visigoths" title="Visigoths">Visigoths</a> here in 403. But, after Verona was conquered by the <a href="/wiki/Ostrogoths" title="Ostrogoths">Ostrogoths</a> in 489, the <a href="/wiki/Goths" title="Goths">Gothic</a> domination of Italy began. <a href="/wiki/Theoderic_the_Great" title="Theoderic the Great">Theoderic the Great</a> was said to have built a palace there. It remained under the power of the Goths throughout the <a href="/wiki/Gothic_War_(535%E2%80%93554)" title="Gothic War (535‚Äì554)">Gothic War (535‚Äì552)</a>, except for a single day in 541, when the Byzantine officer <a href="/wiki/Artabazes_(military_officer)" title="Artabazes (military officer)">Artabazes</a> made an entrance. The defections that took place among the Byzantine generals with regard to the booty made it possible for the Goths to regain possession of the city. In 552 Valerian vainly endeavored to enter the city, but it was only when they were fully overthrown that the Goths surrendered it.</p>
<p>In 569, it was taken by <a href="/wiki/Alboin" title="Alboin">Alboin</a>, King of the <a href="/wiki/Lombards" title="Lombards">Lombards</a>, in whose kingdom it was, in a sense, the second most important city. There, Alboin himself was killed by his own wife in 572. The dukes of Treviso often resided there. At Verona <a href="/wiki/Adalgis" title="Adalgis">Adalgisus</a>, son of <a href="/wiki/Desiderius" title="Desiderius">Desiderius</a>, in 774 made his last desperate resistance to <a href="/wiki/Charlemagne" title="Charlemagne">Charlemagne</a>, who had destroyed the Lombard kingdom. Verona was then the ordinary residence of the <a href="/wiki/King_of_Italy" title="King of Italy">kings of Italy</a>, the government of the city becoming hereditary in the family of Count <a href="/wiki/Milo_of_Verona" title="Milo of Verona">Milo</a>, progenitor of the counts of <a href="/wiki/San_Bonifacio" title="San Bonifacio">San Bonifacio</a>. From 880 to 951 the two Berengarii resided there. <a href="/wiki/Otto_I,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Otto I, Holy Roman Emperor">Otto I</a> ceded to Verona the marquisate dependent on the Duchy of <a href="/wiki/Bavaria" title="Bavaria">Bavaria</a>.</p>
<p>When <a href="/wiki/Ezzelino_III_da_Romano" title="Ezzelino III da Romano">Ezzelino III da Romano</a> was elected podest√†, in 1226, he was able to convert the office into a permanent lordship, and in 1257 he caused the slaughter of 11,000 Paduans on the plain of Verona (Campi di Verona). Upon his death the Great Council elected as podest√† Mastino I della Scala, and he converted the "signoria" into a family possession, though leaving the burghers a share in the government. Failing to be re-elected podest√† in 1262, he effected a coup d'√©tat, and was acclaimed capitano del popolo, with the command of the communal troops. It was not without long internal discord that he succeeded in establishing this new office, to which was attached the function of confirming the podest√†. In 1277, Mastino dello Scala was killed by the faction of the nobles.</p>

<p>The reign of his son Alberto as capitano (1277‚Äì1302) was one incessant war against the counts of San Bonifacio, who were aided by the House of Este. Of his sons, Bartolomeo, Alboino and <a href="/wiki/Cangrande_I_della_Scala" title="Cangrande I della Scala">Cangrande I</a>, only the last shared the government (1308); he was great as warrior, prince, and patron of the arts; he protected <a href="/wiki/Dante_Alighieri" title="Dante Alighieri">Dante</a>, <a href="/wiki/Petrarch" title="Petrarch">Petrarch</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Giotto" title="Giotto">Giotto</a>. By war or treaty, he brought under his control the cities of <a href="/wiki/Padua" title="Padua">Padua</a> (1328), <a href="/wiki/Treviso" title="Treviso">Treviso</a> (1308) and <a href="/wiki/Vicenza" title="Vicenza">Vicenza</a>. At this time before the <a href="/wiki/Black_death" class="mw-redirect" title="Black death">Black death</a> the city was home to more than 40,000 people.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Cangrande was succeeded by <a href="/wiki/Mastino_II_della_Scala" title="Mastino II della Scala">Mastino II</a> (1329‚Äì1351) and Alberto, sons of Alboino. Mastino continued his uncle's policy, conquering <a href="/wiki/Brescia" title="Brescia">Brescia</a> in 1332 and carrying his power beyond the Po. He purchased <a href="/wiki/Parma" title="Parma">Parma</a> (1335) and <a href="/wiki/Lucca" title="Lucca">Lucca</a> (1339). After the <a href="/wiki/List_of_French_monarchs" title="List of French monarchs">King of France</a>, he was the richest prince of his time. But a powerful league was formed against him in 1337 ‚Äì <a href="/wiki/Florence" title="Florence">Florence</a>, <a href="/wiki/Venice" title="Venice">Venice</a>, the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Visconti" title="House of Visconti">Visconti</a>, the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Este" title="House of Este">Este</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Gonzaga" title="House of Gonzaga">Gonzaga</a>. After a three years war, the <a href="/wiki/Scaliger" title="Scaliger">Scaliger</a> dominions were reduced to Verona and <a href="/wiki/Vicenza" title="Vicenza">Vicenza</a> (Mastino's daughter Regina-Beatrice della Scala married to <a href="/wiki/Bernab%C3%B2_Visconti" title="Bernab√≤ Visconti">Barnab√≤ Visconti</a>). Mastino's son <a href="/wiki/Cangrande_II_della_Scala" title="Cangrande II della Scala">Cangrande II</a> (1351‚Äì1359) was a cruel, dissolute, and suspicious tyrant; not trusting his own subjects, he surrounded himself with Brandenburg mercenaries. He was killed by his brother Cansignorio (1359‚Äì1375), who beautified the city with palaces, provided it with aqueducts and bridges, and founded the state treasury. He also killed his other brother, Paolo Alboino. Fratricide seems to have become a family custom, for Antonio (1375‚Äì87), Cansignorio's natural brother, slew his brother Bartolomeo, thereby arousing the indignation of the people, who deserted him when <a href="/wiki/Gian_Galeazzo_Visconti" title="Gian Galeazzo Visconti">Gian Galeazzo Visconti</a> of <a href="/wiki/Milan" title="Milan">Milan</a> made war on him. Having exhausted all his resources, he fled from Verona at midnight (19 October 1387), thus putting an end to the Scaliger domination, which, however, survived in its monuments.</p>
<p>The year 1387 is also the year of the famous <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Castagnaro" title="Battle of Castagnaro">Battle of Castagnaro</a>, between <a href="/wiki/Giovanni_Ordelaffi" title="Giovanni Ordelaffi">Giovanni Ordelaffi</a>, for Verona, and <a href="/wiki/John_Hawkwood" title="John Hawkwood">John Hawkwood</a>, for <a href="/wiki/Padua" title="Padua">Padua</a>, who was the winner.</p>
<p>Antonio's son <a href="/wiki/Canfrancesco_della_Scala" title="Canfrancesco della Scala">Canfrancesco</a> attempted in vain to recover Verona (1390). Guglielmo (1404), natural son of Cangrande II, was more fortunate; with the support of the people, he drove out the <a href="/wiki/Milan" title="Milan">Milanese</a>, but he died ten days after, and Verona then <a href="/wiki/Devotion_of_Verona_to_Venice" title="Devotion of Verona to Venice">submitted to Venice</a> (1405). The last representatives of the Scaligeri lived at the imperial court and repeatedly attempted to recover Verona by the aid of popular risings.</p>
<p>From 1508 to 1517, the city was in the power of the <a href="/wiki/Maximilian_I,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Maximilian I, Holy Roman Emperor">Emperor Maximilian I</a>. There were numerous outbreaks of the <a href="/wiki/Black_Death" title="Black Death">plague</a>, and in <a href="/wiki/Italian_plague_of_1629%E2%80%931631" class="mw-redirect" title="Italian plague of 1629‚Äì1631">1629‚Äì33</a> Italy was struck by its worst outbreak in modern times. Around 33,000 people died in Verona (over 60 per cent of the population at the time) in 1630‚Äì1631.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1776 was developed a method of bellringing called <a href="/wiki/Veronese_bellringing_art" title="Veronese bellringing art">Veronese bellringing art</a>. Verona was occupied by <a href="/wiki/Napoleon" title="Napoleon">Napoleon</a> in 1797, but on <a href="/wiki/Easter_Monday" title="Easter Monday">Easter Monday</a> the populace rose and drove out the French. It was then that Napoleon made an end of the <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Venice" title="Republic of Venice">Venetian Republic</a>. Verona became <a href="/wiki/Austrian_Empire" title="Austrian Empire">Austrian territory</a> when Napoleon signed the <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Campo_Formio" title="Treaty of Campo Formio">Treaty of Campo Formio</a> in October 1797. The Austrians took control of the city on 18 January 1798. It was taken from Austria by the <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Pressburg_(1805)" class="mw-redirect" title="Treaty of Pressburg (1805)">Treaty of Pressburg</a> in 1805 and became part of Napoleon's <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Italy_(Napoleonic)" title="Kingdom of Italy (Napoleonic)">Kingdom of Italy</a>, but was returned to Austria following Napoleon's defeat in 1814, when it became part of the Austrian-held <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Lombardy%E2%80%93Venetia" title="Kingdom of Lombardy‚ÄìVenetia">Kingdom of Lombardy-Venetia</a>. In 1866, following the Six Weeks War, Verona, along with the rest of Venetia, became part of Italy.</p>

<p>The advent of <a href="/wiki/Fascism" title="Fascism">fascism</a> added another dark chapter to the annals of Verona. As throughout Italy, the Jewish population was hit by the <a href="/wiki/Manifesto_of_Race" title="Manifesto of Race">Manifesto of Race</a>, a series of anti-Semitic laws passed in 1938, and after the invasion by <a href="/wiki/Nazi_Germany" title="Nazi Germany">Nazi Germany</a> in 1943, deportations to <a href="/wiki/Nazi_concentration_camps" title="Nazi concentration camps">Nazi concentration camps</a>. An Austrian Fort (now a church, the Santuario della Madonna di Lourdes), was used to incarcerate and torture <a href="/wiki/Allies_of_World_War_II" title="Allies of World War II">Allied troops</a>, Jews and <a href="/wiki/Anti-fascism" title="Anti-fascism">anti-fascists</a>, especially after 1943, when Verona became part of the <a href="/wiki/Italian_Social_Republic" title="Italian Social Republic">Italian Social Republic</a>.</p>
<p>As in Austrian times, Verona became of great strategic importance to the regime. <a href="/wiki/Galeazzo_Ciano" title="Galeazzo Ciano">Galeazzo Ciano</a>, <a href="/wiki/Benito_Mussolini" title="Benito Mussolini">Benito Mussolini</a>'s son-in-law, was accused of plotting against the republic; in a <a href="/wiki/Show_trial" title="Show trial">show trial</a> staged by the Nazi and fascist hierarchy at <a href="/wiki/Castelvecchio_Museum" title="Castelvecchio Museum">Castelvecchio</a> (the <a href="/wiki/Verona_trial" title="Verona trial">Verona trial</a>), Ciano was executed on the banks of the Adige with many other officers on what is today Via Colombo. This marked another turning point in the escalation of violence that would only end with the final liberation by allied troops and partisans in 1945.</p>
<p>After World War II, as Italy entered into <a href="/wiki/NATO" title="NATO">NATO</a>, Verona once again acquired its strategic importance, due to its closeness to the <a href="/wiki/Iron_Curtain" title="Iron Curtain">Iron Curtain</a>. The city became the seat of SETAF (South European Allied Terrestrial Forces) and had during the whole duration of the <a href="/wiki/Cold_War" title="Cold War">Cold War</a> period a strong military presence, especially American, which is decreasing only in these recent years. Now Verona is an important and dynamic city, very active in terms of economy, and also a very important tourist attraction because of its history, where the Roman past lives side by side with the Middle Age Verona, which in some senses brings about its architectural and artistic motifs.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Climate">Climate</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Verona&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Climate">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Verona has a <a href="/wiki/Humid_subtropical_climate" title="Humid subtropical climate">humid subtropical climate</a> characteristic of Northern Italy's inland plains, with hot summers and cold, humid winters, even though <a href="/wiki/Lake_Garda" title="Lake Garda">Lake Garda</a> has a partial influence on the city.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Relative_humidity" title="Relative humidity">relative humidity</a> is high throughout the year, especially in winter when it causes <a href="/wiki/Fog" title="Fog">fog</a>, mainly from dusk until late morning, although the phenomenon has become increasingly less frequent in recent years.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Demographics">Demographics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Verona&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Demographics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 2009, there were 265,368 people residing in Verona, located in the province of Verona, <a href="/wiki/Veneto" title="Veneto">Veneto</a>, of whom 47.6% were male and 52.4% were female. Minors (children aged 0‚Äì17) totalled 16.05% of the population compared to pensioners who number 22.36%. This compares with the Italian average of 18.06% (minors) and 19.94%(pensioners). The average age of Verona residents is 43 compared to the Italian average of 42. In the five years between 2002 and 2007, the population of Verona grew by 3.05%, while Italy as a whole grew by 3.85%.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> The current <a href="/wiki/Birth_rate" title="Birth rate">birth rate</a> of Verona is 9.24 births per 1,000 inhabitants compared to the Italian average of 9.45 births.</p>
<p>As of 2009<sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Verona&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, 87% of the population was <a href="/wiki/Italians" title="Italians">Italian</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> The largest immigrant group comes from other European nations (the largest coming from <a href="/wiki/Romania" title="Romania">Romania</a>): 3.60%, <a href="/wiki/South_Asia" title="South Asia">South Asia</a>: 2.03%, and <a href="/wiki/Sub-Saharan_Africa" title="Sub-Saharan Africa">sub-saharan Africa</a> 1.50%. The city is predominantly <a href="/wiki/Catholic_Church" title="Catholic Church">Roman Catholic</a>, but due to immigration now has some <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Orthodox_Church" title="Eastern Orthodox Church">Orthodox Christian</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Muslim" title="Muslim">Muslim</a> followers.</p>

<p>Since local government political reorganization in 1993, Verona has been governed by the City Council of Verona, which is based in <i>Palazzo Barbieri</i>. Voters elect directly 33 councilors and the Mayor of Verona every five years. Verona is also the capital of its own province. The Provincial Council is seated in <i>Palazzo del Governo</i>. The current Mayor of Verona is Federico Sboarina (<a href="/wiki/Lega_Nord" title="Lega Nord">LN</a>), elected on 26 June 2017.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Main_sights">Main sights</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Verona&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Main sights">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Verona_-_Ponte_di_Castelvecchio.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5c/Verona_-_Ponte_di_Castelvecchio.jpg/220px-Verona_-_Ponte_di_Castelvecchio.jpg" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5c/Verona_-_Ponte_di_Castelvecchio.jpg/330px-Verona_-_Ponte_di_Castelvecchio.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5c/Verona_-_Ponte_di_Castelvecchio.jpg/440px-Verona_-_Ponte_di_Castelvecchio.jpg 2x" data-file-width="968" data-file-height="648" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Verona_-_Ponte_di_Castelvecchio.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The <a href="/wiki/Castelvecchio_Bridge" title="Castelvecchio Bridge">Ponte Scaligero</a>, completed in 1356</div>
</div>

<div class="hatnote selfreference">See also: <a href="/wiki/Category:Buildings_and_structures_in_Verona" title="Category:Buildings and structures in Verona">Category:Buildings and structures in Verona</a>.</div>
<p>Because of the value and importance of its many historical buildings, Verona has been named a <a href="/wiki/UNESCO" title="UNESCO">UNESCO</a> <a href="/wiki/World_Heritage_Site" title="World Heritage Site">World Heritage Site</a>. Verona preserved many ancient Roman monuments, no longer in use, in the <a href="/wiki/Early_Middle_Ages" title="Early Middle Ages">early Middle Ages</a>, but much of this and much of its early medieval edifices were destroyed or heavily damaged by the <a href="/wiki/Earthquake_of_3_January_1117" class="mw-redirect" title="Earthquake of 3 January 1117">earthquake of 3 January 1117</a>, which led to a massive <a href="/wiki/Romanesque_architecture" title="Romanesque architecture">Romanesque</a> rebuilding. The <a href="/wiki/Carolingian_Renaissance" title="Carolingian Renaissance">Carolingian period</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Versus_de_Verona" title="Versus de Verona">Versus de Verona</a></i> contains an important description of Verona in the early medieval era.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Roman_edifices">Roman edifices</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Verona&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Roman edifices">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Verona_arena_2009.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/19/Verona_arena_2009.JPG/220px-Verona_arena_2009.JPG" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/19/Verona_arena_2009.JPG/330px-Verona_arena_2009.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/19/Verona_arena_2009.JPG/440px-Verona_arena_2009.JPG 2x" data-file-width="3872" data-file-height="2592" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Verona_arena_2009.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Verona_Arena" title="Verona Arena">Verona Arena</a></div>
</div>

<p>The <a href="/wiki/Roman_legion" title="Roman legion">Roman military</a> settlement in what is now the centre of the city was to expand through the <a href="/wiki/Cardo" title="Cardo">cardines</a> and <a href="/wiki/Decumanus_Maximus" title="Decumanus Maximus">decumani</a> that intersect at right angles. This structure has been kept to the present day and is clearly visible from the air. Further development has not reshaped the original map. Though the Roman city with its basalt-paved roads is mostly hidden from view it stands virtually intact about 6&#160;m below the surface. Most palazzi and houses have cellars built on Roman artifacts that are rarely accessible to visitors. <a href="/wiki/Piazza_delle_Erbe,_Verona" title="Piazza delle Erbe, Verona">Piazza delle Erbe</a>, near the Roman forum was rebuilt by <a href="/wiki/Cangrande_I_della_Scala" title="Cangrande I della Scala">Cangrande I</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cansignorio_della_Scala" title="Cansignorio della Scala">Cansignorio della Scala I</a>, lords of Verona, using material (such as marble blocks and statues) from Roman spas and villas.</p>
<p>Verona is famous for its Roman <a href="/wiki/Amphitheatre" title="Amphitheatre">amphitheatre</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Verona_Arena" title="Verona Arena">Arena</a>, found in the city's largest piazza, the <a href="/wiki/Piazza_Bra" title="Piazza Bra">Piazza Bra</a>. Completed around 30 AD, it is the third largest in Italy after Rome's <a href="/wiki/Colosseum" title="Colosseum">Colosseum</a> and the arena at <a href="/wiki/Capua" title="Capua">Capua</a>. It measures 139 metres long and 110 metres wide, and could seat some 25,000 spectators in its 44 tiers of marble seats. The <i><a href="/wiki/Ludi" title="Ludi">ludi</a></i> (shows and <a href="/wiki/Gladiator" title="Gladiator">gladiator</a> games) performed within its walls were so famous that they attracted spectators from far beyond the city. The current two-story <a href="/wiki/Facade" title="Facade">fa√ßade</a> is actually the internal support for the tiers; only a fragment of the original outer perimeter wall in white and pink <a href="/wiki/Limestone" title="Limestone">limestone</a> from <a href="/wiki/Valpolicella" title="Valpolicella">Valpolicella</a>, with three stories remains.The interior is very impressive and is virtually intact, and has remained in use even today for public events, fairs, theatre and open-aired <a href="/wiki/Opera" title="Opera">opera</a> during warm summer nights.</p>

<p>There is also a variety of other <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Roman_architecture" title="Ancient Roman architecture">Roman monuments</a> to be found in the town, such as the <a href="/wiki/Roman_theatre_of_Verona" class="mw-redirect" title="Roman theatre of Verona">Roman theatre of Verona</a>. This theatre was built in the 1st century BC, but through the ages had fallen in disuse and had been built upon to provide housing. In the 18th century Andrea Monga, a wealthy Veronese, bought all the houses that in time had been built over the theatre, demolished them, and saved the monument. Not far from it is the <a href="/wiki/Ponte_Pietra_(Verona)" title="Ponte Pietra (Verona)">Ponte di Pietra</a> ("Stone Wall Bridge"), another Roman landmark that has survived to this day.</p>
<p>The <i><a href="/wiki/Arco_dei_Gavi" class="mw-redirect" title="Arco dei Gavi">Arco dei Gavi</a></i> (Gavi Arch) was built in the 1st century AD, and is famous for having the name of the builder (architect <a href="/wiki/Lucius_Vitruvius_Cordo" class="mw-redirect" title="Lucius Vitruvius Cordo">Lucius Vitruvius Cordone</a>) engraved on it, a rare case in the architecture of the epoque. It originally straddled the main <a href="/wiki/Roman_roads" title="Roman roads">Roman road</a> into the city, now the Corso Cavour. It was demolished by French troops in 1805 and rebuilt in 1932.</p>

<p>Nearby is the <i><a href="/wiki/Porta_Borsari" class="mw-redirect" title="Porta Borsari">Porta Borsari</a></i>, an archway at the end of Corso Porta Borsari. This is the fa√ßade of a 3rd-century gate in the original Roman <a href="/wiki/Defensive_wall" title="Defensive wall">city walls</a>. The inscription is dated 245 AD and gives the city name as <i>Colonia Verona Augusta</i>. Corso Porta Borsari, the road passing through the gate is the original <a href="/wiki/Via_Sacra" title="Via Sacra">Via Sacra</a> of the Roman city. Today, it is lined with several <a href="/wiki/Renaissance" title="Renaissance">Renaissance</a> <a href="/wiki/Palazzo" class="mw-redirect" title="Palazzo">palazzi</a> and the ancient <a href="/wiki/Santi_Apostoli,_Verona" title="Santi Apostoli, Verona">Church of Santi Apostoli</a>, a few metres from Piazza delle Erbe.</p>
<p><i><a href="/wiki/Porta_Leoni" title="Porta Leoni">Porta Leoni</a></i> is the 1st century BC ruin of what was once part of the Roman <a href="/wiki/City_gate" title="City gate">city gate</a>. A substantial portion is still standing as part of the wall of a medieval building. The street itself is an open <a href="/wiki/Archaeological_site" title="Archaeological site">archaeological site</a>, and the remains of the original Roman street and gateway foundations can be seen a few feet below the present street level. As can be seen from there, the gate contains a small court guarded by towers. Here, carriages and travelers were inspected before entering or leaving the city.</p>

<li>The <i><a href="/wiki/Basilica_di_San_Zeno" class="mw-redirect" title="Basilica di San Zeno">Basilica of San Zeno Maggiore</a></i> is <a href="/wiki/Romanesque_architecture" title="Romanesque architecture">Romanesque</a> style church, the third such structure on its site, built from 1123‚Äì1135, over the 4th-century shrine to Verona's <a href="/wiki/Patron_saint" title="Patron saint">patron saint</a>, <a href="/wiki/Zeno_of_Verona" title="Zeno of Verona">St. Zeno</a> (died 380). The <a href="/wiki/Facade" title="Facade">fa√ßade</a> dominates the large square, and is flanked with a beautiful 72 metres tall <a href="/wiki/Bell_tower" title="Bell tower">bell tower</a>, which is mentioned by <a href="/wiki/Dante_Alighieri" title="Dante Alighieri">Dante</a> in Canto 18 of Purgatory in the <a href="/wiki/Divine_Comedy" title="Divine Comedy">Divine Comedy</a>. The weathered Veronese stone gives a warm golden glow, and the restrained lines of the pillars, columns, and cornices, and the gallery with its double windows, give the fa√ßade an air of harmonious elegance. The huge <a href="/wiki/Rose_window" title="Rose window">rose window</a> is decorated as a <a href="/wiki/Rota_Fortunae" title="Rota Fortunae">Wheel of Fortune</a>. The lintels above the portal have carvings of the months of the year. Each side of the doorway is embellished with 18 <a href="/wiki/Relief" title="Relief">bas-relief</a> panels of biblical scenes, and the inner <a href="/wiki/Bronze" title="Bronze">bronze</a> door panels have 48 primitive but forceful depictions of Biblical scenes and episodes from the life of St Zeno. The meaning of some of the scenes is now unknown, but the extraordinarily vivid energy of the figures is a superb blend of traditional and <a href="/wiki/Ottonian_dynasty" title="Ottonian dynasty">Ottonian</a> influences. The interior of the church is divided into the Lower Church, occupying about ‚Öî of the structure, and the Upper Church, occupying the remainder. The walls are covered with 12th and 14th century <a href="/wiki/Fresco" title="Fresco">frescos</a> and the ceiling of the <a href="/wiki/Nave" title="Nave">nave</a> is a magnificent example of a ship's keel ceiling. The vaulted <a href="/wiki/Crypt" title="Crypt">crypt</a> contains the tomb of <a href="/wiki/Zeno_of_Verona" title="Zeno of Verona">St. Zeno</a>, the first <a href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic_Diocese_of_Verona" title="Roman Catholic Diocese of Verona">Bishop of Verona</a>, as well as the tombs of several other saints. North of the church is a pleasant cloister. The church also houses the tomb of King <a href="/wiki/Pepin_of_Italy" title="Pepin of Italy">Pippin</a> of Italy (777‚Äì810).</li>
<li>The <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Basilica_of_San_Lorenzo,_Verona&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Basilica of San Lorenzo, Verona (page does not exist)">Basilica of San Lorenzo</a></i> is another Romanesque church, albeit smaller. It dates from around 1177, but was built on the site of a <a href="/wiki/Early_Christianity" title="Early Christianity">Paleochristian</a> church, fragments of which remain. The church is built of alternating tracks of <a href="/wiki/Brick" title="Brick">brick</a> and stone, and has two cylindrical towers, housing spiral staircases to the women's galleries. The interior is sober, but still quiet. The striped bands of stone and brick and the graceful arches complement the setting.</li>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/Santa_Maria_Antica" class="mw-redirect" title="Santa Maria Antica">Santa Maria Antica</a></i> is a huge <a href="/wiki/Romanesque_architecture" title="Romanesque architecture">Romanesque</a> church that served as the <a href="/wiki/Parish_church" title="Parish church">parish church</a> of the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Scaliger" class="mw-redirect" title="House of Scaliger">Scaligeri</a> clan, and is famous for the Gothic <a href="/wiki/Scaliger_Tombs" title="Scaliger Tombs">Scaliger Tombs</a>. The <i><a href="/wiki/Verona_Cathedral" title="Verona Cathedral">Duomo</a></i> is also a notable Romanesque church.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Sant%27Anastasia_(Verona)" title="Sant'Anastasia (Verona)">Sant'Anastasia</a> is a huge and lofty church built from 1290‚Äì1481 by the <a href="/wiki/Dominican_Order" title="Dominican Order">Dominicans</a> to hold the massive congregations attracted by their sermons. The Pellegrini chapel houses the famous fresco <i>St. George and the Princess of Trebizond</i> by <a href="/wiki/Pisanello" title="Pisanello">Pisanello</a> as well as the <a href="/wiki/Wilhelm_von_Bibra" title="Wilhelm von Bibra">grave of Wilhelm von Bibra</a>. An art festival is held in the square each may.</li>
</ul>
<p>With a span length of 48.70&#160;m (159.78&#160;ft), the segmental <a href="/wiki/Arch_bridge" title="Arch bridge">arch bridge</a> <a href="/wiki/Castelvecchio_Bridge" title="Castelvecchio Bridge">Ponte Scaligero</a> featured, at the time of its completion in 1356, the world's largest bridge arch.</p>

<p>Verona was the birthplace of <a href="/wiki/Catullus" title="Catullus">Catullus</a>, and the town that <a href="/wiki/Julius_Caesar" title="Julius Caesar">Julius Caesar</a> chose for relaxing stays. It has had an association with many important people and events that have been significant in the history of Europe, such as <a href="/wiki/Theoderic_the_Great" title="Theoderic the Great">Theoderic the Great</a>, king of <a href="/wiki/Ostrogoths" title="Ostrogoths">Ostrogoths</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alboin" title="Alboin">Alboin</a> and <a href="/wiki/Rosamund_(Gepid)" class="mw-redirect" title="Rosamund (Gepid)">Rosamund</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Lombards" title="Lombards">Lombard</a> Dukes, <a href="/wiki/Charlemagne" title="Charlemagne">Charlemagne</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pepin_of_Italy" title="Pepin of Italy">Pippin of Italy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Berengar_I_of_Italy" title="Berengar I of Italy">Berengar I</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Dante_Alighieri" title="Dante Alighieri">Dante</a>. <a href="/wiki/Papal_conclave" title="Papal conclave">Conclaves</a> were held here, as were important congresses. Verona featured in the travel diaries of <a href="/wiki/Johann_Wolfgang_von_Goethe" title="Johann Wolfgang von Goethe">Goethe</a>, <a href="/wiki/Stendhal" title="Stendhal">Stendhal</a>, <a href="/wiki/Paul_Val%C3%A9ry" title="Paul Val√©ry">Paul Val√©ry</a> and <a href="/wiki/Michel_de_Montaigne" title="Michel de Montaigne">Michel de Montaigne</a>.</p>

<p>The city has three professional <a href="/wiki/Association_football" title="Association football">football</a> teams. Historically, the city's major team has been <a href="/wiki/Hellas_Verona_F.C." title="Hellas Verona F.C.">Hellas Verona</a>. Hellas Verona won the Italian <a href="/wiki/Serie_A" title="Serie A">Serie A</a> championship in <a href="/wiki/1984-85_Serie_A" class="mw-redirect" title="1984-85 Serie A">1984-85</a>, and played in the <a href="/wiki/European_Cup" class="mw-redirect" title="European Cup">European Cup</a> the following year. <a href="/wiki/A.C._ChievoVerona" title="A.C. ChievoVerona">Chievo Verona</a> represents <a href="/wiki/Chievo" title="Chievo">Chievo</a>, a suburb of Verona. As of the 2017-18 season, both clubs play in the first division of <a href="/wiki/Football_in_Italy" title="Football in Italy">Italian football</a>, <a href="/wiki/Serie_A" title="Serie A">Serie A</a>. The teams contest the <a href="/wiki/Derby_della_Scala" title="Derby della Scala">Derby della Scala</a> and share the 38,402-seater <a href="/wiki/Stadio_Marcantonio_Bentegodi" class="mw-redirect" title="Stadio Marcantonio Bentegodi">Stadio Marcantonio Bentegodi</a>, which was used as a venue at the <a href="/wiki/1990_FIFA_World_Cup" title="1990 FIFA World Cup">1990 FIFA World Cup</a>. <a href="/wiki/Virtus_Vecomp_Verona" class="mw-redirect" title="Virtus Vecomp Verona">Virtus Vecomp Verona</a> are another Verona-based football club.</p>
<p>Verona is home to the volleyball team Marmi Lanza Verona (now in Serie A1), the rugby team Franklin and Marshall Cus Verona Rugby (now in Serie A1), and the basketball team <a href="/wiki/Scaligera_Basket_Verona" title="Scaligera Basket Verona">Scaligera Basket</a> (now in Legadue).</p>
<p>The city has twice hosted the <a href="/wiki/UCI_Road_World_Championships" title="UCI Road World Championships">UCI Road World Championships</a>, in <a href="/wiki/1999_UCI_Road_World_Championships" title="1999 UCI Road World Championships">1999</a> (with <a href="/wiki/Treviso" title="Treviso">Treviso</a> as co-host) and in <a href="/wiki/2004_UCI_Road_World_Championships" title="2004 UCI Road World Championships">2004</a>. The city also regularly hosts stages of the <a href="/wiki/Giro_d%27Italia" title="Giro d'Italia">Giro d'Italia</a> annual cycling race. Verona also hosted the baseball world cup in 2009, and the Volleyball World Cup in September‚ÄìOctober 2010. Verona is hosting the Volleyball Women's World Championship in September‚ÄìOctober 2014.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>

<p>Verona lies at a major route crossing where the north-south rail line from the <a href="/wiki/Brenner_Pass" title="Brenner Pass">Brenner Pass</a> to Rome intersects with the east-west line between Milan and Venice, giving the city rail access to most of Europe. The city is, therefore, served by international, regional and local services.</p>
<p>Verona's main station is <a href="/wiki/Verona_Porta_Nuova_railway_station" title="Verona Porta Nuova railway station">Verona Porta Nuova railway station</a>, to the south of the city centre. It is considered to be the ninth busiest railway station in Italy, handling approximately 68,000 passengers per day, or 25 million passengers per year.<sup id="cite_ref-vag_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-vag-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>There is a lesser station to the east of the city at <a href="/w/index.php?title=Porta_Vescovo_station&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Porta Vescovo station (page does not exist)">Porta Vescovo</a>, which used to be the main station in Verona, but now only receives trains between Venice and Porta Nuova.</p>

<p><a href="/wiki/Verona_Airport" class="mw-redirect" title="Verona Airport">Verona Airport</a> is located 5.0&#160;<a href="/wiki/Kilometre" title="Kilometre">km</a> (3.1&#160;mi) southwest of Verona. It handles around 3 million passengers per year. It is linked to Porta Nuova railway station by a frequent bus service.<sup id="cite_ref-vag_14-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-vag-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>There are direct flights between Verona and <a href="/wiki/Leonardo_da_Vinci-Fiumicino_Airport" class="mw-redirect" title="Leonardo da Vinci-Fiumicino Airport">Rome Fiumicino</a>, Munich, Berlin, Moscow, Naples, Frankfurt, Catania, <a href="/wiki/Paris-Charles_de_Gaulle_Airport" class="mw-redirect" title="Paris-Charles de Gaulle Airport">Paris Charles De Gaulle</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gatwick_Airport" title="Gatwick Airport">London Gatwick</a>, Dublin, Palermo,Cork, Manchester, <a href="/wiki/Vienna_International_Airport" title="Vienna International Airport">Vienna Schwechat</a>, Liverpool<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> and Cagliari among others.</p>
