			<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Augustin Hirschvogel</h1>
									<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
									<div id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
								<div id="contentSub"></div>
												<div id="jump-to-nav" class="mw-jump">

				<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Hirschvogel_Self_Portrait.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4e/Hirschvogel_Self_Portrait.jpg/220px-Hirschvogel_Self_Portrait.jpg" width="220" height="371" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4e/Hirschvogel_Self_Portrait.jpg/330px-Hirschvogel_Self_Portrait.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4e/Hirschvogel_Self_Portrait.jpg/440px-Hirschvogel_Self_Portrait.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1185" data-file-height="2000" /></a>

<p><b>Augustin Hirschvogel</b> (1503 – February 1553) was a <a href="/wiki/Germans" title="Germans">German</a> <a href="/wiki/Artist" title="Artist">artist</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mathematician" title="Mathematician">mathematician</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Cartographer" class="mw-redirect" title="Cartographer">cartographer</a> known primarily for his <a href="/wiki/Etching" title="Etching">etchings</a>. His thirty-five small landscape etchings, made between 1545 and 1549, assured him a place in the <a href="/wiki/Danube_School" class="mw-redirect" title="Danube School">Danube School</a>, a circle of artists in sixteenth-century <a href="/wiki/Bavaria" title="Bavaria">Bavaria</a> and <a href="/wiki/Austria" title="Austria">Austria</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Life">Life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Augustin_Hirschvogel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>He began work in his birthplace, <a href="/wiki/Nuremberg" title="Nuremberg">Nuremberg</a>, where he was trained in glass painting by his father, Veit Hirschvogel the elder (1461–1525), who was the city's official <a href="/wiki/Stained_glass" title="Stained glass">stained glass</a> painter. In 1525, Nuremberg accepted the <a href="/wiki/Protestant_Reformation" class="mw-redirect" title="Protestant Reformation">Protestant Reformation</a>, putting an end to lavish stained glass commissions. Veit the elder's workshop was then being run by Augustin's elder brother Veit; their father died the same year. The younger Hirschvogel had his own workshop by 1530, and soon formed a partnership with the potters <a href="/w/index.php?title=Oswald_Reinhart&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Oswald Reinhart (page does not exist)">Oswald Reinhart</a> and <a href="/w/index.php?title=Hanns_Nickel&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Hanns Nickel (page does not exist)">Hanns Nickel</a>.</p>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Hirschvogel_Map_Austria.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/49/Hirschvogel_Map_Austria.jpg/220px-Hirschvogel_Map_Austria.jpg" width="220" height="171" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/49/Hirschvogel_Map_Austria.jpg/330px-Hirschvogel_Map_Austria.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/49/Hirschvogel_Map_Austria.jpg/440px-Hirschvogel_Map_Austria.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1414" data-file-height="1100" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Hirschvogel_Map_Austria.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<i>Map of Austria</i>, 1542.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Hirschvogel left in 1536 for Laibach (the German name for <a href="/wiki/Ljubljana" title="Ljubljana">Ljubljana</a> in present-day <a href="/wiki/Slovenia" title="Slovenia">Slovenia</a>), returning to Nuremberg in 1543. During this period he produced his earliest known work as a cartographer: maps of <a href="/wiki/Turkey" title="Turkey">Turkish</a> borders (1539) and of <a href="/wiki/Austria" title="Austria">Austria</a> (1542), the latter made for <a href="/wiki/Ferdinand_I,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Ferdinand I, Holy Roman Emperor">Ferdinand I</a>. His commissions for <a href="/wiki/Armorial" class="mw-redirect" title="Armorial">armorials</a> (for Franz Igelshofer and Christoph Khevenhüller) show that he had been in contact with the <a href="/wiki/Holy_Roman_Empire" title="Holy Roman Empire">Imperial Court of Vienna</a> by 1543.</p>
<p>With his move to <a href="/wiki/Vienna" title="Vienna">Vienna</a> in 1544, he rendered his services to the city, court, and citizenry. The city employed him in 1547 to develop designs for <a href="/wiki/Bastion" title="Bastion">bastions</a>, to create etched views of Vienna, and to produce a plan for the city following the <a href="/wiki/Siege_of_Vienna" title="Siege of Vienna">Siege of Vienna</a>. These views were the first ever rendered according to scale, and the circular city plan was the first ever produced by <a href="/wiki/Triangulation" title="Triangulation">triangulation</a>, a system of <a href="/wiki/Surveying" title="Surveying">surveying</a> that Hirschvogel developed.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Vienna's council sent him to explain his work to Ferdinand I in <a href="/wiki/Prague" title="Prague">Prague</a> and to <a href="/wiki/Charles_V,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Charles V, Holy Roman Emperor">Charles V</a> in <a href="/wiki/Augsburg" title="Augsburg">Augsburg</a>; Ferdinand granted him a pension of 100 <i><a href="/wiki/Austro-Hungarian_gulden" title="Austro-Hungarian gulden">gulden</a></i> for it.</p>
<p>He died in Vienna in 1553.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Works">Works</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Augustin_Hirschvogel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Works">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:282px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Hirschvogel_Burg_1546.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/30/Hirschvogel_Burg_1546.jpg/280px-Hirschvogel_Burg_1546.jpg" width="280" height="185" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/30/Hirschvogel_Burg_1546.jpg/420px-Hirschvogel_Burg_1546.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/30/Hirschvogel_Burg_1546.jpg/560px-Hirschvogel_Burg_1546.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1000" data-file-height="659" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Hirschvogel_Burg_1546.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Castle yard, c. 1546. Etching, 140 × 213 mm, <a href="/wiki/National_Gallery_of_Art" title="National Gallery of Art">National Gallery of Art</a>, Washington.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Hirschvogel took up etching late in his career, and almost all of his prints date from the last decade of his life, when he resided in Vienna. His etchings, numbering about 300, "reflect his concern with Italianate problems of space, form, and ornament",<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> and include <a href="/wiki/Portrait" title="Portrait">portraiture</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cartography" title="Cartography">cartography</a>, and book ornamentation. He was among the first etchers to use copper rather than iron plates. He contributed 23 etchings to <a href="/wiki/Sigismund_von_Herberstein" title="Sigismund von Herberstein">Sigismund von Herberstein</a>'s 1549 edition of <i><a href="/wiki/Rerum_Moscoviticarum_Commentarii" class="mw-redirect" title="Rerum Moscoviticarum Commentarii">Rerum Moscoviticarum Commentarii</a></i> (<i>Notes on Muscovite Affairs</i>), and more than 100 Old and New Testament illustrations for the verses of Hungarian reformer <a href="/wiki/P%C3%A9ter_Per%C3%A9nyi" title="Péter Perényi">Péter Perényi</a> (1502–48). A series of 53 hunting scenes for stained glass are of questionable authorship, but Peters asserts that they are by Hirschvogel. Hirschvogel is credited with the single authentic portrait of the Swiss physician <a href="/wiki/Paracelsus" title="Paracelsus">Paracelsus</a>, but this attribution is not certain.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>His pen-and-ink landscapes have been described as "strong but light, sure but delicate".<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> The lack of shadow in his landscapes contributes to a tranquil, idyllic mood. Almost 100 drawings are attributed to him; some are likely not his own, but are similar in style to his or <a href="/wiki/Wolf_Huber" title="Wolf Huber">Wolf Huber</a>'s work. His art shows the influence of <a href="/wiki/Albrecht_D%C3%BCrer" title="Albrecht Dürer">Albrecht Dürer</a>, <a href="/wiki/Albrecht_Altdorfer" title="Albrecht Altdorfer">Albrecht Altdorfer</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sebald_Beham" title="Sebald Beham">Sebald Beham</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hans_Burgkmair" title="Hans Burgkmair">Hans Burgkmair</a>, and <a href="/w/index.php?title=Agostino_del_Musi&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Agostino del Musi (page does not exist)">Agostino del Musi</a>—some of whom had provided his father's workshop with designs.</p>
