<p>The <b>Burgraviate of Nuremberg</b> (<a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a>: <span lang="de" xml:lang="de"><i>Burggrafschaft NÃ¼rnberg</i></span>) was a <a href="/wiki/States_of_the_Holy_Roman_Empire" class="mw-redirect" title="States of the Holy Roman Empire">state</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Holy_Roman_Empire" title="Holy Roman Empire">Holy Roman Empire</a> from the early 12th to the late 15th centuries. As a <a href="/wiki/Burgraviate" class="mw-redirect" title="Burgraviate">burgraviate</a>, it was a <a href="/wiki/Graf" title="Graf">county</a> seated in the town of <a href="/wiki/Nuremberg" title="Nuremberg">Nuremberg</a>; the burgraviate soon lost power over the city, which became independent from 1219. Eventually, the burgraviate was partitioned in twain, to form <a href="/wiki/Brandenburg-Ansbach" class="mw-redirect" title="Brandenburg-Ansbach">Brandenburg-Ansbach</a> and <a href="/wiki/Brandenburg-Bayreuth" class="mw-redirect" title="Brandenburg-Bayreuth">Brandenburg-Bayreuth</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Burgraviate_of_Nuremberg&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Nuremberg#History" title="Nuremberg">Nuremberg: History</a></div>
<p>Nuremberg was probably founded around the turn of the 11th century, according to the first documentary mention of the city in 1050, as the location of an Imperial castle between the <a href="/wiki/East_Franks" class="mw-redirect" title="East Franks">East Franks</a> and the Bavarian <a href="/wiki/March_of_the_Nordgau" title="March of the Nordgau">March of the Nordgau</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-HLB:N.C3.BCrnberg_Entwicklung_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HLB:N.C3.BCrnberg_Entwicklung-1">[1]</a></sup> From 1050 to 1571, the city expanded and rose dramatically in importance due to its location on key trade routes. <a href="/wiki/Conrad_III_of_Germany" title="Conrad III of Germany">King Conrad III</a> established the <a href="/wiki/Burgraviate" class="mw-redirect" title="Burgraviate">burgraviate</a> and the first administration and courts over the surrounding Imperial territories. The first burgraves were from the Austrian House of <a href="/wiki/Raabs_an_der_Thaya" title="Raabs an der Thaya">Raabs</a> but, with the extinction of their male line around 1190, the burgraviate was inherited by the last count's son-in-law, of the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Hohenzollern" title="House of Hohenzollern">House of Hohenzollern</a>. From the late 12th century to the <a href="/wiki/Holy_Roman_Empire#Interregnum" title="Holy Roman Empire">Interregnum</a> (1254â73), however, the power of the burgraves diminished as the <a href="/wiki/Hohenstaufen" title="Hohenstaufen">Staufen</a> emperors transferred most non-military powers to a castellan, with the city administration and the municipal courts handed over to an Imperial mayor (<a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a>: <span lang="de" xml:lang="de"><i><a href="https://de.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reichsschulthei%C3%9F_(N%C3%BCrnberg)" class="extiw" title="de:ReichsschultheiÃ (NÃ¼rnberg)">ReichsschultheiÃ</a></i></span>) from 1173/74.<sup id="cite_ref-HLB:N.C3.BCrnberg_Entwicklung_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HLB:N.C3.BCrnberg_Entwicklung-1">[1]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Cath:Nuremberg_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cath:Nuremberg-2">[2]</a></sup> This castellan not only administered the imperial lands surrounding Nuremberg, but levied taxes and constituted the highest judicial court in matters relating to poaching and forestry; he also was the appointed protector of the various ecclesiastical establishments, churches and monasteries, even of the <a href="/wiki/Bishopric_of_Bamberg" class="mw-redirect" title="Bishopric of Bamberg">Bishopric of Bamberg</a>. The privileges of this castellanship were transferred to the city during the late-14th and early-15th centuries. The strained relations between the burgraves and the castellan finally broke out into open enmity, which greatly influenced the history of the city.<sup id="cite_ref-Cath:Nuremberg_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cath:Nuremberg-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Nuremberg is often referred to as having been the 'unofficial <a href="/wiki/Capital_city" title="Capital city">capital</a>' of the <a href="/wiki/Holy_Roman_Empire" title="Holy Roman Empire">Holy Roman Empire</a>, particularly because <a href="/wiki/Imperial_Diet_(Holy_Roman_Empire)" title="Imperial Diet (Holy Roman Empire)">Imperial Diets</a> (<i>Reichstage</i>) and courts met at <a href="/wiki/Nuremberg_Castle" title="Nuremberg Castle">Nuremberg Castle</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Diet_of_Nuremberg" title="Diet of Nuremberg">Diets of Nuremberg</a> were an important part of the administrative structure of the empire. The increasing demand of the royal court and the increasing importance of the city attracted increased trade and commerce to Nuremberg, supported by the Hohenstaufen emperors. <a href="/wiki/Frederick_II,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Frederick II, Holy Roman Emperor">Frederick II</a> (reigned 1212â50) granted the <i><span lang="de" xml:lang="de">GroÃen Freiheitsbrief</span></i> (English: Great Letter of Freedom) in 1219, including <a href="/wiki/German_town_law" title="German town law">town rights</a>, <a href="/wiki/Imperial_immediacy" title="Imperial immediacy">Imperial immediacy</a> (<i>Reichsfreiheit</i>), the privilege to mint coins and an independent <a href="/wiki/Customs" title="Customs">customs</a> policy, almost wholly removing the city from the purview of the burgraves.<sup id="cite_ref-HLB:N.C3.BCrnberg_Entwicklung_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HLB:N.C3.BCrnberg_Entwicklung-1">[1]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Cath:Nuremberg_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cath:Nuremberg-2">[2]</a></sup> Nuremberg soon became, with <a href="/wiki/Augsburg" title="Augsburg">Augsburg</a>, one of the two great trade centers on the route from <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a> to Northern Europe.</p>
