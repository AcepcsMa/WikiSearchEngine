<p><b>Lebkuchen</b> (<small>German pronunciation:</small> <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Standard_German" title="Help:IPA/Standard German">[ˈleːpˌku:xn]</a></span><small class="nowrap metadata">&#160;(<span class="unicode haudio"><span class="fn"><span style="white-space:nowrap"><a href="/wiki/File:Lebkuchen.ogg" title="About this sound"><img alt="About this sound" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/11px-Loudspeaker.svg.png" width="11" height="11" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/17px-Loudspeaker.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/22px-Loudspeaker.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="20" data-file-height="20" /></a>&#160;</span><a href="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/5/58/Lebkuchen.ogg" class="internal" title="Lebkuchen.ogg">listen</a></span></span>)</small>), or <b>Pfefferkuchen</b>, is a traditional <a href="/wiki/German-speaking_Europe" class="mw-redirect" title="German-speaking Europe">German</a> baked <a href="/wiki/Christmas" title="Christmas">Christmas</a> treat, somewhat resembling <a href="/wiki/Gingerbread" title="Gingerbread">gingerbread</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lebkuchen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Etymology" title="Etymology">etymology</a> of <i>Leb-</i> in the term <i>Lebkuchen</i> is uncertain. Proposed derivations include: from the Latin <i>libum</i> (flat bread), from the Germanic word <i>Laib</i> (loaf), and from the Germanic word <i>lebbe</i> (very sweet). Another likely possibility is that comes from the old term <i>Leb-Honig</i>, the rather solid crystallized honey taken from the hive, that cannot be used for much beside baking. <a href="/wiki/Folk_etymology" title="Folk etymology">Folk etymology</a> often associates the name with <i>Leben</i> (life), <i>Leib</i> (body), or <i>Leibspeise</i> (favorite food). <i>Kuchen</i> means 'cake'.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lebkuchen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Lebkuechner_Landauer.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f3/Lebkuechner_Landauer.jpg/220px-Lebkuechner_Landauer.jpg" width="220" height="324" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f3/Lebkuechner_Landauer.jpg/330px-Lebkuechner_Landauer.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/f/f3/Lebkuechner_Landauer.jpg 2x" data-file-width="427" data-file-height="629" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Lebkuechner_Landauer.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A <i>Lebküchner</i> from a manuscript <abbr title="circa">c.</abbr><span style="white-space:nowrap;"> 1520</span>, in the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Stadtbibliothek_N%C3%BCrnberg&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Stadtbibliothek Nürnberg (page does not exist)">Stadtbibliothek Nürnberg</a><span class="noprint" style="font-size:85%; font-style: normal;">&#160;(<a href="https://de.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stadtbibliothek_N%C3%BCrnberg" class="extiw" title="de:Stadtbibliothek Nürnberg">de</a>)</span></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The forerunner of today's <i>Lebkuchen</i> was called the "honey cake", and its history can be traced back to the <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Egypt" title="Ancient Egypt">Egyptians</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greece" title="Ancient Greece">Greeks</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Rome" title="Ancient Rome">Romans</a>. They believed that honey, the only sweetener widely available to them, was a gift of the deities and had <a href="/wiki/Magic_(paranormal)" title="Magic (paranormal)">magical</a> and healing powers. Honey cakes were also worn as a talisman in battle or as protection against evil spirits.</p>
<p>Lebkuchen was invented by monks in <a href="/wiki/Franconia" title="Franconia">Franconia</a>, Germany in the 13th century. <i>Lebkuchen</i> bakers were recorded as early as 1296 in <a href="/wiki/Ulm" title="Ulm">Ulm</a>, and 1395 in <a href="/wiki/N%C3%BCrnberg" class="mw-redirect" title="Nürnberg">Nürnberg</a> (Nuremberg). The latter is the most famous exporter today of the product known as <i>Nürnberger Lebkuchen</i> (Nuremberg Lebkuchen).</p>
<p>Local history in Nuremberg relates that emperor <a href="/wiki/Frederick_III,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Frederick III, Holy Roman Emperor">Friedrich III</a> held a <a href="/wiki/Reichstag_(Holy_Roman_Empire)" class="mw-redirect" title="Reichstag (Holy Roman Empire)">Reichstag</a> there in 1487 and he invited the children of the city to a special event where he presented Lebkuchen bearing his <a href="/wiki/Printed" class="mw-redirect" title="Printed">printed</a> portrait to almost four thousand children. Historically, and due to differences in the ingredients, <i>Lebkuchen</i> is also known as "honey cake" (<a href="https://de.wikipedia.org/wiki/Honigkuchen" class="extiw" title="de:Honigkuchen">Honigkuchen</a>) or "pepper cake" (<a href="https://de.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pfefferkuchen" class="extiw" title="de:Pfefferkuchen">Pfefferkuchen</a>). Traditionally, the cookies are usually quite large and may be 11.5&#160;cm (4.5&#160;in) in diameter if round, and larger if rectangular.</p>
<p>Since 1808, a variety of Nürnberg Lebkuchen made without flour has been called <i>Elisenlebkuchen</i>. It is uncertain whether Elise was the daughter of a gingerbread baker or the wife of a <a href="/wiki/Margrave" title="Margrave">margrave</a>. Her name is associated with some of the Lebkuchen produced by members of the guild. Since 1996, <i><a href="https://de.wikipedia.org/wiki/N%C3%BCrnberger_Lebkuchen" class="extiw" title="de:Nürnberger Lebkuchen">Nürnberger Lebkuchen</a></i> is a <a href="/wiki/Protected_designation_of_origin" class="mw-redirect" title="Protected designation of origin">protected designation of origin</a>, meaning that it must be produced within the boundaries of the city.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Types">Types</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lebkuchen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Types">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>Lebkuchen</i> range in taste from spicy to sweet and come in a variety of shapes with round being the most common. The ingredients usually include <a href="/wiki/Honey" title="Honey">honey</a>, <a href="/wiki/Spice" title="Spice">spices</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Aniseed" class="mw-redirect" title="Aniseed">aniseed</a>, <a href="/wiki/Coriander" title="Coriander">coriander</a>, <a href="/wiki/Clove" title="Clove">cloves</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ginger" title="Ginger">ginger</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cardamom" title="Cardamom">cardamom</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Allspice" title="Allspice">allspice</a>, nuts including <a href="/wiki/Almond" title="Almond">almonds</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hazelnut" title="Hazelnut">hazelnuts</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Walnut" title="Walnut">walnuts</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Candied_fruit" title="Candied fruit">candied fruit</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>In Germany, types of <i>Lebkuchen</i> are distinguished by the kind of nuts used and their proportions. <a href="/wiki/Ammonium_carbonate" title="Ammonium carbonate">Salt of Hartshorn</a> and <a href="/wiki/Potassium_carbonate" title="Potassium carbonate">potash</a> are often used for raising the dough. <i>Lebkuchen</i> dough is usually placed on a thin <a href="/wiki/Wafer" title="Wafer">wafer</a> base called <i>Oblate</i>. This was an idea of the monks, who used un<a href="/wiki/Leaven" class="mw-redirect" title="Leaven">leavened</a> <a href="/wiki/Communion_wafer" class="mw-redirect" title="Communion wafer">communion wafer</a> ingredients to prevent the dough from sticking. Typically, they are glazed or covered with very dark chocolate, but some are left uncoated.</p>
<p><span id="Lebkuchenherz"></span></p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Lebkuchenherzen.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/28/Lebkuchenherzen.jpg/220px-Lebkuchenherzen.jpg" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/28/Lebkuchenherzen.jpg/330px-Lebkuchenherzen.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/28/Lebkuchenherzen.jpg/440px-Lebkuchenherzen.jpg 2x" data-file-width="640" data-file-height="480" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Lebkuchenherzen.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<i>Lebkuchen</i> hearts are sold at fairs</div>
</div>
</div>
<p><i>Lebkuchen</i> is usually soft, but a harder type of <i>Lebkuchen</i> is used to produce <i>Lebkuchenherzen</i> ("<i>Lebkuchen</i> hearts"), usually inscribed with <a href="/wiki/Icing_(food)" title="Icing (food)">icing</a>, which are available at many German regional fairs and <a href="/wiki/Christmas_fair" class="mw-redirect" title="Christmas fair">Christmas fairs</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> They are also sold as souvenirs at the <a href="/wiki/Oktoberfest" title="Oktoberfest">Oktoberfest</a> and are inscribed with affectionate, sarcastic or obscene messages.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Another form is the "witch's house" (<i><a href="https://de.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hexenh%C3%A4usel" class="extiw" title="de:Hexenhäusel">Hexenhäusel</a></i> or <i>Hexenhäuschen</i>), made popular because of the fairy tales about <a href="/wiki/Hansel_and_Gretel" title="Hansel and Gretel">Hansel and Gretel</a>.</p>
<p>The closest German equivalent of the <a href="/wiki/Gingerbread_man" title="Gingerbread man">gingerbread man</a> is the <i><a href="https://de.wikipedia.org/wiki/Honigkuchenpferd" class="extiw" title="de:Honigkuchenpferd">Honigkuchenpferd</a></i> ("honey cake horse").</p>
<p>The Nuremberg type of "Lebkuchen" is also known as "Elisenlebkuchen" and must contain no less than 25 percent nuts and less than 10 percent wheat flour. The finest artisanal lebkuchen bakeries in Nuremberg boast close to 40% nut content. <i>Lebkuchen</i> is sometimes packaged in richly decorated tins, chests, and boxes, which have become nostalgic collector items.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
