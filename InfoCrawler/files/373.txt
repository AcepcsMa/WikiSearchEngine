			<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">List of German monarchs</h1>
									<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
									<div id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
								<div id="contentSub"><span class="mw-redirectedfrom">  (Redirected from <a href="/w/index.php?title=Kings_of_Germany&amp;redirect=no" class="mw-redirect" title="Kings of Germany">Kings of Germany</a>)</span></div>
												<div id="jump-to-nav" class="mw-jump">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>
				</div>
				<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p>This is a list of monarchs who ruled over the German territories of central Europe from <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Verdun" title="Treaty of Verdun">the division</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Francia" title="Francia">Frankish Empire</a> in 843 (by which a separate <a href="/wiki/East_Francia" title="East Francia">Eastern Frankish Kingdom</a> was created), until <a href="/wiki/German_Revolution_of_1918%E2%80%9319" title="German Revolution of 1918–19">the collapse</a> of the <a href="/wiki/German_Empire" title="German Empire">German Empire</a> in 1918. It also includes heads of the various German confederations after <a href="/wiki/Napoleonic_Wars" title="Napoleonic Wars">the collapse</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Holy_Roman_Empire" title="Holy Roman Empire">Holy Roman Empire of the German Nation</a> in 1806, and head of the first federation state, the <a href="/wiki/North_German_Confederation" title="North German Confederation">North German Confederation</a>, from 1867 to 1871.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Note_on_titles">Note on titles</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=List_of_German_monarchs&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Note on titles">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ol>
<li>The Kingdom of Germany started out as the eastern section of the <a href="/wiki/Franks" title="Franks">Frankish kingdom</a>, which was split by the <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Verdun" title="Treaty of Verdun">Treaty of Verdun</a> in 843. The rulers of the eastern area thus called themselves <i>rex Francorum</i>, king of the Franks, and later just <i>rex</i>. A reference to the "Germans," indicating the emergence of a German nation of some sort, did not appear until the eleventh century, when the pope referred to his enemy <a href="/wiki/Henry_IV,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Henry IV, Holy Roman Emperor">Henry IV</a> as <i>rex teutonicorum</i>, king of the <a href="/wiki/Teutons" title="Teutons">Teutons</a>, in order to brand him as a foreigner. The kings reacted by consistently using the title <i>rex Romanorum</i>, <a href="/wiki/King_of_the_Romans" title="King of the Romans">King of the Romans</a>, to emphasize their universal rule even before becoming emperor. This title remained until the end of the Empire in 1806, though after 1508 Emperors-elect added "king in Germany" to their titles. (Note: in this and related entries, the kings are called <i>kings of Germany</i>, for clarity's sake)</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Germany" title="Kingdom of Germany">Kingdom of Germany</a> was never entirely hereditary; rather, ancestry was only one of the factors that determined the succession of kings. The king was formally elected by the leading nobility in the realm, continuing the Frankish tradition. Gradually the election became the privilege of a group of princes called <a href="/wiki/Prince-elector" title="Prince-elector">electors</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Golden_Bull_of_1356" title="Golden Bull of 1356">Golden Bull of 1356</a> formally defined election proceedings.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></li>
<li>In the Middle Ages, the king did not assume the title "Emperor" (since 982 the full title was <i>Imperator Augustus Romanorum</i>, Venerable Emperor of the Romans) until crowned by the pope. Moving to Italy, he was usually first crowned with the <a href="/wiki/Iron_Crown_of_Lombardy" title="Iron Crown of Lombardy">Iron Crown of Lombardy</a>, after which he assumed the title of <i>rex Italiae</i>, <a href="/wiki/King_of_Italy" title="King of Italy">King of Italy</a>. After this he would ride on to Rome and be crowned emperor by the pope. See <a href="/wiki/Coronation_of_the_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Coronation of the Holy Roman Emperor">Coronation of the Holy Roman Emperor</a> for more details.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Maximilian_I,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Maximilian I, Holy Roman Emperor">Maximilian I</a> was the first king to bear the title of <i>Elected Emperor</i>. After the failure in 1508 of his attempt to march to Rome and be crowned by the pope, he had himself proclaimed <i>Elected Emperor</i> with papal consent. His successor <a href="/wiki/Charles_V,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Charles V, Holy Roman Emperor">Charles V</a> also assumed that title after his coronation in 1520 until he was crowned emperor by the pope in 1530. From <a href="/wiki/Ferdinand_I,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Ferdinand I, Holy Roman Emperor">Ferdinand I</a> onwards, all emperors were <i>Elected Emperor</i>, although they were normally referred to as <i>emperors</i>. At the same time, chosen successors of the emperors held the title of king of the Romans, if elected by the college of electors during their predecessor's lifetime. See <a href="/wiki/King_of_the_Romans" title="King of the Romans">King of the Romans</a> for more details.</li>
</ol>
<p>Emperors are listed in <b>bold</b>. Rival kings, anti-kings, and junior co-regents are <i>italicized</i>.</p>

<h2><span id="Germany_within_the_Holy_Roman_Empire,_962–1806"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Germany_within_the_Holy_Roman_Empire.2C_962.E2.80.931806">Germany within the Holy Roman Empire, 962–1806</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=List_of_German_monarchs&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Germany within the Holy Roman Empire, 962–1806">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The title "King of the Romans", used under the Holy Roman Empire, is considered equivalent to King of Germany. A king was chosen by the German electors and would then proceed to Rome to be crowned emperor by the pope.</p>
