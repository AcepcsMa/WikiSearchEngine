<p>A <b>papal bull</b> is a specific kind of public decree, <a href="/wiki/Letters_patent" title="Letters patent">letters patent</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Charter" title="Charter">charter</a> issued by a <a href="/wiki/Pope" title="Pope">pope</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic_Church" class="mw-redirect" title="Roman Catholic Church">Roman Catholic Church</a>. It is named after the leaden <a href="/wiki/Seal_(emblem)" title="Seal (emblem)">seal</a> (<i><a href="/wiki/Bulla_(seal)" title="Bulla (seal)">bulla</a></i>) that was traditionally appended to the end in order to authenticate it.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Papal_bull&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:BullExurgeDomine.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5d/BullExurgeDomine.jpg/220px-BullExurgeDomine.jpg" width="220" height="296" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5d/BullExurgeDomine.jpg/330px-BullExurgeDomine.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/5/5d/BullExurgeDomine.jpg 2x" data-file-width="357" data-file-height="480" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:BullExurgeDomine.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Printed text of <a href="/wiki/Pope_Leo_X" title="Pope Leo X">Pope Leo X</a>'s <i>Bull against the errors of <a href="/wiki/Martin_Luther" title="Martin Luther">Martin Luther</a></i>, also known as <i><a href="/wiki/Exsurge_Domine" title="Exsurge Domine">Exsurge Domine</a></i>, issued in June 1520</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Papal bulls have been in use at least since the 6th century, but the phrase was not used until around the end of the 13th century, and then only internally for unofficial administrative purposes. However, it had become official by the 15th century, when one of the offices of the <a href="/wiki/Apostolic_Chancery" title="Apostolic Chancery">Apostolic Chancery</a> was named the "register of bulls" ("<i>registrum bullarum</i>").<sup id="cite_ref-Thurston_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Thurston-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>By the accession of <a href="/wiki/Pope_Leo_IX" title="Pope Leo IX">Pope Leo IX</a> in 1048, a clear distinction developed between two classes of bulls of greater and less solemnity. The majority of the "great bulls" now in existence are in the nature of confirmations of property or charters of protection accorded to monasteries and religious institutions. In an epoch when there was much fabrication of such documents, those who procured bulls from <a href="/wiki/Rome" title="Rome">Rome</a> wished to ensure that the authenticity of their bull was above suspicion. A papal confirmation, under certain conditions, could be pleaded as itself constituting sufficient evidence of title in cases where the original deed had been lost or destroyed.<sup id="cite_ref-Thurston_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Thurston-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Since the 12th century, papal bulls have carried a leaden seal with the heads of the Apostles <a href="/wiki/Saint_Peter" title="Saint Peter">Saint Peter</a> and <a href="/wiki/Saint_Paul" class="mw-redirect" title="Saint Paul">Saint Paul</a> on one side and the pope’s name on the other. Papal bulls were originally issued by the pope for many kinds of communication of a public nature, but by the 13th century, papal bulls were only used for the most formal or solemn of occasions.<sup id="cite_ref-britannica-bull-papal_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-britannica-bull-papal-2">[2]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Papyrus" title="Papyrus">Papyrus</a> seems to have been used almost uniformly as the material for these documents until the early years of the eleventh century, after which it was rapidly superseded by a rough kind of <a href="/wiki/Parchment" title="Parchment">parchment</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Thurston_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Thurston-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Modern scholars have retroactively used the word "bull" to describe any elaborate papal document issued in the form of a <a href="/wiki/Decree" title="Decree">decree</a> or <a href="/wiki/Privilege_(canon_law)" title="Privilege (canon law)">privilege</a>, solemn or simple, and to some less elaborate ones issued in the form of a letter. Popularly, the name is used for any papal document that contains a metal seal.</p>
<p>Today, the bull is the only written communication in which the pope will refer to himself as "<i><a href="/wiki/Servus_Servorum_Dei" class="mw-redirect" title="Servus Servorum Dei">Episcopus Servus Servorum Dei</a></i>" ("Bishop, Servant of the Servants of God").<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> For example, when <a href="/wiki/Pope_Benedict_XVI" title="Pope Benedict XVI">Pope Benedict XVI</a> issued a decree in bull form, he began the document with "<i>Benedictus, Episcopus, Servus Servorum Dei</i>".</p>
<p>While papal bulls always used to bear a metal seal, they now do so only on the most solemn occasions. A papal bull is today the most formal type of public decree or <a href="/wiki/Letters_patent" title="Letters patent">letters patent</a> issued by the <a href="/wiki/Holy_See" title="Holy See">Vatican</a> Chancery in the name of the pope.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Format">Format</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Papal_bull&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Format">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A bull's format formerly began with one line in tall, elongated letters containing three elements: the pope's name, the papal title "<i>Episcopus Servus Servorum Dei</i>" ("Bishop, Servant of the Servants of God"), and its <a href="/wiki/Incipit" title="Incipit">incipit</a>, i. e., the first few <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> words from which the bull took its title for record keeping purposes, but which might not be directly indicative of the bull's purpose.</p>
<p>The body of the text had no specific conventions for its formatting; it was often very simple in layout. The closing section consisted of a short "<i>datum</i>" that mentioned the place of issuance, day of the month and year of the pope's pontificate on which issued, and signatures, near which was attached the seal.</p>
<p>For the most solemn bulls, the pope signed the document himself, in which case he used the formula "<i>Ego N. Catholicae Ecclesiae Episcopus</i>" ("I, N., Bishop of the Catholic Church"). Following the signature in this case would be an elaborate <a href="/wiki/Monogram" title="Monogram">monogram</a>, the signatures of any witnesses, and then the seal. Nowadays, a member of the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Curia" title="Roman Curia">Roman Curia</a> signs the document on behalf of the pope, usually the <a href="/wiki/Cardinal_Secretary_of_State" title="Cardinal Secretary of State">Cardinal Secretary of State</a>, and thus the monogram is omitted.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Seal">Seal</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Papal_bull&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Seal">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Medieval,_Papal_bulla_(FindID_407324).jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/da/Medieval%2C_Papal_bulla_%28FindID_407324%29.jpg/220px-Medieval%2C_Papal_bulla_%28FindID_407324%29.jpg" width="220" height="113" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/da/Medieval%2C_Papal_bulla_%28FindID_407324%29.jpg/330px-Medieval%2C_Papal_bulla_%28FindID_407324%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/da/Medieval%2C_Papal_bulla_%28FindID_407324%29.jpg/440px-Medieval%2C_Papal_bulla_%28FindID_407324%29.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1335" data-file-height="683" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Medieval,_Papal_bulla_(FindID_407324).jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Lead <i><a href="/wiki/Bulla_(seal)" title="Bulla (seal)">bulla</a></i> (<a href="/wiki/Obverse_and_reverse" title="Obverse and reverse">obverse and reverse</a>) of <a href="/wiki/Pope_Gregory_IX" title="Pope Gregory IX">Gregory IX</a>, pope 1227 to 1241</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The most distinctive characteristic of a bull was the metal seal (<i><a href="/wiki/Bulla_(seal)" title="Bulla (seal)">bulla</a></i>), which was usually made of <a href="/wiki/Lead" title="Lead">lead</a>, but on very solemn occasions was made of <a href="/wiki/Gold" title="Gold">gold</a>, as those on Byzantine imperial instruments often were (see <a href="/wiki/Golden_Bull" class="mw-redirect" title="Golden Bull">Golden Bull</a>). On the <a href="/wiki/Obverse_and_reverse" title="Obverse and reverse">obverse</a> it depicted, originally somewhat crudely, the early Fathers of the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic_Church" class="mw-redirect" title="Roman Catholic Church">Church of Rome</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Twelve_apostles" class="mw-redirect" title="Twelve apostles">Apostles</a> <a href="/wiki/Saint_Peter" title="Saint Peter">Saint Peter</a> and <a href="/wiki/Paul_of_Tarsus" class="mw-redirect" title="Paul of Tarsus">Saint Paul</a>, identified by the letters <i><b>S</b>anctus <b>PA</b>ulus</i> and <i><b>S</b>anctus <b>PE</b>trus</i> (thus, <i><b>SPA •SPE</b></i> or <i><b>SPASPE</b></i>). St. Paul, on the left, was shown with flowing hair and a long pointed beard composed of curved lines, while St. Peter, on the right, was shown with curly hair and a shorter beard made of dome-shaped <i>globetti</i> (beads in relief). Each head was surrounded by a circle of <i>globetti</i>, and the rim of the seal was surrounded by an additional ring of such beads, while the heads themselves were separated by a depiction of a cross.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> On the <a href="/wiki/Obverse_and_reverse" title="Obverse and reverse">reverse</a> was the name of the issuing pope in the <a href="/wiki/Nominative_case" title="Nominative case">nominative</a> <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> form, with the letters "PP", for <i><span lang="la" xml:lang="la">Pastor Pastorum</span></i> ("Shepherd of Shepherds"). This disc was then attached to the document either by cords of <a href="/wiki/Hemp" title="Hemp">hemp</a>, in the case of letters of justice and executory letters, or by red and yellow <a href="/wiki/Silk" title="Silk">silk</a>, in the case of letters of grace, that was looped through slits in the <a href="/wiki/Vellum" title="Vellum">vellum</a> of the document. The term "<i>bulla</i>" derives from the Latin "<i>bullire</i>" (""to boil""), and alludes to the fact that, whether of wax, lead, or gold, the material making the seal had to be melted to soften it for impression.</p>
<p>In 1535, the Florentine engraver <a href="/wiki/Benvenuto_Cellini" title="Benvenuto Cellini">Benvenuto Cellini</a> was paid 50 <i><a href="/wiki/Italian_scudo" title="Italian scudo">scudi</a></i> to recreate the metal matrix which would be used to impress the lead <i>bullae</i> of <a href="/wiki/Pope_Paul_III" title="Pope Paul III">Pope Paul III</a>. Cellini retained definitive iconographic items like the faces of the two Apostles, but he carved them with a much greater attention to detail and artistic sensibility than had previously been in evidence. On the reverse of the seal he added several <a href="/wiki/Fleur-de-lis" title="Fleur-de-lis">fleurs-de-lis</a>, a heraldic device of the Farnese family, from which Pope Paul III descended.</p>
<p>Since the late 18th century, the lead <i>bulla</i> has been replaced with a red ink stamp of Saints Peter and Paul with the reigning pope's name encircling the picture, though very formal letters, e. g. the bull of <a href="/wiki/Pope_John_XXIII" title="Pope John XXIII">Pope John XXIII</a> convoking the <a href="/wiki/Second_Vatican_Council" title="Second Vatican Council">Second Vatican Council</a>, still receive the leaden seal.</p>
<p>Original papal bulls exist in quantity only after the 11th century onward, when the transition from fragile <a href="/wiki/Papyrus" title="Papyrus">papyrus</a> to the more durable <a href="/wiki/Parchment" title="Parchment">parchment</a> was made. None survives in entirety from before 819. Some original lead <i>bullae</i>, however, still survive from as early as the 6th century.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Content">Content</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Papal_bull&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Content">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/List_of_papal_bulls" title="List of papal bulls">List of papal bulls</a></div>
<p>In terms of content, the bull is simply the format in which a decree of the pope appears. Any subject may be treated in a bull, and many were and are, including statutory decrees, episcopal appointments, <a href="/wiki/Dispensation_(Catholic_Church)" class="mw-redirect" title="Dispensation (Catholic Church)">dispensations</a>, <a href="/wiki/Excommunication" title="Excommunication">excommunications</a>, <a href="/wiki/Apostolic_constitution" title="Apostolic constitution">Apostolic constitutions</a>, <a href="/wiki/Canonization" title="Canonization">canonizations</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Convocation" title="Convocation">convocations</a>.</p>
<p>The bull was the exclusive letter format from the Vatican until the 14th century, when the <a href="/wiki/Papal_brief" title="Papal brief">papal brief</a> appeared. The brief is the less formal form of papal communication and was authenticated with a wax impression, now a red ink impression, of the <a href="/wiki/Ring_of_the_Fisherman" title="Ring of the Fisherman">Ring of the Fisherman</a>. There has never been an exact distinction of usage between a bull and a brief, but nowadays most letters, including <a href="/wiki/Encyclical" title="Encyclical">encyclicals</a>, are issued as briefs.</p>
