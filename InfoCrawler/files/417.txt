<p>The <i><b>Adler</b></i> (German for "Eagle") was the first <a href="/wiki/Locomotive" title="Locomotive">locomotive</a> that was successfully used <a href="/wiki/Commerce" title="Commerce">commercially</a> for the <a href="/wiki/Rail_transport" title="Rail transport">rail transport</a> of <a href="/wiki/Passenger" title="Passenger">passengers</a> and <a href="/wiki/Good_(economics)" class="mw-redirect" title="Good (economics)">goods</a> in <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Rolling_stock" title="Rolling stock">railway vehicle</a> was designed and built in 1835 by the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">British</a> railway pioneers <a href="/wiki/George_Stephenson" title="George Stephenson">George</a> and <a href="/wiki/Robert_Stephenson" title="Robert Stephenson">Robert Stephenson</a> in the <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">English</a> city of <a href="/wiki/Newcastle_upon_Tyne" title="Newcastle upon Tyne">Newcastle</a>. It was delivered to the <a href="/wiki/Bavarian_Ludwig_Railway" title="Bavarian Ludwig Railway">Bavarian Ludwig Railway</a> (<i>Bayerische Ludwigsbahn</i>) for service between <a href="/wiki/Nuremberg" title="Nuremberg">Nuremberg</a> and <a href="/wiki/F%C3%BCrth" title="Fürth">Fürth</a>. It ran officially for the first time there on 7 December 1835. The <i>Adler</i> was a <a href="/wiki/Steam_locomotive" title="Steam locomotive">steam locomotive</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Patentee_locomotive" class="mw-redirect" title="Patentee locomotive"><i>Patentee</i> type</a> with a wheel arrangement of <a href="/wiki/2-2-2" title="2-2-2">2-2-2</a> (<a href="/wiki/Whyte_notation" title="Whyte notation">Whyte notation</a>) or 1A1 (<a href="/wiki/UIC_classification" class="mw-redirect" title="UIC classification">UIC classification</a>). The <i>Adler</i> was equipped with a <a href="/wiki/Tender_(rail)" title="Tender (rail)">tender</a> of type 2&#160;T&#160;2.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Adler_(locomotive)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Earlier_locomotives_in_Germany">Earlier locomotives in Germany</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Adler_(locomotive)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Earlier locomotives in Germany">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <i>Adler</i> is often cited as the very first locomotive used by a <a href="/wiki/Railway_company" title="Railway company">railway company</a> on German soil. But as early as 1816 a serviceable steam locomotive was designed by the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Royal_Prussian_Steelworks&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Royal Prussian Steelworks (page does not exist)">Royal Prussian Steelworks</a> (<i>Königlich Preußische Eisengießerei</i>) in <a href="/wiki/Berlin" title="Berlin">Berlin</a>. During a trial run this so-called <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Johann_Friedrich_Krigar&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Johann Friedrich Krigar (page does not exist)">Krigar locomotive</a></i> hauled one railway wagon with a payload of 8,000 <a href="/wiki/Pound_(mass)#German_and_Austrian_Pfund" title="Pound (mass)">German pounds</a> (about 4.48 tonnes or 4.41 long tons). But this <a href="/wiki/Vehicle" title="Vehicle">vehicle</a> was never used commercially.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Nevertheless, the <i>Adler</i> was undoubtedly the first successfully operated locomotive in regular use in Germany.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Origin">Origin</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Adler_(locomotive)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Origin">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Adler_Rechnung_Stephenson_27081835.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2d/Adler_Rechnung_Stephenson_27081835.jpg/220px-Adler_Rechnung_Stephenson_27081835.jpg" width="220" height="276" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2d/Adler_Rechnung_Stephenson_27081835.jpg/330px-Adler_Rechnung_Stephenson_27081835.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2d/Adler_Rechnung_Stephenson_27081835.jpg/440px-Adler_Rechnung_Stephenson_27081835.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1399" data-file-height="1758" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Adler_Rechnung_Stephenson_27081835.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>

<p>During the construction of the Bavarian Ludwig Railway, founded by <a href="/w/index.php?title=Georg_Zacharias_Platner&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Georg Zacharias Platner (page does not exist)">Georg Zacharias Platner</a>, the search for a suitable locomotive started in England.</p>
<p>The first letter of enquiry was sent via the <a href="/wiki/London" title="London">London</a> company, <i>Suse und Libeth</i>, to Robert Stephenson &amp; Co. and <a href="/wiki/John_Braithwaite_(engineer)" title="John Braithwaite (engineer)">Braithwaite</a> &amp; <a href="/wiki/John_Ericsson" title="John Ericsson">Ericsson</a>. The locomotive was to be able to pull a <a href="/wiki/Weight" title="Weight">weight</a> of ten <a href="/wiki/Tonne" title="Tonne">metric tons</a> and cover the distance between Nuremberg and Fürth in a time of eight to ten minutes. Carriages were heated with <a href="/wiki/Charcoal" title="Charcoal">charcoal</a>. Stephenson replied that a locomotive of the same build class as those on the <a href="/wiki/Liverpool_and_Manchester_Railway" title="Liverpool and Manchester Railway">Liverpool and Manchester Railway</a>, with four <a href="/wiki/Wheel" title="Wheel">wheels</a> and a weight of between 7.5 and 8 metric tons, could be supplied. A lighter engine would not have the necessary power of <a href="/wiki/Adhesion" title="Adhesion">adhesion</a> and would be more expensive than a heavier engine. In any case, on 16 June 1833, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Johannes_Scharrer&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Johannes Scharrer (page does not exist)">Johannes Scharrer</a> asked for a quote for two locomotives with a weight of 6.5 metric tons and the necessary package of accessories. Stephenson quoted a cost of about 1,800 <a href="/wiki/Pounds_sterling" class="mw-redirect" title="Pounds sterling">pounds sterling</a>. The German company, Holmes and Rolandson from Unterkochen near <a href="/wiki/Aalen" title="Aalen">Aalen</a>, offered a steam locomotive with a power of between two and six <a href="/wiki/Horsepower" title="Horsepower">horsepower</a> at a price of 4,500 <a href="/wiki/South_German_gulden" title="South German gulden">Gulden</a>. Negotiations with Holmes and Rolandson stalled, and this avenue of enquiry was dropped.</p>
<p>A further bid came from Josef Reaullaux located in <a href="/wiki/Eschweiler" title="Eschweiler">Eschweiler</a>, near <a href="/wiki/Aachen" title="Aachen">Aachen</a>. At the end of April, Platner and Mainberger from Nuremberg were in <a href="/wiki/Neuwied" title="Neuwied">Neuwied</a> near <a href="/wiki/Cologne" title="Cologne">Cologne</a>. There they wanted to award a contract for the <a href="/wiki/Railway_track" class="mw-redirect" title="Railway track">railway track</a>. On 28 April they travelled to Cologne to meet with a friend of Platner called Consul Bartls. Bartls told them about the <a href="/wiki/Belgium" title="Belgium">Belgian</a> engineering works of <a href="/wiki/Cockerill-Sambre" title="Cockerill-Sambre">Cockerill</a> in <a href="/wiki/Li%C3%A8ge" title="Liège">Liège</a>.</p>
<p>As a result, Platner and Mainberger travelled to Liège. There they discovered that Cockerill had not yet built a locomotive, though they did find Stephenson was in <a href="/wiki/Brussels" title="Brussels">Brussels</a> at that time. They reached Brussels on 1 May and stayed in a guesthouse in <a href="/wiki/Flanders" title="Flanders">Flanders</a>, where Stephenson and several of his engineers were staying. Stephenson wanted to be present at the opening of the railway line from Brussels to <a href="/wiki/Mechelen" title="Mechelen">Mechelen</a> which was scheduled for 5 May. On 3 May, both parties signed a <a href="/wiki/Letter_of_intent" title="Letter of intent">letter of intent</a>. Stephenson wanted to deliver a locomotive of the <a href="/wiki/Patentee_locomotive" class="mw-redirect" title="Patentee locomotive"><i>Patentee</i> type</a> with six wheels and a weight of about six metric tons for a price of between 750 and 800 pounds sterling. On 15 May 1835 the new locomotive was ordered from Stephenson's locomotive works in Newcastle upon Tyne to those specifications. Furthermore, a <a href="/wiki/Tender_(rail)" title="Tender (rail)">tender</a> for a <a href="/wiki/Bogie" title="Bogie">bogie</a> <a href="/wiki/Passenger_car_(rail)" title="Passenger car (rail)">passenger coach</a> and a <a href="/wiki/Goods_wagon" title="Goods wagon">goods wagon</a> were ordered. Later it turned out that the locomotive would cost about 900 pounds sterling instead of the sum quoted in Brussels. Stephenson originally promised in Brussels that the locomotive would be delivered by the end of July to <a href="/wiki/Rotterdam" title="Rotterdam">Rotterdam</a><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Different <a href="/wiki/Units_of_measurement" title="Units of measurement">units of measurement</a> were used in Nuremberg and England; the <a href="/wiki/Foot_(unit)#Pre-1959" title="Foot (unit)">English foot</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Foot_(unit)#Metric_foot" title="Foot (unit)">bavarian foot</a> were different. The <a href="/wiki/Track_gauge" title="Track gauge">track gauge</a> was predefined to be the same as that of the <a href="/wiki/Stockton_and_Darlington_Railway" title="Stockton and Darlington Railway">Stockton and Darlington Railway</a> which was <span class="nowrap">1,435&#160;mm</span> (<span class="nowrap">4&#160;ft&#160;<span class="frac">8<span class="visualhide">&#160;</span><sup>1</sup>⁄<sub>2</sub></span>&#160;in</span>). Stephenson persisted with this gauge which meant the existing <a href="/wiki/Track_(rail_transport)" title="Track (rail transport)">track</a> had to be relaid, because its track gauge was too narrow by 5/8&#160;inches. The delivery of the locomotive to Nuremberg, together with all its spares comprising over 100 individual components in 19 boxes with a total weight of 177 <a href="/wiki/Hundredweight" title="Hundredweight">hundredweight</a>, came to a cost of 1,140&#160;pounds, 19&#160;<a href="/wiki/Shilling" title="Shilling">shillings</a> und 3&#160;<a href="/wiki/Penny" title="Penny">pennies</a>. The boxes were shipped late, on 3 September 1835, on the ship <i>Zoar</i> from <a href="/wiki/London" title="London">London</a> to Rotterdam. The freight rate from Rotterdam to Cologne was 700&#160;francs, from Cologne to <a href="/wiki/Offenbach_am_Main" title="Offenbach am Main">Offenbach am Main</a> 507&#160;South German gulden and 9&#160;<a href="/wiki/Kreuzer" title="Kreuzer">kreuzer</a> and from Offenbach to Nuremberg 653&#160;gulden and 11&#160;<a href="/wiki/Kreuzer" title="Kreuzer">kreuzer</a>. The board of directors of the Bavarian Ludwig Railway wanted the purchase to be exempted from import duty. The locomotive was declared as an item of a formerly unknown product which was to be used by factories in the Bavarian interior. After several difficulties the Ministry of Finance approved the tax-free import with <a href="/w/index.php?title=Johann_Wilhelm_Spaeth&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Johann Wilhelm Spaeth (page does not exist)">Johann Wilhelm Spaeth</a> as the recipient of the consignment.<sup id="cite_ref-M.C3.BCck_1985_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-M.C3.BCck_1985-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>The transport boxes containing the locomotive were shipped by the <a href="/wiki/Barge" title="Barge">barge</a> <i>van Hees</i> (owned by its <a href="/wiki/Captain_(nautical)" class="mw-redirect" title="Captain (nautical)">captain</a>, van Hees) and pulled upriver by the <a href="/wiki/Steamboat" title="Steamboat">steamboat</a> <i>Hercules</i> on the <a href="/wiki/Rhine" title="Rhine">Rhine</a> until it reached Cologne.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> As the waterline in the Rhine was low, Captain van Hees had to use horses to pull the barge instead of the steamboat, as originally planned.<sup id="cite_ref-M.C3.BCck_1985_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-M.C3.BCck_1985-4">[4]</a></sup> On 7 October, the train of barges reached Cologne; the remaining distance to Nuremberg had to be covered by road because the <a href="/wiki/Main_(river)" title="Main (river)">Main</a> was too shallow to be navigable by barge. The transport on land was disrupted by a <a href="/wiki/Strike_action" title="Strike action">strike</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Freight_forwarder" title="Freight forwarder">freight forwarders</a> in Offenbach am Main, and a different freight forwarder had to be ordered. On 26 October 1835 the transport reached Nuremberg. The steam engine was assembled in the workshops of the Johann Wilhelm Spaeth engineering works, with the assembly being observed by Stephenson's engineer <a href="/wiki/William_Wilson_(engineer)" title="William Wilson (engineer)">William Wilson</a>, who had travelled with the locomotive to Nuremberg. They used the help of the technical teacher Bauer and local <a href="/wiki/Carpentry" title="Carpentry">carpenters</a>.</p>
<p>On 10 November 1835, the board of directors of the Bavarian Ludwig Railway expressed their hope that the locomotive would be serviceable soon.<sup id="cite_ref-M.C3.BCck_1985_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-M.C3.BCck_1985-4">[4]</a></sup> The locomotive was a symbol for power, daringness and rapidity.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>Both bogies delivered by Robert Stephenson &amp; Co. turned out as too heavy for use at Nuremberg. The German constructor <a href="/wiki/Paul_Camille_von_Denis" title="Paul Camille von Denis">Denis</a> had planned that the railway wagons should be pulled either from the steam locomotive, or from horses, making a lighter construction necessary. Several companies built the wagons:</p>
<ul>
<li>the bogies were produced by Späth, Gemeiner und Manhard;</li>
<li>the wooden coachwork were delivered by the <a href="/wiki/Wheelwright" title="Wheelwright">wheelwright</a> Stahl from Nuremberg.</li>
</ul>
<p>As these companies were used to capacity with different orders three bogies and 16 wheels were produced by the company Stein in <a href="/wiki/Lohr_am_Main" title="Lohr am Main">Lohr</a> near <a href="/wiki/Aschaffenburg" title="Aschaffenburg">Aschaffenburg</a>. Denis threatened these companies to place future orders in England if they would not work faster. At the end of August 1835 the first wagon was completed. In the second half of October that year, the further wagons were nearing completion, with nine wagons produced before the opening of the Bavarian Ludwig Railway. The wagons consisted of two wagons for third class passengers, four for second class and three for first class. On 21 October 1835, the first test run with one horse-pulled wagon took place. Denis had constructed a <a href="/wiki/Brake" title="Brake">brake</a> for the wagons which was tested at this opportunity. The wagon could be stopped in each situation without any effort by the horse.</p>
<p>On 16 November, the first test run of the steam locomotive from Nuremberg to Fürth and back again was accomplished. Due to the cold weather that day, the speed was slowed down. Three days later five fully occupied wagons were transported on the track in between 12 and 13 minutes. On the way back the brakes and also boarding and disembarking of the passengers were tested. During the following tests it was discovered if wood was burned in the locomotive the sparks which came out of the chimney of the locomotive singed the clothing of the passengers. The participation at the test run costed 36 <a href="/wiki/Kreuzer" title="Kreuzer">kreuzers</a>, and the revenues from this were donated to the welfare of <a href="/wiki/Poverty" title="Poverty">the poor</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-M.C3.BCck_1985_4-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-M.C3.BCck_1985-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Construction_and_bodywork">Construction and bodywork</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Adler_(locomotive)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Construction and bodywork">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Locomotive">Locomotive</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Adler_(locomotive)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Locomotive">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The <i>Adler</i> was built on a <a href="/wiki/Wood" title="Wood">wooden</a> framework which was covered with <a href="/wiki/Sheet_metal" title="Sheet metal">sheet metal</a>. Both wet steam driven <a href="/wiki/Cylinder_(engine)" title="Cylinder (engine)">cylinders</a> were placed horizontally inside the frame and drove the driving <a href="/wiki/Wheelset_(rail_transport)" title="Wheelset (rail transport)">wheelset</a> which was placed in the middle of the three axles. The driving wheels had no wheel <a href="/wiki/Flange" title="Flange">flange</a>, so the locomotive could be operated on small radius curves. The forged <a href="/wiki/Spoke" title="Spoke">spokes</a> were rivetted to the <a href="/wiki/Rim_(wheel)" title="Rim (wheel)">rim</a>. The original wheels were made of <a href="/wiki/Cast_iron" title="Cast iron">cast iron</a> and were encircled with a forged <a href="/wiki/Railway_tires" class="mw-redirect" title="Railway tires">tyre</a> made of <a href="/wiki/Wrought_iron" title="Wrought iron">wrought iron</a>. The original wheels made of brittle cast iron were replaced later through wheels made of wrought iron. The hollow spokes had a core made of wood to make them more flexible to cushion unevenness of the track. All wheels of the locomotive were unbraked. A mechanical <a href="/wiki/Railway_brake" title="Railway brake">railway brake</a> braked both wheels of the tender on the right side where the <a href="/wiki/Fireman_(steam_engine)" title="Fireman (steam engine)">fireman</a> was located. There was a fixed connection between the locomotive and the tender. The <a href="/wiki/Buffer_(rail_transport)" title="Buffer (rail transport)">buffers</a> were made of wood. The horseshoe-shaped water box surrounded the coal stored in the tender. At first <a href="/wiki/Coke_(fuel)" title="Coke (fuel)">coke</a> was burned in the <a href="/wiki/Firebox_(steam_engine)" title="Firebox (steam engine)">firebox</a>, later <a href="/wiki/Bituminous_coal" title="Bituminous coal">bituminous coal</a> was used.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Railway_coaches">Railway coaches</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Adler_(locomotive)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Railway coaches">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Passenger_car_(rail)" title="Passenger car (rail)">passenger wagons</a> had the same bodies that were used for <a href="/wiki/Horse-drawn_carriage" class="mw-redirect" title="Horse-drawn carriage">horse-drawn carriages</a>. They were mounted on a <a href="/wiki/Bogie" title="Bogie">bogie</a> made of <a href="/wiki/Iron" title="Iron">iron</a>. The shape of <a href="/wiki/Coup%C3%A9" title="Coupé">coupé</a>-carriages with two axles and three separated compartments in line was the archetype for the first German <a href="/wiki/Railway_wagon" class="mw-redirect" title="Railway wagon">railway wagons</a>. Specific bogies for passenger coaches were first developed in 1842 by the <a href="/wiki/Great_Western_Railway" title="Great Western Railway">Great Western Railway</a>. All wagons were painted in yellow which was the colour of <a href="/wiki/Stagecoach" title="Stagecoach">stagecoaches</a> at that time. The third class wagons originally had no roof, three compartments with eight to ten seats and the entrances had no doors. The second class wagons had originally a roof made of <a href="/wiki/Canvas" title="Canvas">canvas</a>, had doors, unglazed windows and curtains originally made of <a href="/wiki/Silk" title="Silk">silk</a> later made of <a href="/wiki/Leather" title="Leather">leather</a>. All wagons were of the same width but from the cheapest to the most expensive class the number of seats in one line were reduced by one. The first class wagons were lined with a precious blue <a href="/wiki/Foulard" title="Foulard">foulard</a>, had windows made of <a href="/wiki/Glass" title="Glass">glass</a>, the door handles <a href="/wiki/Gilding" title="Gilding">gilded</a> and all metal fittings were made of <a href="/wiki/Brass" title="Brass">brass</a>. The second class wagon No 8 which still exists was rebuilt between 1838 and 1846, it has a length of 5,740&#160;mm, a weight of about 5 t and has 24 seats.</p>

<p>On 7 December 1835 the <i>Adler</i>, driven by William Wilson, ran for the first time on the 6.05 kilometres long track of the <i>Ludwigsbahn</i><sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> in nine minutes. 200 guests of honour were on the <a href="/wiki/Train" title="Train">train</a> and 26-year-old <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotsman</a> William Wilson was in the <a href="/wiki/Cab_(locomotive)" title="Cab (locomotive)">cab</a>. In time intervals of two hours two more test runs were made. The locomotive was in use with up to nine wagons with 192 passengers as a maximum. In normal use it drove at a maximum speed of 28&#160;<a href="/wiki/Kilometres_per_hour" title="Kilometres per hour">km/h</a> because the locomotive should be preserved. The normal run time was about 14 minutes. Demonstration trial could be done at a maximum speed of 65&#160;km/h. In most cases <a href="/wiki/Horse" title="Horse">horses</a> were used as <a href="/wiki/Working_animal" title="Working animal">working animals</a> instead of the steam locomotive. Because the coal was first very expensive most services were done as a <a href="/wiki/Horsecar" title="Horsecar">horsecar</a>. Goods were transported additionally to the passengers beginning from the year 1839. One of the first goods which were transported were <a href="/wiki/Barrel" title="Barrel">beer barrels</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cattle" title="Cattle">cattle</a>. In 1845 there was a considerable transport of goods. After running successfully for twenty-two years the <i>Adler</i> was now the weakest locomotive on the <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">European</a> <a href="/wiki/Continental_Europe" title="Continental Europe">continent</a>. Moreover, the consumption of coal of newer steam engines was much more efficient until then. The locomotive was used in Nuremberg as a stationary <a href="/wiki/Steam_engine" title="Steam engine">steam engine</a>. 1858 the railway company sold the locomotive with the tender but minus its wheels and further accessories to the iron dealer <i>Mr. Director Riedinger</i> located in <a href="/wiki/Augsburg" title="Augsburg">Augsburg</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> A probably unique photograph of the <i>Adler</i> taken around 1851, and probably the only one in existence, is in the Nuremberg city archives (<i>Stadtarchiv Nürnberg</i>).<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> However neither the age of the photograph is documented definitely nor is known if the picture shows the original locomotive or only a <a href="/wiki/Scientific_modelling" title="Scientific modelling">model</a>. The 1835 built and between 1838 and 1846 rebuilt second class passenger wagon No 8 remained in original because <a href="/wiki/Ludwig_I_of_Bavaria" title="Ludwig I of Bavaria">Ludwig I of Bavaria</a> is supposed to have travelled with it.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Replicas">Replicas</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Adler_(locomotive)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Replicas">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Serviceable_replica_of_1935">Serviceable replica of 1935</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Adler_(locomotive)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Serviceable replica of 1935">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft">

<p>In 1925 the establishment of the <a href="/wiki/Nuremberg_Transport_Museum" title="Nuremberg Transport Museum">Nuremberg Transport Museum</a> was planned. The <i>Adler</i> which was scrapped in the past should be reconstructed. The exact plans of that year were lost until now. Only one <a href="/wiki/Engraving" title="Engraving">engraving</a> on the time of the historical <i>Adler</i> provided information. 1929 these plans were stopped by the <a href="/wiki/Great_Depression" title="Great Depression">Great Depression</a>.</p>
<p>To celebrate the centenary of the railways in Germany in 1935 a replica of the <i>Adler</i> was built beginning from 1933 by the <a href="/wiki/Deutsche_Reichsbahn" title="Deutsche Reichsbahn">Deutsche Reichsbahn</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Kaiserslautern" title="Kaiserslautern">Kaiserslautern</a> repair shop (<i><a href="/wiki/Ausbesserungswerk" title="Ausbesserungswerk">Ausbesserungswerk</a></i>), which was largely true to the original. The original idea of the President of the Reichsbahn <a href="/wiki/Julius_Dorpm%C3%BCller" title="Julius Dorpmüller">Julius Dorpmüller</a> and the members of his staff was to use the <i>Adler</i>-replica as an instrument of <a href="/wiki/Propaganda" title="Propaganda">propaganda</a> for the "<i><a href="/wiki/Nazism" title="Nazism">new era</a></i>" in the city of the <a href="/wiki/Nuremberg_Rally" title="Nuremberg Rally">Reichsparteitag</a> Nuremberg. They planned to contrast the <i>Adler</i> with modern gigantic steam locomotives like the high-speed <a href="/wiki/DRG_Class_05" title="DRG Class 05">DRG Class 05</a>. For the realisation of the replica they used the results of the planning in 1925. Besides of different technical data the replica differed from the original with thicker boiler casing and additional cross bracings and spokes wheels made of steel.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> The replica attained an average speed of 33.7&#160;km/h in tests on an 81&#160;kilometre stretch of line. The route had <a href="/wiki/Gradients" class="mw-redirect" title="Gradients">gradients</a> of between 1:110 and 1:140. From 14 July until 13 October 1935 visitors could travel with the reconstructed <i>Adler</i>-train on a track length of two kilometres on the area of the centenary exhibition in Nuremberg. On the driving cab were also the President of the Deutsche Reichsbahn Julius Dorpmüller and the <a href="/wiki/Administrative_divisions_of_Nazi_Germany#Gaue_established_in_1934" title="Administrative divisions of Nazi Germany">Gauleiter</a> of <a href="/wiki/Franconia" title="Franconia">Franconia</a> <a href="/wiki/Julius_Streicher" title="Julius Streicher">Julius Streicher</a>. The <i>Adler</i>-replica was in use after that in 1936 at the <a href="/wiki/Cannstatter_Wasen" title="Cannstatter Wasen">Cannstatter Wasen</a> in <a href="/wiki/Stuttgart" title="Stuttgart">Stuttgart</a> and during the <a href="/wiki/1936_Summer_Olympics" title="1936 Summer Olympics">1936 Summer Olympics</a> in <a href="/wiki/Berlin" title="Berlin">Berlin</a>. During the 100-years-jubilee of the first <a href="/wiki/Prussia" title="Prussia">prussian</a> railway in 1938 the <i>Adler</i>-train was in service between Berlin and <a href="/wiki/Potsdam" title="Potsdam">Potsdam</a>. After this event the <i>Adler</i>-train was sent to the Nuremberg Transport Museum.</p>
<p>In 1950 the <i>Adler</i>-train was displayed by the <a href="/wiki/Deutsche_Bundesbahn" title="Deutsche Bundesbahn">Deutsche Bundesbahn</a> on a street transport vehicle for rolling stock during a <a href="/wiki/Parade" title="Parade">parade</a> of the 900-years-jubilee of Nuremberg.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>For the 125th anniversary of the railway in Germany the <i>Adler</i>-train was in service on the track of the <a href="/wiki/Tram" title="Tram">tram</a> between Nuremberg (Plärrer place) and <a href="/wiki/F%C3%BCrth_Hauptbahnhof" title="Fürth Hauptbahnhof">Fürth Hauptbahnhof</a>. The inner sides of the wheels were needed to turned of for operating on the tram track.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1984 it was rebuilt for the 150th anniversary by the <a href="/wiki/Deutsche_Bundesbahn" title="Deutsche Bundesbahn">Deutsche Bundesbahn</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Offenburg" title="Offenburg">Offenburg</a> repair shop. The inner sides of the wheels which were turned of in 1960 for driving on a tram track had to welded again. The steam boiler was checked under notice of current safety regulations. The <i>Adler</i> was displayed on the great jubilee exhibition in Nuremberg and took part of numerous events in west Germany like for example in <a href="/wiki/Hamburg" title="Hamburg">Hamburg</a>, <a href="/wiki/Konstanz" title="Konstanz">Konstanz</a> and <a href="/wiki/Munich" title="Munich">Munich</a>. On 22 May 1984 it was used for public tours between <a href="/wiki/N%C3%BCrnberg_Hauptbahnhof" class="mw-redirect" title="Nürnberg Hauptbahnhof">Nürnberg Hauptbahnhof</a> and Nuremberg East.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>The locomotive wasn't in service from 1985 until 1999. For the planned services in 1999 it had to be refurbished during several months. On 16 September 1999 the <a href="/wiki/Federal_Railway_Authority" title="Federal Railway Authority">Federal Railway Authority</a> gave the approval of operation.<sup id="cite_ref-eri-1999-456_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-eri-1999-456-16">[16]</a></sup> For the 100th anniversary of the <a href="/wiki/Nuremberg_Transport_Museum" title="Nuremberg Transport Museum">Bavarian Railway Museum</a> and the successor Nuremberg Transport Museum in 1999 the <i>Adler</i>-train was in service at three Sundays in October and took part in the <i>great parade of rolling stock</i> at the Nuremberg <a href="/wiki/Classification_yard" title="Classification yard">classification yard</a>. In the following years the <i>Adler</i>-train was used for several classic railway tours in Germany.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> It stood in the <a href="/wiki/Nuremberg_Transport_Museum" title="Nuremberg Transport Museum">Nuremberg Transport Museum</a> until 2005 when it was damaged by fire.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>At a fire in the museum's <a href="/wiki/Roundhouse" title="Roundhouse">roundhouse</a> at the Nuremberg West <a href="/wiki/Bahnbetriebswerk_(steam_locomotive)" class="mw-redirect" title="Bahnbetriebswerk (steam locomotive)">locomotive depot</a> on 17 October 2005, which at that time contained 24 locomotives, the still-working <i>Adler</i> replica was one of many engines that were badly damaged. Nevertheless, the management of the <a href="/wiki/Deutsche_Bahn_AG" class="mw-redirect" title="Deutsche Bahn AG">Deutsche Bahn</a> decided to restore it. The wreck was lifted from the ruins of the roundhouse on 7 November by a mobile crane in a four-hour operation by a recovery gang from the <a href="/wiki/Pre%C3%9Fnitz_Valley_Railway" class="mw-redirect" title="Preßnitz Valley Railway">Preßnitz Valley Railway</a> and taken by special <a href="/w/index.php?title=Low-loader&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Low-loader (page does not exist)">low-loader</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Meiningen_Steam_Locomotive_Works" title="Meiningen Steam Locomotive Works">Meiningen Steam Locomotive Works</a>. It was discovered that the <a href="/wiki/Boiler" title="Boiler">boiler</a> at least, thanks to its being full of water, was relatively undamaged, although its entire wooden cladding had been burnt and many plates had melted, and it could therefore be used for the reconstruction in 2007.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Reconstruction_in_a_serviceable_state_in_2007">Reconstruction in a serviceable state in 2007</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Adler_(locomotive)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Reconstruction in a serviceable state in 2007">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Adler_Koblenz_Luetzel_03042010.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/33/Adler_Koblenz_Luetzel_03042010.JPG/220px-Adler_Koblenz_Luetzel_03042010.JPG" width="220" height="296" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/33/Adler_Koblenz_Luetzel_03042010.JPG/330px-Adler_Koblenz_Luetzel_03042010.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/33/Adler_Koblenz_Luetzel_03042010.JPG/440px-Adler_Koblenz_Luetzel_03042010.JPG 2x" data-file-width="1917" data-file-height="2576" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Adler_Koblenz_Luetzel_03042010.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The <i>Adler</i> in 2010 in the <a href="/wiki/DB_Museum,_Koblenz" title="DB Museum, Koblenz">DB Museum</a> in <a href="/wiki/Koblenz" title="Koblenz">Koblenz</a>-Lützel</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The reconstruction started in mid-April 2007 and was finished by the October.<sup id="cite_ref-Adler.27.27_rekonstruiert_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Adler.27.27_rekonstruiert-19">[19]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> The metal-sheathed wooden frame was however so badly damaged, that it had to be completely built from scratch. A third-class wagon that had been stored at a different location and so had survived the fire, served as a template for the new coach-like wagons that were built by a <a href="/wiki/Meiningen" title="Meiningen">Meiningen</a> <a href="/wiki/Carpenter" class="mw-redirect" title="Carpenter">carpenter</a>'s shop. The cost ran to about a million euros, of which 200,000 euros was donated by the public.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> The director of the <i>DB Museum Nuremberg</i> assuaged fears before the reconstruction began that the rebuilding work would not be able to replicate the details of the locomotive damaged by the fire and explained: "No compromises will be made!" It was even more accurately built by using historical drawings, so for example the reconstruction of the chimney damaged in the fire was not based on the 1935 replica, but on the original design. The biggest problem was the one-piece driving axle. This could not be made in the Meiningen works, so at short notice the company <i>Grödlitzer Kurbelwelle Wildau GmbH</i> was found which could carry out the work. For the frame of the locomotive between eight and twelve years seasoned wood from <a href="/wiki/Fraxinus" title="Fraxinus">fraxinus</a>-trees was used. This was flexible enough for vibrations by the power transmission during a run. The base frame of the tender was built from hard wood taken from <a href="/wiki/Oak" title="Oak">oak</a>-trees.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>On 23 November 2007 the restored <i>Adler</i> returned to display at the museum together with an old third class wagon from 1935 and two new ones from 2007 in a <a href="/wiki/Roundhouse" title="Roundhouse">locomotive shed</a> near the Nuremberg Transport Museum. In the museum the non-serviceable replica from 1950 is displayed and also the original second class 1835 built and 1838 and 1846 rebuilt passenger wagon No 8 of the Bavarian Ludwig Railway which has not been put on track again because of conservation reasons. On 26 April 2008 the replica was in use between Nuremberg and Fürth accompanied by German members of parliament of various parties, a member of the DB management board and the Bavarian minister-president, Beckstein. In May special runs in Nuremberg, Koblenz and <a href="/wiki/Halle,_Saxony-Anhalt" class="mw-redirect" title="Halle, Saxony-Anhalt">Halle (Saale)</a> were following during the summer. In April 2010 during the 175th anniversary of the railway in Germany the <i>Adler</i> was in use of the area of the DB Museum in Koblenz-Lützel. In May and June runs with visitors between <a href="/wiki/N%C3%BCrnberg_Hauptbahnhof" class="mw-redirect" title="Nürnberg Hauptbahnhof">Nürnberg Hauptbahnhof</a> and <a href="/wiki/F%C3%BCrth_Hauptbahnhof" title="Fürth Hauptbahnhof">Fürth Hauptbahnhof</a> were made.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Non-serviceable_replica_of_the_1950s">Non-serviceable replica of the 1950s</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Adler_(locomotive)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Non-serviceable replica of the 1950s">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Another replica that, unlike the 1935 version, is not operational, was appointed by the advertising office of the Deutsche Bundesbahn and built during the 1950s at the <a href="/wiki/Ausbesserungswerk" title="Ausbesserungswerk">Ausbesserungswerk</a> in Munich-Freimann. This replica was used for <a href="/wiki/Public_relations" title="Public relations">public relations</a> purpose on exhibitions and fairs. It is to be found as a display model in the Nuremberg Transport Museum.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Further_replicas_and_pictures">Further replicas and pictures</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Adler_(locomotive)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Further replicas and pictures">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Since 1964 a 1:2 scale, motorised replica, the "Mini-<i>Adler</i>", had run at <a href="/wiki/Nuremberg_Zoo" title="Nuremberg Zoo">Nuremberg Zoo</a>. It started in the vicinity of the entrance and shuttled to the children's zoo. In the course of the <i><a href="/wiki/Dolphin" title="Dolphin">dolphin</a> lagoon</i> project this line had to be closed, but an extension or move of the route is planned.</p>
<p>The Görlitzer Oldtimer Parkeisenbahn uses a replica on a <a href="/wiki/Narrow_gauge_railway" class="mw-redirect" title="Narrow gauge railway">narrow gauge railway</a> with a <a href="/wiki/Track_gauge" title="Track gauge">track gauge</a> of <span class="nowrap">600&#160;mm</span> (<span class="nowrap">1&#160;ft&#160;<span class="frac">11<span class="visualhide">&#160;</span><sup>5</sup>⁄<sub>8</sub></span>&#160;in</span>). This replica is in fact a <a href="/wiki/Diesel_locomotive" title="Diesel locomotive">diesel locomotive</a>.</p>
<p>As part of the town's millennium celebrations "1000 years in Fürth" a <a href="/wiki/Bus" title="Bus">bus</a> was decorated with the <i>Adler</i>, and advertised an exhibition, at which donations for the reconstruction were collected.</p>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Postage_stamp" title="Postage stamp">stamp</a>-volumes of the <a href="/wiki/Reichspost" title="Reichspost">Reichspost</a> from 1935, the <a href="/wiki/Deutsche_Bundespost" title="Deutsche Bundespost">Deutsche Bundespost</a> from 1960, the <a href="/wiki/Deutsche_Post_of_the_GDR" title="Deutsche Post of the GDR">Deutsche Post</a> of the <a href="/wiki/East_Germany" title="East Germany">GDR</a> from 1960 and the Deutsche Bundespost from 1985 the <i>Adler</i> was appreciated to the jubilees of "100", "125", and "150" years of German railways. At the 175th anniversary in 2010 a 55 <a href="/wiki/Cent_(currency)" title="Cent (currency)">eurocent</a> <a href="/wiki/Commemorative_stamp" title="Commemorative stamp">commemorative stamp</a> with a picture of the <i>Adler</i> was issued by the <a href="/wiki/Deutsche_Post" title="Deutsche Post">Deutsche Post AG</a>. Also a 10 <a href="/wiki/Euro" title="Euro">Euro</a> <a href="/wiki/List_of_commemorative_coins_of_the_Federal_Republic_of_Germany" class="mw-redirect" title="List of commemorative coins of the Federal Republic of Germany">commemorative coin</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Mint_(coin)" class="mw-redirect" title="Mint (coin)">mint</a> in Munich (D) with the following inscription on the edge <i>Auf Vereinten Gleisen 1835 - 2010</i> (= On united tracks 1835 - 2010).</p>
