<p>The 1958 <b>US–UK Mutual Defense Agreement</b>, or <b>UK–US Mutual Defence Agreement</b>, is a <a href="/wiki/Bilateral_treaty" title="Bilateral treaty">bilateral treaty</a> between the United States and the United Kingdom on <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_weapon" title="Nuclear weapon">nuclear weapons</a> cooperation. The treaty's full name is <b>Agreement between the Government of the United States of America and the Government of the United Kingdom of Great Britain and Northern Ireland for Cooperation on the uses of Atomic Energy for Mutual Defense Purposes</b>. It allows the United States and the UK to exchange nuclear materials, technology and information. While the US has <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_sharing" title="Nuclear sharing">nuclear cooperation</a> agreements with other countries, including France and some <a href="/wiki/NATO" title="NATO">NATO</a> countries, this agreement is by far the most comprehensive. <a href="/wiki/Harold_Macmillan" title="Harold Macmillan">Harold Macmillan</a> called it "the Great Prize".<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMacmillan1971323_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMacmillan1971323-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>The treaty was signed on 3 July 1958, after the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet Union</a> shocked the American public with the <a href="/wiki/Sputnik_crisis" title="Sputnik crisis">sputnik crisis</a> on 4 October 1957, and the <a href="/wiki/British_hydrogen_bomb_programme" title="British hydrogen bomb programme">British hydrogen bomb programme</a> successfully tested a <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_bomb" class="mw-redirect" title="Hydrogen bomb">hydrogen bomb</a> in <a href="/wiki/Operation_Grapple" title="Operation Grapple">Operation Grapple</a> on 8 November 1957. The Anglo-American <a href="/wiki/Special_Relationship" title="Special Relationship">Special Relationship</a> proved mutually beneficial, although it was never one of equals; the United States was far larger than Britain both militarily and economically. Britain soon became dependent on the United States for its nuclear weapons, as it lacked the resources to produce a range of designs. The treaty allowed nuclear weapons in American to be supplied to Britain through <a href="/wiki/Project_E" title="Project E">Project E</a>, for the use by the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Air_Force" title="Royal Air Force">Royal Air Force</a> and <a href="/wiki/British_Army_of_the_Rhine" title="British Army of the Rhine">British Army of the Rhine</a>.</p>
<p>The treaty provided for the sale to the UK of one complete nuclear submarine propulsion plant, plus ten years' supply of <a href="/wiki/Enriched_uranium" title="Enriched uranium">enriched uranium</a> to fuel it. Other nuclear material was also acquired from the United States under the treaty. Some 5.4&#160;tonnes of UK produced plutonium was sent to the US in return for 6.7 kilograms (15&#160;lb) of <a href="/wiki/Tritium" title="Tritium">tritium</a> and 7.5&#160;tonnes of highly enriched uranium between 1960 and 1979, although much of the highly enriched uranium was used not for weapons, but as fuel for the growing fleet of UK <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_submarine" title="Nuclear submarine">nuclear submarines</a>. The treaty paved the way for the <a href="/wiki/Polaris_Sales_Agreement" title="Polaris Sales Agreement">Polaris Sales Agreement</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Navy" title="Royal Navy">Royal Navy</a> ultimately acquired entire weapons systems, with the <a href="/wiki/UK_Polaris_programme" title="UK Polaris programme">UK Polaris programme</a> and <a href="/wiki/Trident_nuclear_programme" title="Trident nuclear programme">Trident nuclear programme</a> using American missiles with British nuclear warheads.</p>
<p>The treaty has been amended and renewed nine times. The most recent renewal extended it to 31 December 2024.</p>

<p>During the early part of the <a href="/wiki/Second_World_War" class="mw-redirect" title="Second World War">Second World War</a>, Britain had a <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_weapons" class="mw-redirect" title="Nuclear weapons">nuclear weapons</a> project, codenamed <a href="/wiki/Tube_Alloys" title="Tube Alloys">Tube Alloys</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGowing1964108.E2.80.93111_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGowing1964108.E2.80.93111-2">[2]</a></sup> At the <a href="/wiki/Quebec_Conference,_1943" title="Quebec Conference, 1943">Quebec Conference</a> in August 1943, the <a href="/wiki/Prime_Minister_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Prime Minister of the United Kingdom">Prime Minister of the United Kingdom</a>, <a href="/wiki/Winston_Churchill" title="Winston Churchill">Winston Churchill</a> and the <a href="/wiki/President_of_the_United_States" title="President of the United States">President of the United States</a>, <a href="/wiki/Franklin_Roosevelt" class="mw-redirect" title="Franklin Roosevelt">Franklin Roosevelt</a>, signed the <a href="/wiki/Quebec_Agreement" title="Quebec Agreement">Quebec Agreement</a>, which merged Tube Alloys with the American <a href="/wiki/Manhattan_Project" title="Manhattan Project">Manhattan Project</a> to create a combined British, American and Canadian project.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHewlettAnderson1962277_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHewlettAnderson1962277-3">[3]</a></sup> The Quebec Agreement established the <a href="/wiki/Combined_Policy_Committee" class="mw-redirect" title="Combined Policy Committee">Combined Policy Committee</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Combined_Development_Trust" class="mw-redirect" title="Combined Development Trust">Combined Development Trust</a> to coordinate their efforts.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHewlettAnderson1962285.E2.80.93286_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHewlettAnderson1962285.E2.80.93286-4">[4]</a></sup> Many of Britain's top scientists participated in the <a href="/wiki/British_contribution_to_the_Manhattan_Project" title="British contribution to the Manhattan Project">British contribution to the Manhattan Project</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGowing1964236.E2.80.93242_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGowing1964236.E2.80.93242-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>The September 1944 Hyde Park Agreement extended both commercial and military cooperation into the post-war period,<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGowing1964340.E2.80.93342_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGowing1964340.E2.80.93342-6">[6]</a></sup> but Roosevelt died on 12 April 1945, and the Hyde Park Agreement was not binding on subsequent administrations.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPaul200072.E2.80.9373_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPaul200072.E2.80.9373-7">[7]</a></sup> In fact, it was physically lost. When Field Marshal Sir <a href="/wiki/Henry_Maitland_Wilson" title="Henry Maitland Wilson">Henry Maitland Wilson</a> raised the matter in a Combined Policy Committee meeting in June 1945, the American copy could not be found.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHewlettAnderson1962457.E2.80.93458_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHewlettAnderson1962457.E2.80.93458-8">[8]</a></sup> The Quebec Agreement specified that nuclear weapons would not be used against another country without mutual consent. On 4 July 1945, Wilson gave British agreement for the <a href="/wiki/Atomic_bombing_of_Hiroshima_and_Nagasaki" class="mw-redirect" title="Atomic bombing of Hiroshima and Nagasaki">use of nuclear weapons against Japan</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGowing1964372_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGowing1964372-9">[9]</a></sup> On 8 August 1945 the Prime Minister, <a href="/wiki/Clement_Attlee" title="Clement Attlee">Clement Attlee</a>, sent a message to President <a href="/wiki/Harry_Truman" class="mw-redirect" title="Harry Truman">Harry Truman</a> in which he referred to themselves as "heads of the Governments which have control of this great force".<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGoldberg1964410_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGoldberg1964410-10">[10]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHewlettAnderson1962372.E2.80.93373_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHewlettAnderson1962372.E2.80.93373-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Truman_administration">Truman administration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1958_US%E2%80%93UK_Mutual_Defence_Agreement&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Truman administration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The British government had trusted that the United States would continue to share nuclear technology, which it considered to be a joint discovery.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGoldberg1964410_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGoldberg1964410-10">[10]</a></sup> On 9 November 1945, Attlee and the <a href="/wiki/Prime_Minister_of_Canada" title="Prime Minister of Canada">Prime Minister of Canada</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mackenzie_King" class="mw-redirect" title="Mackenzie King">Mackenzie King</a>, went to Washington, D.C., to confer with Truman about future cooperation in nuclear weapons and nuclear power.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGott1963240_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGott1963240-12">[12]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGowingArnold1974a73.E2.80.9377_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGowingArnold1974a73.E2.80.9377-13">[13]</a></sup> A Memorandum of Intention they signed on 16 November 1945 made Canada a full partner, and replaced the Quebec Agreement's requirement for "mutual consent" before using nuclear weapons with one for "prior consultation". There was to be "full and effective cooperation in the field of atomic energy", but British hopes were soon disappointed;<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGowingArnold1974a92_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGowingArnold1974a92-14">[14]</a></sup> this was only "in the field of basic scientific research".<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPaul200080.E2.80.9383_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPaul200080.E2.80.9383-15">[15]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHewlettAnderson1962468_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHewlettAnderson1962468-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/United_States_Atomic_Energy_Act_of_1946" class="mw-redirect" title="United States Atomic Energy Act of 1946">United States Atomic Energy Act of 1946</a> (McMahon Act) ended technical cooperation. Its control of "restricted data" prevented the United States' allies from receiving any information on pain of death.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGowingArnold1974a106.E2.80.93108_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGowingArnold1974a106.E2.80.93108-17">[17]</a></sup> This partly resulted from the arrest for espionage of British physicist <a href="/wiki/Alan_Nunn_May" title="Alan Nunn May">Alan Nunn May</a> in February 1946, while the legislation was being debated.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGowingArnold1974a105.E2.80.93108_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGowingArnold1974a105.E2.80.93108-18">[18]</a></sup> Fearing a resurgence of <a href="/wiki/United_States_non-interventionism" title="United States non-interventionism">American isolationism</a>, and Britain losing its <a href="/wiki/Great_power" title="Great power">great power</a> status, the British government restarted its own development effort,<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGowingArnold1974a181.E2.80.93184_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGowingArnold1974a181.E2.80.93184-19">[19]</a></sup> now codenamed <a href="/wiki/High_Explosive_Research" title="High Explosive Research">High Explosive Research</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECathcart199523.E2.80.9324.2C_48.2C_57_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECathcart199523.E2.80.9324.2C_48.2C_57-20">[20]</a></sup> By the end of 1947, 1,900 long tons (1,900&#160;t) of uranium ore was stockpiled for the Combined Development Trust at <a href="/wiki/Springfields" title="Springfields">Springfields</a>, near <a href="/wiki/Preston,_Lancashire" title="Preston, Lancashire">Preston</a> in <a href="/wiki/Lancashire" title="Lancashire">Lancashire</a>, because the McMahon Act did not permit it to be exported from the United States, along with 1,350 long tons (1,370&#160;t) for British use. To gain access to this stockpile for their own nuclear weapons project, the Americans opened negotiations that resulted in the <i>Modus Vivendi</i>,<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGowingArnold1974a358.E2.80.93360_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGowingArnold1974a358.E2.80.93360-21">[21]</a></sup> which was signed on 7 January 1948. This officially terminated all previous agreements, including the Quebec Agreement. It removed the British right of consultation on the use of nuclear weapons,<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBotti198734.E2.80.9335_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBotti198734.E2.80.9335-22">[22]</a></sup> allowed for limited sharing of technical information between the United States, Britain and Canada,<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGowingArnold1974a245.E2.80.93254_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGowingArnold1974a245.E2.80.93254-23">[23]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHewlettDuncan1969281.E2.80.93283_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHewlettDuncan1969281.E2.80.93283-24">[24]</a></sup> and continued the Combined Policy Committee and the Combined Development Trust,<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBotti198734.E2.80.9335_22-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBotti198734.E2.80.9335-22">[22]</a></sup> although the latter was renamed the <a href="/wiki/Combined_Development_Agency" title="Combined Development Agency">Combined Development Agency</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGowingArnold1974a352.E2.80.93353_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGowingArnold1974a352.E2.80.93353-25">[25]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHewlettDuncan1969285_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHewlettDuncan1969285-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1949, the Americans offered to make atomic bombs in the United States available for Britain to use if the British agreed to curtail their atomic bomb programme.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHewlettDuncan1969307.E2.80.93308_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHewlettDuncan1969307.E2.80.93308-27">[27]</a></sup> This would have given Britain nuclear weapons much sooner than its own target date of late 1952.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHewlettDuncan1969310_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHewlettDuncan1969310-28">[28]</a></sup> Only those bomb components required by war plans would be stored in the UK, the rest would be kept in the US and Canada.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGowingArnold1974a281.E2.80.93283_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGowingArnold1974a281.E2.80.93283-29">[29]</a></sup> The offer was rejected by the British on the grounds that it was not "compatible with our status as a first class power to depend on others for weapons of this supreme importance."<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBaylis199575_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBaylis199575-30">[30]</a></sup> As a counter-offer, they proposed limiting the British program in return for American bombs.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHewlettDuncan1969309_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHewlettDuncan1969309-31">[31]</a></sup> The opposition of key American officials, including <a href="/wiki/Lewis_Strauss" title="Lewis Strauss">Lewis Strauss</a> from the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Atomic_Energy_Commission" title="United States Atomic Energy Commission">United States Atomic Energy Commission</a> (AEC), and Senators <a href="/wiki/Bourke_B._Hickenlooper" title="Bourke B. Hickenlooper">Bourke B. Hickenlooper</a> and <a href="/wiki/Arthur_Vandenberg" title="Arthur Vandenberg">Arthur Vandenberg</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Joint_Committee_on_Atomic_Energy" class="mw-redirect" title="Joint Committee on Atomic Energy">Joint Committee on Atomic Energy</a> (JCAE), coupled with security concerns aroused by the 2 February 1950 arrest of the British physicist <a href="/wiki/Klaus_Fuchs" title="Klaus Fuchs">Klaus Fuchs</a>, as an <a href="/wiki/Atomic_spy" class="mw-redirect" title="Atomic spy">atomic spy</a>, caused the proposal to be dropped.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHewlettDuncan1969312.E2.80.93314_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHewlettDuncan1969312.E2.80.93314-32">[32]</a></sup> The June 1951 defection of <a href="/wiki/Donald_Maclean_(spy)" title="Donald Maclean (spy)">Donald Maclean</a>, who had served as a British member of the Combined Policy Committee from January 1947 to August 1948, reinforced the Americans' distrust of British security arrangements.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBotti198774.E2.80.9375_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBotti198774.E2.80.9375-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Eisenhower_administration">Eisenhower administration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1958_US%E2%80%93UK_Mutual_Defence_Agreement&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Eisenhower administration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The first British atomic bomb was successfully tested in <a href="/wiki/Operation_Hurricane" title="Operation Hurricane">Operation Hurricane</a> on 3 October 1952,<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGowingArnold1974b493.E2.80.93495_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGowingArnold1974b493.E2.80.93495-34">[34]</a></sup> but although it was more advanced than the American bombs of 1946, Britain was still several years behind in nuclear weapons technology.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGowingArnold1974b474.E2.80.93475_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGowingArnold1974b474.E2.80.93475-35">[35]</a></sup> The United States conducted <a href="/wiki/Ivy_Mike" title="Ivy Mike">Ivy Mike</a>, the first <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_test" class="mw-redirect" title="Nuclear test">nuclear test</a> of a true <a href="/wiki/Thermonuclear_weapon" title="Thermonuclear weapon">thermonuclear device</a> (also known as a hydrogen bomb).<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEArnoldPyne200116.E2.80.9320_36-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEArnoldPyne200116.E2.80.9320-36">[36]</a></sup> The JCAE saw little benefit for the United States from sharing technology with Britain.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEArnoldPyne200127.E2.80.9330_37-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEArnoldPyne200127.E2.80.9330-37">[37]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGowingArnold1974b498.E2.80.93500_38-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGowingArnold1974b498.E2.80.93500-38">[38]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet Union</a> responded with the test of <a href="/wiki/Joe_4" title="Joe 4">Joe 4</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Boosted_fission_weapon" title="Boosted fission weapon">boosted fission weapon</a> on 12 August 1953.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEArnoldPyne200127.E2.80.9330_37-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEArnoldPyne200127.E2.80.9330-37">[37]</a></sup> This prompted President <a href="/wiki/Dwight_Eisenhower" class="mw-redirect" title="Dwight Eisenhower">Dwight Eisenhower</a>, who was <a href="/wiki/United_States_presidential_inauguration" title="United States presidential inauguration">inaugurated</a> in January 1953, to inform the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Congress" title="United States Congress">United States Congress</a> that the McMahon Act, which he considered a "terrible piece of legislation" and "one of the most deplorable incidents in American history of which he personally felt ashamed", was obsolete.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBaylis2008429.E2.80.93430_39-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBaylis2008429.E2.80.93430-39">[39]</a></sup></p>

<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:International_Diplomacy.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/de/International_Diplomacy.jpg/220px-International_Diplomacy.jpg" width="220" height="173" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/de/International_Diplomacy.jpg/330px-International_Diplomacy.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/de/International_Diplomacy.jpg/440px-International_Diplomacy.jpg 2x" data-file-width="700" data-file-height="552" /></a>

President <a href="/wiki/Dwight_D._Eisenhower" title="Dwight D. Eisenhower">Dwight D. Eisenhower</a> (second from right) and Prime Minister <a href="/wiki/Harold_Macmillan" title="Harold Macmillan">Harold Macmillan</a> (second from left) meet for talks in Bermuda in March 1957 to repair the rift created by the 1956 <a href="/wiki/Suez_crisis" class="mw-redirect" title="Suez crisis">Suez crisis</a></div>

<p>At the three-power Bermuda Conference in December 1953, Eisenhower and Churchill discussed the possibility of the United States giving Britain access to American nuclear weapons in wartime.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPaul2000200.E2.80.93201_40-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPaul2000200.E2.80.93201-40">[40]</a></sup> This came to be called <a href="/wiki/Project_E" title="Project E">Project E</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBaylis2008429.E2.80.93430_39-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBaylis2008429.E2.80.93430-39">[39]</a></sup> There were technical and legal issues that had to be overcome before American bombs could be carried in British aircraft. The United States would have to disclose their weights and dimensions, while their delivery would require data concerning their ballistics. Further down the track, there would also be issues of custody, security and targeting. The release of such information was restricted by the McMahon Act.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEYoung2016200.E2.80.93201_41-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEYoung2016200.E2.80.93201-41">[41]</a></sup></p>
<p>This was amended on 30 August 1954 by the <a href="/wiki/Atomic_Energy_Act_of_1954" title="Atomic Energy Act of 1954">Atomic Energy Act of 1954</a>, which allowed for greater exchange of information with foreign nations,<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBotti1987140.E2.80.93141_42-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBotti1987140.E2.80.93141-42">[42]</a></sup> paving the way for the Agreement for Cooperation Regarding Atomic Information for Mutual Defence Purposes, which was signed on 15 June 1955.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBotti1987147.E2.80.93149_43-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBotti1987147.E2.80.93149-43">[43]</a></sup> On 13 June 1956, another agreement was concluded, for the transfer of <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_marine_propulsion" title="Nuclear marine propulsion">nuclear submarine propulsion</a> to Britain, saving the British government millions of pounds in research and development costs. This precipitated a row with the JCAE over whether it was permitted under the Atomic Energy Act of 1954, and whether Britain met the security standards set by the 1955 agreement. With the <a href="/wiki/United_States_presidential_election,_1956" title="United States presidential election, 1956">1956 presidential election</a> approaching, Eisenhower was forced to back down.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBotti1987161.E2.80.93164_44-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBotti1987161.E2.80.93164-44">[44]</a></sup></p>
<p>The October 1956 <a href="/wiki/Suez_crisis" class="mw-redirect" title="Suez crisis">Suez crisis</a> brought relations between Britain and the United States to a low ebb.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBotti1987171.E2.80.93174_45-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBotti1987171.E2.80.93174-45">[45]</a></sup> When Eisenhower met with the new Prime Minister, <a href="/wiki/Harold_Macmillan" title="Harold Macmillan">Harold Macmillan</a>, in Bermuda in March 1957, he raised the possibility of basing US <a href="/wiki/Intermediate_range_ballistic_missile" class="mw-redirect" title="Intermediate range ballistic missile">intermediate range ballistic missile</a> (IRBM) systems in the UK.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBotti1987174.E2.80.93177_46-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBotti1987174.E2.80.93177-46">[46]</a></sup> This came to be called <a href="/wiki/Project_Emily" title="Project Emily">Project Emily</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEYoung201698.E2.80.9399_47-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEYoung201698.E2.80.9399-47">[47]</a></sup> There were also discussions on exchanging nuclear submarine propulsion technology for information on the British <a href="/wiki/Calder_Hall" class="mw-redirect" title="Calder Hall">Calder Hall</a> <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_power_plant" title="Nuclear power plant">nuclear power plant</a>, allowing the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_Atomic_Energy_Authority" title="United Kingdom Atomic Energy Authority">United Kingdom Atomic Energy Authority</a> to purchase uranium ore from Canada, and coordinating the war plans of <a href="/wiki/RAF_Bomber_Command" title="RAF Bomber Command">RAF Bomber Command</a> with those of the <a href="/wiki/Strategic_Air_Command" title="Strategic Air Command">Strategic Air Command</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBotti1987180.E2.80.93181_48-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBotti1987180.E2.80.93181-48">[48]</a></sup> Although the IRBM negotiations pre-dated the Suez crisis, it suited the British government to tout the IRBM deal as a demonstration that the rift had been healed.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEYoung20078_49-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEYoung20078-49">[49]</a></sup> Britain tested its first hydrogen bomb in the <a href="/wiki/Operation_Grapple" title="Operation Grapple">Operation Grapple</a> test at <a href="/wiki/Kiritimati" title="Kiritimati">Christmas Island</a> on 15 May 1957.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBaylis1994166.E2.80.93170_50-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBaylis1994166.E2.80.93170-50">[50]</a></sup> The Grapple tests were facilitated by the United States, which also claimed the island.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBotti1987159.E2.80.93160_51-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBotti1987159.E2.80.93160-51">[51]</a></sup> Although the initial tests were unsuccessful,<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEArnoldPyne2001147_52-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEArnoldPyne2001147-52">[52]</a></sup> the Grapple X test on 8 November 1957 achieved the desired result.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBaylis1994166.E2.80.93170_50-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBaylis1994166.E2.80.93170-50">[50]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEArnoldPyne2001160.E2.80.93162_53-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEArnoldPyne2001160.E2.80.93162-53">[53]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Negotiation">Negotiation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1958_US%E2%80%93UK_Mutual_Defence_Agreement&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Negotiation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sputnik_crisis">Sputnik crisis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1958_US%E2%80%93UK_Mutual_Defence_Agreement&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Sputnik crisis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:HD.3C.001_(13406014683).jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dd/HD.3C.001_%2813406014683%29.jpg/220px-HD.3C.001_%2813406014683%29.jpg" width="220" height="175" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dd/HD.3C.001_%2813406014683%29.jpg/330px-HD.3C.001_%2813406014683%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dd/HD.3C.001_%2813406014683%29.jpg/440px-HD.3C.001_%2813406014683%29.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3200" data-file-height="2540" /></a>

<p>British timing was good. The Soviet Union's launch of <a href="/wiki/Sputnik_1" title="Sputnik 1">Sputnik 1</a>, the world's first <a href="/wiki/Artificial_satellite" class="mw-redirect" title="Artificial satellite">artificial satellite</a>, on 4 October 1957, came as a tremendous shock to the American public, who had trusted that American technological superiority ensured their invulnerability. Now, suddenly, there was incontrovertible proof that, in some areas at least, the Soviet Union was actually ahead. In the widespread calls for action in response to the <a href="/wiki/Sputnik_crisis" title="Sputnik crisis">Sputnik crisis</a>, officials in the United States and Britain seized an opportunity to mend the relationship with Britain that had been damaged by the Suez Crisis.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBotti1987199.E2.80.93201_54-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBotti1987199.E2.80.93201-54">[54]</a></sup> At the suggestion of <a href="/wiki/Harold_Caccia,_Baron_Caccia" title="Harold Caccia, Baron Caccia">Harold Caccia</a>, the <a href="/wiki/List_of_Ambassadors_from_the_United_Kingdom_to_the_United_States" class="mw-redirect" title="List of Ambassadors from the United Kingdom to the United States">British Ambassador to the United States</a>, Macmillan wrote to Eisenhower on 10 October urging that the two countries pool their resources,<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEArnoldPyne2001199_55-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEArnoldPyne2001199-55">[55]</a></sup> as Macmillan put it, "to meet the Soviet challenge on every front, military, political, economic and ideological."<sup id="cite_ref-56" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup></p>
<p>Macmillan flew to Washington, DC, for talks on 25 October,<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDivine199334_57-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDivine199334-57">[57]</a></sup> despite concerns that the 10 October <a href="/wiki/Windscale_fire" title="Windscale fire">Windscale fire</a> might prove a stumbling block in negotiations.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBaylis2008438_58-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBaylis2008438-58">[58]</a></sup> He immediately sensed how shaken the Americans had been by sputnik.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBotti1987199.E2.80.93201_54-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBotti1987199.E2.80.93201-54">[54]</a></sup> It placed the Eisenhower administration under great public pressure to act on the deployment of IRBMs by a shocked and distraught nation.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDivine199373.E2.80.9374_59-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDivine199373.E2.80.9374-59">[59]</a></sup> Eisenhower and Macmillan agreed to form a study group headed by Sir Richard Powell, the Permanent Secretary to Ministry of Defence, and <a href="/wiki/Donald_A._Quarles" title="Donald A. Quarles">Donald A. Quarles</a>, the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Deputy_Secretary_of_Defense" title="United States Deputy Secretary of Defense">United States Deputy Secretary of Defense</a>, to consider how the deployment of IRBMs to Britain might be expedited.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDivine199334_57-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDivine199334-57">[57]</a></sup> Another study group, under Strauss and Sir <a href="/wiki/Edwin_Plowden,_Baron_Plowden" title="Edwin Plowden, Baron Plowden">Edwin Plowden</a>, the head of the United Kingdom Atomic Energy Authority (UKAEA), would investigate nuclear cooperation and the exchange of nuclear information.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBaylis2008438_58-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBaylis2008438-58">[58]</a></sup> The personal relationship developed between Plowden and Strauss would be crucial in converting the latter over to the idea of providing information to Britain.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBaylis2008440_60-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBaylis2008440-60">[60]</a></sup></p>
<p>By December 1957, most of the issues with the IRBM negotiations had been ironed out,<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBaylis2008442_61-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBaylis2008442-61">[61]</a></sup> and a formal agreement was drawn up on 17 December 1957, although it was not until the end of the month that it was definitely determined that Britain would receive <a href="/wiki/PGM-17_Thor" title="PGM-17 Thor">Thor</a> and not <a href="/wiki/PGM-19" class="mw-redirect" title="PGM-19">Jupiter</a> missiles.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWynn1997287_62-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWynn1997287-62">[62]</a></sup> However, the nuclear submarine propulsion effort was running into trouble. Under the July 1956 agreement and a February 1957 directive from Eisenhower, Royal Navy officers had been assigned to study the US Navy's nuclear submarine program. By October 1957, its head, <a href="/wiki/Rear_Admiral_(United_States)" class="mw-redirect" title="Rear Admiral (United States)">Rear Admiral</a> <a href="/wiki/Hyman_G._Rickover" title="Hyman G. Rickover">Hyman G. Rickover</a>, felt that their questions were slowing the deployment of the <a href="/wiki/UGM-27_Polaris" title="UGM-27 Polaris">Polaris</a> submarine-launched IRBM at a critical time. He feared that any delay might cause Congress to favour land-based missiles. By December, the British liaison officers were complaining of slow response to their questions. Rickover proposed that <a href="/wiki/Westinghouse_Electric_Company" title="Westinghouse Electric Company">Westinghouse</a> be permitted to sell the Royal Navy a nuclear submarine reactor, which would allow it to immediately proceed with building its own nuclear-powered submarine. The British government endorsed this idea, as it would save it a great deal of money.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBotti1987203_63-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBotti1987203-63">[63]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBaylis2008441.E2.80.93442_64-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBaylis2008441.E2.80.93442-64">[64]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Amending_the_McMahon_Act">Amending the McMahon Act</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1958_US%E2%80%93UK_Mutual_Defence_Agreement&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Amending the McMahon Act">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>For their part, the British wanted the McMahon Act's restrictions on nuclear cooperation to be relaxed. They wanted to know the weight and dimensions, fusing and firing sequences, safety features, and in-flight procedures; the information that would allow American bombs to be carried in British <a href="/wiki/V-bombers" class="mw-redirect" title="V-bombers">V-bombers</a>, and American warheads to be fitted to British <a href="/wiki/Blue_Streak_(missile)" title="Blue Streak (missile)">Blue Streak</a> missiles.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBotti1987203_63-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBotti1987203-63">[63]</a></sup> This would potentially save millions of pounds and avoid domestic political complications if Britain had to persist with nuclear testing during an international moratorium.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBaylis2008440_60-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBaylis2008440-60">[60]</a></sup> While the British knew what they wanted, there was no consensus among the Americans as to what they wanted to provide.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBotti1987203_63-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBotti1987203-63">[63]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/United_States_Secretary_of_State" title="United States Secretary of State">United States Secretary of State</a>, <a href="/wiki/John_Foster_Dulles" title="John Foster Dulles">John Foster Dulles</a>, was concerned that a Special Relationship with Britain might complicate the United States' relationships with its other allies.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBaylis2008439_65-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBaylis2008439-65">[65]</a></sup> Strauss in particular felt that a proposal to give hydrogen bomb secrets to the British would likely not get past the JCAE, and counselled drafting amendments that were sufficiently vague as to give the president the authority he needed without arousing its ire.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBotti1987203_63-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBotti1987203-63">[63]</a></sup> Eisenhower declared that the US and UK were "interdependent", and pledged to ask Congress to amend the McMahon Act.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMoore201033_66-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMoore201033-66">[66]</a></sup></p>
<p>Crucially, he managed to secure the support of <a href="/wiki/Carl_T._Durham" title="Carl T. Durham">Carl T. Durham</a>, the chairman of the JCAE. Eisenhower met with Congressional leaders on 3 December 1957, and pressed for more discretion to cooperate with all America's <a href="/wiki/NATO" title="NATO">NATO</a> allies, and not just Britain.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBotti1987207.E2.80.93208_67-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBotti1987207.E2.80.93208-67">[67]</a></sup> Indeed the administration negotiated agreements with Australia, Canada and NATO.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBotti1987214_68-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBotti1987214-68">[68]</a></sup> While Eisenhower did not yet have wholehearted support for the proposal, outright opposition from <a href="/wiki/United_States_Senate" title="United States Senate">Senator</a> <a href="/wiki/Clinton_P._Anderson" class="mw-redirect" title="Clinton P. Anderson">Clinton P. Anderson</a> failed to attract much support.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBotti1987207.E2.80.93208_67-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBotti1987207.E2.80.93208-67">[67]</a></sup> On 27 January 1958, Strauss sent Durham the administration's proposed legislative changes,<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBotti1987214_68-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBotti1987214-68">[68]</a></sup> and the JCAE Subcommittee on Agreements for Cooperation, chaired by Senator <a href="/wiki/John_O._Pastore" title="John O. Pastore">John O. Pastore</a>, held hearings from 29 to 31 January. Quarles and <a href="/wiki/Major_General_(United_States)" class="mw-redirect" title="Major General (United States)">Major General</a> <a href="/wiki/Herbert_Loper" title="Herbert Loper">Herbert Loper</a>, the Assistant to the Secretary of Defense for Atomic Energy Affairs, were forced to deal with pointed questions about <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_proliferation" title="Nuclear proliferation">nuclear proliferation</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBotti1987215.E2.80.93219_69-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBotti1987215.E2.80.93219-69">[69]</a></sup> British information security, or the lack thereof, no longer seemed so important now that the Soviet Union was apparently ahead, and the United Kingdom had independently developed the hydrogen bomb,<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBotti1987224.E2.80.93225_70-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBotti1987224.E2.80.93225-70">[70]</a></sup> but the JCAE objected to the terms of the proposed deal to trade British uranium-235 for American plutonium, under which the US would pay the UK $30 per gram for plutonium that cost $12 per gram to produce.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBotti1987224_71-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBotti1987224-71">[71]</a></sup></p>
<p>The amendments were passed by the <a href="/wiki/United_States_House_of_Representatives" title="United States House of Representatives">House of Representatives</a> on 19 June, but not without changes. It now limited exchanges of nuclear weapons data to nations that had made substantial progress in the field. The same restriction applied to the actual transfer of non-nuclear components of nuclear weapons. American nuclear weapons were to remain under US custody and could only be turned over to allies in the event of war. The sale of nuclear reactors for submarines and nuclear fuel for them and other military reactors was permitted. Only Britain qualified as a nation that had made substantial progress.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBotti1987232.E2.80.93233_72-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBotti1987232.E2.80.93233-72">[72]</a></sup> The bill passed Congress on 30 June 1958, and was signed into law by Eisenhower on 2 July 1958.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBotti1987234.E2.80.93236_73-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBotti1987234.E2.80.93236-73">[73]</a></sup> The 1958 US–UK Mutual Defence Agreement was signed by Dulles and <a href="/wiki/Samuel_Hood,_6th_Viscount_Hood" title="Samuel Hood, 6th Viscount Hood">Samuel Hood</a>, the British Minister in Washington, on 3 July,<sup id="cite_ref-full_text_74-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-full_text-74">[74]</a></sup> and approved by Congress on 30 July.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBotti1987238_75-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBotti1987238-75">[75]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Implementation">Implementation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1958_US%E2%80%93UK_Mutual_Defence_Agreement&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Implementation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Details_of_the_agreement">Details of the agreement</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1958_US%E2%80%93UK_Mutual_Defence_Agreement&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Details of the agreement">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Defense.gov_photo_essay_080709-D-7203C-008.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b7/Defense.gov_photo_essay_080709-D-7203C-008.jpg/220px-Defense.gov_photo_essay_080709-D-7203C-008.jpg" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b7/Defense.gov_photo_essay_080709-D-7203C-008.jpg/330px-Defense.gov_photo_essay_080709-D-7203C-008.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b7/Defense.gov_photo_essay_080709-D-7203C-008.jpg/440px-Defense.gov_photo_essay_080709-D-7203C-008.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2174" data-file-height="1449" /></a>

<p>The agreement enables the US and the UK to exchange <a href="/wiki/Classified_information" title="Classified information">classified information</a> with the objective of improving each party's "atomic weapon design, development, and fabrication capability".<sup id="cite_ref-full_text_74-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-full_text-74">[74]</a></sup> While the US has <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_sharing" title="Nuclear sharing">nuclear cooperation</a> agreements with other countries, including France and some <a href="/wiki/NATO" title="NATO">NATO</a> countries, this agreement is by far the most comprehensive.<sup id="cite_ref-76" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-76">[76]</a></sup> Macmillan called it "the Great Prize".<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMacmillan1971323_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMacmillan1971323-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Article 2 of the treaty covered joint development of defence plans; the training of personnel in the use and defence against nuclear weapons; the sharing of intelligence and evaluation of enemy capabilities; the development of nuclear delivery systems; and the research, development and design of military reactors.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMills20143_77-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMills20143-77">[77]</a></sup> The treaty called for the exchange of "classified information concerning atomic weapons when, after consultation with the other Party, the communicating Party determines that the communication of such information is necessary to improve the recipient's atomic weapon design, development and fabrication capability."<sup id="cite_ref-full_text_74-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-full_text-74">[74]</a></sup> The US would communicate information about atomic weapons that were similar to British atomic weapons. For the immediate future, that would exclude information about thermonuclear weapons.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBotti1987236.E2.80.93237_78-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBotti1987236.E2.80.93237-78">[78]</a></sup> There are also confidential intelligence matters covered by the agreement. The UK government has not published these sections "because of the necessity for great confidentiality and because ... it might well assist proliferation".<sup id="cite_ref-79" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-79">[79]</a></sup></p>
<p>Article 3 provided for the sale to the UK of one complete nuclear submarine propulsion plant, plus the uranium needed to fuel it over a ten year period.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMills20143_77-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMills20143-77">[77]</a></sup> Due to concerns expressed by the JCAE, the AEC would determine the price that Britain would pay for HEU.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBotti1987236.E2.80.93237_78-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBotti1987236.E2.80.93237-78">[78]</a></sup> The treaty did not allow for non-nuclear components of nuclear weapons to be given to Britain. It was amended on 7 May 1959 to give Britain access to non-nuclear components,<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBotti1987238_75-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBotti1987238-75">[75]</a></sup> and to permit the transfer of special nuclear material such as <a href="/wiki/Plutonium" title="Plutonium">plutonium</a>, <a href="/wiki/Highly_enriched_uranium" class="mw-redirect" title="Highly enriched uranium">highly enriched uranium</a> (HEU) and <a href="/wiki/Tritium" title="Tritium">tritium</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMoore201084.E2.80.9385_80-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMoore201084.E2.80.9385-80">[80]</a></sup> The treaty paved the way for the subsequent <a href="/wiki/Polaris_Sales_Agreement" title="Polaris Sales Agreement">Polaris Sales Agreement</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBaylis2008455.E2.80.93456_81-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBaylis2008455.E2.80.93456-81">[81]</a></sup> which was signed on 6 April 1963.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEJones2017444_82-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJones2017444-82">[82]</a></sup> The two agreements have been "the cornerstone of the UK-US nuclear relationship for nearly 60 years."<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMills20143_77-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMills20143-77">[77]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Joint_Working_Groups">Joint Working Groups</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1958_US%E2%80%93UK_Mutual_Defence_Agreement&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Joint Working Groups">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Between 2007 and 2009, staff of the <a href="/wiki/Atomic_Weapons_Establishment" title="Atomic Weapons Establishment">Atomic Weapons Establishment</a> paid 2,000 visits to US nuclear facilities.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMills20145_83-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMills20145-83">[83]</a></sup> Most of the activity under the treaty is information exchange through Joint Working Groups (JOWOG). At least 15 of these were established in 1959.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMoore201090_84-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMoore201090-84">[84]</a></sup> Subjects investigated included:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>one-point safety, computer codes, metallurgy and fabrication technology for beryllium, uranium and plutonium, corrosion of uranium in the presence of water and water vapour, underground effects tests, outer-space testing, clandestine testing, the technology of lithium compounds, high explosives, deuterium monitors, extinguishing plutonium fires, high-speed cameras, mechanical safeing, liquid and solid explosive shock initiation, environmental sensing switches, neutron sources, tritium reservoirs, telemetry, hydrodynamic and shock relations for problems with spherical and cylindrical symmetry, cross sections, radiochemistry, atomic demolition munitions, warhead hardening, asymmetric detonations, terrorist nuclear threat response, nuclear weapons accidents and waste management.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMoore201090_84-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMoore201090-84">[84]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>As of 2014<sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=1958_US%E2%80%93UK_Mutual_Defence_Agreement&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup> there are also two enhanced collaborations jointly developing capabilities:</p>
<ul>
<li>Enhanced Nuclear Safety – to develop architectures and technologies related to warhead safety; and</li>
<li>Warhead Electrical System – to develop architectures and technologies for warhead electrical systems.<sup id="cite_ref-parliament-wq209762_85-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-parliament-wq209762-85">[85]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Nuclear_weapons_development">Nuclear weapons development</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1958_US%E2%80%93UK_Mutual_Defence_Agreement&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Nuclear weapons development">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The AEC invited the British government to send representatives to a series of meetings in Washington, DC, on 27 and 28 August 1958 to work out the details. The U.S. delegation included <a href="/wiki/Willard_Libby" title="Willard Libby">Willard Libby</a>, AEC deputy chairman; Major General Herbert Loper, assistant to the <a href="/wiki/Secretary_of_Defense" class="mw-redirect" title="Secretary of Defense">Secretary of Defense</a> for Atomic Energy; <a href="/wiki/Brigadier_General_(United_States)" class="mw-redirect" title="Brigadier General (United States)">Brigadier General</a> <a href="/wiki/Alfred_Starbird" title="Alfred Starbird">Alfred Starbird</a>, AEC Director of Military Applications; <a href="/wiki/Norris_Bradbury" title="Norris Bradbury">Norris Bradbury</a>, director of the <a href="/wiki/Los_Alamos_National_Laboratory" title="Los Alamos National Laboratory">Los Alamos National Laboratory</a>; <a href="/wiki/Edward_Teller" title="Edward Teller">Edward Teller</a>, director of the <a href="/wiki/Lawrence_Livermore_Laboratory" class="mw-redirect" title="Lawrence Livermore Laboratory">Lawrence Livermore Laboratory</a>; and James W. McCrae, president of the <a href="/wiki/Sandia_Corporation" class="mw-redirect" title="Sandia Corporation">Sandia Corporation</a>. The British representatives were Sir <a href="/wiki/Frederick_Brundrett" title="Frederick Brundrett">Frederick Brundrett</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Chief_Scientific_Adviser_to_the_Ministry_of_Defence" title="Chief Scientific Adviser to the Ministry of Defence">Chief Scientific Adviser to the Ministry of Defence</a> and J.H.B. Macklen from the Ministry of Defence; and <a href="/wiki/William_Penney" class="mw-redirect" title="William Penney">William Penney</a>, <a href="/wiki/William_Richard_Joseph_Cook" title="William Richard Joseph Cook">William Cook</a> and E. F. Newly from the Atomic Weapons Establishment at Aldermaston. The Americans disclosed the details of nine of their nuclear weapon designs: the <a href="/wiki/Mark_7_nuclear_bomb" title="Mark 7 nuclear bomb">Mark 7</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mark_15_nuclear_bomb" title="Mark 15 nuclear bomb">Mark 15</a>/<a href="/wiki/Mark_39_nuclear_bomb" title="Mark 39 nuclear bomb">39</a>, <a href="/wiki/W19_(nuclear_artillery_shell)" title="W19 (nuclear artillery shell)">Mark 19</a>, <a href="/wiki/W25_(nuclear_warhead)" title="W25 (nuclear warhead)">Mark 25</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mark_27_nuclear_bomb" title="Mark 27 nuclear bomb">Mark 27</a>, <a href="/wiki/B28_nuclear_bomb" title="B28 nuclear bomb">Mark 28</a>, <a href="/wiki/W31" title="W31">Mark 31</a>, <a href="/wiki/W33_(nuclear_warhead)" title="W33 (nuclear warhead)">Mark 33</a> and <a href="/wiki/W34_(nuclear_warhead)" title="W34 (nuclear warhead)">Mark 34</a>. In return, the British provided the details of seven of theirs, including Green Grass; Pennant, the boosted device which had been detonated in the Grapple Z test on 22 August; Flagpole, the two-stage device scheduled for 2 September; Burgee, scheduled for 23 September; and the three-stage Haillard 3. The Americans were impressed with the British designs, particularly with Haillard 1, the heavier version of Haillard 3. Cook therefore changed the Grapple Z programme to fire Haillard 1 instead of Haillard 3.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEArnoldPyne2001202.E2.80.93205_86-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEArnoldPyne2001202.E2.80.93205-86">[86]</a></sup> Macmillan noted in his diary, with satisfaction that:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>in some respects we are as far, and even further, advanced in the art than our American friends. They thought interchange of information would be all <i>give</i>. They are keen that we should complete our series, especially the last megaton, the character of which is novel and of deep interest to them.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMacmillan1971565_87-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMacmillan1971565-87">[87]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>An early benefit of the agreement was to allow the UK to "Anglicise" the US <a href="/wiki/W28" class="mw-redirect" title="W28">W28</a> nuclear warhead as the <a href="/wiki/Red_Snow" title="Red Snow">Red Snow</a> <a href="/wiki/Thermonuclear_weapon" title="Thermonuclear weapon">thermonuclear weapon</a> for the <a href="/wiki/Blue_Steel_missile" class="mw-redirect" title="Blue Steel missile">Blue Steel missile</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMoore201088.E2.80.9389_88-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMoore201088.E2.80.9389-88">[88]</a></sup> The British designers were impressed by the W28, which was not only lighter than the British <a href="/wiki/Yellow_Sun_(nuclear_weapon)#Green_Grass" title="Yellow Sun (nuclear weapon)">Green Grass</a> warhead used in Yellow Sun, but remarkably more economical in its use of expensive fissile material. The Yellow Sun Mark 2 using Red Snow cost £500,000 compared with £1,200,000 for the Mark 1 with Green Grass.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMoore2010104.E2.80.93105_89-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMoore2010104.E2.80.93105-89">[89]</a></sup> A 1974 CIA proliferation assessment noted that "In many cases [Britain's sensitive technology in nuclear and missile fields] is based on technology received from the US and could not legitimately be passed on without US permission."<sup id="cite_ref-SNIE-4-1-74_90-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SNIE-4-1-74-90">[90]</a></sup> The UK <a href="/wiki/National_Audit_Office_(United_Kingdom)" title="National Audit Office (United Kingdom)">National Audit Office</a> noted that most of the <a href="/wiki/UK_Trident_programme" class="mw-redirect" title="UK Trident programme">UK Trident</a> warhead development and production expenditure was incurred in the US who would supply "certain warhead-related components".<sup id="cite_ref-91" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-91">[91]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-NAO-1987_92-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NAO-1987-92">[92]</a></sup> Some of the <a href="/wiki/Fissile_material" title="Fissile material">fissile materials</a> for the UK Trident warhead were purchased from the US.<sup id="cite_ref-NAO-1987_92-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NAO-1987-92">[92]</a></sup> There is evidence that the warhead design of the <a href="/wiki/British_Trident_system" class="mw-redirect" title="British Trident system">British Trident system</a> is similar to, or even based upon, the US <a href="/wiki/W76" title="W76">W76</a> warhead fitted in US Navy <a href="/wiki/Trident_missile" class="mw-redirect" title="Trident missile">Trident missiles</a>, with design and blast model data supplied to the UK.<sup id="cite_ref-93" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-93">[93]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-94" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-94">[94]</a></sup></p>
<p>Britain soon became dependent on the United States for its nuclear weapons, as it lacked the resources to produce a range of designs.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBotti1987238.E2.80.93241_95-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBotti1987238.E2.80.93241-95">[95]</a></sup> The treaty allowed the UK to receive US nuclear weapons for the RAF and <a href="/wiki/British_Army_of_the_Rhine" title="British Army of the Rhine">British Army of the Rhine</a> under Project E.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMoore2010114.E2.80.93115_96-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMoore2010114.E2.80.93115-96">[96]</a></sup> Similar custody arrangements were made for the Thor missiles supplied under Project Emily.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMoore201098.E2.80.9399_97-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMoore201098.E2.80.9399-97">[97]</a></sup> The UK was able to carry out underground <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_test" class="mw-redirect" title="Nuclear test">nuclear tests</a> at the US <a href="/wiki/Nevada_Test_Site" title="Nevada Test Site">Nevada Test Site</a>, the first taking place on 1 March 1962.<sup id="cite_ref-98" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-98">[98]</a></sup> British nuclear testing continued there until halted by President <a href="/wiki/Bill_Clinton" title="Bill Clinton">Bill Clinton</a> in October 1992.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBaylis2008462_99-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBaylis2008462-99">[99]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Special_nuclear_materials_barter">Special nuclear materials barter</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1958_US%E2%80%93UK_Mutual_Defence_Agreement&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Special nuclear materials barter">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:HMS_Splendid_S106.jpeg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2a/HMS_Splendid_S106.jpeg/220px-HMS_Splendid_S106.jpeg" width="220" height="178" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2a/HMS_Splendid_S106.jpeg/330px-HMS_Splendid_S106.jpeg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2a/HMS_Splendid_S106.jpeg/440px-HMS_Splendid_S106.jpeg 2x" data-file-width="3600" data-file-height="2915" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:HMS_Splendid_S106.jpeg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The Royal Navy's <a href="/wiki/Swiftsure-class_submarine" title="Swiftsure-class submarine"><i>Swiftsure</i>-class&#160;submarine</a> <a href="/wiki/HMS_Splendid_(S106)" title="HMS Splendid (S106)">HMS&#160;<i>Splendid</i></a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Under the agreement 5.37 <a href="/wiki/Tonne" title="Tonne">tonnes</a> (11,800&#160;lb) of UK-produced <a href="/wiki/Plutonium" title="Plutonium">plutonium</a> was sent to the US in exchange for 6.7 kilograms (15&#160;lb) of <a href="/wiki/Tritium" title="Tritium">tritium</a> and 7.5 tonnes (17,000&#160;lb) of <a href="/wiki/Highly_enriched_uranium" class="mw-redirect" title="Highly enriched uranium">highly enriched uranium</a> (HEU) between 1960 and 1979. A further 470 kilograms (1,040&#160;lb) of plutonium was swapped between the US and the UK for reasons that remain classified.<sup id="cite_ref-mod-plutonium-account_100-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mod-plutonium-account-100">[100]</a></sup> Some of the UK produced plutonium was used in 1962 by the US for the only known nuclear weapon test of <a href="/wiki/Reactor-grade_plutonium" title="Reactor-grade plutonium">reactor-grade plutonium</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-101" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-101">[101]</a></sup> The plutonium sent to the US included some produced in UK civil <a href="/wiki/Magnox" title="Magnox">Magnox</a> reactors, and the US gave assurances that this civil plutonium was not used in the US nuclear weapons program. It was used in civil programmes which included <a href="/wiki/Californium" title="Californium">californium</a> production and reactor research.<sup id="cite_ref-mod-plutonium-account_100-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mod-plutonium-account-100">[100]</a></sup></p>
<p>Much of the HEU supplied by the US was used not for weapons, but as fuel for the growing fleet of UK <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_submarine" title="Nuclear submarine">nuclear submarines</a>. Under the treaty, the US supplied the UK with not just nuclear submarine propulsion technology, but a complete <a href="/wiki/S5W" class="mw-redirect" title="S5W">S5W</a> <a href="/wiki/Pressurized_water_reactor" title="Pressurized water reactor">pressurized water reactor</a> of the kind used to power the US <a href="/wiki/Skipjack-class_submarine" title="Skipjack-class submarine"><i>Skipjack</i>-class</a> submarines. This was used in the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Navy" title="Royal Navy">Royal Navy</a>'s first nuclear-powered submarine, <a href="/wiki/HMS_Dreadnought_(S101)" title="HMS Dreadnought (S101)">HMS&#160;<i>Dreadnought</i></a>, which was <a href="/wiki/Ceremonial_ship_launching" title="Ceremonial ship launching">launched</a> in 1960 and <a href="/wiki/Ship_commissioning" title="Ship commissioning">commissioned</a> in 1963. The S5W had a <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_reactor_core" title="Nuclear reactor core">Nuclear reactor core</a> that used uranium enriched to between 93 and 97 per cent <a href="/wiki/Uranium-235" title="Uranium-235">uranium-235</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERitchie20153_102-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERitchie20153-102">[102]</a></sup> Reactor technology was transferred from <a href="/wiki/Westinghouse_Electric_Company" title="Westinghouse Electric Company">Westinghouse</a> to <a href="/wiki/Rolls_Royce" class="mw-redirect" title="Rolls Royce">Rolls Royce</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMoore201035_103-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMoore201035-103">[103]</a></sup> which used it as the basis for its <a href="/wiki/PWR1" class="mw-redirect" title="PWR1">PWR1</a> reactor used in the UK's <a href="/wiki/Valiant-class_submarine" title="Valiant-class submarine"><i>Valiant</i></a> , <a href="/wiki/Resolution-class_submarine" title="Resolution-class submarine"><i>Resolution</i></a> , <a href="/wiki/Churchill-class_submarine" title="Churchill-class submarine"><i>Churchill</i></a> , <a href="/wiki/Swiftsure-class_submarine" title="Swiftsure-class submarine"><i>Swiftsure</i></a> and <a href="/wiki/Trafalgar-class_submarine" title="Trafalgar-class submarine"><i>Trafalgar</i></a> submarines.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERitchie20154_104-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERitchie20154-104">[104]</a></sup></p>
<p>The UK produced HEU at its facility in <a href="/wiki/Capenhurst" title="Capenhurst">Capenhurst</a>, but production for military purposes ceased there in March 1963.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMoore2010197.E2.80.93199_105-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMoore2010197.E2.80.93199-105">[105]</a></sup> Thereafter, uranium oxide was imported from Australia, Canada, Namibia, South Africa, the United States and Zaire, and processed into <a href="/wiki/Uranium_hexafluoride" title="Uranium hexafluoride">uranium hexafluoride</a> at Springfields. It was then shipped to the US, where it was enriched at the <a href="/wiki/Portsmouth_Gaseous_Diffusion_Plant" title="Portsmouth Gaseous Diffusion Plant">Portsmouth Gaseous Diffusion Plant</a> in <a href="/wiki/Piketon,_Ohio" title="Piketon, Ohio">Piketon, Ohio</a>. HEU was then flown back to the UK in RAF aircraft.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERitchie20153_102-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERitchie20153-102">[102]</a></sup> In 1994, with the Portsmouth plant about to close, the treaty was amended, with the US requirement to <i>provide</i> uranium enrichment services changed to one to <i>arrange</i> them.<sup id="cite_ref-full_text_74-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-full_text-74">[74]</a></sup> By March 2002, the UK had a stockpile of 21.86 tonnes of HEU, about 80 years' supply for the Royal Navy's nuclear-powered submarines.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERitchie20157_106-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERitchie20157-106">[106]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mutual_benefit">Mutual benefit</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1958_US%E2%80%93UK_Mutual_Defence_Agreement&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Mutual benefit">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Anglo-American Special Relationship proved mutually beneficial, although it was never one of equals; the United States was far larger than Britain both militarily and economically.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBotti1987238.E2.80.93241_95-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBotti1987238.E2.80.93241-95">[95]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Lorna_Arnold" title="Lorna Arnold">Lorna Arnold</a> noted that:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>The balance of advantage in the exchanges was necessarily in Britain’s favour but they were not entirely one-sided. In some areas, notably electronics and high explosives, the British were equal or perhaps even superior, and in many areas they had valuable ideas to contribute, as the American scientists, and notably Teller, appreciated.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEArnoldPyne2001215_107-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEArnoldPyne2001215-107">[107]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>A 1985 report by the State Department's <a href="/wiki/Bureau_of_Intelligence_and_Research" title="Bureau of Intelligence and Research">Bureau of Intelligence and Research</a> reported that the US was "profoundly involved and benefited greatly" from the treaty.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBaylis2008461_108-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBaylis2008461-108">[108]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Renewal">Renewal</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1958_US%E2%80%93UK_Mutual_Defence_Agreement&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Renewal">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The treaty was amended on 7 May 1959, 27 September 1968, 16 October 1969, 22 June 1974, 5 December 1979, 5 June 1984, 23 May 1994 and 14 June 2004. Most amendments merely extended the treaty for another five or ten years; others added definitions and made minor changes.<sup id="cite_ref-109" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-109">[109]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-110" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-110">[110]</a></sup> As of 2017<sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=1958_US%E2%80%93UK_Mutual_Defence_Agreement&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, the most recent renewal was on 22 July 2014, extending the treaty to 31 December 2024, with minor changes for the <a href="/wiki/Trident_nuclear_programme" title="Trident nuclear programme">Trident nuclear programme</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-2014_amendment_111-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2014_amendment-111">[111]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-112" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-112">[112]</a></sup></p>
<p>A 2004 legal opinion obtained by BASIC, the Acronym Institute for Disarmament Diplomacy and Peace Rights, argued that renewal of the treaty violated Article VI of the <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_Non-Proliferation_Treaty" class="mw-redirect" title="Nuclear Non-Proliferation Treaty">Nuclear Non-Proliferation Treaty</a>, which required signatories to take steps towards nuclear disarmament. This was not accepted by the British government. According to <a href="/wiki/Baroness_Warsi" class="mw-redirect" title="Baroness Warsi">Baroness Warsi</a>, the Senior <a href="/wiki/Minister_of_State_for_Foreign_and_Commonwealth_Affairs" class="mw-redirect" title="Minister of State for Foreign and Commonwealth Affairs">Minister of State for Foreign and Commonwealth Affairs</a>:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>We are committed to the goal of a world without nuclear weapons and firmly believe that the best way to achieve this is through gradual disarmament negotiated through a step-by-step approach within the framework of the Nuclear Non-Proliferation Treaty. The UK has a strong record on nuclear disarmament and continues to be at the forefront of international efforts to control proliferation, and to make progress towards multilateral nuclear disarmament. The UK-USA Mutual Defence Agreement is, and will continue to be, in full compliance with our obligations under the Nuclear Non-Proliferation Treaty.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMills201410_113-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMills201410-113">[113]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
