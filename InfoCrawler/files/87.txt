<p>An <b>encyclopedia</b> or <b>encyclopaedia</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Reference_work" title="Reference work">reference work</a> or <a href="/wiki/Compendium" title="Compendium">compendium</a> providing summaries of <a href="/wiki/Information" title="Information">information</a> from either all branches of <a href="/wiki/Knowledge" title="Knowledge">knowledge</a> or from a particular field or discipline.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Encyclopedias are divided into <a href="/wiki/Article_(publishing)" title="Article (publishing)">articles</a> or entries that are often arranged <a href="/wiki/Alphabetical_order" title="Alphabetical order">alphabetically</a> by article name<sup id="cite_ref-DOLencyclopedia_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DOLencyclopedia-2">[2]</a></sup> and sometimes by thematic categories. Encyclopedia entries are longer and more detailed than those in most <a href="/wiki/Dictionary" title="Dictionary">dictionaries</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-DOLencyclopedia_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DOLencyclopedia-2">[2]</a></sup> Generally speaking, unlike dictionary entries which focus on <a href="/wiki/Linguistics" title="Linguistics">linguistic</a> information about <a href="/wiki/Word" title="Word">words</a>, such as their meaning, pronunciation, use, and grammatical forms, encyclopedia articles focus on <a href="/wiki/Fact" title="Fact">factual</a> information concerning the subject named in the article’s title.<sup id="cite_ref-bejoint_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bejoint-3">[3]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-EB_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EB-4">[4]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-DOLei_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DOLei-5">[5]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-OHEL22_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-OHEL22-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>Encyclopedias have existed for around 2,000 years and have evolved considerably since that time as to language (written in a major international or a vernacular language), size (few or many volumes), intent (presentation of a global or a limited range of knowledge), cultural perceptions (authoritative, ideological, didactic, utilitarian), authorship (qualifications, style), readership (education level, background, interests, capabilities), and the technologies available for their production and distribution (hand-written manuscripts, small or large print runs, internet production). As a valued source of reliable information compiled by experts, printed versions found a prominent place in libraries, schools and other educational institutions.</p>
<p>The appearance of digital and open-source versions in the 20th century has vastly expanded the accessibility, authorship, readership, and variety of encyclopedia entries and called into question the idea of what an encyclopedia is and the relevance of applying to such dynamic productions the traditional criteria for assembling and evaluating print encyclopedias.</p>

<div class="quotebox" style="float: right; clear: right; margin: 0.5em 0 0.8em 1.4em; width:40%; padding: 10px; border: 1px solid #aaa; font-size: 88%; background-color: #F9F9F9;">
<div class="" style="position: relative; text-align: left;">
<div>Indeed, the purpose of an encyclopedia is to collect knowledge disseminated around the globe; to set forth its general system to the men with whom we live, and transmit it to those who will come after us, so that the work of preceding centuries will not become useless to the centuries to come; and so that our offspring, becoming better instructed, will at the same time become more virtuous and happy, and that we should not die without having rendered a service to the human race in the future years to come.</div>
</div>
<cite style="display: block; text-align: left;"><a href="/wiki/Denis_Diderot" title="Denis Diderot">Diderot</a><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></cite></div>
<p>The word <i><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/encyclopedia" class="extiw" title="wikt:encyclopedia">encyclopedia</a></i> comes from the <a href="/wiki/Koine_Greek" title="Koine Greek">Koine Greek</a> <span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc">ἐγκύκλιος παιδεία</span>,<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> transliterated <i>enkyklios paideia</i>, meaning "general education" from <i>enkyklios</i> (ἐγκύκλιος), meaning "circular, recurrent, required regularly, general"<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> and <i><a href="/wiki/Paideia" title="Paideia">paideia</a></i> (παιδεία), meaning "education, rearing of a child";<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> it was reduced to a single word due to an error<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> by copyists of <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> manuscripts. Together, the phrase literally translates as "complete instruction" or "complete knowledge".</p>
<p>Copyists of Latin manuscripts took this phrase to be a single Greek word, <i>enkyklopaidia</i>, with the same meaning, and this spurious Greek word became the <a href="/wiki/New_Latin" title="New Latin">New Latin</a> word "encyclopaedia", which in turn came into English. Though the notion of a compendium of knowledge dates back thousands of years, the term was first used in the title of a book in 1517 by <a href="/wiki/Johannes_Aventinus" title="Johannes Aventinus">Johannes Aventinus</a>: <i>Encyclopedia orbisque doctrinarum, hoc est omnium artium, scientiarum, ipsius philosophiae index ac divisio</i>, and in 1538 by <a href="/wiki/Joachimus_Fortius_Ringelbergius" class="mw-redirect" title="Joachimus Fortius Ringelbergius">Joachimus Fortius Ringelbergius</a>, <i>Lucubrationes vel potius absolutissima kyklopaideia</i>.</p>

<p>The word <i>encyclopaedia</i> was first used as a <a href="/wiki/Noun" title="Noun">noun</a> in the title of his book by the <a href="/wiki/Croats" title="Croats">Croatian</a> encyclopedist <a href="/wiki/Pavao_Skali%C4%87" class="mw-redirect" title="Pavao Skalić">Pavao Skalić</a> in his <i>Encyclopaedia seu orbis disciplinarum tam sacrarum quam prophanarum epistemon</i> (Encyclopaedia, or Knowledge of the World of Disciplines, Basel, 1559). One of the oldest <a href="/wiki/Vernacular" title="Vernacular">vernacular</a> uses was by <a href="/wiki/Fran%C3%A7ois_Rabelais" title="François Rabelais">François Rabelais</a> in his <i><a href="/wiki/Gargantua_and_Pantagruel" title="Gargantua and Pantagruel">Pantagruel</a></i> in 1532.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> Several encyclopedias have names that include the suffix <i>-p(a)edia</i>, e.g., <a href="/wiki/Banglapedia" title="Banglapedia">Banglapedia</a> (on matters relevant for Bangladesh).</p>
<p>Today in English, the word is most commonly spelled <i>encyclopedia</i>, though <i>encyclopaedia</i> (<a href="/wiki/American_and_British_English_spelling_differences#ae_and_oe" title="American and British English spelling differences">from <i>encyclopædia</i></a>) is also used in Britain.<sup id="cite_ref-Oxford_English_Dictionary_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Oxford_English_Dictionary-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Characteristics">Characteristics</span></h2>
<p>The modern encyclopedia was developed from the <a href="/wiki/Dictionary" title="Dictionary">dictionary</a> in the 18th century. Historically, both encyclopedias and dictionaries have been researched and written by well-educated, well-informed content <a href="/wiki/Expert" title="Expert">experts</a>, but they are significantly different in structure. A dictionary is a linguistic work which primarily focuses on alphabetical listing of <a href="/wiki/Words" class="mw-redirect" title="Words">words</a> and their <a href="/wiki/Definitions" class="mw-redirect" title="Definitions">definitions</a>. <a href="/wiki/Synonym" title="Synonym">Synonymous</a> words and those related by the subject matter are to be found scattered around the dictionary, giving no obvious place for in-depth treatment. Thus, a dictionary typically provides limited <a href="/wiki/Information" title="Information">information</a>, <a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Analysis" class="extiw" title="wikt:Analysis">analysis</a> or background for the word defined. While it may offer a definition, it may leave the reader lacking in <a href="/wiki/Understanding" title="Understanding">understanding</a> the meaning, significance or limitations of a <a href="/wiki/Term_(language)" class="mw-redirect" title="Term (language)">term</a>, and how the term relates to a broader field of knowledge. An encyclopedia is, theoretically, not written in order to convince, although one of its goals is indeed to convince its reader of its own veracity.</p>
<p>To address those needs, an encyclopedia article is typically not limited to simple definitions, and is not limited to defining an individual word, but provides a more extensive meaning for a <i>subject or <a href="/wiki/List_of_academic_disciplines" class="mw-redirect" title="List of academic disciplines">discipline</a></i>. In addition to defining and listing synonymous terms for the topic, the article is able to treat the topic's more extensive meaning in more depth and convey the most relevant accumulated knowledge on that subject. An encyclopedia article also often includes many <a href="/wiki/Map" title="Map">maps</a> and <a href="/wiki/Illustration" title="Illustration">illustrations</a>, as well as <a href="/wiki/Bibliography" title="Bibliography">bibliography</a> and <a href="/wiki/Statistics" title="Statistics">statistics</a>.</p>
<p>Four major elements define an encyclopedia: its subject matter, its scope, its method of organization, and its method of production:</p>
<ul>
<li>Encyclopedias can be general, containing articles on topics in every field (the English-language <i>Encyclopædia Britannica</i> and German <i><a href="/wiki/Brockhaus_Enzyklop%C3%A4die" title="Brockhaus Enzyklopädie">Brockhaus</a></i> are well-known examples). General encyclopedias may contain guides on how to do a variety of things, as well as embedded dictionaries and <a href="/wiki/Gazetteer" title="Gazetteer">gazetteers</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> There are also encyclopedias that cover a wide variety of topics from a particular cultural, ethnic, or national perspective, such as the <i><a href="/wiki/Great_Soviet_Encyclopedia" title="Great Soviet Encyclopedia">Great Soviet Encyclopedia</a></i> or <i><a href="/wiki/Encyclopaedia_Judaica" title="Encyclopaedia Judaica">Encyclopaedia Judaica</a></i>.</li>
<li>Works of encyclopedic scope aim to convey the important accumulated knowledge for their subject domain, such as an encyclopedia of <a href="/wiki/Medicine" title="Medicine">medicine</a>, <a href="/wiki/Philosophy" title="Philosophy">philosophy</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Law" title="Law">law</a>. Works vary in the breadth of material and the depth of discussion, depending on the <a href="/wiki/Target_audience" title="Target audience">target audience</a>.</li>
<li>Some systematic method of organization is essential to making an encyclopedia usable for reference. There have historically been two main methods of organizing printed encyclopedias: the <a href="/wiki/Alphabetical_order" title="Alphabetical order">alphabetical</a> method (consisting of a number of separate articles, organized in alphabetical order) and organization by <a href="/wiki/Hierarchy" title="Hierarchy">hierarchical</a> categories. The former method is today the more common, especially for general works. The fluidity of electronic media, however, allows new possibilities for multiple methods of organization of the same content. Further, electronic media offer new capabilities for search, <a href="/wiki/Subject_indexing" title="Subject indexing">indexing</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cross_reference" class="mw-redirect" title="Cross reference">cross reference</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Epigraph_(literature)" title="Epigraph (literature)">epigraph</a> from <a href="/wiki/Horace" title="Horace">Horace</a> on the title page of the 18th century <i>Encyclopédie</i> suggests the importance of the structure of an encyclopedia: "What grace may be added to commonplace matters by the power of order and connection."</li>
<li>As modern multimedia and the information age have evolved, new methods have emerged for the collection, verification, summation, and presentation of information of all kinds. Projects such as <a href="/wiki/Everything2" title="Everything2">Everything2</a>, <a href="/wiki/Encarta" title="Encarta">Encarta</a>, <a href="/wiki/H2g2" title="H2g2">h2g2</a>, and Wikipedia are examples of new forms of the encyclopedia as <a href="/wiki/Information_retrieval" title="Information retrieval">information retrieval</a> becomes simpler. The method of production for an encyclopedia historically has been supported in both for-profit and non-profit contexts. The <i><a href="/wiki/Great_Soviet_Encyclopedia" title="Great Soviet Encyclopedia">Great Soviet Encyclopedia</a></i> mentioned above was entirely state sponsored, while the <i>Britannica</i> was supported as a for-profit institution. By comparison, <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia" title="Wikipedia">Wikipedia</a> is supported by volunteers contributing in a non-profit environment under the organization of the <a href="/wiki/Wikimedia_Foundation" title="Wikimedia Foundation">Wikimedia Foundation</a>.</li>
</ul>
<p>Some works entitled "dictionaries" are actually similar to encyclopedias, especially those concerned with a particular field (such as the <i><a href="/wiki/Dictionary_of_the_Middle_Ages" title="Dictionary of the Middle Ages">Dictionary of the Middle Ages</a></i>, the <i><a href="/wiki/Dictionary_of_American_Naval_Fighting_Ships" title="Dictionary of American Naval Fighting Ships">Dictionary of American Naval Fighting Ships</a></i>, and <i><a href="/wiki/Black%27s_Law_Dictionary" title="Black's Law Dictionary">Black's Law Dictionary</a></i>). The <i><a href="/wiki/Macquarie_Dictionary" title="Macquarie Dictionary">Macquarie Dictionary</a>,</i> Australia's national dictionary, became an <a href="/wiki/Encyclopedic_dictionary" title="Encyclopedic dictionary">encyclopedic dictionary</a> after its first edition in recognition of the use of proper nouns in common communication, and the words derived from such proper nouns.</p>
<p>There are some broad differences between encyclopedias and dictionaries. Most noticeably, encyclopedia articles are longer, fuller and more thorough than entries in most general-purpose dictionaries.<sup id="cite_ref-DOLencyclopedia_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DOLencyclopedia-2">[2]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-DOLencyclopedicdefinition_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DOLencyclopedicdefinition-15">[15]</a></sup> There are differences in content as well. Generally speaking, dictionaries provide <a href="/wiki/Linguistics" title="Linguistics">linguistic</a> information about words themselves, while encyclopedias focus more on the thing for which those words stand.<sup id="cite_ref-bejoint_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bejoint-3">[3]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-EB_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EB-4">[4]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-DOLei_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DOLei-5">[5]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-OHEL22_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-OHEL22-6">[6]</a></sup> Thus, while dictionary entries are inextricably fixed to the word described, encyclopedia articles can be given a different entry name. As such, dictionary entries are not fully translatable into other languages, but encyclopedia articles can be.<sup id="cite_ref-bejoint_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bejoint-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>In practice, however, the distinction is not concrete, as there is no clear-cut difference between factual, "encyclopedic" information and linguistic information such as appears in dictionaries.<sup id="cite_ref-DOLei_5-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DOLei-5">[5]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-DOLencyclopedicdefinition_15-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DOLencyclopedicdefinition-15">[15]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Bejoint31_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bejoint31-16">[16]</a></sup> Thus encyclopedias may contain material that is also found in dictionaries, and vice versa.<sup id="cite_ref-Bejoint31_16-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bejoint31-16">[16]</a></sup> In particular, dictionary entries often contain factual information about the thing named by the word.<sup id="cite_ref-DOLencyclopedicdefinition_15-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DOLencyclopedicdefinition-15">[15]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Bejoint31_16-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bejoint31-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/History_of_encyclopedias" title="History of encyclopedias">History of encyclopedias</a></div>
<p>Encyclopedias have progressed from written form in antiquity, to print in modern times. Today they can also be distributed and displayed electronically.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ancient_times">Ancient times</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Naturalishistoria.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/67/Naturalishistoria.jpg/220px-Naturalishistoria.jpg" width="220" height="371" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/6/67/Naturalishistoria.jpg 1.5x" data-file-width="235" data-file-height="396" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Naturalishistoria.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<i>Naturalis Historiæ</i>, 1669 edition, title page</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>One of the earliest encyclopedic works to have survived to modern times is the <i><a href="/wiki/Naturalis_Historia" class="mw-redirect" title="Naturalis Historia">Naturalis Historia</a></i> of <a href="/wiki/Pliny_the_Elder" title="Pliny the Elder">Pliny the Elder</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Rome" title="Ancient Rome">Roman</a> statesman living in the first century AD. He compiled a work of 37 chapters covering natural history, architecture, medicine, geography, geology, and other aspects of the world around him. He stated in the preface that he had compiled 20,000 facts from 2000 works by over 200 authors, and added many others from his own experience. The work was published around AD 77–79, although Pliny probably never finished editing the work before his death in the eruption of <a href="/wiki/Vesuvius" class="mw-redirect" title="Vesuvius">Vesuvius</a> in AD 79.<sup id="cite_ref-naturalis_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-naturalis-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Middle_Ages">Middle Ages</span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Isidore_of_Seville" title="Isidore of Seville">Isidore of Seville</a>, one of the greatest scholars of the early Middle Ages, is widely recognized for writing the first encyclopedia of the Middle Ages, the <i><a href="/wiki/Etymologiae" title="Etymologiae">Etymologiae</a></i> (<i>The Etymologies</i>) or <i>Origines</i> (around 630), in which he compiled a sizable portion of the learning available at his time, both ancient and contemporary. The work has 448 chapters in 20 volumes, and is valuable because of the quotes and fragments of texts by other authors that would have been lost had he not collected them.</p>
<p>The most popular encyclopedia of the <a href="/wiki/Carolingian" class="mw-redirect" title="Carolingian">Carolingian</a> Age was the <i>De universo</i> or <i>De rerum naturis</i> by <a href="/wiki/Rabanus_Maurus" title="Rabanus Maurus">Rabanus Maurus</a>, written about 830; it was based on <i>Etymologiae</i>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>The encyclopedia of <a href="/wiki/Suda" title="Suda">Suda</a>, a massive 10th-century Byzantine encyclopedia, had 30 000 entries, many drawing from ancient sources that have since been lost, and often derived from medieval Christian compilers. The text was arranged alphabetically with some slight deviations from common vowel order and place in the Greek alphabet.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Historiography_of_early_Islam" title="Historiography of early Islam">early Muslim compilations of knowledge</a> in the Middle Ages included many comprehensive works. Around year 960, the <a href="/wiki/Brethren_of_Purity" title="Brethren of Purity">Brethren of Purity</a> of <a href="/wiki/Basra" title="Basra">Basra</a> were engaged in their <i><a href="/wiki/Encyclopedia_of_the_Brethren_of_Purity" title="Encyclopedia of the Brethren of Purity">Encyclopedia of the Brethren of Purity</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> Notable works include <a href="/wiki/Al-Razi" title="Al-Razi">Abu Bakr al-Razi</a>'s encyclopedia of science, the <a href="/wiki/Mutazilite" class="mw-redirect" title="Mutazilite">Mutazilite</a> <a href="/wiki/Al-Kindi" title="Al-Kindi">Al-Kindi</a>'s prolific output of 270 books, and <a href="/wiki/Ibn_Sina" class="mw-redirect" title="Ibn Sina">Ibn Sina</a>'s medical encyclopedia, which was a standard reference work for centuries. Also notable are works of <a href="/wiki/Universal_history" title="Universal history">universal history</a> (or sociology) from <a href="/wiki/Asharite" class="mw-redirect" title="Asharite">Asharites</a>, <a href="/wiki/Al-Tabri" class="mw-redirect" title="Al-Tabri">al-Tabri</a>, <a href="/wiki/Masudi" class="mw-redirect" title="Masudi">al-Masudi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Muhammad_ibn_Jarir_al-Tabari" class="mw-redirect" title="Muhammad ibn Jarir al-Tabari">Tabari</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/History_of_the_Prophets_and_Kings" title="History of the Prophets and Kings">History of the Prophets and Kings</a></i>, <a href="/wiki/Ibn_Rustah" class="mw-redirect" title="Ibn Rustah">Ibn Rustah</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ali_ibn_al-Athir" title="Ali ibn al-Athir">al-Athir</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Ibn_Khaldun" title="Ibn Khaldun">Ibn Khaldun</a>, whose <i><a href="/wiki/The_Muqadimmah" class="mw-redirect" title="The Muqadimmah">Muqadimmah</a></i> contains cautions regarding trust in written records that remain wholly applicable today.</p>
<p>The enormous encyclopedic work in China of the <i><a href="/wiki/Four_Great_Books_of_Song" title="Four Great Books of Song">Four Great Books of Song</a></i>, compiled by the 11th century AD during the early <a href="/wiki/Song_dynasty" title="Song dynasty">Song dynasty</a> (960–1279), was a massive literary undertaking for the time. The last encyclopedia of the four, the <i><a href="/wiki/Prime_Tortoise_of_the_Record_Bureau" class="mw-redirect" title="Prime Tortoise of the Record Bureau">Prime Tortoise of the Record Bureau</a></i>, amounted to 9.4 million <a href="/wiki/Chinese_characters" title="Chinese characters">Chinese characters</a> in 1000 written volumes.</p>
<p>In late medieval Europe, several authors had the ambition of compiling the sum of human knowledge in a certain field or overall, for example <a href="/wiki/Bartholomew_of_England" class="mw-redirect" title="Bartholomew of England">Bartholomew of England</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vincent_of_Beauvais" title="Vincent of Beauvais">Vincent of Beauvais</a>, <a href="/wiki/Radulfus_Ardens" title="Radulfus Ardens">Radulfus Ardens</a>, <a href="/wiki/Book_of_Sydrac" title="Book of Sydrac">Sydrac</a>, <a href="/wiki/Brunetto_Latini" title="Brunetto Latini">Brunetto Latini</a>, Giovanni da Sangiminiano, <a href="/wiki/Pierre_Bersuire" title="Pierre Bersuire">Pierre Bersuire</a>. Some were women, like <a href="/wiki/Hildegard_of_Bingen" title="Hildegard of Bingen">Hildegard of Bingen</a> and <a href="/wiki/Herrad_of_Landsberg" title="Herrad of Landsberg">Herrad of Landsberg</a>. The most successful of those publications were the <i>Speculum maius (Great Mirror)</i> of <a href="/wiki/Vincent_of_Beauvais" title="Vincent of Beauvais">Vincent of Beauvais</a> and the <i>De proprietatibus rerum (On the Properties of Things)</i> by <a href="/wiki/Bartholomew_of_England" class="mw-redirect" title="Bartholomew of England">Bartholomew of England</a>. The latter was translated (or adapted) into French, Provençal, Italian, English, Flemish, Anglo-Norman, Spanish, and German during the Middle Ages. Both were written in the middle of the 13th century. No medieval encyclopedia bore the title <i>Encyclopaedia</i> – they were often called <i>On nature (De natura, De naturis rerum)</i>, <i>Mirror (Speculum maius, Speculum universale)</i>, <i>Treasure (Trésor)</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Renaissance">Renaissance</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Margarita_Philosopica_by_Reisch_2.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/58/Margarita_Philosopica_by_Reisch_2.jpg/220px-Margarita_Philosopica_by_Reisch_2.jpg" width="220" height="166" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/58/Margarita_Philosopica_by_Reisch_2.jpg/330px-Margarita_Philosopica_by_Reisch_2.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/58/Margarita_Philosopica_by_Reisch_2.jpg/440px-Margarita_Philosopica_by_Reisch_2.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2592" data-file-height="1952" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Margarita_Philosopica_by_Reisch_2.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Anatomy in <i>Margarita Philosophica</i>, 1565</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Medieval encyclopedias were all hand-copied and thus available mostly to wealthy patrons or monastic men of learning; they were expensive, and usually written for those extending knowledge rather than those using it.<sup id="cite_ref-dotma_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dotma-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>During the <a href="/wiki/Renaissance" title="Renaissance">Renaissance</a>, the creation of <a href="/wiki/Printing" title="Printing">printing</a> allowed a wider diffusion of encyclopedias and every scholar could have his or her own copy. The <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=De_expetendis_et_fugiendis_rebus&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="De expetendis et fugiendis rebus (page does not exist)">De expetendis et fugiendis rebus</a></i> by <a href="/wiki/Giorgio_Valla" title="Giorgio Valla">Giorgio Valla</a> was posthumously printed in 1501 by <a href="/wiki/Aldo_Manuzio" class="mw-redirect" title="Aldo Manuzio">Aldo Manuzio</a> in <a href="/wiki/Venice" title="Venice">Venice</a>. This work followed the traditional scheme of liberal arts. However, Valla added the translation of ancient Greek works on mathematics (firstly by <a href="/wiki/Archimedes" title="Archimedes">Archimedes</a>), newly discovered and translated. The <i>Margarita Philosophica</i> by <a href="/wiki/Gregor_Reisch" title="Gregor Reisch">Gregor Reisch</a>, printed in 1503, was a complete encyclopedia explaining the <a href="/wiki/Seven_liberal_arts" class="mw-redirect" title="Seven liberal arts">seven liberal arts</a>.</p>
<p>The term encyclopaedia was coined by 16th-century humanists who misread copies of their texts of <a href="/wiki/Pliny_the_Elder" title="Pliny the Elder">Pliny</a> and <a href="/wiki/Quintilian" title="Quintilian">Quintilian</a>, and combined the two Greek words "<i>enkyklios paideia</i>" into one word, έγκυκλοπαιδεία.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> The phrase <i>enkyklios paideia</i> (ἐγκύκλιος παιδεία) was used by Plutarch and the Latin word Encyclopedia came from him.</p>
<p>The first work titled in this way was the <i>Encyclopedia orbisque doctrinarum, hoc est omnium artium, scientiarum, ipsius philosophiae index ac divisio</i> written by <a href="/wiki/Johannes_Aventinus" title="Johannes Aventinus">Johannes Aventinus</a> in 1517.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>The English physician and philosopher, Sir <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Browne" title="Thomas Browne">Thomas Browne</a> used the word 'encyclopaedia' in 1646 in the preface <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://penelope.uchicago.edu/pseudodoxia/pseudo1.html">to the reader</a> to define his <i><a href="/wiki/Pseudodoxia_Epidemica" title="Pseudodoxia Epidemica">Pseudodoxia Epidemica</a></i>, a major work of the 17th-century scientific revolution. Browne structured his encyclopaedia upon the time-honoured scheme of the Renaissance, the so-called 'scale of creation' which ascends through the mineral, vegetable, animal, human, planetary, and cosmological worlds. <i>Pseudodoxia Epidemica</i> was a European best-seller, translated into French, Dutch, and German as well as Latin it went through no fewer than five editions, each revised and augmented, the last edition appearing in 1672.</p>
<p>Financial, commercial, legal, and intellectual factors changed the size of encyclopedias. During the <a href="/wiki/Renaissance" title="Renaissance">Renaissance</a>, middle classes had more time to read and encyclopedias helped them to learn more. Publishers wanted to increase their output so some countries like Germany started selling books missing alphabetical sections, to publish faster. Also, publishers could not afford all the resources by themselves, so multiple publishers would come together with their resources to create better encyclopedias. When publishing at the same rate became financially impossible, they turned to subscriptions and serial publications. This was risky for publishers because they had to find people that would pay all upfront or make payments. When this worked, capital would rise and there would be a steady income for encyclopedias. Later, rivalry grew, causing copyright to occur due to weak underdeveloped laws. Some publishers would copy another publisher’s work to produce an encyclopedia faster and cheaper so consumers did not have to pay a lot and they would sell more. Encyclopedias made it to where middle-class citizens could basically have a small library in their own house. Europeans were becoming more curious about their society around them causing them to revolt against their government.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span id="18th–19th_centuries"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="18th.E2.80.9319th_centuries">18th–19th centuries</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Encyclopedie_de_D%27Alembert_et_Diderot_-_Premiere_Page_-_ENC_1-NA5.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2b/Encyclopedie_de_D%27Alembert_et_Diderot_-_Premiere_Page_-_ENC_1-NA5.jpg/220px-Encyclopedie_de_D%27Alembert_et_Diderot_-_Premiere_Page_-_ENC_1-NA5.jpg" width="220" height="360" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2b/Encyclopedie_de_D%27Alembert_et_Diderot_-_Premiere_Page_-_ENC_1-NA5.jpg/330px-Encyclopedie_de_D%27Alembert_et_Diderot_-_Premiere_Page_-_ENC_1-NA5.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2b/Encyclopedie_de_D%27Alembert_et_Diderot_-_Premiere_Page_-_ENC_1-NA5.jpg/440px-Encyclopedie_de_D%27Alembert_et_Diderot_-_Premiere_Page_-_ENC_1-NA5.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3133" data-file-height="5120" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Encyclopedie_de_D%27Alembert_et_Diderot_-_Premiere_Page_-_ENC_1-NA5.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Encyclop%C3%A9die" title="Encyclopédie">Encyclopédie</a>, 1773</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The beginnings of the modern idea of the general-purpose, widely distributed printed encyclopedia precede the 18th century encyclopedists. However, <a href="/wiki/Ephraim_Chambers" title="Ephraim Chambers">Chambers</a>' <i><a href="/wiki/Cyclopaedia,_or_Universal_Dictionary_of_Arts_and_Sciences" class="mw-redirect" title="Cyclopaedia, or Universal Dictionary of Arts and Sciences">Cyclopaedia, or Universal Dictionary of Arts and Sciences</a></i> (1728), and the <i><a href="/wiki/Encyclop%C3%A9die" title="Encyclopédie">Encyclopédie</a></i> of <a href="/wiki/Denis_Diderot" title="Denis Diderot">Denis Diderot</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jean_le_Rond_d%27Alembert" title="Jean le Rond d'Alembert">Jean le Rond d'Alembert</a> (1751 onwards), as well as <i><a href="/wiki/Encyclop%C3%A6dia_Britannica" title="Encyclopædia Britannica">Encyclopædia Britannica</a></i> and the <i><a href="/wiki/Brockhaus_encyclopedia" class="mw-redirect" title="Brockhaus encyclopedia">Conversations-Lexikon</a></i>, were the first to realize the form we would recognize today, with a comprehensive scope of topics, discussed in depth and organized in an accessible, systematic method. Chambers, in 1728, followed the earlier lead of John Harris's <i><a href="/wiki/Lexicon_Technicum" title="Lexicon Technicum">Lexicon Technicum</a></i> of 1704 and later editions (see also below); this work was by its title and content "A Universal English Dictionary of Arts and Sciences: Explaining not only the Terms of Art, but the Arts Themselves".</p>
<p>During the 19th and early 20th century, many smaller or less developed languages<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Avoid_weasel_words" class="mw-redirect" title="Wikipedia:Avoid weasel words"><span title="The material near this tag possibly uses too vague attribution or weasel words. (December 2014)">which?</span></a></i>]</sup> saw their first encyclopedias, using French, German, and English role models. While encyclopedias in larger languages, having large markets that could support a large editorial staff, churned out new 20-volume works in a few years and new editions with brief intervals, such publication plans often spanned a decade or more in smaller languages.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="20th_century">20th century</span></h3>
<p>Popular and affordable encyclopedias such as <i><a href="/wiki/Harmsworth%27s_Universal_Encyclopaedia" title="Harmsworth's Universal Encyclopaedia">Harmsworth's Universal Encyclopaedia</a></i> and the <i><a href="/wiki/Children%27s_Encyclopaedia" class="mw-redirect" title="Children's Encyclopaedia">Children's Encyclopaedia</a></i> appeared in the early 1920s.</p>
<p>In the United States, the 1950s and 1960s saw the introduction of several large popular encyclopedias, often sold on installment plans. The best known of these were <i><a href="/wiki/World_Book" class="mw-redirect" title="World Book">World Book</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Funk_and_Wagnalls" class="mw-redirect" title="Funk and Wagnalls">Funk and Wagnalls</a></i>.</p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:EncycBrit1913.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7d/EncycBrit1913.jpg/220px-EncycBrit1913.jpg" width="220" height="130" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7d/EncycBrit1913.jpg/330px-EncycBrit1913.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/7/7d/EncycBrit1913.jpg 2x" data-file-width="433" data-file-height="256" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:EncycBrit1913.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
1913 advertisement for <i><a href="/wiki/Encyclop%C3%A6dia_Britannica" title="Encyclopædia Britannica">Encyclopædia Britannica</a></i>, the oldest and one of the largest contemporary English encyclopedias</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The second half of the 20th century also saw the proliferation of specialized encyclopedias that compiled topics in specific fields. This trend has continued. Encyclopedias of at least one volume in size now exist for most if not all <a href="/wiki/Academic_discipline" class="mw-redirect" title="Academic discipline">academic disciplines</a>, including such narrow topics such as <a href="/wiki/Bioethics" title="Bioethics">bioethics</a>.</p>
<p>By the late 20th century, encyclopedias were being published on <a href="/wiki/CD-ROM" title="CD-ROM">CD-ROMs</a> for use with personal computers. <a href="/wiki/Microsoft" title="Microsoft">Microsoft</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Encarta" title="Encarta">Encarta</a></i>, launched in 1993, was a landmark example as it had no printed equivalent. Articles were supplemented with both video and audio files as well as numerous high-quality images. After sixteen years, Microsoft discontinued the Encarta line of products in 2009.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="21st_century">21st century</span></h3>
<p>In 2001, <a href="/wiki/Jimmy_Wales" title="Jimmy Wales">Jimmy Wales</a> and <a href="/wiki/Larry_Sanger" title="Larry Sanger">Larry Sanger</a> launched <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia" title="Wikipedia">Wikipedia</a>, a multilingual, <a href="/wiki/Open-source_software" title="Open-source software">open-source</a>, free <a href="/wiki/Online_encyclopedia" title="Online encyclopedia">Internet encyclopedia</a> supported by the non-profit <a href="/wiki/Wikimedia_Foundation" title="Wikimedia Foundation">Wikimedia Foundation</a>. Unlike commercial online encyclopedias such as <a href="/wiki/Britannica_Online" class="mw-redirect" title="Britannica Online">Britannica Online</a>, which are written by experts, Wikipedia is collaboratively edited by volunteers. As of 25 October 2017, there are 5,498,758 articles in the English Wikipedia. There are 287 different editions of Wikipedia. As of February 2014, it had 18 billion page views and nearly 500 million unique visitors each month.<sup id="cite_ref-small_screen2_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-small_screen2-24">[24]</a></sup> Wikipedia has more than 25 million accounts, out of which there were over 118,000 active editors globally, as of August 2015. Wikipedia's accuracy was found by a <i><a href="/wiki/Nature_(journal)" title="Nature (journal)">Nature</a></i> study to be close to that of <i><a href="/wiki/Encyclop%C3%A6dia_Britannica" title="Encyclopædia Britannica">Encyclopædia Britannica</a></i>,<sup id="cite_ref-GilesJ2005Internet_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GilesJ2005Internet-25">[25]</a></sup> with Wikipedia being much larger.</p>
<p>However, <a href="/wiki/Criticism_of_Wikipedia" title="Criticism of Wikipedia">critics</a> argue Wikipedia exhibits <a href="/wiki/Systemic_bias" title="Systemic bias">systemic bias</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> Many academics, historians, teachers, and journalists reject Wikipedia as a reliable source of information.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>While Wikipedia is by far the largest web-based encyclopedia, it is not the only one in existence. There are several much smaller, usually more specialized, encyclopedias on various themes, sometimes dedicated to a specific geographic region or time period.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> One example is the <a href="/wiki/Stanford_Encyclopedia_of_Philosophy" title="Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy">Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy</a>.</p>
