<p><b>Würzburg</b> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'v' in 'vie'">v</span><span title="/ɜːr/: 'ur' in 'fur'">ɜːr</span><span title="'t' in 'tie'">t</span><span title="'s' in 'sigh'">s</span><span title="'b' in 'buy'">b</span><span title="/ɜːr/: 'ur' in 'fur'">ɜːr</span><span title="/ɡ/: 'g' in 'guy'">ɡ</span></span>, <span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'w' in 'wind'">w</span><span title="/ɜːr/: 'ur' in 'fur'">ɜːr</span><span title="'t' in 'tie'">t</span><span title="'s' in 'sigh'">s</span><span title="'b' in 'buy'">b</span><span title="/ɜːr/: 'ur' in 'fur'">ɜːr</span><span title="/ɡ/: 'g' in 'guy'">ɡ</span></span>/</a></span></span>;<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> <small>German pronunciation:</small> <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Standard_German" title="Help:IPA/Standard German">[ˈvʏɐ̯tsbʊɐ̯k]</a></span><small class="nowrap metadata">&#160;(<span class="unicode haudio"><span class="fn"><span style="white-space:nowrap"><a href="/wiki/File:De-W%C3%BCrzburg.ogg" title="About this sound"><img alt="About this sound" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/11px-Loudspeaker.svg.png" width="11" height="11" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/17px-Loudspeaker.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/22px-Loudspeaker.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="20" data-file-height="20" /></a>&#160;</span><a href="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/4/4e/De-W%C3%BCrzburg.ogg" class="internal" title="De-Würzburg.ogg">listen</a></span></span>)</small>; <a href="/wiki/Main-Franconian" class="mw-redirect" title="Main-Franconian">Main-Franconian</a>: <i>Wörtzburch</i>) is a city in the region of <a href="/wiki/Franconia" title="Franconia">Franconia</a>, northern <a href="/wiki/Bavaria" title="Bavaria">Bavaria</a>, Germany. Located on the <a href="/wiki/Main_(river)" title="Main (river)">Main</a> River, it is the capital of the <i><a href="/wiki/Regierungsbezirk" title="Regierungsbezirk">Regierungsbezirk</a></i> of <a href="/wiki/Lower_Franconia" title="Lower Franconia">Lower Franconia</a>. The regional dialect is <a href="/wiki/Franconian_languages" title="Franconian languages">Franconian</a>.</p>
<p>Würzburg lies about equidistant from <a href="/wiki/Frankfurt" title="Frankfurt">Frankfurt am Main</a> and from <a href="/wiki/Nuremberg" title="Nuremberg">Nuremberg</a> (each about 120 kilometers or 75 miles away). Although the city of Würzburg is not part of the <i>Landkreis Würzburg</i>, (i.e., <a href="/wiki/W%C3%BCrzburg_(district)" title="Würzburg (district)">district of Würzburg</a>), it is the seat of the district's administration. The city has a population of around 124,000 people.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=W%C3%BCrzburg&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Timeline_of_W%C3%BCrzburg" title="Timeline of Würzburg">Timeline of Würzburg</a></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_and_medieval_history">Early and medieval history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=W%C3%BCrzburg&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Early and medieval history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:W%C3%BCrzburg_Siegel.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ec/W%C3%BCrzburg_Siegel.jpg/220px-W%C3%BCrzburg_Siegel.jpg" width="220" height="211" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ec/W%C3%BCrzburg_Siegel.jpg/330px-W%C3%BCrzburg_Siegel.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ec/W%C3%BCrzburg_Siegel.jpg/440px-W%C3%BCrzburg_Siegel.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1950" data-file-height="1868" /></a>

<p>A <a href="/wiki/Bronze_Age_Europe" title="Bronze Age Europe">Bronze Age</a> (<a href="/wiki/Urnfield_culture" title="Urnfield culture">Urnfield culture</a>) <a href="/wiki/Refuge_castle" title="Refuge castle">refuge castle</a> stood on the site of the present <a href="/wiki/Fortress_Marienberg" class="mw-redirect" title="Fortress Marienberg">Fortress Marienberg</a>. The former <a href="/wiki/Celts" title="Celts">Celtic</a> territory was settled by the <a href="/wiki/Alamanni" class="mw-redirect" title="Alamanni">Alamanni</a> in the 4th or 5th century, and by the <a href="/wiki/Franks" title="Franks">Franks</a> in the 6th to 7th. Würzburg was the seat of a <a href="/wiki/Merovingian" class="mw-redirect" title="Merovingian">Merovingian</a> duke from about 650. It was Christianized in 686 by <a href="/wiki/Irish_missionaries" class="mw-redirect" title="Irish missionaries">Irish missionaries</a> <a href="/wiki/Saint_Kilian" title="Saint Kilian">Kilian</a>, <a href="/wiki/St._Colman_(martyr)" class="mw-redirect" title="St. Colman (martyr)">Kolonat</a> and <a href="/wiki/Saint_Totnan" title="Saint Totnan">Totnan</a>. The city is mentioned in a donation by Duke <a href="/wiki/Hedan_II" title="Hedan II">Hedan II</a> to bishop <a href="/wiki/Willibrord" title="Willibrord">Willibrord</a>, dated 1 May 704, <i>in castellum Virteburch</i>. The <a href="/wiki/Ravenna_Cosmography" title="Ravenna Cosmography">Ravenna Cosmography</a> lists the city as <i>Uburzis</i> at about the same time.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> The name is presumably of <a href="/wiki/Celtic_toponyms" class="mw-redirect" title="Celtic toponyms">Celtic</a> origin, but based on a <a href="/wiki/Folk_etymology" title="Folk etymology">folk etymological</a> connection to the German word <i><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/W%C3%BCrze" class="extiw" title="wikt:Würze">Würze</a></i> "herb, spice", the name was <a href="/wiki/Medieval_Latin" title="Medieval Latin">Latinized</a> as <i>Herbipolis</i> in the medieval period.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Beginning in 1237, the city seal depicted the cathedral and a portrait of Saint Kilian, with the inscription <i>SIGILLVM CIVITATIS HERBIPOLENSIS</i>. It shows a banner on a tilted lance, formerly in a blue field, with the banner quarterly argent and gules (1532), later or and gules (1550). This coat of arms replaced the older seal of the city, showing Saint Kilian, from 1570.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>The first diocese was founded by <a href="/wiki/Saint_Boniface" title="Saint Boniface">Saint Boniface</a> in 742 when he appointed the first <a href="/wiki/Bishopric_of_W%C3%BCrzburg" title="Bishopric of Würzburg">bishop of Würzburg</a>, <a href="/wiki/Burchard_of_W%C3%BCrzburg" title="Burchard of Würzburg">Saint Burkhard</a>. The bishops eventually created a duchy with its center in the city, which extended in the 12th century to Eastern <a href="/wiki/Duchy_of_Franconia" title="Duchy of Franconia">Franconia</a>. The city was the seat of several <a href="/wiki/Imperial_Diet_(Holy_Roman_Empire)" title="Imperial Diet (Holy Roman Empire)">Imperial Diets</a>, including the one of 1180, in which <a href="/wiki/Henry_the_Lion" title="Henry the Lion">Henry the Lion</a> was banned from the Empire and his <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Bavaria" title="Kingdom of Bavaria">duchy</a> was handed over to <a href="/wiki/Otto_I_of_Wittelsbach,_Duke_of_Bavaria" class="mw-redirect" title="Otto I of Wittelsbach, Duke of Bavaria">Otto of Wittelsbach</a>. Massacres of Jews took place in 1147 and 1298.</p>
<p>The first church on the site of the present <a href="/wiki/W%C3%BCrzburg_Cathedral" title="Würzburg Cathedral">Würzburg Cathedral</a> was built as early as 788, and consecrated that same year by <a href="/wiki/Charlemagne" title="Charlemagne">Charlemagne</a>; the current building was constructed from 1040 to 1225 in <a href="/wiki/Romanesque_architecture" title="Romanesque architecture">Romanesque</a> style. The <a href="/wiki/University_of_W%C3%BCrzburg" title="University of Würzburg">University of Würzburg</a> was founded in 1402 and re-founded in 1582. The citizens of the city revolted several times against the prince-bishop.</p>
<p>In 1397, King <a href="/wiki/Wenceslaus_IV_of_Bohemia" title="Wenceslaus IV of Bohemia">Wenceslaus IV of Bohemia</a> had visited the city and promised its people the status of a <a href="/wiki/Free_Imperial_City" class="mw-redirect" title="Free Imperial City">free Imperial City</a>. However, the German ruling princes forced him to withdraw these promises. In 1400, the citizenry was decisively defeated by the troops of the bishop in the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Schlacht_von_Bergtheim&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Schlacht von Bergtheim (page does not exist)">Schlacht von Bergtheim</a><span class="noprint" style="font-size:85%; font-style: normal;">&#160;(<a href="https://de.wikipedia.org/wiki/Schlacht_von_Bergtheim" class="extiw" title="de:Schlacht von Bergtheim">de</a>)</span>, and the city fell under his control permanently until the dissolution of the fiefdom.<sup id="cite_ref-Dumont_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dumont-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:41</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Modern_history">Modern history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=W%C3%BCrzburg&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Modern history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/W%C3%BCrzburg_witch_trial" title="Würzburg witch trial">Würzburg witch trials</a>, which occurred between 1626 and 1631, are one of the largest peace-time mass trials. In Würzburg, under Bishop <a href="/wiki/Philipp_Adolf_von_Ehrenberg" title="Philipp Adolf von Ehrenberg">Philip Adolf</a> an estimated number between 600 and 900 alleged witches were burnt.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> In 1631, Swedish King <a href="/wiki/Gustavus_Adolphus_of_Sweden" title="Gustavus Adolphus of Sweden">Gustaf Adolf</a> invaded the town and plundered the castle.</p>
<p>In 1720, the foundations of the <a href="/wiki/W%C3%BCrzburg_Residence" title="Würzburg Residence">Würzburg Residence</a> were laid. In 1796, the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_W%C3%BCrzburg" title="Battle of Würzburg">Battle of Würzburg</a> between <a href="/wiki/Habsburg_Austria" class="mw-redirect" title="Habsburg Austria">Habsburg Austria</a> and the <a href="/wiki/First_French_Republic" class="mw-redirect" title="First French Republic">First French Republic</a> took place. The city passed to the <a href="/wiki/Electorate_of_Bavaria" title="Electorate of Bavaria">Electorate of Bavaria</a> in 1803, but two years later, in the course of the <a href="/wiki/Napoleonic_Wars" title="Napoleonic Wars">Napoleonic Wars</a>, it became the seat of the Electorate of Würzburg (until September 1806), the later <a href="/wiki/Grand_Duchy_of_W%C3%BCrzburg" title="Grand Duchy of Würzburg">Grand Duchy of Würzburg</a>. In 1814, the town became part of the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Bavaria" title="Kingdom of Bavaria">Kingdom of Bavaria</a> and <a href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic_Diocese_of_W%C3%BCrzburg" title="Roman Catholic Diocese of Würzburg">a new bishopric</a> was created seven years later, as the former one had been <a href="/wiki/German_mediatization" title="German mediatization">secularized in 1803</a> (see also <a href="/wiki/Reichsdeputationshauptschluss" title="Reichsdeputationshauptschluss">Reichsdeputationshauptschluss</a>).</p>
<p>In 1817, <a href="/wiki/Friedrich_Koenig" title="Friedrich Koenig">Friedrich Koenig</a> and <a href="/wiki/Andreas_Friedrich_Bauer" title="Andreas Friedrich Bauer">Andreas Bauer</a> founded <i>Schnellpressenfabrik Koenig &amp; Bauer</i> (the world's first <a href="/wiki/Printing_press" title="Printing press">printing press</a> manufacturer).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>On the eve of the <a href="/wiki/Nazis" class="mw-redirect" title="Nazis">Nazis</a>' rise to power, 2,000 Jews lived in Würzburg; it was a community of tradesmen and professionals. Würzburg was a rabbinic center and home to many Jewish communal organisations and the Jewish Teachers Seminary. In November 1941, the first Jews from Würzburg were sent to the <a href="/wiki/Nazi_concentration_camps" title="Nazi concentration camps">Nazi concentration camps</a> in Eastern Europe. The final transport departed in June 1943. Few<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Avoid_weasel_words" class="mw-redirect" title="Wikipedia:Avoid weasel words"><span title="Who says this? (May 2017)">weasel&#160;words</span></a></i>]</sup> survived.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>On 16 March 1945, about 90% of the city was destroyed in 17 minutes by fire bombing from 225 British <a href="/wiki/Avro_Lancaster" title="Avro Lancaster">Lancaster bombers</a> during a <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a> air raid, despite the presence of 40 hospitals and the absence of any major armament producer. Wüzburg became a target for its role as a traffic hub and to break the spirit of the population.<sup id="cite_ref-Dumont_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dumont-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:19</sup></p>
<p>All of the city's churches, cathedrals, and other monuments were heavily damaged or destroyed. The city center, which mostly dated from medieval times, was totally destroyed in a <a href="/wiki/Firestorm" title="Firestorm">firestorm</a> in which 5,000 people perished.</p>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Bombing_of_W%C3%BCrzburg_in_World_War_II" title="Bombing of Würzburg in World War II">Bombing of Würzburg in World War II</a></div>
<p>Over the next 20 years, the buildings of historical importance were painstakingly and accurately reconstructed. The citizens who rebuilt the city immediately after the end of the war were mostly women – <i><a href="/wiki/Tr%C3%BCmmerfrauen" class="mw-redirect" title="Trümmerfrauen">Trümmerfrauen</a></i> ("rubble women") – because the men were either dead or still <a href="/wiki/Prisoners_of_war" class="mw-redirect" title="Prisoners of war">prisoners of war</a>. On a relative scale, Würzburg was destroyed to a larger extent than was <a href="/wiki/Bombing_of_Dresden_in_World_War_II" title="Bombing of Dresden in World War II">Dresden in a firebombing</a> the previous month.</p>
<p>On 3 April 1945, Würzburg was occupied by the U.S. <a href="/wiki/12th_Armored_Division_(United_States)" title="12th Armored Division (United States)">12th Armored Division</a> and U.S. <a href="/wiki/42nd_Infantry_Division_(United_States)" title="42nd Infantry Division (United States)">42nd Infantry Division</a> in a series of frontal assaults masked by smokescreens. The battle continued until the final Wehrmacht resistance was defeated on 5 April 1945.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/2016_W%C3%BCrzburg_train_attack" title="2016 Würzburg train attack">2016 Würzburg train attack</a> took place at the Würzburg-Heidingsfeld railway station on 18 July.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geography">Geography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=W%C3%BCrzburg&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Geography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Wuerzburg_mit_Festung_Marienberg_Luftansicht_20160813.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/ba/Wuerzburg_mit_Festung_Marienberg_Luftansicht_20160813.jpg/220px-Wuerzburg_mit_Festung_Marienberg_Luftansicht_20160813.jpg" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/ba/Wuerzburg_mit_Festung_Marienberg_Luftansicht_20160813.jpg/330px-Wuerzburg_mit_Festung_Marienberg_Luftansicht_20160813.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/ba/Wuerzburg_mit_Festung_Marienberg_Luftansicht_20160813.jpg/440px-Wuerzburg_mit_Festung_Marienberg_Luftansicht_20160813.jpg 2x" data-file-width="6016" data-file-height="4016" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Wuerzburg_mit_Festung_Marienberg_Luftansicht_20160813.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Würzburg with Fortress Marienberg</div>

</div>
<p>Würzburg is located on both banks of the river Main in the region of Lower Franconia in Bavaria, Germany. The main body of the town is on the eastern (right) bank of the river. The town is completely enclosed by the <i>Landkreis Würzburg</i>, but is not a part of it.</p>
<p>Würzburg covers an area of 87.6 square-kilometres and lies at an altitude of around 177 metres. <sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>Of the total municipal area, in 2007, building area accounted for 30%, followed by agricultural land (27.9%), forestry/wood (15.5%), green spaces (12.7%), traffic (5.4%), water (1.2%) and others (7.3%).<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>The centre of Würzburg is surrounded by hills. To the west lies the 266 metre Marienberg and the Nikolausberg (359 m) to the south of it. The Main flows through Würzburg from the south-east to the north-west.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Economy">Economy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=W%C3%BCrzburg&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Economy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Würzburg is mainly known as an administrative center. Its largest employers are the <a href="/wiki/University_of_W%C3%BCrzburg" title="University of Würzburg">Julius-Maximilians-University</a> and the municipality. The largest private employers are <a href="/wiki/Brose_Fahrzeugteile" title="Brose Fahrzeugteile">Brose Fahrzeugteile</a> followed by <a href="/wiki/Koenig_%26_Bauer" title="Koenig &amp; Bauer">Koenig &amp; Bauer</a>, a maker of printing machines. Würzburg is also the capital of the German wine region <a href="/wiki/Franconia_(wine_region)" title="Franconia (wine region)">Franconia</a> which is famous for its mineralic dry white wines especially from the <a href="/wiki/Silvaner" title="Silvaner">Silvaner</a> grape. <a href="/wiki/W%C3%BCrzburger_Hofbr%C3%A4u" title="Würzburger Hofbräu">Würzburger Hofbräu</a> brewery also locally produces a well-known pilsner beer.</p>
<p>Würzburg is home of the oldest Pizzeria in Germany. Nick di Camillo opened his restaurant named <i>Bier- und Speisewirtschaft Capri</i> on 24 March 1952.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> Mr Camillo received the honor of the <a href="/wiki/Order_of_Merit_of_the_Italian_Republic" title="Order of Merit of the Italian Republic">Italian Order of Merit</a>.</p>
<p>In 2013, <a href="/wiki/Gross_domestic_product" title="Gross domestic product">GDP</a> per inhabitant was €52,290 in Würzburg, placing the city 16th out of 96 rural and urban districts in Bavaria (Bavarian average: €39,691).<sup id="cite_ref-Stat2_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Stat2-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Military">Military</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=W%C3%BCrzburg&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Military">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Following World War II, Würzburg was host to the U.S. Army's <a href="/wiki/U.S._1st_Infantry_Division" class="mw-redirect" title="U.S. 1st Infantry Division">1st</a> and <a href="/wiki/U.S._3d_Infantry_Division" class="mw-redirect" title="U.S. 3d Infantry Division">3rd</a> Infantry Divisions as well as an Army Hospital and various other U.S. military units that maintained a presence in Germany. The last troops were withdrawn from Würzburg in 2008, thus concluding more than 60 years of U.S. presence there.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Arts_and_culture">Arts and culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=W%C3%BCrzburg&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Arts and culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Notable artists who lived in Würzburg include poet <a href="/wiki/Walther_von_der_Vogelweide" title="Walther von der Vogelweide">Walther von der Vogelweide</a> (12th and 13th centuries), philosopher <a href="/wiki/Albertus_Magnus" title="Albertus Magnus">Albertus Magnus</a> and painter <a href="/wiki/Mathias_Gr%C3%BCnewald" class="mw-redirect" title="Mathias Grünewald">Mathias Grünewald</a>. Sculptor <a href="/wiki/Tilman_Riemenschneider" title="Tilman Riemenschneider">Tilman Riemenschneider</a> (1460–1531) served as mayor and participated in the <a href="/wiki/German_Peasants%27_War" title="German Peasants' War">German Peasants' War</a>.</p>
<p>Some of the city's "100 churches" survived intact. In style they range from <a href="/wiki/Romanesque_architecture" title="Romanesque architecture">Romanesque</a> (<a href="/wiki/W%C3%BCrzburg_Cathedral" title="Würzburg Cathedral">Würzburg Cathedral</a>), <a href="/wiki/Gothic_architecture" title="Gothic architecture">Gothic</a> (<i><a href="/wiki/Marienkapelle,_W%C3%BCrzburg" title="Marienkapelle, Würzburg">Marienkapelle</a></i>), <a href="/wiki/Renaissance_architecture" title="Renaissance architecture">Renaissance</a> (<i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Neubaukirche_(W%C3%BCrzburg)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Neubaukirche (Würzburg) (page does not exist)">Neubaukirche</a><span class="noprint" style="font-size:85%; font-style: normal;">&#160;(<a href="https://de.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neubaukirche_(W%C3%BCrzburg)" class="extiw" title="de:Neubaukirche (Würzburg)">de</a>)</span></i>), <a href="/wiki/Baroque_architecture" title="Baroque architecture">Baroque</a> (<i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kollegiatstift_Haug&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Kollegiatstift Haug (page does not exist)">Stift Haug Kirche</a><span class="noprint" style="font-size:85%; font-style: normal;">&#160;(<a href="https://de.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kollegiatstift_Haug" class="extiw" title="de:Kollegiatstift Haug">de</a>)</span></i>) to modern (St. Andreas).</p>
<p>Major festivals include the <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Africa_Festival&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Africa Festival (page does not exist)">Africa Festival</a></i> in May, the <i><a href="/wiki/Mozart_Festival_W%C3%BCrzburg" title="Mozart Festival Würzburg">Mozart Festival</a></i> in June/July and the <i>Kiliani Volksfest</i> in mid-July.</p>

<ul>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/W%C3%BCrzburg_Residence" title="Würzburg Residence">Würzburger Residenz</a></i>: A <a href="/wiki/UNESCO" title="UNESCO">UNESCO</a> <a href="/wiki/World_Heritage_Site" title="World Heritage Site">World Heritage Site</a>, the vast compound near the center of the town was commissioned by two prince-bishops, the brothers <a href="/wiki/Johann_Philipp_Franz_von_Sch%C3%B6nborn" title="Johann Philipp Franz von Schönborn">Johann Philipp Franz</a> and <a href="/wiki/Friedrich_Karl_von_Sch%C3%B6nborn" title="Friedrich Karl von Schönborn">Friedrich Karl von Schönborn</a>. Several architects, including <a href="/wiki/Johann_Lukas_von_Hildebrandt" title="Johann Lukas von Hildebrandt">Johann Lukas von Hildebrandt</a> and <a href="/wiki/Maximilian_von_Welsch" title="Maximilian von Welsch">Maximilian von Welsch</a>, supervised the construction between 1720 and 1744, but it is mainly associated with the name of <a href="/wiki/Balthasar_Neumann" title="Balthasar Neumann">Balthasar Neumann</a>, the creator of its famous Baroque staircase. The palace suffered severe damage in the British bombing of March 1945, but has been completely rebuilt. The main attractions are:
<ul>
<li><i>Hofkirche</i>: The church interior is richly decorated with paintings, sculptures and stucco ornaments. The altars were painted by <a href="/wiki/Giovanni_Battista_Tiepolo" title="Giovanni Battista Tiepolo">Giovanni Battista Tiepolo</a>.</li>
<li><i>Treppenhaus</i>: Here Giovanni Battista Tiepolo created the largest fresco in the world, which adorns the vault over the staircase designed by <a href="/wiki/Balthasar_Neumann" title="Balthasar Neumann">Balthasar Neumann</a>.</li>
<li><i>Kaisersaal</i>: The "Imperial Hall", the centerpiece of the palace, testifies to the close relationship between Würzburg and the <a href="/wiki/Holy_Roman_Empire" title="Holy Roman Empire">Holy Roman Empire</a>.</li>

<ul>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/Marienberg_Fortress" title="Marienberg Fortress">Festung Marienberg</a></i> is a fortress on <i>Marienberg</i>, the hill to the west of the town centre, overlooking the whole town area as well as the surrounding hills. Most current structures date to the Renaissance and Baroque periods, but the foundations of the chapel go back to the 8th century.</li>
<li><i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alte_Mainbr%C3%BCcke&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Alte Mainbrücke (page does not exist)">Alte Mainbrücke</a><span class="noprint" style="font-size:85%; font-style: normal;">&#160;(<a href="https://de.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alte_Mainbr%C3%BCcke" class="extiw" title="de:Alte Mainbrücke">de</a>)</span></i> (Old Main Bridge) was built 1473–1543 to replace the destroyed Romanesque bridge dated from 1133. In two phases, beginning in 1730, the bridge was adorned with twelve 4.5 meter statues of saints and historically important figures like <a href="/wiki/John_of_Nepomuk" title="John of Nepomuk">John of Nepomuk</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mary,_mother_of_Jesus" title="Mary, mother of Jesus">Mary</a> and <a href="/wiki/Saint_Joseph" title="Saint Joseph">Saint Joseph</a>, <a href="/wiki/Charlemagne" title="Charlemagne">Charlemagne</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pepin_the_Short" title="Pepin the Short">Pepin the Short</a>. The bridge was damaged by explosives in the final days of World War II. US troops threw the original Pepin into the river to make way for an anti-aircraft gun.<sup id="cite_ref-Dumont_6-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dumont-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:32</sup></li>
<li>The <i>Rathaus</i> or city hall of Würzburg differs from those of most <a href="/wiki/Imperial_Cities" class="mw-redirect" title="Imperial Cities">Imperial Cities</a> in that it was not a sumptuous edifice purpose-built in Renaissance style. Rather, the motley collection of buildings and wings reflects the fact that after 1400 the city was permanently under the control of the bishop who did not allow a representative new building. The <i>Rathaus</i> consists of parts dating from 1339 (chapel), 1453 (tower with the town's first public clock), 1544 (southwest oriel), 1659/60 (<i>Roter Bau</i>). In 1822 the three-winged structure of the neighbouring <i>Karmeliterkloster</i> (monastery of the <a href="/wiki/Carmelites" title="Carmelites">Carmelites</a>) was added to the city hall. The "Renaissance" row on Karmeliterstrasse was built only in 1898.<sup id="cite_ref-Dumont_6-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dumont-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:41</sup></li>
<li>Among Würzburg's many notable churches are the <i><a href="/wiki/K%C3%A4ppele" title="Käppele">Käppele</a></i>, a small Baroque/Rococo chapel by Balthasar Neumann, perched on a hill facing the fortress, and the <i>Dom</i>(<a href="/wiki/W%C3%BCrzburg_Cathedral" title="Würzburg Cathedral">Würzburg Cathedral</a>). The Baroque <i>Schönbornkapelle</i>, a side-chapel of the cathedral, has interior decoration of (artificial) human bones and skulls. Also in the cathedral are two of <a href="/wiki/Tilman_Riemenschneider" title="Tilman Riemenschneider">Tilman Riemenschneider</a>'s most famous works, the tomb stones of <a href="/wiki/Rudolf_von_Scherenberg" title="Rudolf von Scherenberg">Rudolf II von Scherenberg</a> (1466–1495) and <a href="/wiki/Lorenz_von_Bibra" title="Lorenz von Bibra">Lorenz von Bibra</a> (1495–1519). At the entrance to the <i>Marienkapelle</i> (on the market square) stand replicas of the statues of Adam and Eve by Riemenschneider. The <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kollegiatstift_Neum%C3%BCnster&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Kollegiatstift Neumünster (page does not exist)">Neumünster</a><span class="noprint" style="font-size:85%; font-style: normal;">&#160;(<a href="https://de.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kollegiatstift_Neum%C3%BCnster" class="extiw" title="de:Kollegiatstift Neumünster">de</a>)</span></i> is a Romanesque minster church with a Baroque façade and dome. Its crypt (<i>Kiliansgruft</i>) houses the relics of Kilian, Totnan and Kolonat. There are also two stone sarcophagi from the 8th century, the tombs of the first and second Bishop of Würzburg, Burkard and Megingaud. The latter's tomb features the oldest post-Roman <a href="/wiki/Monumental_inscription" title="Monumental inscription">monumental inscription</a> in Franconia.<sup id="cite_ref-Dumont_6-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dumont-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:45</sup> Next to the <i>Neumünster</i> is the <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lusamg%C3%A4rtchen&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Lusamgärtchen (page does not exist)">Lusamgärtchen</a><span class="noprint" style="font-size:85%; font-style: normal;">&#160;(<a href="https://de.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lusamg%C3%A4rtchen" class="extiw" title="de:Lusamgärtchen">de</a>)</span></i>. It contains a memorial from 1930 to <a href="/wiki/Walther_von_der_Vogelweide" title="Walther von der Vogelweide">Walther von der Vogelweide</a>, who very likely was buried here in 1230.<sup id="cite_ref-Dumont_6-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dumont-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:47</sup> Among the Baroque churches in the centre of the city are <i>Stift Haug</i>, <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=St._Michael_(W%C3%BCrzburg)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="St. Michael (Würzburg) (page does not exist)">St. Michael</a><span class="noprint" style="font-size:85%; font-style: normal;">&#160;(<a href="https://de.wikipedia.org/wiki/St._Michael_(W%C3%BCrzburg)" class="extiw" title="de:St. Michael (Würzburg)">de</a>)</span></i>, <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=St._Stephan_(W%C3%BCrzburg)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="St. Stephan (Würzburg) (page does not exist)">St. Stephan</a><span class="noprint" style="font-size:85%; font-style: normal;">&#160;(<a href="https://de.wikipedia.org/wiki/St._Stephan_(W%C3%BCrzburg)" class="extiw" title="de:St. Stephan (Würzburg)">de</a>)</span></i> and <i>St. Peter</i>.</li>
<li>The <i><a href="/wiki/Stiftung_Juliusspital_W%C3%BCrzburg" title="Stiftung Juliusspital Würzburg">Juliusspital</a></i> is a Baroque hospital with a courtyard and a church originally established by prince-bishop <a href="/wiki/Julius_Echter" class="mw-redirect" title="Julius Echter">Julius Echter</a> in 1576. The 160 m long northern wing was added by Italian architect <a href="/w/index.php?title=Antonio_Petrini&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Antonio Petrini (page does not exist)">Antonio Petrini</a><span class="noprint" style="font-size:85%; font-style: normal;">&#160;(<a href="https://it.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antonio_Petrini" class="extiw" title="it:Antonio Petrini">it</a>)</span> in 1700-4. Beneath it lies the similarly-sized wine cellar, which (together with those of the Würzburg Residence and the <i><a href="/wiki/B%C3%BCrgerspital_zum_Heiligen_Geist" title="Bürgerspital zum Heiligen Geist">Bürgerspital</a></i>) offers a chance to taste the local <i><a href="/wiki/Franconia_(wine_region)" title="Franconia (wine region)">Frankenwein</a></i> in a special environment. The Juliussital is the second largest winery in Germany, growing wine on 1.68 square kilometres (1 square mile).<sup id="cite_ref-Dumont_6-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dumont-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:58–9</sup></li>
<li>The <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Haus_zum_Falken&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Haus zum Falken (page does not exist)">Haus zum Falken</a><span class="noprint" style="font-size:85%; font-style: normal;">&#160;(<a href="https://de.wikipedia.org/wiki/Haus_zum_Falken" class="extiw" title="de:Haus zum Falken">de</a>)</span></i> on Marktplatz, next to the <i>Marienkapelle</i>, with its ornate stucco façade, is an achievement of the Würzburg Rococo period. In the past it served as an inn and a public library. Today, it houses the tourist information office.<sup id="cite_ref-Dumont_6-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dumont-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:62</sup></li>
<li>The <i>Stift Haug</i> (formally the <i>Stiftskirche St. Johannis im Haug</i>, dedicated to <a href="/wiki/John_the_Baptist" title="John the Baptist">John the Baptist</a> and <a href="/wiki/John_the_Evangelist" title="John the Evangelist">John the Evangelist</a>) was built in the years 1670–1691 as the first Baroque church in Franconia. It was designed by Antonio Petrini. The former church had been demolished as it was in the way of new city fortifications built by <a href="/wiki/Johann_Philipp_von_Sch%C3%B6nborn" title="Johann Philipp von Schönborn">Johann Philipp von Schönborn</a>. In 1945 most of the church's interior was destroyed. Works of art include a <a href="/wiki/Crucifixion_of_Jesus" title="Crucifixion of Jesus">crucifixion</a> by <a href="/wiki/Tintoretto" title="Tintoretto">Tintoretto</a> loaned by the <a href="/wiki/Bavarian_State_Painting_Collections" title="Bavarian State Painting Collections">Bavarian State Painting Collections</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Dumont_6-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dumont-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:59–60</sup></li>
<li>The <i><a href="/wiki/W%C3%BCrzburger_Stein" title="Würzburger Stein">Würzburger Stein</a></i> vineyard just outside the city is one of Germany's oldest and largest vineyards.</li>
</ul>

<ul>
<li>The <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mainfr%C3%A4nkisches_Museum,_W%C3%BCrzburg&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Mainfränkisches Museum, Würzburg (page does not exist)">Mainfränkisches Museum</a><span class="noprint" style="font-size:85%; font-style: normal;">&#160;(<a href="https://de.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mainfr%C3%A4nkisches_Museum" class="extiw" title="de:Mainfränkisches Museum">de</a>)</span></i> in the fortress is home to the world's largest collection of works by <a href="/wiki/Tilman_Riemenschneider" title="Tilman Riemenschneider">Tilman Riemenschneider</a>. In a space of 5,400&#160;m<sup>2</sup> (58,125&#160;sq&#160;ft), art by regional artists is exhibited. Exhibitions include a pre-historic collection, artifacts of the Franconian wine culture and an anthropological collection with traditional costumes.</li>
<li><i>Fürstenbaumuseum</i>: Also in the fortress, the restored <i>Fürstenbau</i> (former residence of the prince-bishops) houses not only the renovated living quarters, but also an exhibit on the history of Würzburg. Another exhibit features ecclesial gold jewelry and a collection of liturgical vestments. The museum also displays two models of the city: Würzburg in 1525 and Würzburg in 1945.</li>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/Museum_im_Kulturspeicher_W%C3%BCrzburg" title="Museum im Kulturspeicher Würzburg">Museum im Kulturspeicher</a></i>, housed in a historic grain storage building combined with modern architecture, has more than 3,500&#160;m² of exhibit space. Collections include the "Peter C. Ruppert Collection", with <a href="/wiki/Concrete_art" title="Concrete art">European Concrete art</a> after 1945 from artists such as <a href="/wiki/Max_Bill" title="Max Bill">Max Bill</a> and <a href="/wiki/Victor_Vasarely" title="Victor Vasarely">Victor Vasarely</a>; works from the <a href="/wiki/Age_of_Romanticism" class="mw-redirect" title="Age of Romanticism">Age of Romanticism</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Biedermeier" title="Biedermeier">Biedermeier period</a>, <a href="/wiki/Impressionism" title="Impressionism">Impressionism</a>, <a href="/wiki/Expressionism" title="Expressionism">Expressionism</a> as well as contemporary art.</li>
<li><i>Museum am Dom</i> (Museum at the Cathedral), opened in 2003. It features about 700 pieces of art spanning the past 1000 years. The 1,800 m² exhibit contrasts contemporary art with older works.</li>
<li><i>Shalom Europe</i>, a Jewish museum. Built around 1,504 tombstones discovered and excavated in the old city, the museum uses modern information technology to portray present and traditional Jewish lifestyles and their survival over the past 900 years in Würzburg.</li>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/Martin_von_Wagner_Museum" title="Martin von Wagner Museum">Martin von Wagner Museum</a></i>, with objects from ancient Egypt, Greece, and Rome. It is housed in the south wing of the Residence and displays ancient marble statues and burial objects. There are also ten exhibition halls with art from the 14th to the 19th centuries.</li>
<li><i>Siebold-Museum</i>, which houses permanent and temporary exhibits, including the estate of the 19th-century local physician and Japan researcher <a href="/wiki/Philipp_Franz_von_Siebold" title="Philipp Franz von Siebold">Philipp Franz von Siebold</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></li>
<li>The <i><a href="/wiki/R%C3%B6ntgen_Memorial_Site" title="Röntgen Memorial Site">Röntgen Memorial Site</a></i> in Würzburg, Germany is dedicated to the work of the German physicist Wilhelm Conrad Röntgen (1845–1923) and his discovery of X-rays, for which he was granted the Nobel Prize in physics. It contains an exhibition of historical instruments, machines and documents.</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sports">Sports</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=W%C3%BCrzburg&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Sports">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Basketball player <a href="/wiki/Dirk_Nowitzki" title="Dirk Nowitzki">Dirk Nowitzki</a> grew up in Würzburg. Nowitzki and numerous other German national team players started their careers at the local <a href="/wiki/Baskets_W%C3%BCrzburg" class="mw-redirect" title="Baskets Würzburg">Baskets Würzburg</a> club that plays in the <a href="/wiki/Basketball_Bundesliga" title="Basketball Bundesliga">Basketball Bundesliga</a> as of 2016<sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=W%C3%BCrzburg&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>. In the past, the club played at international competitions such as the <a href="/wiki/Eurocup_Basketball" class="mw-redirect" title="Eurocup Basketball">Eurocup</a>.</p>
<p>Würzburg is also home to the <a href="/wiki/Association_football" title="Association football">football</a> teams <i><a href="/wiki/W%C3%BCrzburger_Kickers" title="Würzburger Kickers">Würzburger Kickers</a></i> playing in the <i><a href="/wiki/3._Liga" title="3. Liga">3. Liga</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/W%C3%BCrzburger_FV" title="Würzburger FV">Würzburger FV</a></i> playing in the <i><a href="/wiki/Fu%C3%9Fball-Bayernliga" class="mw-redirect" title="Fußball-Bayernliga">Fußball-Bayernliga</a></i>.</p>
<p><i>SV Würzburg 05</i> is a swimming and water polo club, active in the <a href="/wiki/Deutsche_Wasserball-Liga" title="Deutsche Wasserball-Liga">German Water Polo League</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Governance">Governance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=W%C3%BCrzburg&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Governance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Würzburg is the administrative seat of the <i><a href="/wiki/Regierungsbezirk" title="Regierungsbezirk">Regierungsbezirk</a></i> <a href="/wiki/Lower_Franconia" title="Lower Franconia">Lower Franconia</a>. The administration of the <i>Landkreis Würzburg</i> (district) is also located in the town.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Education_and_research">Education and research</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=W%C3%BCrzburg&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Education and research">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Würzburg has several internationally recognized institutions in science and research:</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="University">University</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=W%C3%BCrzburg&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: University">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Wuerzburg_old_university_2001.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/59/Wuerzburg_old_university_2001.jpg/200px-Wuerzburg_old_university_2001.jpg" width="200" height="294" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/59/Wuerzburg_old_university_2001.jpg/300px-Wuerzburg_old_university_2001.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/59/Wuerzburg_old_university_2001.jpg/400px-Wuerzburg_old_university_2001.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1884" data-file-height="2766" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Wuerzburg_old_university_2001.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<i>Alte Universität</i>, the old <a href="/wiki/Renaissance_architecture" title="Renaissance architecture">Renaissance</a> building of Würzburg University</div>

<p>The <a href="/wiki/University_of_W%C3%BCrzburg" title="University of Würzburg">University of Würzburg</a> (official name <i>Julius-Maximilians-Universität Würzburg</i>) was founded in 1402 and is one of the oldest universities in Germany.</p>
<p>Academic disciplines are <a href="/wiki/Astronomy" title="Astronomy">astronomy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Biology" title="Biology">biology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Catholic_theology" title="Catholic theology">Catholic theology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chemistry" title="Chemistry">chemistry</a>, <a href="/wiki/Computer_science" title="Computer science">computer science</a>, <a href="/wiki/Culture" title="Culture">culture</a>, <a href="/wiki/Economics" title="Economics">economics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pedagogy" title="Pedagogy">educational</a> and <a href="/wiki/Social_sciences" class="mw-redirect" title="Social sciences">social sciences</a>, <a href="/wiki/Geography" title="Geography">geography</a>, <a href="/wiki/History" title="History">history</a>, <a href="/wiki/Languages" class="mw-redirect" title="Languages">languages</a> and <a href="/wiki/Linguistics" title="Linguistics">linguistics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Law" title="Law">law</a>, <a href="/wiki/Literature" title="Literature">literature</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mathematics" title="Mathematics">mathematics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Medicine" title="Medicine">medicine</a> (<a href="/wiki/Human_medicine" class="mw-redirect" title="Human medicine">human medicine</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dentistry" title="Dentistry">dentistry</a> and <a href="/wiki/Biomedicine" title="Biomedicine">biomedicine</a>), <a href="/wiki/Pharmacy" title="Pharmacy">pharmacy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Philosophy" title="Philosophy">philosophy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Physics" title="Physics">physics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Political_science" title="Political science">political science</a>, <a href="/wiki/Psychology" title="Psychology">psychology</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sociology" title="Sociology">sociology</a>.</p>
<p>Today, the ten faculties are spread throughout the city. The university currently enrolls approximately 29,000 students, out of which more than 1,000 come from other countries.</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Wilhelm_R%C3%B6ntgen" title="Wilhelm Röntgen">Wilhelm Röntgen</a>'s original laboratory, where he discovered <a href="/wiki/X-ray" title="X-ray">X-rays</a> in 1895, is at the University of Würzburg.</li>
<li>The University awarded <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Graham_Bell" title="Alexander Graham Bell">Alexander Graham Bell</a> an honorary <a href="/wiki/Ph.D" class="mw-redirect" title="Ph.D">Ph.D</a> for his pioneering scientific work.</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Botanischer_Garten_der_Universit%C3%A4t_W%C3%BCrzburg" title="Botanischer Garten der Universität Würzburg">Botanischer Garten der Universität Würzburg</a> is the university's <a href="/wiki/Botanical_garden" title="Botanical garden">botanical garden</a>.</li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="University_of_Applied_Science">University of Applied Science</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=W%C3%BCrzburg&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: University of Applied Science">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:138px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Fh_wuerzburg_hauptgebaeude.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/d/d7/Fh_wuerzburg_hauptgebaeude.jpg" width="136" height="102" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="136" data-file-height="102" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Fh_wuerzburg_hauptgebaeude.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/University_of_Applied_Sciences_W%C3%BCrzburg-Schweinfurt" title="University of Applied Sciences Würzburg-Schweinfurt">University of Applied Sciences Würzburg-Schweinfurt</a> main building in the city centre</div>

<p>The <a href="/wiki/University_of_Applied_Sciences_W%C3%BCrzburg-Schweinfurt" title="University of Applied Sciences Würzburg-Schweinfurt">University of Applied Sciences Würzburg-Schweinfurt</a> was founded in 1971 as an <a href="/wiki/Institute_of_technology" title="Institute of technology">institute of technology</a> with departments in Würzburg and <a href="/wiki/Schweinfurt" title="Schweinfurt">Schweinfurt</a>. Academic disciplines are <a href="/wiki/Architecture" title="Architecture">architecture</a>, <a href="/wiki/Business_economics" title="Business economics">business economics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Business_informatics" title="Business informatics">business informatics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Civil_engineering" title="Civil engineering">civil engineering</a>, <a href="/wiki/Computational_engineering" title="Computational engineering">computational engineering</a>, <a href="/wiki/Computer_science" title="Computer science">computer science</a>, <a href="/wiki/Electrical_engineering" title="Electrical engineering">electrical engineering</a>, <a href="/wiki/Engineering_management" title="Engineering management">engineering management</a>, <a href="/wiki/Geodesy" title="Geodesy">geodesy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Graphic_design" title="Graphic design">graphic design</a>, <a href="/wiki/Logistics" title="Logistics">logistics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mechanical_engineering" title="Mechanical engineering">mechanical engineering</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mass_media" title="Mass media">media</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nursing_theory" title="Nursing theory">nursing theory</a>, <a href="/wiki/Plastics_engineering" title="Plastics engineering">plastics engineering</a>, <a href="/wiki/Social_work" title="Social work">social work</a>.</p>
<p>With nearly 8,000 students it is the second largest university of applied science in Franconia.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Conservatory">Conservatory</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=W%C3%BCrzburg&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Conservatory">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Conservatory of Würzburg is an institution with a long tradition as well as an impressive success story of more than 200 years. It was founded in 1797 as <i>Collegium musicum academicum</i> and is Germany’s oldest conservatory. Nowadays it is known as <a href="/wiki/Hochschule_f%C3%BCr_Musik_W%C3%BCrzburg" title="Hochschule für Musik Würzburg">University of Music Würzburg</a>. After the commutation from conservatory to university of music in the early 1970s, science and research were added to complement music education.</p>

<p>The "Fraunhofer ISC" in Würzburg is part of the <a href="/wiki/Fraunhofer_Society" title="Fraunhofer Society">Fraunhofer Society</a>, Europe’s largest application-oriented research organization. <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Media">Media</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=W%C3%BCrzburg&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Media">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Transportation">Transportation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=W%C3%BCrzburg&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Transportation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Roads">Roads</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=W%C3%BCrzburg&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Roads">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The city is located near the intersection of the <a href="/wiki/Autobahn" title="Autobahn">Autobahns</a> <a href="/wiki/Bundesautobahn_3" title="Bundesautobahn 3">A 3</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bundesautobahn_7" title="Bundesautobahn 7">A 7</a>. <a href="/wiki/Bundesstra%C3%9Fe_8" title="Bundesstraße 8">Bundesstraße 8</a> passes through the town.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Rail">Rail</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=W%C3%BCrzburg&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Rail">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The city's <a href="/wiki/W%C3%BCrzburg_Hauptbahnhof" title="Würzburg Hauptbahnhof">main station</a> is at the southern end of the <a href="/wiki/Hanover-W%C3%BCrzburg_high-speed_rail_line" class="mw-redirect" title="Hanover-Würzburg high-speed rail line">Hanover-Würzburg high-speed rail line</a> and offers frequent <a href="/wiki/InterCityExpress" class="mw-redirect" title="InterCityExpress">InterCityExpress</a> and <a href="/wiki/InterCity" title="InterCity">InterCity</a> connections to cities such as <a href="/wiki/Frankfurt" title="Frankfurt">Frankfurt</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cologne" title="Cologne">Cologne</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nuremberg" title="Nuremberg">Nuremberg</a>, <a href="/wiki/Munich" title="Munich">Munich</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hanover" title="Hanover">Hanover</a> or <a href="/wiki/Hamburg" title="Hamburg">Hamburg</a>. It also is an important hub in the regional rail network.</p>

<p>The proposed Line 6 from Hauptbahnhof (Main Station) to Hubland university campus via Residenz is scheduled to be completed after 2018.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Buses">Buses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=W%C3%BCrzburg&amp;action=edit&amp;section=27" title="Edit section: Buses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>27 bus lines connect several parts of the city and the inner suburbs. 25 bus lines connect the <i><a href="/wiki/W%C3%BCrzburg_district" class="mw-redirect" title="Würzburg district">Landkreis Würzburg</a></i> to the city.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Port">Port</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=W%C3%BCrzburg&amp;action=edit&amp;section=28" title="Edit section: Port">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Main_(river)" title="Main (river)">Main</a> river flows into the <a href="/wiki/Rhine" title="Rhine">Rhine</a> and is connected to the <a href="/wiki/Danube" title="Danube">Danube</a> via the <a href="/wiki/Rhine-Main-Danube_Canal" class="mw-redirect" title="Rhine-Main-Danube Canal">Rhine-Main-Danube Canal</a>. This makes it part of a trans-European waterway connecting the <a href="/wiki/North_Sea" title="North Sea">North Sea</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Black_Sea" title="Black Sea">Black Sea</a>.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Bicycle">Bicycle</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=W%C3%BCrzburg&amp;action=edit&amp;section=29" title="Edit section: Bicycle">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Designated bicycle paths are located throughout the city and the <a href="/wiki/Main-Radweg" class="mw-redirect" title="Main-Radweg">Main-Radweg</a> long-distance bicycle trail passes through the old town.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Utilities">Utilities</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=W%C3%BCrzburg&amp;action=edit&amp;section=30" title="Edit section: Utilities">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The local public utility is <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=W%C3%BCrzburger_Versorgungs-_und_Verkehrs-GmbH&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Würzburger Versorgungs- und Verkehrs-GmbH (page does not exist)">Würzburger Versorgungs- und Verkehrs-GmbH</a><span class="noprint" style="font-size:85%; font-style: normal;">&#160;(<a href="https://de.wikipedia.org/wiki/W%C3%BCrzburger_Versorgungs-_und_Verkehrs-GmbH" class="extiw" title="de:Würzburger Versorgungs- und Verkehrs-GmbH">de</a>)</span></i> supplying power, natural gas and water as well as public transportation and parking services. It also owns a majority stake in the port and runs local garbage collection/recycling. <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Heizkraftwerk_W%C3%BCrzburg&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Heizkraftwerk Würzburg (page does not exist)">Heizkraftwerk Würzburg</a><span class="noprint" style="font-size:85%; font-style: normal;">&#160;(<a href="https://de.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heizkraftwerk_W%C3%BCrzburg" class="extiw" title="de:Heizkraftwerk Würzburg">de</a>)</span></i> is owned by the utility.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Health_care">Health care</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=W%C3%BCrzburg&amp;action=edit&amp;section=31" title="Edit section: Health care">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Universit%C3%A4tsklinikum_W%C3%BCrzburg&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Universitätsklinikum Würzburg (page does not exist)">Universitätsklinikum Würzburg</a><span class="noprint" style="font-size:85%; font-style: normal;">&#160;(<a href="https://de.wikipedia.org/wiki/Universit%C3%A4tsklinikum_W%C3%BCrzburg" class="extiw" title="de:Universitätsklinikum Würzburg">de</a>)</span></i> provides health care services, with over 5,300 employees and over 1,400 hospital beds. <i>Juliusspital</i> also offers hospital services with 342 beds.</p>
