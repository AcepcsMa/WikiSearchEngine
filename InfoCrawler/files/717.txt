<p><b>Siegfried Bettmann</b> was a bicycle, motorcycle and car manufacturer and initiator of the <a href="/wiki/Triumph_Engineering_Co_Ltd" class="mw-redirect" title="Triumph Engineering Co Ltd">Triumph Motorcycle Company</a>. In 1914 he established the Annie Bettmann Foundation to help young people start businesses<sup id="cite_ref-Gloria_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gloria-1">[1]</a></sup> Triumph became one of the most famous motorcycle trade-names of the world.<sup id="cite_ref-Triumph_over_adversity_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Triumph_over_adversity-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Siegfried_Bettmann&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Born in 1863 in <a href="/wiki/Nuremberg" title="Nuremberg">Nuremberg</a>, <a href="/wiki/German_Confederation" title="German Confederation">Germany</a>, Siegfried Bettmann moved to <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_of_Great_Britain_and_Ireland" title="United Kingdom of Great Britain and Ireland">Britain</a> in 1885,<sup id="cite_ref-Triumph_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Triumph-3">[3]</a></sup> and settled in <a href="/wiki/Coventry" title="Coventry">Coventry</a>, <a href="/wiki/Warwickshire" title="Warwickshire">Warwickshire</a>. He found work with Kelly &amp; Co. compiling foreign directories for their publications. After six months, he obtained a job with the White Sewing Machine Co. as a translator and worked as the company's sales representative in <a href="/wiki/Northern_Europe" title="Northern Europe">northern Europe</a>. Fluent in several languages, he perfected his English, married a local woman, Annie Meyrick (known as Millie).<sup id="cite_ref-Gloria_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gloria-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Business">Business</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Siegfried_Bettmann&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Business">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Bettmann founded S. Bettmann &amp; Co and started selling bicycles by the name 'Triumph' from premises in London. In 1886, Bettmann sought a more general name, and the company became known as the <a href="/wiki/Triumph_Cycle_Co._Ltd." class="mw-redirect" title="Triumph Cycle Co. Ltd.">Triumph Cycle Company</a>. A year later, the company registered as the New Triumph Co. Ltd., with funding from the <a href="/wiki/Dunlop_Pneumatic_Tyre_Company" class="mw-redirect" title="Dunlop Pneumatic Tyre Company">Dunlop Pneumatic Tyre Company</a>. In that year, Bettmann was joined by another Nuremberg native, an engineer named Moritz (Maurice) Schulte, as a partner. Schulte encouraged Bettmann to transform Triumph into a manufacturing company, and in 1888 Bettmann purchased a site in Coventry using money lent by his and Schulte's families. The company began producing the first Triumph-branded bicycles in 1889. In 1896, Triumph initiated a subsidiary, <a href="/wiki/Triumph_(TWN)" title="Triumph (TWN)">Orial TWN (Triumph Werke Nuremberg)</a> a <a href="https://de.wikipedia.org/wiki/Triumph_(D)" class="extiw" title="de:Triumph (D)">German subsidiary</a> for cycle production in his native city.</p>
<p>In 1902 the company diversified into making motorcycles at their works in Much Park Street. In 1902 the first Triumph motorcycle was developed. It was a strengthened bicycle with a 2.25&#160;bhp <a href="/wiki/Minerva" title="Minerva">Minerva</a> engine.<sup id="cite_ref-Triumph_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Triumph-3">[3]</a></sup> Once the business grew the purchased engines were replaced with their own and in 1907 the company expanded into a new factory in Priory Street, in premises of a former mill.</p>

<p>At the beginning of <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a> the War Office called a meeting of <a href="/wiki/Coventry" title="Coventry">Coventry</a> industrialists at St Mary's Hall and asked them to put their resources at the disposal of the military and two weeks after Britain had declared war on Germany, Bettmann received a telephone call from Capt. C. V. Holdsworth of the Army Service Corps, (who later became Triumph's managing director) with an order for a hundred Triumph motorcycles for the <a href="/wiki/British_Expeditionary_Force_(World_War_I)" title="British Expeditionary Force (World War I)">BEF</a> who were soon to go to France. Despite being a Saturday afternoon, Bettmann and his staff worked non-stop to crate the required motorcycles and by Sunday evening they were delivered to Coventry railway station in time for the evening train.<sup id="cite_ref-Gloria_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gloria-1">[1]</a></sup> The British Army subsequently placed large orders for the Triumph 550 cc Model H and by 1918 they were Britain's largest motorcycle manufacturer.</p>

<p>In 1921 Bettmann further diversified into car production and bought the Dawson Car Company to produce the Triumph 10/20. In 1927 he developed the Triumph Super 7 which sold well until 1934. In 1930 the company changed its name to the Triumph Motor Company and produced the Triumph Southern Cross and Gloria ranges. In 1936 the company had financial problems however and in 1936 the Triumph bicycle and motorcycle businesses were both sold. The motorcycle business was bought by <a href="/wiki/Jack_Sangster" title="Jack Sangster">Jack Sangster</a> of <a href="/wiki/Ariel_Motorcycles" title="Ariel Motorcycles">Ariel Motorcycles</a> to become <a href="/wiki/Triumph_Engineering_Co_Ltd" class="mw-redirect" title="Triumph Engineering Co Ltd">Triumph Engineering Co Ltd</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Later_life">Later life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Siegfried_Bettmann&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Later life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Siegfried Bettmann's success enabled him to become President of the Coventry <a href="/wiki/Liberal_Party_(UK)" title="Liberal Party (UK)">Liberal</a> Association. A <a href="/wiki/Freemason" class="mw-redirect" title="Freemason">Freemason</a> and founder member of Coventry's <a href="/wiki/Chamber_of_Commerce" class="mw-redirect" title="Chamber of Commerce">Chamber of Commerce</a>, he was also a <a href="/wiki/Justice_of_the_Peace" class="mw-redirect" title="Justice of the Peace">Justice of the Peace</a> and in 1913 became <a href="/wiki/Mayor_of_Coventry" class="mw-redirect" title="Mayor of Coventry">Mayor of Coventry</a>, the first non-British subject to have the position.<sup id="cite_ref-Gloria_1-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gloria-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>His German origins resulted in him being removed from this position on the beginning of the <a href="/wiki/First_World_War" class="mw-redirect" title="First World War">First World War</a>. <sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>. It is interesting to note that in a publication by the Coventry Synagogue he is listed one of "Coventry's famous Jews" though not attending the synagogue. Anti-semitic prejudice was not strong enough to affect his role as Lord Mayor but being German was!!<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>In 1914 Siegfried Bettmann founded the Annie Bettmann Foundation with his wife to help young Coventry men and women between the ages of eighteen and forty who wished to start a business. Preference was given to ex-servicemen and the fund, which is still in existence, was extended subsequently to include grants for further education.<sup id="cite_ref-Gloria_1-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gloria-1">[1]</a></sup> Bettmann also commissioned a memorial for the 66 employees of the Triumph company killed in action during the First World War, built in Coventry's London Road cemetery in 1921.<sup id="cite_ref-Gloria_1-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gloria-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Triumph Motorcycle Company became one of the most famous motorcycle marques of the world and Bettmann retained an association with the company until his death.<sup id="cite_ref-Triumph_over_adversity_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Triumph_over_adversity-2">[2]</a></sup> Siegfried Bettmann died on 23 September 1951 at his home "Elm Bank", Stoke Park, <a href="/wiki/Coventry" title="Coventry">Coventry</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>. In 2015 the Coventry Society installed a <a href="/wiki/Blue_Plaque" class="mw-redirect" title="Blue Plaque">Blue Plaque</a> onto this residence.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
