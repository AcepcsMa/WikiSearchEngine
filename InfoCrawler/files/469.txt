<p>Upon the defeat of <a href="/wiki/Nazi_Germany" title="Nazi Germany">Nazi Germany</a> in <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>, the victorious <a href="/wiki/Allies_of_World_War_II" title="Allies of World War II">Allied powers</a> asserted their joint authority and sovereignty over 'Germany as a whole', defined as all territories of the former <a href="/wiki/German_Reich" title="German Reich">German Reich</a> which lay west of the <a href="/wiki/Oder%E2%80%93Neisse_line" title="Oder–Neisse line">Oder–Neisse line</a>; having declared the extinction of <a href="/wiki/Nazi_Germany" title="Nazi Germany">Nazi Germany</a> at the death of <a href="/wiki/Adolf_Hitler" title="Adolf Hitler">Adolf Hitler</a> (see <a href="/wiki/Berlin_Declaration_(1945)" title="Berlin Declaration (1945)">1945 Berlin Declaration</a>). The four powers divided 'Germany as a whole' into four <b>occupation zones</b> for administrative purposes, creating what became collectively known as <b>Allied-occupied Germany</b> (<a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a>: <span lang="de" xml:lang="de"><i>Alliierten-besetztes Deutschland</i></span>). This division was ratified at the <a href="/wiki/Potsdam_Conference" title="Potsdam Conference">Potsdam Conference</a> (17 July to 2 August 1945).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources in the body of the article. (July 2013)">not verified in body</span></a></i>]</sup> In autumn 1944 the United States, United Kingdom and <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet Union</a> had agreed on the zones by the <a href="/wiki/London_Protocol_(1944)" title="London Protocol (1944)">London Protocol</a>. The powers at Potsdam approved the detachment from 'Germany as a whole' of the German eastern territories east of the Oder-Neisse line; with the exact line of the boundary to be determined at a final German Peace Treaty. This treaty was expected to confirm the "shifting westward" of Poland's borders (back to approximately as they were before 1722), as the United Kingdom and the United States committed themselves to support there the permanent incorporation of former eastern German territories into Poland and the Soviet Union. In the closing weeks of fighting in Europe, United States forces had pushed beyond the agreed boundaries for the future zones of occupation, in some places by as much as 320 kilometres (200&#160;mi). The so-called <a href="/wiki/Line_of_contact" title="Line of contact">line of contact</a> between Soviet and <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">American</a> forces at the end of hostilities, mostly lying eastward of the July 1945-established <a href="/wiki/Inner_German_border" title="Inner German border">inner German border</a> was temporary. After two months in which they had held areas that had been assigned to the Soviet zone, U.S. forces withdrew in the first days of July 1945.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Some have concluded that this was a crucial move that persuaded the Soviet Union to allow American, British and French forces into their designated sectors in Berlin, which occurred at roughly the same time (July 1945), although the need for intelligence gathering (see <a href="/wiki/Operation_Paperclip" title="Operation Paperclip">Operation Paperclip</a>) may also have been a factor.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span id="Territories_annexed_by_Germany_1938–1945"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Territories_annexed_by_Germany_1938.E2.80.931945">Territories annexed by Germany 1938–1945</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Allied-occupied_Germany&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Territories annexed by Germany 1938–1945">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Areas_annexed_by_Nazi_Germany" title="Areas annexed by Nazi Germany">All territories annexed by Germany</a> before the war from <a href="/wiki/Federal_State_of_Austria" title="Federal State of Austria">Austria</a> and <a href="/wiki/First_Czechoslovak_Republic" title="First Czechoslovak Republic">Czechoslovakia</a> were returned to these countries. The <a href="/wiki/Memel_Territory" class="mw-redirect" title="Memel Territory">Memel Territory</a>, annexed by Germany from <a href="/wiki/Lithuania" title="Lithuania">Lithuania</a> before the war, was annexed by the Soviet Union in 1945 and transferred to the <a href="/wiki/Lithuanian_Soviet_Socialist_Republic" title="Lithuanian Soviet Socialist Republic">Lithuanian SSR</a>. All territories annexed by Germany during the war from <a href="/wiki/Belgium" title="Belgium">Belgium</a>, <a href="/wiki/French_Third_Republic" title="French Third Republic">France</a>, <a href="/wiki/Luxembourg" title="Luxembourg">Luxembourg</a>, <a href="/wiki/Second_Polish_Republic" title="Second Polish Republic">Poland</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Yugoslavia" title="Kingdom of Yugoslavia">Yugoslavia</a> were returned to their respective countries.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="American_Zone_of_Occupation">American Zone of Occupation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Allied-occupied_Germany&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: American Zone of Occupation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">"American occupation zone" redirects here. For the zone in Austria, see <a href="/wiki/Allied-occupied_Austria" title="Allied-occupied Austria">Allied-occupied Austria</a>.</div>
<p>The American zone consisted of <a href="/wiki/Bavaria" title="Bavaria">Bavaria</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hesse" title="Hesse">Hesse</a> in <a href="/wiki/Southern_Germany" title="Southern Germany">Southern Germany</a>, and the northern portions of the present-day German state of <a href="/wiki/Baden-W%C3%BCrttemberg" title="Baden-Württemberg">Baden-Württemberg</a>. The ports of <a href="/wiki/Bremen" title="Bremen">Bremen</a> (on the lower <a href="/wiki/Weser_River" class="mw-redirect" title="Weser River">Weser River</a>) and <a href="/wiki/Bremerhaven" title="Bremerhaven">Bremerhaven</a> (at the Weser estuary of the <a href="/wiki/North_Sea" title="North Sea">North Sea</a>) were also placed under American control because of the American request to have certain toeholds in <a href="/wiki/Northern_Germany" title="Northern Germany">Northern Germany</a>. The headquarters of the <a href="/wiki/Office_of_Military_Government,_United_States" title="Office of Military Government, United States">American military government</a> was the former <a href="/wiki/IG_Farben_Building" title="IG Farben Building">IG Farben Building</a> in <a href="/wiki/Frankfurt_am_Main" class="mw-redirect" title="Frankfurt am Main">Frankfurt am Main</a>.</p>
<p>Beginning in May 1945, many of the American combat troops and airmen in and around Germany were sent back to the United States based on their <a href="/wiki/Advanced_Service_Rating_Score" title="Advanced Service Rating Score">Advanced Service Rating Scores</a>. Some of the experienced officers and <a href="/wiki/Non-commissioned_officer" title="Non-commissioned officer">non-commissioned officers</a> were selected to be sent to the <a href="/wiki/Asiatic-Pacific_Theater" title="Asiatic-Pacific Theater">Pacific Theater of Operations</a> for the proposed <a href="/wiki/Invasion_of_Japan" class="mw-redirect" title="Invasion of Japan">Invasion of Japan</a>, but most of those men who had served the longest in combat were discharged from the <a href="/wiki/U.S._Army" class="mw-redirect" title="U.S. Army">U.S. Army</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Army_Air_Forces" class="mw-redirect" title="Army Air Forces">Army Air Forces</a> and the <a href="/wiki/U.S._Navy" class="mw-redirect" title="U.S. Navy">U.S. Navy</a> upon their return home. Following the surrender of the <a href="/wiki/Japanese_Empire" class="mw-redirect" title="Japanese Empire">Japanese Empire</a> in mid-August 1945 – by its acceptance of the <a href="/wiki/Potsdam_Declaration" title="Potsdam Declaration">Potsdam Declaration</a> – a higher percentage of soldiers, airmen and sailors were granted their final discharges from service. The signing of the <a href="/wiki/Surrender_of_Japan" title="Surrender of Japan">surrender of Japan</a> took place on 2 September 1945 officially ending hostilities in World War II in the Pacific, but active combat within the <a href="/wiki/Pacific_War" title="Pacific War">Pacific theater</a> had ended weeks earlier.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="British_Zone_of_Occupation">British Zone of Occupation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Allied-occupied_Germany&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: British Zone of Occupation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Road_sign_delimiting_British_zone_of_occupation_in_Berlin_1984.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e6/Road_sign_delimiting_British_zone_of_occupation_in_Berlin_1984.jpg/220px-Road_sign_delimiting_British_zone_of_occupation_in_Berlin_1984.jpg" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e6/Road_sign_delimiting_British_zone_of_occupation_in_Berlin_1984.jpg/330px-Road_sign_delimiting_British_zone_of_occupation_in_Berlin_1984.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e6/Road_sign_delimiting_British_zone_of_occupation_in_Berlin_1984.jpg/440px-Road_sign_delimiting_British_zone_of_occupation_in_Berlin_1984.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2048" data-file-height="1536" /></a>

<p>The <a href="/wiki/Canadian_Army" title="Canadian Army">Canadian Army</a> was tied down in surrounding the <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Netherlands</a> until the Germans there surrendered on 5 May 1945 – just two days before the final surrender of the <a href="/wiki/Wehrmacht" title="Wehrmacht">Wehrmacht</a> in Western Europe to U.S. General <a href="/wiki/Dwight_D._Eisenhower" title="Dwight D. Eisenhower">Dwight D. Eisenhower</a>. After the liberation of the Netherlands and the conquest of Northern Germany by the <a href="/wiki/British_Army" title="British Army">British Army</a>, the bulk of the Canadian Army returned home, leaving Northern Germany to be occupied by the British Army and (around <a href="/wiki/Bremen" title="Bremen">Bremen</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bremerhaven" title="Bremerhaven">Bremerhaven</a>) by the <a href="/wiki/U.S._Army" class="mw-redirect" title="U.S. Army">U.S. Army</a>.</p>
<p>Then in July 1945, the British Army withdrew from small slices of Germany that had previously been agreed to be occupied by the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Army" title="Soviet Army">Soviet Army</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Control_Commission_for_Germany_-_British_Element" class="mw-redirect" title="Control Commission for Germany - British Element">Control Commission for Germany - British Element</a> (CCG/BE) ceded some slices of its area of occupation to the Soviet Union – specifically the <a href="/wiki/Amt_Neuhaus" title="Amt Neuhaus">Amt Neuhaus</a> of <a href="/wiki/Province_of_Hanover" title="Province of Hanover">Hanover</a> and some exclaves and fringes of <a href="/wiki/Free_State_of_Brunswick" title="Free State of Brunswick">Brunswick</a>, for example, the <a href="/wiki/County_of_Blankenburg" title="County of Blankenburg">County of Blankenburg</a> and exchanged some villages between British Holstein and Soviet Mecklenburg by the <a href="/wiki/Barber-Lyashchenko_Agreement" class="mw-redirect" title="Barber-Lyashchenko Agreement">Barber-Lyashchenko Agreement</a>.</p>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p>Within the British Zone of Occupation, the CCG/BE re-established the German state of <a href="/wiki/Hamburg" title="Hamburg">Hamburg</a>, but with borders that had been drawn by Nazi Germany in 1937. The British also created the new German states of <a href="/wiki/Schleswig-Holstein" title="Schleswig-Holstein">Schleswig-Holstein</a> – emerging in 1946 from the <a href="/wiki/Prussian" class="mw-redirect" title="Prussian">Prussian</a> <a href="/wiki/Province_of_Schleswig-Holstein" title="Province of Schleswig-Holstein">Province of Schleswig-Holstein</a>; <a href="/wiki/Lower_Saxony" title="Lower Saxony">Lower Saxony</a> – the merger of Brunswick, <a href="/wiki/Free_State_of_Oldenburg" title="Free State of Oldenburg">Oldenburg</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Free_State_of_Schaumburg-Lippe" title="Free State of Schaumburg-Lippe">Schaumburg-Lippe</a> with the <a href="/wiki/State_of_Hanover" title="State of Hanover">state of Hanover</a> in 1946; and <a href="/wiki/North_Rhine-Westphalia" title="North Rhine-Westphalia">North Rhine-Westphalia</a> – the merger of <a href="/wiki/Free_State_of_Lippe" title="Free State of Lippe">Lippe</a> with the Prussian provinces of the <a href="/wiki/Rhine_Province" title="Rhine Province">Rhineland</a> (northern part) and <a href="/wiki/Province_of_Westphalia" title="Province of Westphalia">Westphalia</a> – during 1946–47. Also in 1947, the German state <a href="/wiki/Bremen_(state)" title="Bremen (state)">Free Hanseatic City of Bremen</a> became an exclave of the American Zone of Occupation located within the British Zone.</p>
<p>In 1946, the <a href="/wiki/Independent_Norwegian_Brigade_Group_in_Germany" title="Independent Norwegian Brigade Group in Germany">Norwegian Brigade Group in Germany</a> had 4,000 soldiers in Hanover.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="French_Zone_of_Occupation">French Zone of Occupation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Allied-occupied_Germany&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: French Zone of Occupation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">"French occupation zone" redirects here. For the zone in Austria, see <a href="/wiki/Allied-occupied_Austria" title="Allied-occupied Austria">Allied-occupied Austria</a>.</div>

<p>Despite its being one of the Allied Powers, the <a href="/wiki/French_Republic" class="mw-redirect" title="French Republic">French Republic</a> was at first not granted an occupation zone in Germany. Later, however, the British and American governments recognised the role of France during World War II in Europe, and agreed to cede some western parts of their zones of occupation to the <a href="/wiki/French_Army" title="French Army">French Army</a>. This created a French zone of occupation in the westernmost part of Germany. It consisted of two barely contiguous areas of Germany along the French border that met at just a single point along the River <a href="/wiki/Rhine" title="Rhine">Rhine</a>. It included the <a href="/wiki/Saargebiet" class="mw-redirect" title="Saargebiet">Saargebiet</a>, which was disentangled from it on 16 February 1946. By 18 December 1946 customs controls were established between the Saar area and allied occupied Germany. The French zone ceded further adjacent municipalities to the Saar (in mid-1946, early 1947, and early 1949).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Included in the French zone was the town of <a href="/wiki/B%C3%BCsingen_am_Hochrhein" title="Büsingen am Hochrhein">Büsingen am Hochrhein</a>, a German exclave separated from the rest of the country by a narrow strip of neutral <a href="/wiki/Switzerland" title="Switzerland">Swiss</a> territory. The Swiss government agreed to allow limited numbers of French troops to pass through its territory in order to maintain law and order in Büsingen.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Minor_zones">Minor zones</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Allied-occupied_Germany&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Minor zones">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Belgian_zone">Belgian zone</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Allied-occupied_Germany&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Belgian zone">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>At the end of the Second World War, 75,000 Belgian soldiers were serving in the Allied armies in Germany, mostly within <a href="/wiki/Fusilier_Battalions_(Belgium)" title="Fusilier Battalions (Belgium)">military units raised after Liberation of Belgium</a> in September 1944.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>As only the Americans, Soviets, British and French were considered official occupying powers under the terms of the Potsdam Conference, the Belgian government was unable to form a military government of its own in Germany. It was, however, allocated a territory within the British zone which was garrisoned by Belgian troops.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> The zone formed a 200 kilometres (120&#160;mi) strip from the Belgian-German border at the south of the British zone, and included the important cities of <a href="/wiki/Cologne" title="Cologne">Cologne</a> and <a href="/wiki/Aachen" title="Aachen">Aachen</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Belgian_Forces_in_Germany" title="Belgian Forces in Germany">Belgian army of occupation in Germany</a> (known as the Belgian Forces in Germany from 1951) became autonomous in 1946 under the command, initially, of <a href="/wiki/Jean-Baptiste_Piron" title="Jean-Baptiste Piron">Jean-Baptiste Piron</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Belgian soldiers would remain in Germany until 31 December 2005.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Luxembourg_zone">Luxembourg zone</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Allied-occupied_Germany&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Luxembourg zone">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>From November 1945, <a href="/wiki/Luxembourg" title="Luxembourg">Luxembourg</a> was allocated a zone within the French sector.<sup id="cite_ref-Luxembourg1_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Luxembourg1-7">[7]</a></sup> The Luxembourg <a href="/wiki/Luxembourg_Army" title="Luxembourg Army">2nd Infantry Battalion</a> was garrisoned in <a href="/wiki/Bitburg" title="Bitburg">Bitburg</a> and the 1st Battalion was sent to <a href="/wiki/Saarburg" title="Saarburg">Saarburg</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Luxembourg1_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Luxembourg1-7">[7]</a></sup> The final Luxembourg forces in Germany, in Bitburg, left in 1955.<sup id="cite_ref-Luxembourg1_7-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Luxembourg1-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Berlin">Berlin</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Allied-occupied_Germany&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Berlin">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/History_of_Berlin#The_divided_city" title="History of Berlin">History of Berlin §&#160;The divided city</a></div>
<p>While located wholly within the Soviet zone, because of its symbolic importance as the nation's capital and seat of the former Nazi government, the city of Berlin was jointly occupied by the Allied powers and subdivided into four sectors. Berlin was not considered to be part of the Soviet zone.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_German_territory">Other German territory</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Allied-occupied_Germany&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Other German territory">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1945 <a href="/wiki/Historical_eastern_Germany" class="mw-redirect" title="Historical eastern Germany">Germany east</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Oder-Neisse_line" class="mw-redirect" title="Oder-Neisse line">Oder-Neisse line</a> (<a href="/wiki/Farther_Pomerania" title="Farther Pomerania">Farther Pomerania</a>, the <a href="/wiki/New_March" class="mw-redirect" title="New March">New March</a>, <a href="/wiki/Silesia" title="Silesia">Silesia</a> and southerly <a href="/wiki/East_Prussia" title="East Prussia">East Prussia</a>) was assigned to <a href="/wiki/Poland" title="Poland">Poland</a> by the Potsdam Conference to be "Temporarily Administered" pending the Final Peace Treaty on Germany; eventually (per the September 1990 2+4 Peace Treaty) the northern portion of East Prussia became the <a href="/wiki/Kaliningrad_Oblast" title="Kaliningrad Oblast">Kaliningrad Oblast</a> within the Soviet Union. A small area west of the Oder, near <a href="/wiki/Szczecin" title="Szczecin">Szczecin</a>, also fell to Poland. Most German citizens residing in these areas were subsequently expropriated and expelled. Returning refugees, who had fled from war hostilities, were denied return.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Saargebiet" class="mw-redirect" title="Saargebiet">Saargebiet</a>, an important area of Germany because of its large deposits of coal, was turned into the <a href="/wiki/Saar_(protectorate)" class="mw-redirect" title="Saar (protectorate)">Saar protectorate</a>. The Saar was disengaged from the French zone on 16 February 1946. In the speech <a href="/wiki/Restatement_of_Policy_on_Germany" title="Restatement of Policy on Germany">Restatement of Policy on Germany</a> on 6 September 1946 the U.S. Secretary of State <a href="/wiki/James_F._Byrnes" title="James F. Byrnes">James F. Byrnes</a> stated the U.S.' motive in detaching the Saar from Germany as "The United States does not feel that it can deny to France, which has been invaded three times by Germany in 70 years, its claim to the Saar territory."</p>
<p>By 18 December 1946 customs controls were established between the Saar and Allied occupied Germany. Most German citizens residing in the Saar area were allowed to stay and keep their property. Returning refugees, who had fled from war hostilities, were allowed to return, especially refugees who had fled the <a href="/wiki/Nazi_dictatorship" class="mw-redirect" title="Nazi dictatorship">Nazi dictatorship</a> were invited and welcomed to return to the Saar.</p>
<p>The protectorate was a state nominally independent of Germany and France, but with its economy integrated into that of France. The Saar territory was enlarged on the expense of the French zone in mid-1946, early 1947 (when 61 municipalities were returned to the French zone), and in early 1949. On 15 November 1947 the French currency became legal tender in the Saar Protectorate, followed by the full integration of the Saar into the French economy (customs union as of 23 March 1948). In July the Saar population was stripped of its German citizenship and became of Sarrois nationality.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Governance_and_the_emergence_of_two_German_states">Governance and the emergence of two German states</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Allied-occupied_Germany&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Governance and the emergence of two German states">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The original Allied plan to govern Germany as a single unit through the <a href="/wiki/Allied_Control_Council" title="Allied Control Council">Allied Control Council</a> broke down in 1946–1947 due to growing tensions between the Allies, with Britain and the US wishing cooperation, France obstructing any collaboration in order to unwind Germany into many independent states, and the Soviet Union unilaterally implementing from early on elements of its political-economic system (mass expropriations of land, nationalisation of businesses). Another dispute was the absorption of post-war expellees. While the UK, the US and the Soviet Union had agreed to accept, house and feed about six million expelled German citizens from former eastern Germany and four million <a href="/wiki/Expulsion_of_Germans_from_Czechoslovakia" title="Expulsion of Germans from Czechoslovakia">expelled and denaturalised Czechoslovaks</a>, Poles, Hungarians and Yugoslavs of German ethnicity in their zones, France generally had not agreed to the expulsions approved by the Potsdam agreement (a decision made without input from France). Therefore, France strictly refused to absorb war refugees who were denied return to their homes in seized eastern German territories or destitute post-war expellees who had been expropriated there, into the French zone, let alone into the separated Saar protectorate.<sup id="cite_ref-Vor_50_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Vor_50-8">[8]</a></sup> However, the native population, returning after Nazi-imposed removals (e.g., political and Jewish refugees) and war-related relocations (e.g., evacuation from air raids), were allowed to return home in the areas under French control. The other Allies complained that they had to shoulder the burden to feed, house and clothe the expellees who had to leave their belongings behind.</p>
<p>In practice, each of the four occupying powers wielded government authority in their respective zones and carried out different policies toward the population and local and state governments there. A uniform administration of the western zones evolved, known first as the <a href="/wiki/Bizone" title="Bizone">Bizone</a> (the American and British zones merged as of 1 January 1947) and later the Trizone (after inclusion of the French zone). The complete breakdown of east-west allied cooperation and joint administration in Germany became clear with the Soviet imposition of the <a href="/wiki/Berlin_Blockade" title="Berlin Blockade">Berlin Blockade</a> that was enforced from June 1948 to May 1949. The three western zones were merged to form the <a href="/wiki/West_Germany" title="West Germany">Federal Republic of Germany</a> in May 1949, and the Soviets followed suit in October 1949 with the establishment of the <a href="/wiki/German_Democratic_Republic" class="mw-redirect" title="German Democratic Republic">German Democratic Republic</a> (GDR).</p>
<p>In the west, the occupation continued until 5 May 1955, when the <a href="/wiki/General_Treaty" title="General Treaty">General Treaty</a> (German: <span lang="de" xml:lang="de"><i>Deutschlandvertrag</i></span>) entered into force. However, upon the creation of the Federal Republic in May 1949, the military governors were replaced by civilian <a href="/wiki/High_commissioner" title="High commissioner">high commissioners</a>, whose powers lay somewhere between those of a governor and those of an ambassador. When the <i>Deutschlandvertrag</i> became law, the occupation ended, the western occupation zones ceased to exist, and the high commissioners were replaced by normal ambassadors. West Germany was also allowed to build a military, and the <a href="/wiki/Bundeswehr" title="Bundeswehr">Bundeswehr</a>, or Federal Defense Force, was established on 12 November 1955.</p>
<p>A similar situation occurred in East Germany. The GDR was founded on 7 October 1949. On 10 October the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Military_Administration_in_Germany" title="Soviet Military Administration in Germany">Soviet Military Administration in Germany</a> was replaced by the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Control_Commission" class="mw-redirect" title="Soviet Control Commission">Soviet Control Commission</a>, although limited sovereignty was not granted to the GDR government until 11 November 1949. After the death of <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Stalin" title="Joseph Stalin">Joseph Stalin</a> in March 1953, the Soviet Control Commission was replaced with the office of the Soviet High Commissioner on 28 May 1953. This office was abolished (and replaced by an ambassador) and (general) sovereignty was granted to the GDR, when the Soviet Union concluded a state treaty <i>(Staatsvertrag)</i> with the GDR on 20 September 1955. On 1 March 1956, the GDR established a military, the <a href="/wiki/National_People%27s_Army" title="National People's Army">National People's Army</a> (NVA).</p>
<p>Despite the grants of general sovereignty to both German states in 1955, full and unrestricted sovereignty under international law was not enjoyed by any German government until after the <a href="/wiki/Reunification_of_Germany" class="mw-redirect" title="Reunification of Germany">reunification of Germany</a> in October 1990. Though West Germany was generally independent, the Allies maintained some responsibilities for West Germany. At the same time, East Germany was a <a href="/wiki/Satellite_state" title="Satellite state">satellite state</a> of the Soviet Union. The provisions of the <a href="/wiki/Treaty_on_the_Final_Settlement_with_Respect_to_Germany" title="Treaty on the Final Settlement with Respect to Germany">Treaty on the Final Settlement with Respect to Germany</a>, also known as the "Two-plus-Four Treaty," granting full sovereign powers to Germany did not become law until 15 March 1991, after all of the participating nations had ratified the treaty. As envisaged by the Treaty, the last Occupation troops departed from Germany when the Russian presence was terminated in 1994.</p>
<p>A 1956 plebiscite ended the French administration of the Saar protectorate, and it joined the Federal Republic as <i><a href="/wiki/Saarland" title="Saarland">Saarland</a></i> on 1 January 1957, being its 10th state.</p>
<p>The city of Berlin was not part of either state and continued to be under Allied occupation until the reunification of Germany in October 1990. For administrative purposes, the three western sectors of Berlin were merged into the entity of <a href="/wiki/West_Berlin" title="West Berlin">West Berlin</a>. The Soviet sector became known as <a href="/wiki/East_Berlin" title="East Berlin">East Berlin</a> and while not recognised by the Western powers as a part of East Germany, the GDR declared it its capital <i>(Hauptstadt der DDR)</i>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Occupation_policy">Occupation policy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Allied-occupied_Germany&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Occupation policy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Denazification" title="Denazification">Denazification</a>, <a href="/wiki/Industrial_plans_for_Germany" class="mw-redirect" title="Industrial plans for Germany">Industrial plans for Germany</a>, <a href="/wiki/American_food_policy_in_occupied_Germany" class="mw-redirect" title="American food policy in occupied Germany">American food policy in occupied Germany</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Forced_labor_of_Germans_after_World_War_II" title="Forced labor of Germans after World War II">Forced labor of Germans after World War II</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Buchenwald_Samuelson_62779.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d7/Buchenwald_Samuelson_62779.jpg/220px-Buchenwald_Samuelson_62779.jpg" width="220" height="271" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d7/Buchenwald_Samuelson_62779.jpg/330px-Buchenwald_Samuelson_62779.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/d/d7/Buchenwald_Samuelson_62779.jpg 2x" data-file-width="376" data-file-height="463" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Buchenwald_Samuelson_62779.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
American propaganda poster, using images of concentration camp victims to warn against "<a href="/wiki/Fraternization" title="Fraternization">fraternization</a>"</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>At the end of the war, General Eisenhower issued a non-<a href="/wiki/Fraternization" title="Fraternization">fraternization</a> policy to troops under his command in occupied Germany. This policy was relaxed in stages. By June 1945 the prohibition on speaking with German children was made less strict. In July it became possible to speak to German adults in certain circumstances. In September the policy was completely dropped in Austria and Germany.</p>
<p>Nevertheless, due to the large numbers of <a href="/wiki/Disarmed_Enemy_Forces" title="Disarmed Enemy Forces">Disarmed Enemy Forces</a> being held in <i><a href="/wiki/Rheinwiesenlager" title="Rheinwiesenlager">Rheinwiesenlagers</a></i> throughout western Germany, the Americans and the British – not the Soviets – used armed units of <i><a href="/wiki/Feldgendarmerie" title="Feldgendarmerie">Feldgendarmerie</a></i> to maintain control and discipline in the camps. In June 1946, these German military police units became the last <i><a href="/wiki/Wehrmacht" title="Wehrmacht">Wehrmacht</a></i> troops to surrender their arms to the western powers.</p>
<p>By December 1945 over 100,000 <a href="/wiki/Civilian_Internee#Internment_of_German_Civilians_by_Allies_in_Occupied_Germany_after_WWII" class="mw-redirect" title="Civilian Internee">German civilians were interned</a> as security threats and for possible trial and sentencing as members of criminal organisations.</p>
<p>The food situation in occupied Germany was initially very dire. By the spring of 1946 the official ration in the American zone was no more than 1,275 calories (5,330&#160;kJ) per day, with some areas probably receiving as little as 700 calories (2,900&#160;kJ) per day. In the British zone the food situation was dire, as found during a visit by the British (and Jewish) publisher <a href="/wiki/Victor_Gollancz" title="Victor Gollancz">Victor Gollancz</a> in October and November 1946. In <a href="/wiki/D%C3%BCsseldorf" title="Düsseldorf">Düsseldorf</a> the normal 28-day allocation should have been 1,548 calories (6,480&#160;kJ) including 10 kilograms (22&#160;lb) of bread, but as there was limited grain the bread ration was only 8.5 kilograms (19&#160;lb). However, as there was only sufficient bread for about 50% of this "called up" ration, the total deficiency was about 50%, not 15% as stated in a ministerial reply in the British Parliament on 11 December. So only about 770 calories (3,200&#160;kJ) would have been supplied, and he said the German winter ration would be 1,000 calories (4,200&#160;kJ) as the recent increase was "largely mythical". His book includes photos taken on the visit and critical letters and newspaper articles by him published in several British newspapers; <i>The Times, the Daily Herald, the Manchester Guardian</i>, etc.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>Some occupation soldiers took advantage of the desperate food situation by exploiting their ample supply of food and cigarettes (the currency of the black market) to get to the local German girls as what became known as <i>frau bait</i> (<i><a href="/wiki/The_New_York_Times" title="The New York Times">The New York Times</a></i>, 25 June 1945). Some soldiers still felt the girls were the enemy, but used them for sex nevertheless.<sup id="cite_ref-Biddiscombe_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Biddiscombe-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>The often destitute mothers of the resulting children usually received no <a href="/wiki/Child_support" title="Child support">child support</a>. In the earliest stages of the occupation, U.S. soldiers were not allowed to pay maintenance for a child they admitted having fathered, since to do so was considered "aiding the enemy". Marriages between white U.S. soldiers and Austrian women were not permitted until January 1946, and with German women until December 1946.<sup id="cite_ref-Biddiscombe_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Biddiscombe-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>The children of black American soldiers, commonly called <i>Negermischlinge</i><sup id="cite_ref-Spiegel_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Spiegel-11">[11]</a></sup> ("Negro half-breeds"), comprising about three percent of the total number of children fathered by GIs, were particularly disadvantaged because of their inability to conceal the foreign identity of their father. For many white U.S. soldiers of this era, <a href="/wiki/Miscegenation" title="Miscegenation">miscegenation</a> even with an "enemy" white population was regarded as an intolerable outrage. Black soldiers were therefore reluctant to admit to fathering such children since this would invite reprisals, and even in the cases where a soldier was willing to take responsibility, until 1948 the U.S. Army prohibited <a href="/wiki/Interracial_marriage" title="Interracial marriage">interracial marriages</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Spiegel_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Spiegel-11">[11]</a></sup> The mothers of the children would often face particularly harsh ostracism.<sup id="cite_ref-Hitchcock.2C_William_I._2008_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hitchcock.2C_William_I._2008-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>Between 1950 and 1955 the <a href="/wiki/Allied_High_Commission" title="Allied High Commission">Allied High Commission</a> for Germany prohibited "proceedings to establish paternity or liability for maintenance of children."<sup id="cite_ref-Spiegel_11-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Spiegel-11">[11]</a></sup> Even after the lifting of the ban West German courts had little power over American soldiers.</p>
<p>In general, the British authorities were less strict than the Americans about fraternisation, whereas the French and Soviet authorities were more strict.</p>
<p>While Allied servicemen were ordered to obey local laws while in Germany, soldiers could not be prosecuted by German courts for crimes committed against German citizens except as authorised by the occupation authorities. Invariably, when a soldier was accused of criminal behaviour the occupation authorities preferred to handle the matter within the military justice system. This sometimes led to harsher punishments than would have been available under German law – in particular, U.S. servicemen could be executed if court-martialed and convicted of rape.<sup id="cite_ref-Hitchcock.2C_William_I._2008_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hitchcock.2C_William_I._2008-12">[12]</a></sup> See <a href="/wiki/John_A._Bennett" title="John A. Bennett">United States v. Private First Class John A. Bennett</a>, 7 C.M.A. 97, 21 C.M.R. 223 (1956).</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Insurgency">Insurgency</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Allied-occupied_Germany&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Insurgency">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The last Allied war advances into Germany and Allied occupation plans were affected by rumors of Nazi plans for <a href="/wiki/Insurgency" title="Insurgency">insurgency</a> (the Nazi <a href="/wiki/Werwolf" title="Werwolf">Werwolf</a> plan), and successful Nazi deception about plans to withdraw forces to <a href="/wiki/Alpine_Fortress" title="Alpine Fortress"><i>Alpenfestung</i> redoubt</a>. This base was to be used to conduct guerrilla warfare, but the rumours turned out to be false. It has been estimated that no Allied deaths can be reliably attributed to any Nazi insurgency.<sup id="cite_ref-Benjamin_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Benjamin-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Expulsion_policy">Expulsion policy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Allied-occupied_Germany&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Expulsion policy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Flight_and_expulsion_of_Germans_(1944%E2%80%9350)" title="Flight and expulsion of Germans (1944–50)">Flight and expulsion of Germans (1944–50)</a></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Potsdam_conference" class="mw-redirect" title="Potsdam conference">Potsdam conference</a>, where the victorious Allies drew up plans for the future of Germany, noted in article XIII of the <a href="/wiki/Potsdam_Agreement" title="Potsdam Agreement">Potsdam Agreement</a> on 1 August 1945 that "the transfer to Germany of German populations (...) in Poland, Czechoslovakia and Hungary will have to be undertaken"; "wild expulsion" was already going on.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Hungary" title="Hungary">Hungary</a>, which had been allied with Germany and whose population was opposed to an expulsion of the German minority, tried to resist the transfer. Hungary had to yield to the pressure exerted mainly by the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet Union</a> and by the <a href="/wiki/Allied_Control_Council" title="Allied Control Council">Allied Control Council</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> Millions of people were expelled from <a href="/wiki/Former_eastern_territories_of_Germany" title="Former eastern territories of Germany">former eastern territories of Germany</a>, Poland, <a href="/wiki/Czechoslovakia" title="Czechoslovakia">Czechoslovakia</a>, Hungary and elsewhere to the occupation zones of the UK, USA, and USSR, which agreed in the Potsdam Agreement to absorb the post-war expellees into their zones. Many remained in refugee camps for a long time. Some Germans remained in the Soviet Union and were <a href="/wiki/Forced_labor_of_Germans_in_the_Soviet_Union" title="Forced labor of Germans in the Soviet Union">used for forced labour</a> for a period of years.</p>
<p>France was not invited to the Potsdam Conference. As a result, it chose to adopt some decisions of the Potsdam Agreements and to dismiss others. France maintained the position that it did not approve post-war expulsions and that therefore it was not responsible to accommodate and nourish the destitute expellees in its zone. While the few war-related refugees who had reached the area to become the French zone before July 1945 were taken care of, the French military government for Germany refused to absorb post-war expellees deported from the East into its zone. In December 1946, the French military government for Germany absorbed into its zone German refugees from Denmark, where 250,000 Germans had found a refuge from the Soviets by sea vessels between February and May 1945.<sup id="cite_ref-Vor_50_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Vor_50-8">[8]</a></sup> These clearly were war-related refugees from the eastern parts of Germany however, and not post-war expellees.</p>
