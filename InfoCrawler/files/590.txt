<p><b>St. Lorenz</b> (St. Lawrence) is a medieval church of the former free imperial city of <a href="/wiki/Nuremberg" title="Nuremberg">Nuremberg</a> in southern <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a>. It is dedicated to <a href="/wiki/Lawrence_of_Rome" class="mw-redirect" title="Lawrence of Rome">Saint Lawrence</a>. The church was badly damaged during the <a href="/wiki/Second_World_War" class="mw-redirect" title="Second World War">Second World War</a> and later restored. It is one of the most prominent churches of the <a href="/wiki/Evangelical_Lutheran_Church_in_Bavaria" title="Evangelical Lutheran Church in Bavaria">Evangelical Lutheran Church in Bavaria</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Architecture">Architecture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=St._Lorenz,_Nuremberg&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Architecture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The nave of the church was completed by around 1400. In 1439, work began on the choir in the form of a <a href="/wiki/Hall_church" title="Hall church">hall church</a> in the late <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">German</a> <a href="/wiki/Sondergotik" title="Sondergotik">Sondergotik</a> style of <a href="/wiki/Gothic_architecture" title="Gothic architecture">Gothic architecture</a>. The choir was largely completed by 1477 by <a href="/wiki/Konrad_Roriczer" class="mw-redirect" title="Konrad Roriczer">Konrad Roriczer</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> although <a href="/w/index.php?title=Jakob_Grimm_(architect)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Jakob Grimm (architect) (page does not exist)">Jakob Grimm</a> completed the intricate <a href="/wiki/Vault_(architecture)" title="Vault (architecture)">vaults</a>.</p>
<p>In the choir one can find the carving of the <a href="/wiki/Angelic_Salutation_(Stoss)" title="Angelic Salutation (Stoss)">Angelic Salutation</a> by <a href="/wiki/Veit_Stoss" title="Veit Stoss">Veit Stoss</a>, and the monumental <a href="/wiki/Tabernacle" title="Tabernacle">tabernacle</a> by <a href="/wiki/Adam_Kraft" title="Adam Kraft">Adam Kraft</a>. The latter includes a prominent figure of the sculptor himself.</p>
<p>The building and furnishing of the church was cared of by the city council and by wealthy citizens. This is probably the reason why the art treasures of St. Lawrence were spared during the iconoclasm during the Reformation period. Despite St. Lawrence being one of the first churches in Germany to be Lutheran (1525), the wealthy citizens of Nuremberg wanted to preserve the memory of their ancestors and refused the removal of the donated works of art.</p>
<p>The west facade is richly articulated, reflecting the wealth of the Nuremberg citizens. The facade is dominated by the two towers, mirroring St. Sebald and indirectly Bamberg Cathedral with a sharp towering West portal doorway, and an indented rose window 9 metres in diameter.</p>

<p>The church has three organs.</p>
<ul>
<li>Main organ. Steinmeyer, Oettingen, 1937 rebuilt by <a href="/wiki/Klais_Orgelbau" title="Klais Orgelbau">Klais Orgelbau</a>, Bonn, 2003. 5 manuals</li>
