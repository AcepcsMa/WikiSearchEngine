<p><b>Ernst Ludwig Kirchner</b> (6 May 1880 – 15 June 1938) was a German <a href="/wiki/Expressionism" title="Expressionism">expressionist</a> <a href="/wiki/Painting" title="Painting">painter</a> and <a href="/wiki/Printmaker" class="mw-redirect" title="Printmaker">printmaker</a> and one of the founders of the artists group <a href="/wiki/Die_Br%C3%BCcke" title="Die Brücke">Die Brücke</a> or "The Bridge", a key group leading to the foundation of <a href="/wiki/Expressionism" title="Expressionism">Expressionism</a> in 20th-century art. He volunteered for army service in the <a href="/wiki/First_World_War" class="mw-redirect" title="First World War">First World War</a>, but soon suffered a breakdown and was discharged. In 1933, his work was branded as "<a href="/wiki/Degenerate_art" title="Degenerate art">degenerate</a>" by the <a href="/wiki/Nazism" title="Nazism">Nazis</a> and in 1937, over 600 of his works were sold or destroyed. In 1938, he committed <a href="/wiki/Suicide" title="Suicide">suicide</a> by gunshot.<sup id="cite_ref-MoMA_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MoMA-1">[1]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life_and_work">Early life and work</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ernst_Ludwig_Kirchner&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life and work">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Ernst Ludwig Kirchner was born in <a href="/wiki/Aschaffenburg" title="Aschaffenburg">Aschaffenburg</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bavaria" title="Bavaria">Bavaria</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-bruckemuseumkirchner_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bruckemuseumkirchner-2">[2]</a></sup> His parents were of Prussian descent and his mother was a descendant of the <a href="/wiki/Huguenots" title="Huguenots">Huguenots</a>, a fact to which Kirchner often referred.<sup id="cite_ref-kirchnermuseum.ch_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kirchnermuseum.ch-3">[3]</a></sup> As Kirchner's father searched for a job, the family moved frequently and Kirchner attended schools in Frankfurt and Perlen until his father earned the position of Professor of Paper Sciences at the <a href="/wiki/Chemnitz_University_of_Technology" title="Chemnitz University of Technology">College of technology in Chemnitz</a>, where Kirchner attended secondary school.<sup id="cite_ref-kirchnermuseum.ch_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kirchnermuseum.ch-3">[3]</a></sup> Although Kirchner's parents encouraged his artistic career they also wanted him to complete his formal education so in 1901, he began studying architecture at the Königliche Technische Hochschule (royal <a href="/wiki/Technical_university" class="mw-redirect" title="Technical university">technical university</a>) of <a href="/wiki/Dresden" title="Dresden">Dresden</a>. The institution provided a wide range of studies in addition to architecture, such as freehand drawing, perspective drawing and the historical study of art.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> While in attendance, he became close friends with <a href="/wiki/Fritz_Bleyl" title="Fritz Bleyl">Fritz Bleyl</a>, whom Kirchner met during the first term.<sup id="cite_ref-bruckemuseumbleyl_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bruckemuseumbleyl-5">[5]</a></sup> They discussed art together and also studied nature,<sup id="cite_ref-bruckemuseumbleyl_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bruckemuseumbleyl-5">[5]</a></sup> having a radical outlook in common.<sup id="cite_ref-ra_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ra-6">[6]</a></sup> Kirchner continued studies in Munich 1903–1904, returning to Dresden in 1905 to complete his degree.<sup id="cite_ref-bruckemuseumkirchner_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bruckemuseumkirchner-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<p>In 1905, Kirchner, along with Bleyl and two other architecture students, <a href="/wiki/Karl_Schmidt-Rottluff" title="Karl Schmidt-Rottluff">Karl Schmidt-Rottluff</a> and <a href="/wiki/Erich_Heckel" title="Erich Heckel">Erich Heckel</a>, founded the artists group <a href="/wiki/Die_Br%C3%BCcke" title="Die Brücke">Die Brücke</a> ("The Bridge"). From then on, he committed himself to art.<sup id="cite_ref-bruckemuseumkirchner_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bruckemuseumkirchner-2">[2]</a></sup> The group aimed to eschew the prevalent traditional academic style and find a new mode of artistic expression, which would form a bridge (hence the name) between the past and the present.<sup id="cite_ref-ra_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ra-6">[6]</a></sup> They responded both to past artists such as <a href="/wiki/Albrecht_D%C3%BCrer" title="Albrecht Dürer">Albrecht Dürer</a>, <a href="/wiki/Matthias_Gr%C3%BCnewald" title="Matthias Grünewald">Matthias Grünewald</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lucas_Cranach_the_Elder" title="Lucas Cranach the Elder">Lucas Cranach the Elder</a>, as well as contemporary international <a href="/wiki/Avant-garde" title="Avant-garde">avant-garde</a> movements.<sup id="cite_ref-ra_6-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ra-6">[6]</a></sup> As part of the affirmation of their national heritage, they revived older media, particularly <a href="/wiki/Woodcut" title="Woodcut">woodcut prints</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-ra_6-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ra-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Vier_Holzplastiken_1912.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2e/Vier_Holzplastiken_1912.jpg/200px-Vier_Holzplastiken_1912.jpg" width="200" height="235" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2e/Vier_Holzplastiken_1912.jpg/300px-Vier_Holzplastiken_1912.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2e/Vier_Holzplastiken_1912.jpg/400px-Vier_Holzplastiken_1912.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1701" data-file-height="2000" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Vier_Holzplastiken_1912.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<i>Vier Holzplastiken</i>, 1912, <a href="/wiki/Dallas_Museum_of_Art" title="Dallas Museum of Art">Dallas Museum of Art</a></div>

<p>Their group was one of the seminal ones which in due course had a major impact on the evolution of <a href="/wiki/Modern_art" title="Modern art">modern art</a> in the 20th century and created the style of <a href="/wiki/Expressionism" title="Expressionism">Expressionism</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-bruckemuseumbrucke_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bruckemuseumbrucke-7">[7]</a></sup> The group met initially in Kirchner's first studio, which had previously been a butcher's shop. Bleyl described it as "that of a real bohemian, full of paintings lying all over the place, drawings, books and artist’s materials — much more like an artist’s romantic lodgings than the home of a well-organised architecture student".<sup id="cite_ref-ra_6-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ra-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>Kirchner's studio became a venue which overthrew social conventions to allow casual love-making and frequent nudity.<sup id="cite_ref-ra_6-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ra-6">[6]</a></sup> Group life-drawing sessions took place using models from the social circle, rather than professionals, and choosing quarter-hour poses to encourage spontaneity.<sup id="cite_ref-ra_6-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ra-6">[6]</a></sup> Bleyl described one such model, Isabella, a fifteen-year-old girl from the neighbourhood, as "a very lively, beautifully built, joyous individual, without any deformation caused by the silly fashion of the corset and completely suitable to our artistic demands, especially in the blossoming condition of her girlish buds."<sup id="cite_ref-simmons_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-simmons-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>A group manifesto written by Kirchner in 1906 stated that "Everyone who reproduces, directly and without illusion, whatever he senses the urge to create, belongs to us".<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>In September and October 1906, the first group exhibition was held, focused on the female nude, in the showroom of K.F.M. Seifert and Co. in Dresden.<sup id="cite_ref-simmons_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-simmons-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1906, he met <a href="/wiki/Doris_Gro%C3%9Fe" title="Doris Große">Doris Große</a>, who was his favoured model until 1911.<sup id="cite_ref-bruckemuseumkirchner_2-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bruckemuseumkirchner-2">[2]</a></sup> Between 1907 and 1911, he stayed during the summer at the <a href="/wiki/Moritzburg,_Saxony" title="Moritzburg, Saxony">Moritzburg</a> lakes and on the island of <a href="/wiki/Fehmarn" title="Fehmarn">Fehmarn</a> (which he revisited until 1914) with other Brücke members; his work featured the female nude in natural settings.<sup id="cite_ref-bruckemuseumkirchner_2-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bruckemuseumkirchner-2">[2]</a></sup> In 1911, he moved to Berlin, where he founded a private art school, MIUM-Institut, in collaboration with <a href="/wiki/Max_Pechstein" title="Max Pechstein">Max Pechstein</a> with the aim of promulgating "Moderner Unterricht im Malen" (modern teaching of painting). This was not a success and closed the following year, when he also began a relationship with Erna Schilling that lasted the rest of his life.<sup id="cite_ref-bruckemuseumkirchner_2-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bruckemuseumkirchner-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1913, his writing of <i>Chronik der Brücke</i> (Brücke chronicle) led to the ending of the group. At this time, he established an individual identity with his first solo exhibition, which took place at the Essen <a href="/wiki/Folkwang_Museum" class="mw-redirect" title="Folkwang Museum">Folkwang Museum</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-bruckemuseumkirchner_2-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bruckemuseumkirchner-2">[2]</a></sup> During the next two years, he painted a series of "<a href="/wiki/Street_scenes" title="Street scenes">Straßenszenen</a>" (street scenes) showing the streets of Berlin,<sup id="cite_ref-bruckemuseumkirchner_2-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bruckemuseumkirchner-2">[2]</a></sup> with the central characters of <a href="/wiki/Prostitute" class="mw-redirect" title="Prostitute">street walkers</a>.</p>

<p>At the onset of the <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">First World War</a> in September 1914, Kirchner volunteered for military service. In July 1915 he was sent to Halle an der Saale to train as a driver in the reserve unit of the 75th Mansfeld Field Artillery Regiment.<sup id="cite_ref-kirchnermuseum.ch_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kirchnermuseum.ch-3">[3]</a></sup> Kirchner's riding instructor, Professor Hans Fehr, arranged for Kirchner to be discharged after a mental breakdown.<sup id="cite_ref-kirchnermuseum.ch_3-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kirchnermuseum.ch-3">[3]</a></sup> Kirchner then returned to Berlin and continued to work, producing many paintings including <i>Self-Portrait as a Soldier</i> (1915); in December 1915 he was admitted to Dr. <a href="/wiki/Oskar_Kohnstamm" title="Oskar Kohnstamm">Kohnstamm’s</a> sanatorium in Königstein in Taunus, where he was diagnosed with a strong dependency on <a href="/wiki/Veronal" class="mw-redirect" title="Veronal">Veronal</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Alcoholism" title="Alcoholism">alcoholism</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-kirchnermuseum.ch_3-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kirchnermuseum.ch-3">[3]</a></sup> In a letter to Dr. Karl Hagemann, a friend and patron, Kirchner writes: "After lengthy struggles I now find myself here for a time to put my mind into some kind of order. It is a terribly difficult thing, of course, to be among strangers so much of the day. But perhaps I’ll be able to see and create something new. For the time being, I would like more peace and absolute seclusion. Of course, I long more and more for my work and my studio. Theories may be all very well for keeping a spiritual balance, but they are grey and shadowy compared with work and life".<sup id="cite_ref-kirchnermuseum.ch_3-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kirchnermuseum.ch-3">[3]</a></sup> Throughout 1916, Kirchner periodically returned to Berlin for a few weeks at a time to continue his work at his studio; he also produced a series of oil paintings, and many drawings, during his stays in Königstein.<sup id="cite_ref-kirchnermuseum.ch_3-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kirchnermuseum.ch-3">[3]</a></sup> After an exhibition of his work in at the gallery of <a href="/w/index.php?title=Ludwig_Schames&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Ludwig Schames (page does not exist)">Ludwig Schames</a>, in <a href="/wiki/Frankfurt_am_Main" class="mw-redirect" title="Frankfurt am Main">Frankfurt am Main</a>, in October 1916, Kirchner sold many works and began to do well financially.<sup id="cite_ref-kirchnermuseum.ch_3-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kirchnermuseum.ch-3">[3]</a></sup> In December, he suffered from a nervous breakdown and was admitted to Dr. Edel’s sanatorium in Berlin Charlottenburg.<sup id="cite_ref-kirchnermuseum.ch_3-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kirchnermuseum.ch-3">[3]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Davos">Davos</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ernst_Ludwig_Kirchner&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Davos">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1917, at the suggestion of Eberhard Grisebach, Helene Spengler invited Kirchner to <a href="/wiki/Davos" title="Davos">Davos</a> where he viewed an exhibition of <a href="/wiki/Ferdinand_Hodler" title="Ferdinand Hodler">Ferdinand Hodler</a>'s paintings.<sup id="cite_ref-kirchnermuseum.ch_3-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kirchnermuseum.ch-3">[3]</a></sup> Kirchner's first visit to Davos coincided with a spell of exceptionally cold weather and he returned to Berlin after a stay of only ten days.<sup id="cite_ref-kirchnermuseum.ch_3-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kirchnermuseum.ch-3">[3]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-:0_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>Eberhard Grisebach visited him in March, writing to Helen Spengler of Kirchner's condition: "I spent two mornings with Kirchner which I shall never forget. I found him sitting on a very low chair next to a small, hot stove in a yellow-painted, sloping-roofed attic. Only with the help of a stick was he able to walk, staggering around the room. ... A colourfully painted curtain concealed a large collection of paintings. When we began to look at them, he came alive. Together with me, he saw all his experiences drift by on canvas, the small, timid-looking woman set aside what we had seen and brought a bottle of wine. He made short explanatory remarks in a weary voice. Each picture had its own particular colourful character, a great sadness was present in all of them; what I had previously found to be incomprehensible and unfinished now created the same delicate and sensitive impression as his personality. Everywhere a search for style, for psychological understanding of his figures. The most moving was a self- portrait in uniform with his right hand cut off. Then he showed me his travel permit for Switzerland. He wanted to go back to Davos... and implored me to ask father for a medical certificate. ... As the woman with him rightly said, though many people want to help him, nobody is able to do so any longer. ... When I was leaving, I thought of Van Gogh’s fate and thought that it would be his as well, sooner or later. Only later will people understand and see how much he has contributed to painting".<sup id="cite_ref-kirchnermuseum.ch_3-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kirchnermuseum.ch-3">[3]</a></sup></p>

<i>Standing Nude with Hat</i>, 1910</div>

<p>Soon after, Kirchner's close friend and mentor, <a href="/wiki/Botho_Graef" title="Botho Graef">Botho Graef</a> died and Kirchner decided to return to Davos for treatment.<sup id="cite_ref-kirchnermuseum.ch_3-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kirchnermuseum.ch-3">[3]</a></sup> There he was under the care of Dr. Lucius Spengler who forced Kirchner to adhere to strict rules and routine.<sup id="cite_ref-kirchnermuseum.ch_3-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kirchnermuseum.ch-3">[3]</a></sup> Kirchner deeply resented this and did everything in his power to deceive the doctor: "Spengler didn’t know what to do with me, for my deception was totally alien to this excellent man’s way of thinking".<sup id="cite_ref-kirchnermuseum.ch_3-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kirchnermuseum.ch-3">[3]</a></sup> To avoid being under constant watch, Kirchner moved to the Reusch Hut on the Stafelalp in the summer of 1917.<sup id="cite_ref-kirchnermuseum.ch_3-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kirchnermuseum.ch-3">[3]</a></sup> Kirchner continued to experience depression, occasional pain and paralysis of his limbs but wrote in a more cheerful tone to Dr. Hagemann at the end of July: "I wish to remain in the world and for the world. The high mountains here will help me".<sup id="cite_ref-kirchnermuseum.ch_3-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kirchnermuseum.ch-3">[3]</a></sup> The summer was very productive despite Kirchner's illness.<sup id="cite_ref-kirchnermuseum.ch_3-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kirchnermuseum.ch-3">[3]</a></sup> He finished some important pieces during the two month, such as "View of the Church in Monstein" and "Rising Moon in the Stafelalp". Together with 11 woodcuts, these works marked the start of Kirchner's Alpine life.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>Kirchner was then admitted to The Bellevue Sanatorium, run by <a href="/wiki/Ludwig_Binswanger" title="Ludwig Binswanger">Ludwig Binswanger</a>, in Kreuzlingen where he continued to produce paintings and woodcuts.<sup id="cite_ref-kirchnermuseum.ch_3-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kirchnermuseum.ch-3">[3]</a></sup> In 1918, Kirchner was given a residence permit and he moved to "In den Lärchen" in Frauenkirch Davos where he rented a room on the first floor and eventually the above rooms in 1919, which he decorated with furniture that he carved.<sup id="cite_ref-kirchnermuseum.ch_3-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kirchnermuseum.ch-3">[3]</a></sup> He wrote of the house to <a href="/wiki/Henry_van_de_Velde" title="Henry van de Velde">Henry van de Velde</a>: "I’m living in a beautiful old Grisons house with a kitchen that looks like Rembrandt’s studio".<sup id="cite_ref-kirchnermuseum.ch_3-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kirchnermuseum.ch-3">[3]</a></sup> Kirchner overcame his illness and, although he was still dependent on morphine, his doctor was slowly decreasing his doses.<sup id="cite_ref-kirchnermuseum.ch_3-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kirchnermuseum.ch-3">[3]</a></sup> He also writes "A Painter's Credo" where he states: "There is an intellectual guardianship over the world, it is man…. This is the last judgement, before them you stand….. They help you when you work. You can thank them only through work. When you want to die, they sometimes appear to you. When you are completely empty and completely open, you belong to them".<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> Erna Schilling, his life partner, visited him periodically in Frauenkirch, while also maintaining a residence in Berlin to take care of Kirchner's business there.<sup id="cite_ref-kirchnermuseum.ch_3-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kirchnermuseum.ch-3">[3]</a></sup></p>

<p>Kirchner continued to work through 1919 and 1920 as his health also rapidly improved.<sup id="cite_ref-kirchnermuseum.ch_3-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kirchnermuseum.ch-3">[3]</a></sup> His reputation grew with several exhibitions in Germany and Switzerland in 1920.<sup id="cite_ref-bruckemuseumkirchner_2-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bruckemuseumkirchner-2">[2]</a></sup> He was provided with many subjects to paint as he came to know the farmers of the area, who were amazed by Kirchner's gramophone.<sup id="cite_ref-kirchnermuseum.ch_3-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kirchnermuseum.ch-3">[3]</a></sup> Kirchner writes of the people of Davos: "The people who live here are proud. The hard work, which is done with great love, the way they treat animals (you very seldom see an animal being mishandled) entitle them to be proud. In most cases, work here has reached the ideal standard of being done with love. You can see it in the movements of their hands. And that, in turn, ennobles the facial expression and imbues all personal contacts with a great delicacy. This is a country in which democracy has become reality. Here a man’s word still counts, and you need have no fears about sleeping with your doors open. I am so happy to be allowed to be here, and through hard work I should like to thank the people for the kindness they have shown me".<sup id="cite_ref-kirchnermuseum.ch_3-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kirchnermuseum.ch-3">[3]</a></sup> Kirchner began writing critiques of his own art under the pseudonym of Louis de Marsalle in order to control public opinion of him and free himself of dependence upon the art critics of the day.<sup id="cite_ref-kirchnermuseum.ch_3-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kirchnermuseum.ch-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1921, there was a major display of Kirchner's work in Berlin; the reviews were favourable.<sup id="cite_ref-kirchnermuseum.ch_3-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kirchnermuseum.ch-3">[3]</a></sup> Kirchner's father died on the 14 February.<sup id="cite_ref-kirchnermuseum.ch_3-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kirchnermuseum.ch-3">[3]</a></sup> Kirchner visited Zurich at the beginning of May and met the dancer, Nina Hard, whom he invited back to Frauenkirch (despite Erna's objections). Nina Hard would become an important model for Kirchner and would be featured in many of his works.<sup id="cite_ref-kirchnermuseum.ch_3-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kirchnermuseum.ch-3">[3]</a></sup> Kirchner began creating designs for carpets which were then woven by Lise Gujer.<sup id="cite_ref-kirchnermuseum.ch_3-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kirchnermuseum.ch-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1923, Kirchner moved to the Wildboden house, writing in his diary: "Our new little house is a real joy to us. We shall live here comfortably and in great new order. This will really come to be a turning point of my life. Everything must be put in clear order and the little house furnished as simply and modestly as possible, while still being beautiful and intimate".<sup id="cite_ref-kirchnermuseum.ch_3-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kirchnermuseum.ch-3">[3]</a></sup> The house looked over Frauenkirch and the Stafelalp on one side and on the other, Davos and Kirchner used these landscapes as subjects for many of his paintings.<sup id="cite_ref-kirchnermuseum.ch_3-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kirchnermuseum.ch-3">[3]</a></sup></p>

<p>In 1925, Kirchner became close friends with fellow artist, Albert Müller and his family.<sup id="cite_ref-kirchnermuseum.ch_3-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kirchnermuseum.ch-3">[3]</a></sup> Rot-Blau, a new art group based in Basle, was formed by <a href="/wiki/Hermann_Scherer" title="Hermann Scherer">Hermann Scherer</a>, Albert Müller, <a href="/wiki/Paul_Camenisch" title="Paul Camenisch">Paul Camenisch</a> and Hans Schiess, who all visited Kirchner and worked under his guidance.<sup id="cite_ref-kirchnermuseum.ch_3-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kirchnermuseum.ch-3">[3]</a></sup> At the end of 1925, Kirchner returned to Germany and made his rounds to Frankfurt, Chemnitz (where he mother was living), and Berlin where he met with <a href="/wiki/Karl_Schmidt-Rottluff" title="Karl Schmidt-Rottluff">Karl Schmidt-Rottluff</a> who wanted Kirchner to form a new artist's group; Kirchner politely refused.<sup id="cite_ref-kirchnermuseum.ch_3-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kirchnermuseum.ch-3">[3]</a></sup> He then returned to Frauenkirch and wrote to Dr. Hageman on 26 March 1926: "Now I’m sitting quietly at home again and I’m happy to be able to work undisturbed. I made a lot of sketches of life in Germany and it was very intriguing to see life there. I was also glad to see the old pictures of Rembrandt, Dürer, etc. again and to have the confirmation and encouragement they gave me. As for the moderns, I saw damned little that gripped me".<sup id="cite_ref-kirchnermuseum.ch_3-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kirchnermuseum.ch-3">[3]</a></sup> In December 1926, Kirchner's close friend, Albert Müller, died of typhus along with his wife, Anni Müller.<sup id="cite_ref-kirchnermuseum.ch_3-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kirchnermuseum.ch-3">[3]</a></sup> In 1927, Kirchner organized a memorial exhibition for Albert Müller at the <a href="/wiki/Kunsthalle_Basel" title="Kunsthalle Basel">Kunsthalle Basel</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-kirchnermuseum.ch_3-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kirchnermuseum.ch-3">[3]</a></sup> There was a major exhibition of Kirchner's work at the schoolhouse in Davos; the reviews were positive.<sup id="cite_ref-kirchnermuseum.ch_3-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kirchnermuseum.ch-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Kirchner continued to work in Frauenkirch, his style growing increasingly abstract.<sup id="cite_ref-kirchnermuseum.ch_3-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kirchnermuseum.ch-3">[3]</a></sup> In 1929, Kirchner was forced to distance himself from Rot-Blau after they pledged allegiance to him, which upset Kirchner greatly. He addressed them in "An open letter to the Basle Red-Blue group" in No. 5 of <i>Das Kunstbaltt</i>, where stated that he was not their patron.<sup id="cite_ref-kirchnermuseum.ch_3-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kirchnermuseum.ch-3">[3]</a></sup> In the same year, Kirchner visited Zurich, Berlin, and Essen.<sup id="cite_ref-kirchnermuseum.ch_3-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kirchnermuseum.ch-3">[3]</a></sup> He was also visited in Frauenkirch by the painter <a href="/wiki/Fritz_Winter" title="Fritz Winter">Fritz Winter</a>.</p>
<p>In 1930, Kirchner began to experience health problems due to smoking and in 1931 Erna had to undergo surgery in Berlin due to a suspected growth.<sup id="cite_ref-kirchnermuseum.ch_3-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kirchnermuseum.ch-3">[3]</a></sup> In 1931, he was made a member of the <a href="/wiki/Prussian_Academy_of_Arts" title="Prussian Academy of Arts">Prussian Academy of Arts</a> in Berlin. As the Nazi party took power in Germany, it became impossible for Kirchner to sell his paintings. In 1933, he was forced to resign from the <a href="/wiki/Prussian_Academy_of_Arts" title="Prussian Academy of Arts">Prussian Academy of Arts</a>. Kirchner became increasingly disturbed by the situation in Germany, writing: "Here we have been hearing terrible rumours about torture of the Jews, but it’s all surely untrue. I’m a little tired and sad about the situation up there. There is a war in the air. In the museums, the hard-won cultural achievements of the last 20 years are being destroyed, and yet the reason why we founded the Brücke was to encourage truly German art, made in Germany. And now it is supposed to be un-German. Dear God. It does upset me".<sup id="cite_ref-kirchnermuseum.ch_3-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kirchnermuseum.ch-3">[3]</a></sup></p>

</div>
</div>
<p>In 1934, Kirchner visited Berne and Zurich, finding the former more pleasing than the latter, and met <a href="/wiki/Paul_Klee" title="Paul Klee">Paul Klee</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-kirchnermuseum.ch_3-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kirchnermuseum.ch-3">[3]</a></sup> In the winter of 1935, a new school was being planned to be built in Frauenkirch and Kirchner offered to paint a mural. This project was dropped and instead Kirchner created a sculpture to be placed above the door of the schoolhouse.<sup id="cite_ref-kirchnermuseum.ch_3-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kirchnermuseum.ch-3">[3]</a></sup> Reflecting on the inauguration of the schoolhouse in 1936 he writes, "the new school was inaugurated yesterday. It was a celebration with songs, dancing and speeches, followed by drinking such as I have not seen or experienced in decades...They made a point of including me and so there I was, sitting once again amongst these people who had received me with such kindness and friendliness on the alp twenty years ago. The relief has found favour and was mentioned often in the speeches".<sup id="cite_ref-kirchnermuseum.ch_3-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kirchnermuseum.ch-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Throughout 1936 and 1937, Kirchner began to experience health problems and was prescribed <a href="/wiki/Ovaltine" title="Ovaltine">Ovaltine</a> and <a href="/wiki/Eukodal" class="mw-redirect" title="Eukodal">Eukodal</a> by his doctors.<sup id="cite_ref-kirchnermuseum.ch_3-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kirchnermuseum.ch-3">[3]</a></sup> In 1937, the <a href="/wiki/Degenerate_Art_Exhibition" title="Degenerate Art Exhibition">Degenerate Art Exhibition</a> took place in Germany; a total of 639 works by Kirchner were taken out of museums and 25 were displayed in the exhibition.<sup id="cite_ref-kirchnermuseum.ch_3-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kirchnermuseum.ch-3">[3]</a></sup> The Academy of Arts in Berlin expelled Kirchner as a member.<sup id="cite_ref-kirchnermuseum.ch_3-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kirchnermuseum.ch-3">[3]</a></sup> Kirchner continued to work and organised a major exhibit in Basle, which received mixed reviews.<sup id="cite_ref-kirchnermuseum.ch_3-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kirchnermuseum.ch-3">[3]</a></sup> Throughout 1938, Kirchner became increasingly upset with the situation in Germany. After Austria was annexed by Germany in the <a href="/wiki/Anschluss" title="Anschluss">Anschluss</a>, Kirchner became disturbed by the idea that Germany might invade Switzerland.<sup id="cite_ref-kirchnermuseum.ch_3-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kirchnermuseum.ch-3">[3]</a></sup> On 15 June 1938, Kirchner took his own life by gunshot in front of his home in Frauenkirch.<sup id="cite_ref-MoMA_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MoMA-1">[1]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-kirchnermuseum.ch_3-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kirchnermuseum.ch-3">[3]</a></sup> Three days later, Kirchner was laid to rest in the Waldfriedhof cemetery.<sup id="cite_ref-kirchnermuseum.ch_3-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kirchnermuseum.ch-3">[3]</a></sup> Erna continued to live in the house until her death in 1945.<sup id="cite_ref-kirchnermuseum.ch_3-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kirchnermuseum.ch-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legacy">Legacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ernst_Ludwig_Kirchner&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Legacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1913, the first public showing in the United States of Kirchner's work took place at the <a href="/wiki/Armory_Show" title="Armory Show">Armory Show</a>, which was also the first major display of <a href="/wiki/Modern_art" title="Modern art">modern art</a> in America.<sup id="cite_ref-nga_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nga-12">[12]</a></sup> In 1921, U.S. museums began to acquire his work and did so increasingly thereafter.<sup id="cite_ref-nga_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nga-12">[12]</a></sup> His first solo museum show in the US was at the <a href="/wiki/Detroit_Institute_of_Arts" title="Detroit Institute of Arts">Detroit Institute of Arts</a> in 1937.<sup id="cite_ref-nga_12-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nga-12">[12]</a></sup> In 1969, a major retrospective of paintings, drawings,and prints traveled to the <a href="/wiki/Seattle_Art_Museum" title="Seattle Art Museum">Seattle Art Museum</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Norton_Simon_Museum" title="Norton Simon Museum">Pasadena Art Museum</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Museum_of_Fine_Arts,_Boston" title="Museum of Fine Arts, Boston">Museum of Fine Arts, Boston</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> In 1992, the <a href="/wiki/National_Gallery_of_Art" title="National Gallery of Art">National Gallery of Art</a>, Washington, held a monographic show, using its existing collection; a major international loan exhibition took place in 2003.<sup id="cite_ref-nga_12-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nga-12">[12]</a></sup> In November 2006 at <a href="/wiki/Christie%27s" title="Christie's">Christie's</a>, Kirchner's Street Scene, <i>Berlin</i> (1913) fetched $38 million, a record for the artist.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> From 3 August to 10 November 2008, the <a href="/wiki/Museum_of_Modern_Art" title="Museum of Modern Art">Museum of Modern Art</a> in New York held a major exhibition that "probably comprises the very best of his oeuvre."<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
