			<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">German town law</h1>
									<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
									<div id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
								<div id="contentSub"></div>
												<div id="jump-to-nav" class="mw-jump">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>
				</div>
				<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p>The <b>German town law</b> (<a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a>: <span lang="de" xml:lang="de"><i>Deutsches Stadtrecht</i></span>) or <b>German municipal concerns</b> (<i>Deutsches Städtewesen</i>) was a set of early <a href="/wiki/Town_privileges" title="Town privileges">town privileges</a> based on the <a href="/wiki/Magdeburg_rights" title="Magdeburg rights">Magdeburg rights</a> developed by <a href="/wiki/Otto_I" class="mw-redirect" title="Otto I">Otto I</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Magdeburg" title="Magdeburg">Magdeburg</a> Law became the inspiration for regional town charters not only in Germany, but also in <a href="/wiki/Central_Europe" title="Central Europe">Central</a> and <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Europe" title="Eastern Europe">Eastern Europe</a> who modified it during the <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">Middle Ages</a>. The German town law (based on Magdeburg rights) was used in the founding of many German cities, towns, and villages beginning in the 13th century.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Town_law_in_Germany">Town law in Germany</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=German_town_law&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Town law in Germany">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>As <a href="/wiki/Germans" title="Germans">Germans</a> began establishing towns throughout northern Europe as early as the 10th century, they often received <a href="/wiki/Town_privileges" title="Town privileges">town privileges</a> granting them autonomy from local secular or religious rulers. Such privileges often included the right to self-governance, economic autonomy, criminal courts, and <a href="/wiki/Militia" title="Militia">militia</a>. Town laws were more or less entirely copied from neighboring towns, such as the <a href="/wiki/Westphalia" title="Westphalia">Westphalian</a> towns of <a href="/wiki/Soest,_Germany" title="Soest, Germany">Soest</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dortmund" title="Dortmund">Dortmund</a>, <a href="/wiki/Minden" title="Minden">Minden</a>, and <a href="/wiki/M%C3%BCnster" title="Münster">Münster</a>. As Germans began settling eastward, the colonists modelled their town laws on the pre-existing 12th century laws of <a href="/wiki/Cologne" title="Cologne">Cologne</a> in the west, <a href="/wiki/L%C3%BCbeck" title="Lübeck">Lübeck</a> in the north (<a href="/wiki/L%C3%BCbeck_law" title="Lübeck law">Lübeck law</a>), <a href="/wiki/Magdeburg" title="Magdeburg">Magdeburg</a> in the east (<a href="/wiki/Magdeburg_rights" title="Magdeburg rights">Magdeburg rights</a>), and either <a href="/wiki/Nuremberg" title="Nuremberg">Nuremberg</a> or <a href="/wiki/Vienna" title="Vienna">Vienna</a> in the south.</p>
<p>The granting of German city rights modelled after an established town to a new town regarded the original model as a <i>Rechtsvorort</i>, or roughly a legal sponsor of the newly chartered town. For instance, Magdeburg became the sponsor of towns using Magdeburg Rights, and its lay judges could rule in ambiguous legal cases in towns using such rights. Certain city rights became known under different names, although they originally came from the same source; the name of some city variants designates the <i>Rechtsvorort</i> they became famous from, not necessarily that that specific style of rights originated from the <i>Rechtsvorort</i>. As territorial borders changed through the passage of time, changes to German city rights were inevitable.</p>

<p>During the course of the 15th, 16th, and 17th centuries, the town laws of many places were modified with aspects of <a href="/wiki/Roman_law" title="Roman law">Roman law</a> by legal experts. Ultimately, the older towns' laws, along with local autonomy and jurisdiction, gave way to landed territorial rulers. With the <i><a href="/wiki/Reichsdeputationshauptschluss" title="Reichsdeputationshauptschluss">Reichsdeputationshauptschluss</a></i> of 1803, almost all of the 51 <a href="/wiki/Reichsfrei" class="mw-redirect" title="Reichsfrei">reichsfrei</a> cities of the <a href="/wiki/Holy_Roman_Empire" title="Holy Roman Empire">Holy Roman Empire</a> were <a href="/wiki/German_Mediatisation" class="mw-redirect" title="German Mediatisation">mediatised</a> by the territorial princes; the remaining <a href="/wiki/Imperial_free_cities" class="mw-redirect" title="Imperial free cities">imperial free cities</a> of <a href="/wiki/Frankfurt" title="Frankfurt">Frankfurt</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bremen" title="Bremen">Bremen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hamburg" title="Hamburg">Hamburg</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Free_City_of_L%C3%BCbeck" title="Free City of Lübeck">Lübeck</a> became sovereign <a href="/wiki/City-state" title="City-state">city-states</a>. The only remnants of medieval town rights (statutes) included in the <i><a href="/wiki/B%C3%BCrgerliches_Gesetzbuch" title="Bürgerliches Gesetzbuch">Bürgerliches Gesetzbuch</a></i> of 1 January 1900 were single articles concerning family and inheritance laws. The cities of Hamburg, Bremen, and <a href="/wiki/Berlin" title="Berlin">Berlin</a> are currently administered under <i>Landesrechte</i>, or laws of the <a href="/wiki/States_of_Germany" title="States of Germany">federal states</a> of <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a>.</p>
<p>Many towns granted German city rights had already existed for some time, but the granting of town law codified the legal status of the settlement. Many European localities date their foundation to their reception of a town charter, even though they had existed as a settlement beforehand.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Eastward_colonization">Eastward colonization</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=German_town_law&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Eastward colonization">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>German town law was frequently applied during the <i><a href="/wiki/Ostsiedlung" title="Ostsiedlung">Ostsiedlung</a></i> of Central and Eastern Europe by German <a href="/wiki/Colonist" class="mw-redirect" title="Colonist">colonists</a> beginning in the early 13th century. Because many areas were considered underpopulated or underdeveloped, local rulers offered urban privileges to peasants from German lands to induce them to immigrate eastward. Some towns which received a German town law charter were based on pre-existing settlements, while others were constructed anew by colonists. Many towns were formed in conjunction with the settlement of nearby rural communities, but the towns' urban rights were jealously guarded. Initially German town law was applied only to ethnic Germans, but gradually in most localities all town-dwellers were regarded as citizens, regardless of ethnic origin.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/L%C3%BCbeck_law" title="Lübeck law">Lübeck law</a> spread rapidly among the maritime settlements along the southern shore of the <a href="/wiki/Baltic_Sea" title="Baltic Sea">Baltic Sea</a> and was used in northern <a href="/wiki/Mecklenburg" title="Mecklenburg">Mecklenburg</a>, <a href="/wiki/Western_Pomerania" title="Western Pomerania">Western Pomerania</a>, and parts of <a href="/wiki/Pomerelia" title="Pomerelia">Pomerelia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Warmia" title="Warmia">Warmia</a>. It formed the basis of <a href="/w/index.php?title=Riga_law&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Riga law (page does not exist)">Riga law</a> in <a href="/wiki/Riga" title="Riga">Riga</a>, used in the lands of the <a href="/wiki/Livonian_Order" title="Livonian Order">Livonian Order</a> in <a href="/wiki/Livonia" title="Livonia">Livonia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Estonia" title="Estonia">Estonia</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Courland" title="Courland">Courland</a>.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Magdeburg_law" class="mw-redirect" title="Magdeburg law">Magdeburg law</a> was popular around the <a href="/wiki/March_of_Mei%C3%9Fen" class="mw-redirect" title="March of Meißen">March of Meißen</a> and <a href="/wiki/Upper_Saxony" title="Upper Saxony">Upper Saxony</a> and was the source of several variants, including <a href="/w/index.php?title=Neumarkt-Magdeburg_law&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Neumarkt-Magdeburg law (page does not exist)">Neumarkt-Magdeburg law</a> (<a href="/wiki/%C5%9Aroda_%C5%9Al%C4%85ska" title="Środa Śląska">Środa Śląska</a>), used extensively in <a href="/wiki/Upper_Silesia" title="Upper Silesia">Upper Silesia</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Kulm_law" title="Kulm law">Kulm law</a>, used in the <a href="/wiki/Monastic_State_of_the_Teutonic_Order" class="mw-redirect" title="Monastic State of the Teutonic Order">territory</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Teutonic_Knights" class="mw-redirect" title="Teutonic Knights">Teutonic Knights</a> in <a href="/wiki/Prussia_(region)" title="Prussia (region)">Prussia</a> and along the lower <a href="/wiki/Vistula" title="Vistula">Vistula</a> in Eastern Pomerania. Other variants included <a href="/w/index.php?title=Brandenburg_law&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Brandenburg law (page does not exist)">Brandenburg</a>, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Litom%C4%9B%C5%99ice_law&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Litoměřice law (page does not exist)">Litoměřice</a>, and <a href="/w/index.php?title=Olomouc_law&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Olomouc law (page does not exist)">Olomouc law</a>.</p>
<p>Litoměřice law and codes based on that of <a href="/wiki/Nuremberg" title="Nuremberg">Nuremberg</a>, such as <a href="/w/index.php?title=Old_Prague_law&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Old Prague law (page does not exist)">Old Prague</a> and <a href="/w/index.php?title=Cheb_law&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Cheb law (page does not exist)">Cheb law</a>, were introduced into <a href="/wiki/Bohemia" title="Bohemia">Bohemia</a> during the reign of King <a href="/wiki/Wenceslaus_I,_King_of_Bohemia" class="mw-redirect" title="Wenceslaus I, King of Bohemia">Wenceslaus I</a>, while German colonists introduced Brünn (<a href="/w/index.php?title=Brno_law&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Brno law (page does not exist)">Brno</a>) and Olmütz (<a href="/w/index.php?title=Olomouc_law&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Olomouc law (page does not exist)">Olomouc</a>) law in <a href="/wiki/Moravia" title="Moravia">Moravia</a>. <a href="/w/index.php?title=South_German_law&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="South German law (page does not exist)">South German law</a>, broadly referring to the codes of Nuremberg and <a href="/wiki/Vienna" title="Vienna">Vienna</a>, was used in <a href="/wiki/Bavaria" title="Bavaria">Bavaria</a>, <a href="/wiki/Austria" title="Austria">Austria</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Slovenia" title="Slovenia">Slovenia</a>, and was introduced into the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Hungary" title="Kingdom of Hungary">Kingdom of Hungary</a> during the rule of King <a href="/wiki/B%C3%A9la_IV_of_Hungary" title="Béla IV of Hungary">Béla IV</a>. <a href="/w/index.php?title=Jihlava_law&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Jihlava law (page does not exist)">Jihlava law</a> was a variant used frequently by <a href="/wiki/Mining" title="Mining">mining</a> communities in Bohemia, Moravia, the mountains of <a href="/wiki/Slovakia" title="Slovakia">Slovakia</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Transylvania" title="Transylvania">Transylvania</a>. Other town laws were only suitable for or were modified to fit local conditions, such as <a href="/w/index.php?title=G%C5%82ubczyce_law&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Głubczyce law (page does not exist)">Głubczyce</a>, <a href="/w/index.php?title=G%C3%B6rlitz_law&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Görlitz law (page does not exist)">Görlitz</a>, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Goslar_law&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Goslar law (page does not exist)">Goslar</a>, <a href="/w/index.php?title=L%C3%BCneburg_law&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Lüneburg law (page does not exist)">Lüneburg</a>, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lw%C3%B3wek_%C5%9Al%C4%85ski_law&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Lwówek Śląski law (page does not exist)">Lwówek Śląski</a>, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Nysa_law&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Nysa law (page does not exist)">Nysa</a>, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Spi%C5%A1_law&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Spiš law (page does not exist)">Spiš</a>, and <a href="/w/index.php?title=Sz%C3%A9kesfeh%C3%A9rv%C3%A1r_law&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Székesfehérvár law (page does not exist)">Székesfehérvár</a> laws.</p>
<p>Resulting from the reign of King <a href="/wiki/Casimir_III_of_Poland" class="mw-redirect" title="Casimir III of Poland">Casimir III of Poland</a>, numerous towns were chartered with Neumarkt town law throughout the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Poland_(1320%E2%80%931385)" class="mw-redirect" title="Kingdom of Poland (1320–1385)">Kingdom of Poland</a> in the 14th century, especially in <a href="/wiki/Masovia" class="mw-redirect" title="Masovia">Masovia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Galicia_(Central_Europe)" class="mw-redirect" title="Galicia (Central Europe)">Galicia</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Volhynia" title="Volhynia">Volhynia</a>. Many <a href="/wiki/Transylvanian_Saxons" title="Transylvanian Saxons">Transylvanian Saxon</a> settlements in Transylvania, especially in the regions of <a href="/w/index.php?title=Altland&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Altland (page does not exist)">Altland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Burzenland" title="Burzenland">Burzenland</a>, and <a href="/wiki/N%C3%B6snerland" title="Nösnerland">Nösnerland</a>, received South German town law in the 14th century.</p>
<p>In the 15th century, many towns in the <a href="/wiki/Grand_Duchy_of_Lithuania" title="Grand Duchy of Lithuania">Grand Duchy of Lithuania</a> were chartered with the Neumarkt town law used in much of Poland, although this was done through the duplication of Polish administrative methods instead of German colonization. In the 16th century <a href="/wiki/Muscovy" class="mw-redirect" title="Muscovy">Muscovy</a> granted or reaffirmed Magdeburg rights to various towns along the <a href="/wiki/Dnieper" title="Dnieper">Dnieper</a> acquired from the <a href="/wiki/Polish-Lithuanian_Commonwealth" class="mw-redirect" title="Polish-Lithuanian Commonwealth">Polish-Lithuanian Commonwealth</a>. After the <a href="/wiki/Partitions_of_Poland" title="Partitions of Poland">Partitions of Poland</a>, Magdeburg law continued to be used in western <a href="/wiki/Imperial_Russia" class="mw-redirect" title="Imperial Russia">Imperial Russia</a> until the 1830s.</p>
<p>It is important to keep in mind that although many towns outside of the <a href="/wiki/Holy_Roman_Empire" title="Holy Roman Empire">Holy Roman Empire</a> had significant German populations, in some cases a town being a "German city" referred to its legal system based on German law, not necessarily meaning it had a German population. Especially in Eastern Europe, most towns had minimal German populations, if any at all.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
