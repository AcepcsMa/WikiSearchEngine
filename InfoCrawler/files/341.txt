<p>The <b>Imperial City of Nuremberg</b> (<a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a>: <span lang="de" xml:lang="de"><i>Reichsstadt Nürnberg</i></span>) was a <a href="/wiki/Free_imperial_city" title="Free imperial city">free imperial city</a> — independent <a href="/wiki/City-state" title="City-state">city-state</a> — within the <a href="/wiki/Holy_Roman_Empire" title="Holy Roman Empire">Holy Roman Empire</a>. After <a href="/wiki/Nuremberg" title="Nuremberg">Nuremberg</a> gained piecemeal independence from the <a href="/wiki/Burgraviate_of_Nuremberg" title="Burgraviate of Nuremberg">Burgraviate of Nuremberg</a> in the <a href="/wiki/High_Middle_Ages" title="High Middle Ages">High Middle Ages</a> and considerable territory from <a href="/wiki/Duchy_of_Bavaria" title="Duchy of Bavaria">Bavaria</a> in the <a href="/wiki/War_of_the_Succession_of_Landshut" title="War of the Succession of Landshut">Landshut War of Succession</a>, it grew to become one of the largest and most important Imperial cities, the 'unofficial <a href="/wiki/Capital_city" title="Capital city">capital</a>' of the Empire, particularly because <a href="/wiki/Imperial_Diet_(Holy_Roman_Empire)" title="Imperial Diet (Holy Roman Empire)">Imperial Diets</a> (<i><span lang="de" xml:lang="de">Reichstage</span></i>) and courts met at <a href="/wiki/Nuremberg_Castle" title="Nuremberg Castle">Nuremberg Castle</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Diet_of_Nuremberg" title="Diet of Nuremberg">Diets of Nuremberg</a> were an important part of the administrative structure of the Empire. The <a href="/wiki/Golden_Bull_of_1356" title="Golden Bull of 1356">Golden Bull of 1356</a>, issued by <a href="/wiki/Charles_IV,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Charles IV, Holy Roman Emperor">Emperor Charles IV</a> (reigned 1346–78), named Nuremberg as the city where newly elected <a href="/wiki/Kings_of_Germany" class="mw-redirect" title="Kings of Germany">kings of Germany</a> must hold their first Imperial Diet, making Nuremberg one of the three highest cities of the Empire.<sup id="cite_ref-HLB:N.C3.BCrnberg_Entwicklung_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HLB:N.C3.BCrnberg_Entwicklung-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>The cultural flowering of Nuremberg, in the 15th and 16th centuries, made it the center of the <a href="/wiki/German_Renaissance" title="German Renaissance">German Renaissance</a>. Increased trade routes elsewhere and the ravages of the major European wars of the 17th and 18th centuries caused the city to decline and incur sizeable debts, resulting in the city's absorption into the new <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Bavaria" title="Kingdom of Bavaria">Kingdom of Bavaria</a> on the signing of the <a href="/wiki/Confederation_of_the_Rhine" title="Confederation of the Rhine">Confederation of the Rhine</a> in 1806, becoming one of the many territorial casualties of the <a href="/wiki/Napoleonic_Wars" title="Napoleonic Wars">Napoleonic Wars</a> in a period known as the <a href="/wiki/German_mediatization" title="German mediatization">German mediatisation</a>.</p>

<p>First evidence of a settlement in the Nuremberg area can be detected as early as the year 1050 BC. Later the <a href="/wiki/Celts" title="Celts">Celts</a> settled in the Nuremberg area, <abbr title="circa">c.</abbr> 400 BC. The area of the city of Nuremberg itself — and especially today's old town — has detectable traces of a settlement as early as the 9th century.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> At that time, present-day Nuremberg was on the border between the <a href="/wiki/Bavarian_Nordgau" class="mw-redirect" title="Bavarian Nordgau">Bavarian <span lang="de" xml:lang="de">Nordgau</span></a> and the <a href="/wiki/Stem_duchy" title="Stem duchy">stem duchy</a> of <a href="/wiki/Duchy_of_Franconia" title="Duchy of Franconia">Franconia</a>. Nuremberg was probably founded around the turn of the 11th century, according to the first documentary mention of the city in 1050, as the location of an Imperial castle between the <a href="/wiki/East_Francia" title="East Francia">East Franks</a> and the Bavarian <a href="/wiki/March_of_the_Nordgau" title="March of the Nordgau">March of the <span lang="de" xml:lang="de">Nordgau</span></a>.<sup id="cite_ref-HLB:N.C3.BCrnberg_Entwicklung_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HLB:N.C3.BCrnberg_Entwicklung-1">[1]</a></sup> From 1050 to 1571, the city expanded and rose dramatically in importance due to its location on key trade routes.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Conrad_III_of_Germany" title="Conrad III of Germany">King Conrad III</a> established a <a href="/wiki/Burgrave" title="Burgrave">burgraviate</a> and the first administration and courts over the surrounding Imperial territories. The first burgraves were from the Austrian House of <a href="/wiki/Raabs_an_der_Thaya" title="Raabs an der Thaya">Raab</a> but, with the extinction of their male line around 1190, the burgraviate was inherited by the last count's son-in-law, of the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Hohenzollern" title="House of Hohenzollern">House of Hohenzollern</a>. From the late 12th century to the <a href="/wiki/Holy_Roman_Empire#Interregnum" title="Holy Roman Empire">Interregnum</a> (1254–73), however, the power of the burgraves diminished as the <a href="/wiki/Hohenstaufen" title="Hohenstaufen">Staufen</a> emperors transferred most non-military powers to a castellan, with the city administration and the municipal courts handed over to an Imperial mayor (<a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a>: <span lang="de" xml:lang="de"><i><a href="https://de.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reichsschulthei%C3%9F_(N%C3%BCrnberg)" class="extiw" title="de:Reichsschultheiß (Nürnberg)">Reichsschultheiß</a></i></span>) from 1173/74.<sup id="cite_ref-HLB:N.C3.BCrnberg_Entwicklung_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HLB:N.C3.BCrnberg_Entwicklung-1">[1]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Cath:Nuremberg_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cath:Nuremberg-3">[3]</a></sup> This castellan not only administered the imperial lands surrounding Nuremberg, but levied taxes and constituted the highest judicial court in matters relating to poaching and forestry; he also was the appointed protector of the various ecclesiastical establishments, churches and monasteries, even of the <a href="/wiki/Prince-Bishopric_of_Bamberg" title="Prince-Bishopric of Bamberg">Prince-Bishopric of Bamberg</a>. The privileges of this castellanship were transferred to the city during the late-14th and early-15th centuries. The strained relations between the burgraves and the castellan finally broke out into open enmity, which greatly influenced the history of the city.<sup id="cite_ref-Cath:Nuremberg_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cath:Nuremberg-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Nuremberg is often referred to as having been the 'unofficial <a href="/wiki/Capital_city" title="Capital city">capital</a>' of the <a href="/wiki/Holy_Roman_Empire" title="Holy Roman Empire">Holy Roman Empire</a>, particularly because <a href="/wiki/Imperial_Diet_(Holy_Roman_Empire)" title="Imperial Diet (Holy Roman Empire)">Imperial Diets</a> (<i><span lang="de" xml:lang="de">Reichstage</span></i>) and courts met at <a href="/wiki/Nuremberg_Castle" title="Nuremberg Castle">Nuremberg Castle</a> The <a href="/wiki/Diet_of_Nuremberg" title="Diet of Nuremberg">Diets of Nuremberg</a> were an important part of the administrative structure of the empire. The increasing demands of the royal court and the increasing importance of the city attracted increased trade and commerce to Nuremberg, supported by the Hohenstaufen emperors. <a href="/wiki/Frederick_II,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Frederick II, Holy Roman Emperor">Frederick II</a> (reigned 1212–50) granted the <i><span lang="de" xml:lang="de">Großen Freiheitsbrief</span></i> ("Great Letter of Freedom") in 1219, including <a href="/wiki/German_town_law" title="German town law">town rights</a>, <a href="/wiki/Imperial_immediacy" title="Imperial immediacy">Imperial immediacy</a> (<i><span lang="de" xml:lang="de">Reichsfreiheit</span></i>), the privilege to mint coins, and an independent <a href="/wiki/Customs" title="Customs">customs</a> policy, almost wholly removing the city from the purview of the burgraves.<sup id="cite_ref-HLB:N.C3.BCrnberg_Entwicklung_1-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HLB:N.C3.BCrnberg_Entwicklung-1">[1]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Cath:Nuremberg_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cath:Nuremberg-3">[3]</a></sup> Nuremberg soon became, with <a href="/wiki/Augsburg" title="Augsburg">Augsburg</a>, one of the two great trade centers on the route from <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a> to Northern Europe.</p>
<p>In 1298, the <a href="/wiki/Jews" title="Jews">Jews</a> of the town were accused of having <a href="/wiki/Host_desecration" title="Host desecration">desecrated</a> the <a href="/wiki/Sacramental_bread" title="Sacramental bread">host</a> and 698 were slain in one of the many <a href="/wiki/Rintfleisch_massacres" title="Rintfleisch massacres"><span lang="de" xml:lang="de">Rintfleisch</span> massacres</a>. Behind the massacre in 1298 was also the desire to combine the northern and southern parts of the city, which were divided by the <span lang="de" xml:lang="de"><a href="/wiki/Pegnitz_(river)" title="Pegnitz (river)">Pegnitz</a></span> river. Jews had been settled in that flood-prone area, but as the city leaders realised, this center of town was crucial to its future development. Hence, they decided that the Jewish population had to be removed. This area is now the place of the city market, the <span lang="de" xml:lang="de"><a href="/wiki/Frauenkirche,_Nuremberg" title="Frauenkirche, Nuremberg">Frauenkirche</a></span> and the <span lang="de" xml:lang="de"><a href="/wiki/Seat_of_local_government" title="Seat of local government">Rathaus</a></span>.</p>
<p>The largest gains for Nuremberg were in the 14th century, with <a href="/wiki/Louis_IV,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Louis IV, Holy Roman Emperor">Louis the Bavarian</a> (reigned 1314–47) and <a href="/wiki/Charles_IV,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Charles IV, Holy Roman Emperor">Charles IV</a> (reigned 1346–78) expanding the city's powers and granting improved customs privileges. Charles's <a href="/wiki/Golden_Bull_of_1356" title="Golden Bull of 1356">Golden Bull of 1356</a> named Nuremberg as the city where newly elected <a href="/wiki/Kings_of_Germany" class="mw-redirect" title="Kings of Germany">kings of Germany</a> must hold their first Imperial Diet, making Nuremberg one of the three highest cities of the Empire, along with <a href="/wiki/Frankfurt" title="Frankfurt">Frankfurt</a>, where kings were elected, and <a href="/wiki/Aachen" title="Aachen">Aachen</a>, where Emperors were crowned and which had been the capital of the old <a href="/wiki/Francia" title="Francia">Frankish Empire</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-HLB:N.C3.BCrnberg_Entwicklung_1-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HLB:N.C3.BCrnberg_Entwicklung-1">[1]</a></sup> The royal and Imperial connection was strengthened when <a href="/wiki/Sigismund,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Sigismund, Holy Roman Emperor">Sigismund of Luxembourg</a> (reigned 1411–37) granted the Imperial regalia to be kept permanently in Nuremberg in 1423. These remained in Nuremberg until 1796, when the advance of French troops required their removal to <a href="/wiki/Regensburg" title="Regensburg">Regensburg</a> and thence to <a href="/wiki/Vienna" title="Vienna">Vienna</a>, where they found a new home.<sup id="cite_ref-HLB:N.C3.BCrnberg_Entwicklung_1-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HLB:N.C3.BCrnberg_Entwicklung-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Charles IV had strong ties to Nuremberg, staying within its city walls 52 times and thereby strengthening its reputation amongst German cities. Charles was the patron of the <span lang="de" xml:lang="de">Frauenkirche</span>, built between 1352 and 1362 (the architect was likely <a href="/wiki/Peter_Parler" title="Peter Parler">Peter Parler</a>), where the Imperial court worshipped during its stays in Nuremberg.</p>
<p>Until the mid-13th century, the Lesser, reigning, Council consisted of 13 magistrates and 13 councillors; towards the end of the century 8 members of the practically unimportant Great Council were added, and, from 1370, 8 representatives of <a href="/wiki/Guild" title="Guild">artisans' associations</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Cath:Nuremberg_3-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cath:Nuremberg-3">[3]</a></sup> The members of the council were chosen by the wealthier class; this custom led to the establishment of a circle of "eligibles", to which the artisan class was strongly opposed since it excluded them politically.<sup id="cite_ref-Cath:Nuremberg_3-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cath:Nuremberg-3">[3]</a></sup> With the increasing importance of handicraft, a spirit of independence developed among the artisans, and they determined to have a voice in the city government. In 1349 the members of the <a href="/wiki/Guild" title="Guild">guilds</a> unsuccessfully rebelled against the patricians in the <i><span lang="de" xml:lang="de">Handwerkeraufstand</span></i> ("Craftsmen's Uprising"), supported by merchants and some councillors. This uprising was mainly political, with the agitators siding with the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Wittelsbach" title="House of Wittelsbach">Wittelsbachs</a> in the dispute over the German kingship between Louis's Bavarian heirs and the patricians, who sided with Emperor Charles. The result of this uprising was a ban on any self-organisation of the artisans in the city, abolishing the guilds that were customary elsewhere in Europe; the unions were then dissolved, and the oligarchs remained in power while Nuremberg was a free city.<sup id="cite_ref-HLB:N.C3.BCrnberg_Entwicklung_1-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HLB:N.C3.BCrnberg_Entwicklung-1">[1]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Cath:Nuremberg_3-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cath:Nuremberg-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Charles IV conferred upon the city the right to conclude alliances independently, thereby placing it upon a politically equal footing with the princes of the empire.<sup id="cite_ref-Cath:Nuremberg_3-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cath:Nuremberg-3">[3]</a></sup> The city protected itself from hostile attacks by a <a href="/wiki/Defensive_wall" title="Defensive wall">wall</a> and successfully defended its extensive trade against the burgraves. Frequent fights took place with the burgraves without, however, inflicting lasting damage upon the city. After the castle had been destroyed by fire in 1420 during a feud between <a href="/wiki/Frederick_I,_Elector_of_Brandenburg" title="Frederick I, Elector of Brandenburg">Frederick IV</a> (since 1417 <a href="/wiki/Margrave_of_Brandenburg" class="mw-redirect" title="Margrave of Brandenburg">margrave of Brandenburg</a>) and the duke of <a href="/wiki/Bavaria-Ingolstadt" title="Bavaria-Ingolstadt">Bavaria-Ingolstadt</a>, the ruins and the forest belonging to the castle were purchased by the city (1427), resulting in the city's total sovereignty within its borders; The castle had been ceded to the city by Emperor Sigismund in 1422, on the sole condition that the Imperial suite of rooms be reserved for the Emperor's use. Through these and other acquisitions the city accumulated considerable territory.<sup id="cite_ref-Cath:Nuremberg_3-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cath:Nuremberg-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1431, the population was about 22,800 including 7146 persons qualified to bear arms, 381 secular and regular priests, 744 Jews and non-citizens.<sup id="cite_ref-Cath:Nuremberg_3-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cath:Nuremberg-3">[3]</a></sup> As an emerging regional power, however Nuremberg soon came into conflict with the old dynasty, the former burgraves, who had brought large areas of the region around the city under their control as the <a href="/wiki/Margrave_of_Brandenburg-Kulmbach" class="mw-redirect" title="Margrave of Brandenburg-Kulmbach">Margrave of Brandenburg-Kulmbach</a> and <a href="/wiki/Elector_of_Brandenburg" class="mw-redirect" title="Elector of Brandenburg">Elector of Brandenburg</a>. This conflict came to a head in the <a href="/wiki/First_Margrave_War" title="First Margrave War">First Margrave War</a> in 1449–50, when <a href="/wiki/Albrecht_III_Achilles,_Elector_of_Brandenburg" title="Albrecht III Achilles, Elector of Brandenburg">Albert III <i>Achilles</i>, Elector of Brandenburg</a>, tried in vain to restore his former rights over the city. The <a href="/wiki/Hussite_Wars" title="Hussite Wars">Hussite Wars</a>, <a href="/wiki/Black_Death#Recurrence" title="Black Death">recurrence of the Black Death</a> in 1437 and the First Margrave War had reduced the city's population to 20,800 by 1450.<sup id="cite_ref-Cath:Nuremberg_3-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cath:Nuremberg-3">[3]</a></sup></p>

<p>The cultural flowering of Nuremberg, in the 15th and 16th centuries, made it the center of the <a href="/wiki/German_Renaissance" title="German Renaissance">German Renaissance</a>. The years between 1470 and 1530 are generally regarded as the city's heyday. Nuremberg traded in virtually all of the then-known world: <i><span lang="de" xml:lang="de">Nürnberger Tand geht durch alle Land</span></i> ("Nuremberg trinkets go all through the land") and Nuremberg's wealth was known as "the Imperial Treasure Chest". The city's revenues were said to have been greater than those of the whole <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Bohemia" title="Kingdom of Bohemia">kingdom of Bohemia</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Nuremberg cities maintained trade offices in many cities, such as the <i><span lang="de" xml:lang="de"><a href="https://de.wikipedia.org/wiki/N%C3%BCrnberger_Hof" class="extiw" title="de:Nürnberger Hof">Nürnberger Hof</a></span></i> in Frankfurt. At that time, many notable artists lived and worked in Nuremberg, such as <a href="/wiki/Albrecht_D%C3%BCrer" title="Albrecht Dürer">Albrecht Dürer</a> (1471–1528), <a href="/wiki/Martin_Behaim" title="Martin Behaim">Martin Behaim</a> (1459–1507) built the first <a href="/wiki/Globe" title="Globe">globe</a> and <a href="/wiki/Peter_Henlein" title="Peter Henlein">Peter Henlein</a> (c. 1485–1542) produced the first <a href="/wiki/Pocket_watch" title="Pocket watch">pocket watch</a>. Also notable from this period are the <a href="/wiki/Wood_carving" title="Wood carving">woodcarver</a> <a href="/wiki/Veit_Stoss" title="Veit Stoss">Veit Stoss</a> (1447–1533), the sculptor <a href="/wiki/Adam_Kraft" title="Adam Kraft">Adam Kraft</a> (c. 1460–1508/09) and the master <a href="/wiki/Foundry" title="Foundry">founder</a> and sculptor <a href="/wiki/Peter_Vischer_the_Elder" title="Peter Vischer the Elder">Peter Vischer the Elder</a> (c. 1460–1529). Only literature was not as dominant as the other arts, but <i><span lang="de" xml:lang="de"><a href="/wiki/Meistersinger" title="Meistersinger">meistersinger</a></span></i> (<a href="/wiki/Lyric_poetry" title="Lyric poetry">lyric poet</a>), playwright and shoemaker <a href="/wiki/Hans_Sachs" title="Hans Sachs">Hans Sachs</a> (1494–1576) provides at least one major literary figure who lived at this time in Nuremberg.</p>
<p>Nuremberg was one of the founding 27 territories of the <a href="/wiki/Franconian_Circle" title="Franconian Circle">Franconian Circle</a> at the Diet of Augsburg on 2 July 1500. At the beginning of the 16th century, siding with <a href="/wiki/Albert_IV,_Duke_of_Bavaria" title="Albert IV, Duke of Bavaria">Albert IV</a>, Duke of <a href="/wiki/Bavaria-Munich" title="Bavaria-Munich">Bavaria-Munich</a>, in the <a href="/wiki/War_of_the_Succession_of_Landshut" title="War of the Succession of Landshut">Landshut War of Succession</a> led the city to gain substantial territory, resulting in lands of 25&#160;sq&#160;mi (65&#160;km<sup>2</sup>), becoming the largest Imperial city in the Empire,<sup id="cite_ref-Cath:Nuremberg_3-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cath:Nuremberg-3">[3]</a></sup> acquisitions confirmed by <a href="/wiki/Maximilian_I,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Maximilian I, Holy Roman Emperor">Maximilian I</a> in 1505. In 1525, Nuremberg accepted the <a href="/wiki/Protestant_Reformation" class="mw-redirect" title="Protestant Reformation">Protestant Reformation</a>, and in 1532, the religious <a href="/w/index.php?title=Peace_of_Nuremberg&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Peace of Nuremberg (page does not exist)">Peace of Nuremberg</a>, by which the <a href="/wiki/Lutheranism" title="Lutheranism">Lutherans</a> gained important concessions, was signed there.<sup id="cite_ref-Cath:Nuremberg_3-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cath:Nuremberg-3">[3]</a></sup> During the 1552 revolution against <a href="/wiki/Charles_V,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Charles V, Holy Roman Emperor">Charles V</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Second_Margrave_War" title="Second Margrave War">Second Margrave War</a>, Nuremberg endeavoured to purchase its neutrality by the payment of 100,000 <a href="/wiki/Guilder" title="Guilder">guilder</a>; but <a href="/wiki/Albert_Alcibiades,_Margrave_of_Brandenburg-Kulmbach" title="Albert Alcibiades, Margrave of Brandenburg-Kulmbach">Albert Alcibiades, Margrave of Brandenburg-Kulmbach</a>, one of the leaders of the revolt, attacked the city without declaring war and forced the conclusion of a disadvantageous peace.<sup id="cite_ref-Cath:Nuremberg_3-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cath:Nuremberg-3">[3]</a></sup> At the <a href="/wiki/Peace_of_Augsburg" title="Peace of Augsburg">Peace of Augsburg</a>, the possessions of the Protestants were confirmed by the Emperor, their religious privileges extended and their independence from the jurisdiction of the Bishop of Bamberg affirmed, while the 1520s' secularisation of the monasteries was also approved.<sup id="cite_ref-Cath:Nuremberg_3-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cath:Nuremberg-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>The state of affairs in the early 16th century, <a href="/wiki/Christopher_Columbus" title="Christopher Columbus">Columbus's</a> discovery of the New World and <a href="/wiki/Bartolomeu_Dias" title="Bartolomeu Dias">Dias</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Circumnavigation" title="Circumnavigation">circumnavigation</a> of Africa and the territorial fragmentation in the Empire led to a decline in trade and, thus, the city's affluence.<sup id="cite_ref-Cath:Nuremberg_3-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cath:Nuremberg-3">[3]</a></sup> The ossification of the social hierarchy and legal structures contributed to the decline in trade; under <a href="/wiki/Leopold_I,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Leopold I, Holy Roman Emperor">Leopold I</a> (reigned 1658–1705) the patriciate was converted to a hereditary corporation, leading the merchant class to appeal to the Imperial counsellor, albeit unsuccessfully.<sup id="cite_ref-HLB:N.C3.BCrnberg_Entwicklung_1-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HLB:N.C3.BCrnberg_Entwicklung-1">[1]</a></sup> During the <a href="/wiki/Thirty_Years%27_War" title="Thirty Years' War">Thirty Years' War</a> it did not always succeed in preserving its policy of neutrality. Frequent quartering of Imperial, Swedish and <a href="/wiki/Catholic_League_(German)" title="Catholic League (German)">League</a> soldiers, war-contributions, demands for arms, semi-compulsory presents to commanders of the warring armies and the cessation of trade, caused irreparable damage to the city. The population, which in 1620 had been over 45,000, sank to 25,000.<sup id="cite_ref-Cath:Nuremberg_3-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cath:Nuremberg-3">[3]</a></sup> In 1632 during the Thirty Years' War, the city, occupied by the forces of <a href="/wiki/Gustavus_Adolphus_of_Sweden" title="Gustavus Adolphus of Sweden">Gustavus Adolphus of Sweden</a>, was <a href="/wiki/Siege_of_Nuremberg" title="Siege of Nuremberg">besieged</a> by the army of Imperial general <a href="/wiki/Albrecht_von_Wallenstein" title="Albrecht von Wallenstein">Albrecht von Wallenstein</a>. The city declined after the war and recovered its importance only in the 19th century, when it grew as an industrial center. Even after the Thirty Years' War, however, there was a late flowering of architecture and culture — secular <a href="/wiki/Baroque_architecture" title="Baroque architecture">Baroque architecture</a> is exemplified in the layout of the civic gardens built outside the city walls, and in the Protestant city's rebuilding of the <span lang="de" xml:lang="de">Egidienkirche</span>, destroyed by fire at the beginning of the 18th century and considered a significant contribution to the baroque church architecture of Middle Franconia.<sup id="cite_ref-HLB:N.C3.BCrnberg_Entwicklung_1-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HLB:N.C3.BCrnberg_Entwicklung-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>After the Thirty Years' War, Nuremberg attempted to remain detached from external affairs, but contributions were demanded for the <a href="/wiki/War_of_the_Austrian_Succession" title="War of the Austrian Succession">War of the Austrian Succession</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Seven_Years%27_War" title="Seven Years' War">Seven Years' War</a>, the former amounting to 6.5 million <a href="/wiki/Guilder" title="Guilder">guilder</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Cath:Nuremberg_3-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cath:Nuremberg-3">[3]</a></sup> Restrictions of imports and exports deprived the city of many markets for its manufactures, especially in <a href="/wiki/Archduchy_of_Austria" title="Archduchy of Austria">Austria</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Prussia" title="Kingdom of Prussia">Prussia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Electorate_of_Bavaria" title="Electorate of Bavaria">Bavaria</a>, eastern and northern Europe.<sup id="cite_ref-Cath:Nuremberg_3-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cath:Nuremberg-3">[3]</a></sup> In 1790/91, the Bavarian elector, <a href="/wiki/Charles_Theodore,_Elector_of_Bavaria" title="Charles Theodore, Elector of Bavaria">Charles Theodore</a>, appropriated part of the land obtained by the city during the Landshut War of succession, to which Bavaria had maintained its claim; Prussia claimed and occupied part of the territory in 1796.<sup id="cite_ref-Cath:Nuremberg_3-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cath:Nuremberg-3">[3]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-HLB:N.C3.BCrnberg_Territorium_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HLB:N.C3.BCrnberg_Territorium-5">[5]</a></sup> Realising its weakness, the city asked to be incorporated into Prussia but <a href="/wiki/Frederick_William_II_of_Prussia" title="Frederick William II of Prussia">Frederick William II</a> refused, fearing to offend Austria, <a href="/wiki/Russian_Empire" title="Russian Empire">Russia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Early_modern_France" title="Early modern France">France</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Cath:Nuremberg_3-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cath:Nuremberg-3">[3]</a></sup> At the Imperial diet in 1803, the independence of Nuremberg was affirmed, but on the signing of the <a href="/wiki/Confederation_of_the_Rhine" title="Confederation of the Rhine">Confederation of the Rhine</a> on 12 July 1806, it was agreed to hand the city over to Bavaria from 8 September; its population was then 25,200 and its public debt totalled 12.5 million guilder, with Bavaria guaranteeing their <a href="/wiki/Amortization" title="Amortization">amortisation</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Cath:Nuremberg_3-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cath:Nuremberg-3">[3]</a></sup></p>

<p>The Imperial City comprised some 1,200 square kilometres (460&#160;sq&#160;mi), making it one of the largest imperial cities territories; after the <a href="/wiki/History_of_Bern" title="History of Bern">Imperial City of Bern</a> left to join the <a href="/wiki/Old_Swiss_Confederacy" title="Old Swiss Confederacy">Old Swiss Confederacy</a> in 1353, only the Imperial Cities of <a href="/wiki/Ulm" title="Ulm">Ulm</a> and <a href="/wiki/Strasbourg" title="Strasbourg">Strasbourg</a> had anything like the same amount of land.<sup id="cite_ref-HLB:N.C3.BCrnberg_Territorium_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HLB:N.C3.BCrnberg_Territorium-5">[5]</a></sup> The area was divided into the Old and New Districts (<a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a>: <span lang="de" xml:lang="de"><i>Alte Landschaft</i></span> and <i><span lang="de" xml:lang="de">Neue Landschaft</span></i>). The Old District, which also included Imperial forests (<a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a>: <span lang="de" xml:lang="de"><i><a href="https://de.wikipedia.org/wiki/N%C3%BCrnberger_Reichswald" class="extiw" title="de:Nürnberger Reichswald">Nürnberger Reichswald</a></i></span>), was a conglomeration of lordships and possessions of Nuremberg burghers, monasteries and social facilities. That <a href="/wiki/High_justice" class="mw-redirect" title="High justice">high justice</a> (<i><span lang="de" xml:lang="de"><a href="https://de.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zentgericht" class="extiw" title="de:Zentgericht">Zentgericht</a></span></i> and <i><span lang="de" xml:lang="de">Freisgericht</span></i>) was administered by the <a href="/wiki/Burgraviate_of_Nuremberg" title="Burgraviate of Nuremberg">burgraviate</a> — and subsequently the margraviates of <a href="/wiki/Brandenburg-Ansbach" class="mw-redirect" title="Brandenburg-Ansbach">Brandenburg-Ansbach</a> and <a href="/wiki/Brandenburg-Bayreuth" class="mw-redirect" title="Brandenburg-Bayreuth">Brandenburg-Bayreuth</a> — was a source of constant conflict. The New District is made up of the territory gained by Nuremberg in the <a href="/wiki/War_of_the_Succession_of_Landshut" title="War of the Succession of Landshut">Landshut War of Succession</a>; in this territory, the city had full sovereignty.<sup id="cite_ref-HLB:N.C3.BCrnberg_Territorium_5-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HLB:N.C3.BCrnberg_Territorium-5">[5]</a></sup> In 1790, around 25,000 lived with city walls and a further 35,000 in the extramural territories of the city.<sup id="cite_ref-HLB:N.C3.BCrnberg_Territorium_5-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HLB:N.C3.BCrnberg_Territorium-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>The territorial expansion of imperial cities since the mid-14th century had several general causes, all found in the case of Nuremberg — the weakness of imperial power and an inability to maintain law and order; the debt crisis of neighbouring landed and knightly nobles in comparison with the capital income of the burgeoning urban middle classes; and the growing need for cities to secure an adequate supply of food for its inhabitants, raw materials for its craftsmen, and military self-protection.<sup id="cite_ref-HLB:N.C3.BCrnberg_Territorium_5-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HLB:N.C3.BCrnberg_Territorium-5">[5]</a></sup> Before the end of the 18th century, with Bavarian and Prussian annexation of Nuremberger territory, the city territory was as described below:</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Old_District">Old District</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Free_Imperial_City_of_Nuremberg&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Old District">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Old District was located mostly between the <i><span lang="de" xml:lang="de">Grenzwässern</span></i> ("border waters") of <span lang="de" xml:lang="de"><a href="https://de.wikipedia.org/wiki/Schwabach_(Regnitz)" class="extiw" title="de:Schwabach (Regnitz)">Erlanger Schwabach</a></span>, <span lang="de" xml:lang="de"><a href="/wiki/Regnitz" title="Regnitz">Regnitz</a> / <a href="/wiki/Rednitz" title="Rednitz">Rednitz</a></span> and <span lang="de" xml:lang="de"><a href="https://de.wikipedia.org/wiki/Schwarzach_(Rednitz)" class="extiw" title="de:Schwarzach (Rednitz)">Schwarzach</a></span>. It included the suburbs of <span lang="de" xml:lang="de"><a href="https://de.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gostenhof" class="extiw" title="de:Gostenhof">Gostenhof</a></span> (since 1342 a burgravial fief of the <span lang="de" xml:lang="de">Waldstromer</span> family of Nuremberg, since 1477 a Nuremberg protectorate) and <span lang="de" xml:lang="de">Wöhrd</span> (part of the burgravial <i><span lang="de" xml:lang="de"><a href="/wiki/Amt_(country_subdivision)" title="Amt (country subdivision)">Amt</a></span></i> of <span lang="de" xml:lang="de">Veste</span>, over which Nuremberg gained jurisdiction in 1427) as well as the <span lang="de" xml:lang="de">Sebald</span> and <span lang="de" xml:lang="de">St Lorenz</span> Imperial forests and the <span lang="de" xml:lang="de"><a href="https://de.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knoblauchsland" class="extiw" title="de:Knoblauchsland">Knoblauchsland</a></span>; the forests were territory directly belonging to the Empire (<a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a>: <span lang="de" xml:lang="de"><i><a href="https://de.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reichsgut" class="extiw" title="de:Reichsgut">Reichsgut</a></i></span>).<sup id="cite_ref-HLB:N.C3.BCrnberg_Territorium_5-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HLB:N.C3.BCrnberg_Territorium-5">[5]</a></sup> The fiefdom in the southern (<span lang="de" xml:lang="de">St Lorenz</span>) Imperial forest was jointly held by the Nuremberg families of <span lang="de" xml:lang="de">Waldstromer</span> (acquired by Nuremberg in 1396) and <span lang="de" xml:lang="de">Koler</span> (acquired in 1372); the northern (<span lang="de" xml:lang="de">Sebald</span>) forest, including the <span lang="de" xml:lang="de">Knoblauchsland</span>, was held by the burgraves and, thus, was acquired by Nuremberg in 1427 when it purchased the burgravial holdings, including the castle and, importantly, the right of high justice.<sup id="cite_ref-HLB:N.C3.BCrnberg_Territorium_5-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HLB:N.C3.BCrnberg_Territorium-5">[5]</a></sup> Whilst this was later disputed by the Hohenzollern margraves, the <i><span lang="de" xml:lang="de"><a href="/wiki/Reichskammergericht" title="Reichskammergericht">Reichskammergericht</a></span></i> ("Imperial Chamber Court") confirmed these rights to Nuremberg in the <i><span lang="de" xml:lang="de">Fraischprozess</span></i> in 1583, though it remained a constant source of friction.<sup id="cite_ref-HLB:N.C3.BCrnberg_Territorium_5-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HLB:N.C3.BCrnberg_Territorium-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Before 1790, Nuremberg held the <i><span lang="de" xml:lang="de"><a href="/wiki/Vogt" title="Vogt">Vogt</a></span></i> and seigneurial rights for both woodland <i><span lang="de" xml:lang="de">Ämter</span></i> of <span lang="de" xml:lang="de">Sebaldi</span> and <span lang="de" xml:lang="de">Laurenzi</span> in the Old District, the <i><span lang="de" xml:lang="de"><a href="https://de.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pflegamt" class="extiw" title="de:Pflegamt">Pflegamt</a></span></i> of <span lang="de" xml:lang="de">Gostenhof</span> and the <i><span lang="de" xml:lang="de">Amt</span></i> of the fortress with the judicial office of <span lang="de" xml:lang="de">Wöhrd</span>. At the time, the high courts held jurisdiction over the Nuremberg farmers' courts, the forest courts of the two Imperial forests and the <a href="/wiki/Beekeeper" title="Beekeeper">beekeepers</a>' courts in <span lang="de" xml:lang="de"><a href="/wiki/Feucht" title="Feucht">Feucht</a></span>.<sup id="cite_ref-HLB:N.C3.BCrnberg_Territorium_5-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HLB:N.C3.BCrnberg_Territorium-5">[5]</a></sup> Within but especially outside of the Old District, there were also <a href="/wiki/Enclave_and_exclave" title="Enclave and exclave">exclaves</a> (<a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a>: <span lang="de" xml:lang="de"><i>Straubesitz</i></span>) that were indirect estates and possessions of Nuremberg citizens and of former religious institutions (such as the monasteries secularised by the city in the 16th century) and charitable institutions (in particular the <span lang="de" xml:lang="de"><a href="https://de.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heilig-Geist-Spital_(N%C3%BCrnberg)" class="extiw" title="de:Heilig-Geist-Spital (Nürnberg)">Heilig-Geist-Spital</a></span>). These territories extended geographically from the <span lang="de" xml:lang="de"><a href="https://de.wikipedia.org/wiki/Steigerwald" class="extiw" title="de:Steigerwald">Steigerwald</a></span> and <a href="/wiki/Franconian_Switzerland" title="Franconian Switzerland">Franconian Switzerland</a> in the north to the region of <span lang="de" xml:lang="de"><a href="/wiki/Gunzenhausen" title="Gunzenhausen">Gunzenhausen</a></span> and <span lang="de" xml:lang="de"><a href="/wiki/Greding" title="Greding">Greding</a></span> in the south, from <span lang="de" xml:lang="de"><a href="/wiki/Ansbach" title="Ansbach">Ansbach</a></span> in the west to the arc of the <a href="/wiki/Franconian_Jura" title="Franconian Jura">Franconian Jura</a> in the east.<sup id="cite_ref-HLB:N.C3.BCrnberg_Territorium_5-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HLB:N.C3.BCrnberg_Territorium-5">[5]</a></sup> The <i><span lang="de" xml:lang="de">Nürnberger Landalmosenamt</span></i> ("Nuremberg rural alms <span lang="de" xml:lang="de">Amt</span>") alone — responsible, amongst other things, for the land of the former Nuremberger monasteries — managed around 1790 properties in over 500 locations. In 1497, excluding the exclaves, the Old District available to Nuremberg, there were over 28,000 people, living in 5780 households in 780 towns. These tenants owed to the Imperial city allegiance, obedience, military service and tax obligations.<sup id="cite_ref-HLB:N.C3.BCrnberg_Territorium_5-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HLB:N.C3.BCrnberg_Territorium-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="New_District">New District</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Free_Imperial_City_of_Nuremberg&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: New District">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1504/05, the New District comprised <span lang="de" xml:lang="de">Pflegämtern</span> in the following places, all now in <span lang="de" xml:lang="de"><i><a href="/wiki/Landkreis" class="mw-redirect" title="Landkreis">Landkreis</a></i> <a href="/wiki/N%C3%BCrnberger_Land" title="Nürnberger Land">Nürnberger Land</a></span> except where indicated:<sup id="cite_ref-HLB:N.C3.BCrnberg_Territorium_5-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HLB:N.C3.BCrnberg_Territorium-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li><span lang="de" xml:lang="de"><a href="/wiki/Altdorf_bei_N%C3%BCrnberg" title="Altdorf bei Nürnberg">Altdorf</a></span></li>
<li><span lang="de" xml:lang="de"><a href="/wiki/Betzenstein" title="Betzenstein">Betzenstein</a></span> with <span lang="de" xml:lang="de"><a href="https://de.wikipedia.org/wiki/Burgruine_Stierberg" class="extiw" title="de:Burgruine Stierberg">Stierberg</a></span>, now in <span lang="de" xml:lang="de"><i>Lkr</i> <a href="/wiki/Bayreuth_(district)" title="Bayreuth (district)">Bayreuth</a></span></li>
<li><span lang="de" xml:lang="de"><a href="/wiki/Engelthal" title="Engelthal">Engelthal</a></span></li>
<li><span lang="de" xml:lang="de"><a href="/wiki/Hersbruck" title="Hersbruck">Hersbruck</a></span></li>
<li><span lang="de" xml:lang="de"><a href="/wiki/Hiltpoltstein" title="Hiltpoltstein">Hiltpoltstein</a></span>, bought in 1503 now in <span lang="de" xml:lang="de"><i>Lkr</i> <a href="/wiki/Forchheim_(district)" title="Forchheim (district)">Forchheim</a></span>)</li>
<li><span lang="de" xml:lang="de"><a href="/wiki/Kirchensittenbach" title="Kirchensittenbach">Hohenstein</a></span> and <span lang="de" xml:lang="de"><a href="/wiki/Simmelsdorf" title="Simmelsdorf">Wildenfels</a></span>, bought in 1505 and 1511</li>
<li><span lang="de" xml:lang="de"><a href="/wiki/Lauf_an_der_Pegnitz" title="Lauf an der Pegnitz">Lauf</a></span>, <span lang="de" xml:lang="de"><a href="/wiki/Happurg" title="Happurg">Reicheneck</a></span> and <span lang="de" xml:lang="de"><a href="/wiki/Velden_(Pegnitz)" title="Velden (Pegnitz)">Velden</a></span></li>
<li><span lang="de" xml:lang="de"><a href="/wiki/Etzelwang" title="Etzelwang">Hauseck</a></span>, now in <span lang="de" xml:lang="de"><i>Lkr</i> <a href="/wiki/Amberg-Sulzbach" title="Amberg-Sulzbach">Amberg-Sulzbach</a></span></li>
<li><span lang="de" xml:lang="de">Pflegamt <a href="/wiki/Gr%C3%A4fenberg,_Bavaria" title="Gräfenberg, Bavaria">Gräfenberg</a></span> (now in <span lang="de" xml:lang="de"><i>Lkr</i> Forchheim</span>) was successively acquired, in 1347 by Nuremberger families and in 1536 by the city itself</li>
<li><span lang="de" xml:lang="de"><a href="https://de.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pflegamt_Lichtenau" class="extiw" title="de:Pflegamt Lichtenau">Pflegamt Lichtenau</a></span>, purchased in 1406, now in <span lang="de" xml:lang="de"><i>Lkr</i> <a href="/wiki/Ansbach" title="Ansbach">Ansbach</a></span></li>
</ul>
<p>The structure of the Nuremberg <i><span lang="de" xml:lang="de">Pflegämter</span></i> resembles the administrative structure of the <a href="/wiki/Electoral_Palatinate" title="Electoral Palatinate">Electoral Palatinate</a> and the <a href="/wiki/History_of_Bavaria#Under_the_Wittelsbach_dynasty" title="History of Bavaria">duchies of Bavaria</a> office structure prior to 1504.<sup id="cite_ref-HLB:N.C3.BCrnberg_Territorium_5-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HLB:N.C3.BCrnberg_Territorium-5">[5]</a></sup> In 1513, the Nuremberg <i><span lang="de" xml:lang="de">Pflegämter</span></i> were placed under the newly created <i><span lang="de" xml:lang="de">Landpflegamt</span></i> as an intermediate authority. In contrast to the Old District, the <i><span lang="de" xml:lang="de">Pflegämter</span></i> of the New District were demarcated with stones showing the limits of the city's judicial, financial and administrative powers exercised by the district.<sup id="cite_ref-HLB:N.C3.BCrnberg_Territorium_5-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HLB:N.C3.BCrnberg_Territorium-5">[5]</a></sup> Only in the <i><span lang="de" xml:lang="de">Pflegämter</span></i> of <span lang="de" xml:lang="de">Altdorf</span> and <span lang="de" xml:lang="de">Lauf</span> and extending into parts of the Imperial forests, did the margraviate deny high justice to the city authorities; the self-government of cities <span lang="de" xml:lang="de">Altdorf</span> (from 1575 including the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Altdorf" title="University of Altdorf">University of Altdorf</a>), <span lang="de" xml:lang="de">Lauf</span>, <span lang="de" xml:lang="de">Hersbruck</span>, <span lang="de" xml:lang="de">Velden</span>, <span lang="de" xml:lang="de">Betzenstein</span> and <span lang="de" xml:lang="de">Gräfenberg</span> remained under Nuremberger administration.<sup id="cite_ref-HLB:N.C3.BCrnberg_Territorium_5-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HLB:N.C3.BCrnberg_Territorium-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Gradual_mediatisation_from_1790">Gradual mediatisation from 1790</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Free_Imperial_City_of_Nuremberg&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Gradual mediatisation from 1790">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:502px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Ansbach-Bayreuth_1805.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f0/Ansbach-Bayreuth_1805.png/500px-Ansbach-Bayreuth_1805.png" width="500" height="350" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f0/Ansbach-Bayreuth_1805.png/750px-Ansbach-Bayreuth_1805.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f0/Ansbach-Bayreuth_1805.png/1000px-Ansbach-Bayreuth_1805.png 2x" data-file-width="1180" data-file-height="825" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Ansbach-Bayreuth_1805.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Prussia" title="Kingdom of Prussia">Prussian</a> provinces of <span lang="de" xml:lang="de"><a href="/wiki/Brandenburg-Ansbach" class="mw-redirect" title="Brandenburg-Ansbach">Ansbach</a></span> and <span lang="de" xml:lang="de"><a href="/wiki/Brandenburg-Bayreuth" class="mw-redirect" title="Brandenburg-Bayreuth">Bayreuth</a></span> in 1805, with Nuremberg just south of center, showing most of the city's erstwhile possessions now in <span lang="de" xml:lang="de"><a href="/wiki/House_of_Hohenzollern" title="House of Hohenzollern">Hohenzollern</a></span> hands.

<div class="legend" style="-webkit-column-break-inside: avoid;page-break-inside: avoid;break-inside: avoid-column"><span class="legend-color" style="display:inline-block; width:1.5em; height:1.5em; margin:1px 0; border:1px solid silver; background-color: #C1EAFF; color:black; font-size:100%; text-align:center;">&#160;</span>&#160;<a href="/wiki/Principality_of_Ansbach" title="Principality of Ansbach">Province of Ansbach</a></div>
<div class="legend" style="-webkit-column-break-inside: avoid;page-break-inside: avoid;break-inside: avoid-column"><span class="legend-color" style="display:inline-block; width:1.5em; height:1.5em; margin:1px 0; border:1px solid silver; background-color: #CEF7D6; color:black; font-size:100%; text-align:center;">&#160;</span>&#160;<a href="/wiki/Principality_of_Bayreuth" title="Principality of Bayreuth">Province of Bayreuth</a></div>
</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>In both Margraves' Wars (<a href="/wiki/First_Margrave_War" title="First Margrave War">1449/50</a> and <a href="/wiki/Second_Margrave_War" title="Second Margrave War">1552–54</a>) and in the <a href="/wiki/Thirty_Years%27_War" title="Thirty Years' War">Thirty Years' War</a>, the city's territory and its population were violently affected by quartering of troops, looting, troop movements and disease.<sup id="cite_ref-HLB:N.C3.BCrnberg_Territorium_5-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HLB:N.C3.BCrnberg_Territorium-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>After the ducal line of Bavaria fell extinct and the <a href="/wiki/Electorate_of_Bavaria" title="Electorate of Bavaria">Electorate of Bavaria</a> was inherited by <a href="/wiki/Charles_Theodore,_Elector_of_Bavaria" title="Charles Theodore, Elector of Bavaria">Charles Theodore</a>, <a href="/wiki/Palatinate-Sulzbach" title="Palatinate-Sulzbach">Count Palatine of Sulzbach</a> in 1777, the Electorate began to claim Nuremberg's exclaves in the <a href="/wiki/Upper_Palatinate" title="Upper Palatinate">Upper Palatinate</a> as well as counting the <i><span lang="de" xml:lang="de">Ämter</span></i> of <span lang="de" xml:lang="de"><a href="/wiki/Heideck" title="Heideck">Heideck</a></span> and <span lang="de" xml:lang="de"><a href="/wiki/Hilpoltstein" title="Hilpoltstein">Hilpoltstein</a></span> into the <a href="/wiki/Palatinate-Neuburg" title="Palatinate-Neuburg">County Palatine of <span lang="de" xml:lang="de">Neuburg</span></a> for judicial and tax purposes.<sup id="cite_ref-HLB:N.C3.BCrnberg_Territorium_5-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HLB:N.C3.BCrnberg_Territorium-5">[5]</a></sup> In 1790/91, the Electorate used its historic claim from before the <a href="/wiki/War_of_the_Succession_of_Landshut" title="War of the Succession of Landshut">Landshut War of Succession</a> to occupy Nuremberger territories in what became known as the Bavarian Sequestrations (<i><span lang="de" xml:lang="de">Bayerische Sequestrationen</span></i>).<sup id="cite_ref-HLB:N.C3.BCrnberg_Territorium_5-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HLB:N.C3.BCrnberg_Territorium-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Large parts of the <i><span lang="de" xml:lang="de">Pflegämter</span></i> <span lang="de" xml:lang="de">Hiltpoltstein</span>, <span lang="de" xml:lang="de">Gräfenberg</span> and <span lang="de" xml:lang="de">Velden</span> and were now occupied, which led to corresponding tax losses for Nuremberg; protests to the Emperor and the Empire were in vain, due to the military-political situation at the time.<sup id="cite_ref-HLB:N.C3.BCrnberg_Territorium_5-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HLB:N.C3.BCrnberg_Territorium-5">[5]</a></sup> The power-play over the Nuremberger legacy saw the Electorate providing goodwill and support to <a href="/wiki/French_Revolution" title="French Revolution">Revolutionary France</a> in competition with <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Prussia" title="Kingdom of Prussia">Prussia</a>, to whom the two Franconian margraviates had fallen in 1791.<sup id="cite_ref-HLB:N.C3.BCrnberg_Territorium_5-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HLB:N.C3.BCrnberg_Territorium-5">[5]</a></sup> Since then, the <a href="/wiki/Minister_President_of_Prussia" title="Minister President of Prussia">Minister President of Prussia</a>, <span lang="de" xml:lang="de"><a href="/wiki/Karl_August_von_Hardenberg" title="Karl August von Hardenberg">Karl August von Hardenberg</a></span> (1750–1822), had been trying to create an integral Prussian Province of Franconia. When Prussia, in the course of her <i><span lang="de" xml:lang="de">Revindikationspolitik</span></i>, had already claimed the margravial rights of <a href="/wiki/High_justice" class="mw-redirect" title="High justice">high justice</a> (<i><span lang="de" xml:lang="de">Fraischbezirk</span></i>) over the Old District in 1796, Nuremberg was all but restricted to the territory circumscribed by town walls; Nuremberg retained the right of high justice only over the reduced <span lang="de" xml:lang="de"><i>Pflegamt</i> <a href="/wiki/Lichtenau,_Bavaria" title="Lichtenau, Bavaria">Lichtenau</a></span> and the exclaves within the <a href="/wiki/Prince-Bishopric_of_Bamberg" title="Prince-Bishopric of Bamberg">Prince-Bishopric of Bamberg</a>, also diminished by annexation by the Electorate.<sup id="cite_ref-HLB:N.C3.BCrnberg_Territorium_5-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HLB:N.C3.BCrnberg_Territorium-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1972, most of the former territories of Nuremberg — particularly those in the New District — were reunited into the Bavarian <i><span lang="de" xml:lang="de"><a href="/wiki/Landkreis" class="mw-redirect" title="Landkreis">Landkreis</a></span></i> of <span lang="de" xml:lang="de"><a href="/wiki/N%C3%BCrnberger_Land" title="Nürnberger Land">Nürnberger Land</a></span>.<sup id="cite_ref-HLB:N.C3.BCrnberg_Territorium_5-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HLB:N.C3.BCrnberg_Territorium-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
