<h2 id="mp-tfa-h2" style="margin:0.5em; background:#cef2e0; font-family:inherit; font-size:120%; font-weight:bold; border:1px solid #a3bfb1; color:#000; padding:0.2em 0.4em;"><span id="From_today's_featured_article"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="From_today.27s_featured_article">From today's featured article</span></h2>
<div id="mp-tfa" style="padding:0.1em 0.6em;">
<div id="mp-tfa-img" style="float: left; margin: 0.5em 0.9em 0.4em 0em;">
<div class="thumbinner mp-thumb" style="background: transparent; border: none; padding: 0; max-width: 100px;"><a href="/wiki/File:St_Botolph,_Quarrington_-_geograph.org.uk_-_105619.jpg" class="image" title="St Botolph's Church"><img alt="St Botolph's Church" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/37/St_Botolph%2C_Quarrington_-_geograph.org.uk_-_105619.jpg/100px-St_Botolph%2C_Quarrington_-_geograph.org.uk_-_105619.jpg" width="100" height="133" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/37/St_Botolph%2C_Quarrington_-_geograph.org.uk_-_105619.jpg/150px-St_Botolph%2C_Quarrington_-_geograph.org.uk_-_105619.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/37/St_Botolph%2C_Quarrington_-_geograph.org.uk_-_105619.jpg/200px-St_Botolph%2C_Quarrington_-_geograph.org.uk_-_105619.jpg 2x" data-file-width="480" data-file-height="640" /></a></div>

<p><b><a href="/wiki/St_Botolph%27s_Church,_Quarrington" title="St Botolph's Church, Quarrington">St Botolph's Church</a></b> is an <a href="/wiki/Church_of_England" title="Church of England">Anglican</a> place of worship in the village of <a href="/wiki/Quarrington,_Lincolnshire" title="Quarrington, Lincolnshire">Quarrington</a>, part of the <a href="/wiki/Civil_parish" title="Civil parish">civil parish</a> of <a href="/wiki/Sleaford" title="Sleaford">Sleaford</a> in <a href="/wiki/Lincolnshire" title="Lincolnshire">Lincolnshire</a>, England. By the time <i><a href="/wiki/Domesday_Book" title="Domesday Book">Domesday Book</a></i> was compiled in 1086, a church in Quarrington was part of <a href="/wiki/Ramsey_Abbey" title="Ramsey Abbey">Ramsey Abbey</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Fief" title="Fief">fee</a>, and around 1165 it was granted to <a href="/wiki/Haverholme_Priory" title="Haverholme Priory">Haverholme Priory</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Advowson" title="Advowson">right to present the rector</a> was claimed by the Abbey in the 13th century, by the <a href="/wiki/Bishop_of_Lincoln" title="Bishop of Lincoln">Bishop of Lincoln</a> in the early 16th century, and by Robert Carre and his descendants after Carre acquired a manor at Quarrington. The oldest parts of the current building date to the 13th century, although substantial rebuilding took place over the following century. Renovations followed and the local architect Charles Kirk the Younger carried out <a href="/wiki/Victorian_restoration" title="Victorian restoration">restoration</a> work in 1862 and 1863, when he added a <a href="/wiki/Chancel" title="Chancel">chancel</a> in his parents' memory. The church consists of a tower and spire with a <a href="/wiki/Nave" title="Nave">nave</a> and north aisle spanning eastwards to the chancel. With capacity for 124 people, the church serves the <a href="/wiki/Ecclesiastic_parish" class="mw-redirect" title="Ecclesiastic parish">ecclesiastic parish</a> of Quarrington with Old Sleaford. Recognised for its age and <a href="/wiki/Tracery" title="Tracery">tracery</a>, the church has been designated a grade II* <a href="/wiki/Listed_building" title="Listed building">listed building</a>. (<a href="/wiki/St_Botolph%27s_Church,_Quarrington" title="St Botolph's Church, Quarrington"><b>Full&#160;article...</b></a>)</p>

<h2 id="mp-tfp-h2" style="margin:0.5em; background:#ddcef2; font-family:inherit; font-size:120%; font-weight:bold; border:1px solid #afa3bf; color:#000; padding:0.2em 0.4em"><span id="Today's_featured_picture"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Today.27s_featured_picture">Today's featured picture</span></h2>
<div id="mp-tfp" style="margin:0.1em 0.4em 0.6em;">
<table role="presentation" style="margin:0 3px 3px; width:100%; text-align:left; background-color:transparent; border-collapse: collapse;">
<tr>
<td style="padding:0 0.9em 0 0;"><a href="/wiki/File:The_Miracle_at_the_Grave_of_Elisha_by_Jan_Nagel_(d_1602).jpg" class="image" title="Elisha"><img alt="Elisha" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4f/The_Miracle_at_the_Grave_of_Elisha_by_Jan_Nagel_%28d_1602%29.jpg/300px-The_Miracle_at_the_Grave_of_Elisha_by_Jan_Nagel_%28d_1602%29.jpg" width="300" height="349" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4f/The_Miracle_at_the_Grave_of_Elisha_by_Jan_Nagel_%28d_1602%29.jpg/450px-The_Miracle_at_the_Grave_of_Elisha_by_Jan_Nagel_%28d_1602%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4f/The_Miracle_at_the_Grave_of_Elisha_by_Jan_Nagel_%28d_1602%29.jpg/600px-The_Miracle_at_the_Grave_of_Elisha_by_Jan_Nagel_%28d_1602%29.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2362" data-file-height="2750" /></a></td>
<td style="padding:0 6px 0 0">
<p><b><a href="/wiki/Elisha" title="Elisha">Elisha</a></b> was a <a href="/wiki/Hebrew" class="mw-redirect" title="Hebrew">Hebrew</a> <a href="/wiki/Prophet" title="Prophet">prophet</a> and a <a href="/wiki/Thaumaturgy" title="Thaumaturgy">wonder-worker</a> who is venerated in <a href="/wiki/Judaism" title="Judaism">Judaism</a>, <a href="/wiki/Christianity" title="Christianity">Christianity</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Islam" title="Islam">Islam</a>. According to the <a href="/wiki/Books_of_Kings" title="Books of Kings">Books of Kings</a>, he was anointed the successor of <a href="/wiki/Elijah" title="Elijah">Elijah</a> and served as the "prophet in Israel" for more than six decades. During this time, many miracles were attributed to him.</p>
<p>Shown here is <i>The Miracle at the Grave of Elisha</i>, an oil painting on panel which was completed by the Dutch painter <a href="/wiki/Jan_Nagel_(painter)" title="Jan Nagel (painter)">Jan Nagel</a> in 1596. It depicts part of the <a href="/wiki/Bible" title="Bible">Biblical</a> account of Elisha, a <a href="/wiki/Miracle" title="Miracle">miracle</a> that occurred after his death. Following 2&#160;Kings&#160;13:21, the painting shows a dead man who was "revived, and stood up on his feet" after touching Elisha's interred bones.</p>
