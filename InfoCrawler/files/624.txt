<p>A <b>bus</b> (archaically also <b>omnibus</b>, <b>multibus</b>, <b>motorbus</b>, <b>autobus</b>) is a <a href="/wiki/Road_vehicle" class="mw-redirect" title="Road vehicle">road vehicle</a> designed to carry many <a href="/wiki/Passenger" title="Passenger">passengers</a>. Buses can have a capacity as high as 300 passengers.<sup id="cite_ref-Jongo_News_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jongo_News-1">[1]</a></sup> The most common type of bus is the <a href="/wiki/Single-decker_bus" class="mw-redirect" title="Single-decker bus">single-decker</a> <a href="/wiki/Rigid_bus" title="Rigid bus">rigid bus</a>, with larger loads carried by <a href="/wiki/Double-decker_bus" title="Double-decker bus">double-decker</a> and <a href="/wiki/Articulated_bus" title="Articulated bus">articulated buses</a>, and smaller loads carried by <a href="/wiki/Midibus" title="Midibus">midibuses</a> and <a href="/wiki/Minibus" title="Minibus">minibuses</a>; <a href="/wiki/Coach_(vehicle)" class="mw-redirect" title="Coach (vehicle)">coaches</a> are used for longer-distance services. Many types of buses, such as city transit buses and inter-city coaches, charge a fare. Other types, such as elementary or secondary school buses or shuttle buses within a post-secondary education campus do not charge a fare. In many jurisdictions, <a href="/wiki/Bus_driver" title="Bus driver">bus drivers</a> require a special licence above and beyond a regular <a href="/wiki/Driver%27s_licence" class="mw-redirect" title="Driver's licence">driver's licence</a>.</p>
<p>Buses may be used for <a href="/wiki/Public_transport_bus_service" title="Public transport bus service">scheduled bus transport</a>, <a href="/wiki/Coach_(scheduled_transport)" class="mw-redirect" title="Coach (scheduled transport)">scheduled coach transport</a>, <a href="/wiki/School_transport" class="mw-redirect" title="School transport">school transport</a>, private hire, or <a href="/wiki/Tour_bus" class="mw-redirect" title="Tour bus">tourism</a>; promotional buses may be used for <a href="/wiki/Political_campaign" title="Political campaign">political campaigns</a> and others are privately operated for a wide range of purposes, including rock and pop band tour vehicles.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Horsebus" title="Horsebus">Horse-drawn buses</a> were used from the 1820s, followed by <a href="/wiki/Steam_bus" title="Steam bus">steam buses</a> in the 1830s, and electric <a href="/wiki/Trolleybus" title="Trolleybus">trolleybuses</a> in 1882. The first <a href="/wiki/Internal_combustion_engine" title="Internal combustion engine">internal combustion engine</a> buses, or <a href="/wiki/Motor_bus" title="Motor bus">motor buses</a>, were used in 1895.<sup id="cite_ref-Eckermann2001_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Eckermann2001-2">[2]</a></sup> Recently, interest has been growing in <a href="/wiki/Hybrid_electric_bus" title="Hybrid electric bus">hybrid electric buses</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fuel_cell_bus" title="Fuel cell bus">fuel cell buses</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Electric_bus" title="Electric bus">electric buses</a>, as well as ones powered by <a href="/wiki/Compressed_natural_gas" title="Compressed natural gas">compressed natural gas</a> or <a href="/wiki/Biodiesel" title="Biodiesel">biodiesel</a>. As of the 2010s, <a href="/wiki/Bus_manufacturing" title="Bus manufacturing">bus manufacturing</a> is increasingly <a href="/wiki/Globalisation" class="mw-redirect" title="Globalisation">globalised</a>, with the same designs appearing around the world.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span></h2>
<p>Bus is a <a href="/wiki/Clipping_(morphology)" title="Clipping (morphology)">clipped</a> form of the Latin word <i>omnibus</i>. The first horse-drawn omnibus service was started by a businessman named Stanislas Baudry in the French city of <a href="/wiki/Nantes" title="Nantes">Nantes</a> in 1823. The first vehicles stopped in front of the shop of a hatter named Omn√©s, which had a large sign reading "Omnes Omnibus", a pun on the Latin-sounding name of that hatter; <i>omnes</i> means "all" and <i>omnibus</i> means (among other things) "for all" in <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a>. Nantes citizens soon gave the nickname omnibus to the vehicle. The omnibus in Nantes was a success and Baudry moved to Paris and launched the first omnibus service there in April 1828. A similar service was introduced in London in 1829.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFierro19961031_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFierro19961031-3">[3]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-amtuir_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-amtuir-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Steam_buses">Steam buses</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Obeissante.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3b/Obeissante.jpg/220px-Obeissante.jpg" width="220" height="167" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3b/Obeissante.jpg/330px-Obeissante.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/3/3b/Obeissante.jpg 2x" data-file-width="401" data-file-height="305" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">

<a href="/wiki/Am%C3%A9d%C3%A9e_Boll%C3%A9e" title="Am√©d√©e Boll√©e">Am√©d√©e Boll√©e</a>'s <i>L'Ob√©issante</i> (1875)</div>

<p>Regular intercity bus services by steam-powered buses were pioneered in England in the 1830s by <a href="/wiki/Walter_Hancock" title="Walter Hancock">Walter Hancock</a> and by associates of <a href="/wiki/Goldsworthy_Gurney" title="Goldsworthy Gurney">Sir Goldsworthy Gurney</a>, among others, running reliable services over road conditions which were too hazardous for horse-drawn transportation.</p>
<p>The first mechanically propelled omnibus appeared on the streets of London on 22 April 1833.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Steam carriages were much less likely to overturn, they travelled faster than horse-drawn carriages, they were much cheaper to run, and caused much less damage to the road surface due to their wide tyres.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>However, the heavy road <a href="/wiki/Toll_road" title="Toll road">tolls</a> imposed by the <a href="/wiki/Turnpike_trusts" title="Turnpike trusts">turnpike trusts</a> discouraged steam road vehicles and left the way clear for the horse bus companies, and from 1861 onwards, harsh legislation virtually eliminated mechanically propelled vehicles from the roads of Great Britain for 30 years, the <a href="/wiki/Locomotive_Act" class="mw-redirect" title="Locomotive Act">Locomotive Act</a> of that year imposing restrictive speed limits on "road locomotives" of 5&#160;mph in towns and cities, and 10&#160;mph in the country.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Trolleybuses">Trolleybuses</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:First_Trolleybuss_of_Siemens_in_Berlin_1882.gif" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/60/First_Trolleybuss_of_Siemens_in_Berlin_1882.gif/220px-First_Trolleybuss_of_Siemens_in_Berlin_1882.gif" width="220" height="171" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/6/60/First_Trolleybuss_of_Siemens_in_Berlin_1882.gif 1.5x" data-file-width="318" data-file-height="247" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:First_Trolleybuss_of_Siemens_in_Berlin_1882.gif" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
World's first trolleybus, Berlin 1882</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>In parallel to the development of the bus was the invention of the electric trolleybus, typically fed through <a href="/wiki/Trolley_pole" title="Trolley pole">trolley poles</a> by <a href="/wiki/Overhead_lines" class="mw-redirect" title="Overhead lines">overhead wires</a>. The Siemens brothers, <a href="/wiki/William_Siemens" class="mw-redirect" title="William Siemens">William</a> in England and <a href="/wiki/Ernst_Werner_von_Siemens" class="mw-redirect" title="Ernst Werner von Siemens">Ernst Werner</a> in Germany, collaborated on the development of the trolleybus concept. Sir William first proposed the idea in an article to the <i><a href="/wiki/Royal_Society_of_Arts" title="Royal Society of Arts">Journal of the Society of Arts</a></i> in 1881 as an "...arrangement by which an ordinary omnibus...would have a suspender thrown at intervals from one side of the street to the other, and two wires hanging from these suspenders; allowing contact rollers to run on these two wires, the current could be conveyed to the tram-car, and back again to the dynamo machine at the station, without the necessity of running upon rails at all."<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>The first such vehicle, the <a href="/wiki/Electromote" title="Electromote">Electromote</a>, was made by his brother Dr. Ernst Werner von Siemens and presented to the public in 1882 in <a href="/wiki/Halensee" title="Halensee">Halensee</a>, <a href="/wiki/German_Empire" title="German Empire">Germany</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Although this experimental vehicle fulfilled all the technical criteria of a typical trolleybus, it was dismantled in the same year after the demonstration.<sup id="cite_ref-CSDunbar_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CSDunbar-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>Max Schiemann opened a passenger-carrying trolleybus in 1901 near <a href="/wiki/Dresden" title="Dresden">Dresden</a>, in Germany. Although this system operated only until 1904, Schiemann had developed what is now the standard trolleybus current collection system. In the early days, a few other methods of current collection were used. <a href="/wiki/Leeds" title="Leeds">Leeds</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bradford" title="Bradford">Bradford</a> became the first cities to put trolleybuses into service in Great Britain on 20 June 1911.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Motor_buses">Motor buses</span></h3>

<p>In <a href="/wiki/Siegerland" title="Siegerland">Siegerland</a>, Germany, two passenger bus lines ran briefly, but unprofitably, in 1895 using a six-passenger motor carriage developed from the 1893 <a href="/wiki/Benz_Viktoria" title="Benz Viktoria">Benz Viktoria</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Eckermann2001_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Eckermann2001-2">[2]</a></sup> Another commercial bus line using the same model Benz omnibuses ran for a short time in 1898 in the rural area around <a href="/wiki/Llandudno" title="Llandudno">Llandudno</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wales" title="Wales">Wales</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Ward1974_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ward1974-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Daimler_Motoren_Gesellschaft" class="mw-redirect" title="Daimler Motoren Gesellschaft">Daimler</a> also produced one of the earliest motor-bus models in 1898, selling a double-decker bus to the Motor Traction Company which was first used on the streets of London on 23 April 1898. <sup id="cite_ref-Daimler_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Daimler-13">[13]</a></sup> The vehicle had a maximum speed of 18 kph and accommodated up to 20 passengers, in an enclosed area below and on an open-air platform above. With the success and popularity of this bus, Daimler expanded production, selling more buses to companies in London and, in 1899, to <a href="/wiki/Stockholm" title="Stockholm">Stockholm</a> and <a href="/wiki/Speyer" title="Speyer">Speyer</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Daimler_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Daimler-13">[13]</a></sup>Daimler also entered into a partnership with the British company Milnes and developed a new double-decker in 1902 that became the market standard.</p>

<p>The first mass-produced bus model was the <a href="/wiki/LGOC_B-type" title="LGOC B-type">B-type</a> <a href="/wiki/Double-decker_bus" title="Double-decker bus">double-decker bus</a>, designed by <a href="/wiki/Frank_Searle_(1874-1948)" class="mw-redirect" title="Frank Searle (1874-1948)">Frank Searle</a> and operated by the <a href="/wiki/London_General_Omnibus_Company" title="London General Omnibus Company">London General Omnibus Company</a> ‚Äì it entered service in 1910, and almost 3,000 had been built by the end of the decade. Hundreds saw military service on the <a href="/wiki/Western_Front_(World_War_I)" title="Western Front (World War I)">Western Front</a> during the <a href="/wiki/First_World_War" class="mw-redirect" title="First World War">First World War</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>

<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Daimler_CC_Bus_(1912).jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>

</div>
</div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Yellow_Coach_Manufacturing_Company" title="Yellow Coach Manufacturing Company">Yellow Coach Manufacturing Company</a>, which rapidly became a major manufacturer of buses in the US, was founded in Chicago in 1923 by <a href="/wiki/John_D._Hertz" title="John D. Hertz">John D. Hertz</a>. <a href="/wiki/General_Motors" title="General Motors">General Motors</a> purchased a majority stake in 1925 and changed its name to the Yellow Truck and Coach Manufacturing Company. They then purchased the balance of the shares in 1943 to form the <a href="/wiki/GMC_(Truck)" class="mw-redirect" title="GMC (Truck)">GM Truck and Coach Division</a>.</p>
<p>Models expanded in the 20th century, leading to the widespread introduction of the contemporary recognizable form of full-sized buses from the 1950s. The <a href="/wiki/AEC_Routemaster" title="AEC Routemaster">AEC Routemaster</a>, developed in the 1950s, was a pioneering design and remains an icon of London to this day.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> The innovative design used lightweight aluminium and techniques developed in aircraft production during <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> As well as a novel weight-saving integral design, it also introduced for the first time on a bus <a href="/wiki/Independent_suspension" title="Independent suspension">independent front suspension</a>, <a href="/wiki/Power_steering" title="Power steering">power steering</a>, a fully <a href="/wiki/Automatic_gearbox" class="mw-redirect" title="Automatic gearbox">automatic gearbox</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Hydraulic_brake" title="Hydraulic brake">power-hydraulic braking</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-routemasterorghome_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-routemasterorghome-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Types">Types</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Athens_bus_interior_in_2013.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/ff/Athens_bus_interior_in_2013.jpg/220px-Athens_bus_interior_in_2013.jpg" width="220" height="166" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/ff/Athens_bus_interior_in_2013.jpg/330px-Athens_bus_interior_in_2013.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/ff/Athens_bus_interior_in_2013.jpg/440px-Athens_bus_interior_in_2013.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2592" data-file-height="1952" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Athens_bus_interior_in_2013.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Interior of a bus</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Formats include <a href="/wiki/Single-decker_bus" class="mw-redirect" title="Single-decker bus">single-decker bus</a>, double-decker bus (both usually with a <a href="/wiki/Rigid_bus" title="Rigid bus">rigid chassis</a>) and <a href="/wiki/Articulated_bus" title="Articulated bus">articulated bus</a> (or 'bendy-bus') the prevalence of which varies from country to country. <a href="/wiki/Bi-articulated_bus" title="Bi-articulated bus">Bi-articulated buses</a> are also manufactured, and passenger-carrying trailers‚Äîeither towed behind a <a href="/wiki/Rigid_bus" title="Rigid bus">rigid bus</a> (a <a href="/wiki/Trailer_(vehicle)" title="Trailer (vehicle)">bus trailer</a>) or hauled as a trailer by a <a href="/wiki/Truck" title="Truck">truck</a> (a <a href="/wiki/Trailer_bus" title="Trailer bus">trailer bus</a>). Smaller <a href="/wiki/Midibus" title="Midibus">midibuses</a> have a lower capacity and <a href="/wiki/Open-top_bus" class="mw-redirect" title="Open-top bus">open-top buses</a> are typically used for leisure purposes. In many new fleets, particularly in local transit systems, a shift to <a href="/wiki/Low-floor_bus" title="Low-floor bus">low-floor buses</a> is occurring, primarily for easier accessibility. Coaches are designed for longer-distance travel and are typically fitted with individual high-backed reclining seats, seat belts, toilets, and <a href="/wiki/Audio-visual" class="mw-redirect" title="Audio-visual">audio-visual</a> entertainment systems, and can operate at higher speeds with more capacity for luggage. Coaches may be single- or double-deckers, articulated, and often include a separate luggage compartment under the passenger floor. <a href="/wiki/Guided_bus" title="Guided bus">Guided buses</a> are fitted with technology to allow them to run in designated guideways, allowing the controlled alignment at <a href="/wiki/Bus_stop" title="Bus stop">bus stops</a> and less space taken up by guided lanes than conventional roads or <a href="/wiki/Bus_lane" title="Bus lane">bus lanes</a>.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Bus_manufacturing" title="Bus manufacturing">Bus manufacturing</a> may be by a single company (an integral manufacturer), or by one manufacturer's building a bus body over a chassis produced by another manufacturer.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Design">Design</span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Accessibility">Accessibility</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Praha,_DOD_2006_Hostiva%C5%99,_Plo%C5%A1ina_autobusu_Karosa_pro_invalidy.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/05/Praha%2C_DOD_2006_Hostiva%C5%99%2C_Plo%C5%A1ina_autobusu_Karosa_pro_invalidy.JPG/220px-Praha%2C_DOD_2006_Hostiva%C5%99%2C_Plo%C5%A1ina_autobusu_Karosa_pro_invalidy.JPG" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/05/Praha%2C_DOD_2006_Hostiva%C5%99%2C_Plo%C5%A1ina_autobusu_Karosa_pro_invalidy.JPG/330px-Praha%2C_DOD_2006_Hostiva%C5%99%2C_Plo%C5%A1ina_autobusu_Karosa_pro_invalidy.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/05/Praha%2C_DOD_2006_Hostiva%C5%99%2C_Plo%C5%A1ina_autobusu_Karosa_pro_invalidy.JPG/440px-Praha%2C_DOD_2006_Hostiva%C5%99%2C_Plo%C5%A1ina_autobusu_Karosa_pro_invalidy.JPG 2x" data-file-width="2160" data-file-height="1440" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Praha,_DOD_2006_Hostiva%C5%99,_Plo%C5%A1ina_autobusu_Karosa_pro_invalidy.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Bus with <a href="/wiki/Wheelchair_lift" title="Wheelchair lift">wheelchair lift</a> extended</div>
</div>
</div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Transit_bus" title="Transit bus">Transit buses</a> used to be mainly <a href="/wiki/High-floor" title="High-floor">high-floor</a> vehicles. However, they are now increasingly of <a href="/wiki/Low-floor_bus" title="Low-floor bus">low-floor</a> design and optionally also 'kneel' <a href="/wiki/Air_suspension" title="Air suspension">air suspension</a> and have electrically or hydraulically extended under-floor ramps to provide level access for <a href="/wiki/Wheelchair" title="Wheelchair">wheelchair</a> users and people with <a href="/wiki/Baby_transport" title="Baby transport">baby carriages</a>. Prior to more general use of such technology, these wheelchair users could only use specialist <a href="/wiki/Paratransit" title="Paratransit">paratransit</a> mobility buses.</p>
<p>Accessible vehicles also have wider entrances and interior gangways and space for wheelchairs. Interior fittings and <a href="/wiki/Rollsign" class="mw-redirect" title="Rollsign">destination displays</a> may also be designed to be usable by the <a href="/wiki/Visually_impaired" class="mw-redirect" title="Visually impaired">visually impaired</a>. Coaches generally use <a href="/wiki/Wheelchair_lift" title="Wheelchair lift">wheelchair lifts</a> instead of low-floor designs. In some countries, vehicles are required to have these features by <a href="/wiki/Disability_discrimination_act" title="Disability discrimination act">disability discrimination laws</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Configuration">Configuration</span></h3>
<p>Buses were initially configured with an engine in the front and an entrance at the rear. With the transition to one-man operation, many manufacturers moved to mid- or rear-engined designs, with a single door at the front or multiple doors. The move to the low-floor design has all but eliminated the mid-engined design, although some coaches still have mid-mounted engines. Front-engined buses still persist for niche markets such as American school buses, some minibuses, and buses in less developed countries, which may be derived from truck chassis, rather than purpose-built bus designs. Most buses have two <a href="/wiki/Axles" class="mw-redirect" title="Axles">axles</a>, articulated buses have three.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Guidance">Guidance</span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Guided_bus" title="Guided bus">Guided buses</a> are fitted with technology to allow them to run in designated guideways, allowing the controlled alignment at bus stops and less space taken up by guided lanes than conventional roads or <a href="/wiki/Bus_lane" title="Bus lane">bus lanes</a>. Guidance can be mechanical, optical, or electromagnetic. Extensions of the guided technology include the <a href="/wiki/Bombardier_Guided_Light_Transit" title="Bombardier Guided Light Transit">Guided Light Transit</a> and <a href="/wiki/Translohr" title="Translohr">Translohr</a> systems, although these are more often termed 'rubber-tyred trams' as they have limited or no mobility away from their guideways.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Liveries">Liveries</span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Transit_bus" title="Transit bus">Transit buses</a> are normally painted to identify the operator or a route, function, or to demarcate low-cost or premium service buses. Liveries may be painted onto the vehicle, applied using adhesive <a href="/wiki/Vinyl_(fabric)" class="mw-redirect" title="Vinyl (fabric)">vinyl</a> technologies, or using <a href="/wiki/Decal" title="Decal">decals</a>. Vehicles often also carry <a href="/wiki/Bus_advertising" title="Bus advertising">bus advertising</a> or part or all of their visible surfaces (as <a href="/wiki/Mobile_billboard" title="Mobile billboard">mobile billboard</a>). <a href="/wiki/Campaign_bus" title="Campaign bus">Campaign buses</a> may be decorated with key campaign messages; these can be to promote an event or initiative.</p>

<p>The most common power source since the 1920s has been the <a href="/wiki/Diesel_engine" title="Diesel engine">diesel engine</a>. Early buses, known as trolleybuses, were powered by electricity supplied from <a href="/wiki/Overhead_lines" class="mw-redirect" title="Overhead lines">overhead lines</a>. Nowadays, electric buses often carry their own battery, which is sometimes <a href="/wiki/Phileas_(public_transport)" title="Phileas (public transport)">recharged on stops/stations</a> to keep the size of the battery small/lightweight. Currently, interest exists in <a href="/wiki/Hybrid_electric_bus" title="Hybrid electric bus">hybrid electric buses</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fuel_cell_bus" title="Fuel cell bus">fuel cell buses</a>, <a href="/wiki/Electric_bus" title="Electric bus">electric buses</a>, and ones powered by <a href="/wiki/Compressed_natural_gas" title="Compressed natural gas">compressed natural gas</a> or <a href="/wiki/Biodiesel" title="Biodiesel">biodiesel</a>. <a href="/wiki/Gyrobus" title="Gyrobus">Gyrobuses</a>, which are powered by the momentum stored by a <a href="/wiki/Flywheel" title="Flywheel">flywheel</a>, were tried in the 1940s.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Manufacture">Manufacture</span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Bus_manufacturing" title="Bus manufacturing">Bus manufacturing</a></div>
<p>Early bus manufacturing grew out of carriage <a href="/wiki/Coachbuilding" class="mw-redirect" title="Coachbuilding">coachbuilding</a>, and later out of automobile or truck manufacturers. Early buses were merely a bus body fitted to a truck chassis. This body+chassis approach has continued with modern specialist manufacturers, although there also exist integral designs such as the <a href="/wiki/Leyland_National" title="Leyland National">Leyland National</a> where the two are practically inseparable. Specialist builders also exist and concentrate on building buses for special uses or modifying standard buses into specialised products.</p>
<p>Integral designs have the advantages that they are have been well-tested for strength and stability, and also are <a href="/wiki/Commercial_off-the-shelf" title="Commercial off-the-shelf">off-the-shelf</a>. However, two incentives cause use of the chassis+body model. First, it allows the buyer and manufacturer both to shop for the best deal for their needs, rather than having to settle on one fixed design‚Äîthe buyer can choose the body and the chassis separately. Second, over the lifetime of a vehicle (in constant service and heavy traffic), it will likely get minor damage now and again, and being able easily to replace a body panel or window etc. can vastly increase its <a href="/wiki/Service_life" title="Service life">service life</a> and save the cost and inconvenience of removing it from service.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>As with the rest of the <a href="/wiki/Automotive_industry" title="Automotive industry">automotive industry</a>, into the 20th century, bus manufacturing increasingly became globalized, with manufacturers producing buses far from their intended market to exploit labour and material cost advantages. As with the cars, new models are often exhibited by manufacturers at prestigious industry shows to gain new orders.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> A typical city bus costs almost US$450,000.<sup id="cite_ref-Gross_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gross-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Uses">Uses</span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Public_transport">Public transport</span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Public_transport_bus_service" title="Public transport bus service">Public transport bus service</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:NYC_Transit_New_Flyer_840.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/88/NYC_Transit_New_Flyer_840.jpg/220px-NYC_Transit_New_Flyer_840.jpg" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/88/NYC_Transit_New_Flyer_840.jpg/330px-NYC_Transit_New_Flyer_840.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/88/NYC_Transit_New_Flyer_840.jpg/440px-NYC_Transit_New_Flyer_840.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1024" data-file-height="768" /></a>

<p><a href="/wiki/Transit_bus" title="Transit bus">Transit buses</a>, used on <a href="/wiki/Public_transport_bus_service" title="Public transport bus service">public transport bus services</a>, have utilitarian fittings designed for efficient movement of large numbers of people, and often have multiple doors. Coaches are used for longer-distance routes. High-capacity <a href="/wiki/Bus_rapid_transit" title="Bus rapid transit">bus rapid transit</a> services may use the bi-articulated bus or tram-style buses such as the <a href="/wiki/Wright_StreetCar" title="Wright StreetCar">Wright StreetCar</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Iveco_Bus" title="Iveco Bus">Irisbus</a> Civis.</p>
<p>Buses and coach services often operate to a predetermined published <a href="/wiki/Public_transport_timetable" title="Public transport timetable">public transport timetable</a> defining the route and the timing, but smaller vehicles may be used on more flexible <a href="/wiki/Demand_responsive_transport" title="Demand responsive transport">demand responsive transport</a> services.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Tourism">Tourism</span></h3>
<p>Buses play a major part in the tourism industry. <a href="/wiki/Tour_bus" class="mw-redirect" title="Tour bus">Tour buses</a> around the world allow tourists to view local attractions or scenery. These are often <a href="/wiki/Open-top_bus" class="mw-redirect" title="Open-top bus">open-top buses</a>, but can also be by regular bus or coach.</p>

<p>In local <a href="/wiki/Sightseeing" class="mw-redirect" title="Sightseeing">sightseeing</a>, <a href="/wiki/City_Sightseeing" title="City Sightseeing">City Sightseeing</a> is the largest operator of local tour buses, operating on a franchised basis all over the world. Specialist tour buses are also often owned and operated by <a href="/wiki/Safari_park" title="Safari park">safari parks</a> and other <a href="/wiki/Amusement_park" title="Amusement park">theme parks or resorts</a>. Longer-distance tours are also carried out by bus, either on a turn up and go basis or through a <a href="/wiki/Tour_operator" title="Tour operator">tour operator</a>, and usually allow disembarkation from the bus to allow touring of sites of interest on foot. These may be day trips or longer <a href="/wiki/Excursion" title="Excursion">excursions</a> incorporating hotel stays. Tour buses often carry a <a href="/wiki/Tour_guide" title="Tour guide">tour guide</a>, although the driver or a recorded <a href="/wiki/Audio_commentary" title="Audio commentary">audio commentary</a> may also perform this function. The tour operator may itself be a subsidiary of a company that operates buses and coaches for other uses or an independent company that charters buses or coaches. <a href="/wiki/Commuter" class="mw-redirect" title="Commuter">Commuter</a> transport operators may also use their coaches to conduct tours within the target city between the morning and evening commuter transport journey.</p>
<p>Buses and coaches are also a common component of the wider <a href="/wiki/Package_holiday" class="mw-redirect" title="Package holiday">package holiday</a> industry, providing private airport transfers (in addition to general <a href="/wiki/Airport_bus" title="Airport bus">airport buses</a>) and organised tours and day trips for holidaymakers on the package.</p>
<p>Tour buses can also be hired as chartered buses by groups as part of sightseeing at popular holiday destinations. These private tour buses may offer specific stops like all the historical sights or specifically casinos or allow the customers the comfort to make their own itineraries as per the places and activities they want to cover while on their tour. Tour buses come with professional and informed staff, insurance and maintain state governed safety standards. Not only this to make the experience for the tourists more comfortable provide facilities like entertainment units, luxurious reclining seats, large scenic windows, and even lavatories if needed.</p>
<p>Public long-distance coach networks are also often used as a low-cost method of travel by students or young people travelling the world. Some companies such as <a href="/wiki/Topdeck_Travel" class="mw-redirect" title="Topdeck Travel">Topdeck Travel</a> were set up to specifically use buses to drive the <a href="/wiki/Hippie_trail" title="Hippie trail">hippie trail</a> or travel to places such as North Africa.</p>
<p>In many tourist or travel destinations, a bus is part of the <a href="/wiki/Tourist_attraction" title="Tourist attraction">tourist attraction</a>, such as the North American <a href="/wiki/Tourist_trolley" title="Tourist trolley">tourist trolleys</a>, London's <a href="/wiki/AEC_Routemaster" title="AEC Routemaster">AEC Routemaster</a> <a href="/wiki/London_Buses_heritage_routes_(disambiguation)" class="mw-redirect mw-disambig" title="London Buses heritage routes (disambiguation)">heritage routes</a>, or the <a href="/wiki/Customised_buses" title="Customised buses">customised buses</a> of Malta, Asia, and the Americas.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Student_transport">Student transport</span></h3>

<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:First_Student_IC_school_bus_202076.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>

<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Student_transport" title="Student transport">Student transport</a></div>
<p>In some countries, particularly the USA and Canada, buses used to transport school children have evolved into a specific design with specified mandatory features. American states have also adopted laws regarding motorist conduct around school buses, including serious fines and the possibility of prison time for passing a stopped school bus in the process of offloading children passengers. These <a href="/wiki/School_bus" title="School bus">school buses</a> feature things such as the <a href="/wiki/School_bus_yellow" title="School bus yellow">school bus yellow</a> livery and <a href="/wiki/School_bus_crossing_arm" title="School bus crossing arm">crossing guards</a>. Other countries may mandate the use of <a href="/wiki/Seat_belts" class="mw-redirect" title="Seat belts">seat belts</a>. As a minimum, many countries require a bus carrying students to display a <a href="https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/Category:School_bus_signs" class="extiw" title="commons:Category:School bus signs">sign</a>, and may also adopt yellow liveries. Student transport often uses older buses cascaded from service use, retrofitted with more seats or seatbelts. Student transport may be operated by local authorities or private contractors. Schools may also own and operate their own buses for other transport needs, such as class <a href="/wiki/Field_trip" title="Field trip">field trips</a>, or transport to associated sports, music, or other school events.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Private_charter">Private charter</span></h3>
<p>Due to the costs involved in owning, operating, and driving buses and coaches, many bus and coach use a <a href="/wiki/Private_hire" class="mw-redirect" title="Private hire">private hire</a> of vehicles from <a href="/wiki/Charter" title="Charter">charter</a> bus companies, either for a day or two or a longer contract basis, where the charter company provides the vehicles and qualified drivers.</p>

<p>Charter bus operators may be completely independent businesses, or charter hire may be a subsidiary business of a public transport operator that might maintain a separate fleet or use surplus buses, coaches, and dual-purpose coach-seated buses. Many private <a href="/wiki/Taxicab" title="Taxicab">taxicab</a> companies also operate larger <a href="/wiki/Minibus" title="Minibus">minibus</a> vehicles to cater for group fares. Companies, private groups, and social clubs may hire buses or coaches as a cost-effective method of transporting a group to an event or site, such as a group meeting, <a href="/wiki/Racing" title="Racing">racing</a> event, or organised recreational activity such as a <a href="/wiki/Summer_camp" title="Summer camp">summer camp</a>. <a href="/wiki/Schools" class="mw-redirect" title="Schools">Schools</a> often hire charter bus services on regular basis for transportation of children to and from their homes. Chartered buses are also used by education institutes for transport to <a href="/wiki/Convention_(meeting)" title="Convention (meeting)">conventions</a>, <a href="/wiki/Exhibitions" class="mw-redirect" title="Exhibitions">exhibitions</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Field_trips" class="mw-redirect" title="Field trips">field trips</a>. Entertainment or event companies may also hire temporary shuttles buses for transport at events such as festivals or <a href="/wiki/Academic_conference" title="Academic conference">conferences</a>. <a href="/wiki/Party_bus" title="Party bus">Party buses</a> are used by companies in a similar manner to <a href="/wiki/Limousine" title="Limousine">limousine</a> hire, for luxury private transport to social events or as a touring experience. <a href="/wiki/Sleeper_bus" title="Sleeper bus">Sleeper buses</a> are used by <a href="/wiki/Band_(music)" class="mw-redirect" title="Band (music)">bands</a> or other organisations that tour between entertainment venues and require mobile rest and recreation facilities. Some couples hire preserved buses for their <a href="/w/index.php?title=Wedding_transport&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Wedding transport (page does not exist)">wedding transport</a>, instead of the traditional car. Buses are often hired for <a href="/wiki/Parade" title="Parade">parades</a> or <a href="/wiki/Procession" title="Procession">processions</a>. <a href="/wiki/Victory_parade" title="Victory parade">Victory parades</a> are often held for triumphant sports teams, who often tour their home town or city in an <a href="/wiki/Open-top_bus" class="mw-redirect" title="Open-top bus">open-top bus</a>. Sports teams may also contract out their transport to a <a href="/w/index.php?title=Team_bus&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Team bus (page does not exist)">team bus</a>, for travel to <a href="/wiki/Road_(sports)" title="Road (sports)">away games</a>, to a <a href="/wiki/Sports_competition" class="mw-redirect" title="Sports competition">competition</a> or to a <a href="/wiki/Final_(competition)" title="Final (competition)">final event</a>. These buses are often specially decorated in a livery matching the team colours. Private companies often contract out private shuttle bus services, for transport of their customers or patrons, such as hotels, <a href="/wiki/Amusement_park" title="Amusement park">amusement parks</a>, <a href="/wiki/University_campus" class="mw-redirect" title="University campus">university campuses</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Airport_bus" title="Airport bus">private airport transfer</a> services. This shuttle usage can be as transport between locations, or to and from <a href="/wiki/Parking_lot" title="Parking lot">parking lots</a>. High specification luxury coaches are often chartered by companies for <a href="/wiki/Executive_officer" title="Executive officer">executive</a> or <a href="/wiki/Very_Important_Person" class="mw-redirect" title="Very Important Person">VIP</a> transport. Charter buses may also be used in tourism and for promotion (See Tourism and Promotion sections).</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Private_ownership">Private ownership</span></h3>
<p>Many organisations, including the police, <a href="/wiki/Not_for_profit" class="mw-redirect" title="Not for profit">not for profit</a>, social or charitable groups with a regular need for group transport may find it practical or cost-effective to own and operate a bus for their own needs. These are often <a href="/wiki/Minibus" title="Minibus">minibuses</a> for practical, tax and driver licensing reasons, although they can also be full-size buses. <a href="/wiki/Cadet" title="Cadet">Cadet</a> or <a href="/wiki/Scouting" title="Scouting">scout</a> groups or other youth organizations may also own buses. Specific charities may exist to fund and operate bus transport, usually using specially modified mobility buses or otherwise accessible buses (See Accessibility section). Some use their contributions to buy vehicles and provide volunteer drivers.</p>
<p>Airport operators make use of special airside <a href="/wiki/Airport_bus" title="Airport bus">airport buses</a> for crew and passenger transport in the secure <a href="/wiki/Airside_(airport)" class="mw-redirect" title="Airside (airport)">airside</a> parts of an airport. Some public authorities, police forces, and military forces make use of <a href="/wiki/Armoured_bus" title="Armoured bus">armoured buses</a> where there is a special need to provide increased passenger protection. The <a href="/wiki/United_States_Secret_Service" title="United States Secret Service">United States Secret Service</a> acquired two in 2010 for transporting dignitaries needing special protection.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Police_department" class="mw-redirect" title="Police department">Police departments</a> make use of <a href="/wiki/Police_bus" title="Police bus">police buses</a> for a variety of reasons, such as <a href="/wiki/Prisoner_transport_vehicle" title="Prisoner transport vehicle">prisoner transport</a>, <a href="/wiki/Police_officer" title="Police officer">officer</a> transport, temporary detention facilities, and as <a href="/wiki/Command_post" class="mw-redirect" title="Command post">command and control vehicles</a>. Some <a href="/wiki/Fire_department" title="Fire department">fire departments</a> also use a converted bus as a command post<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> while those in cold climates might retain a bus as a heated shelter at fire scenes.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> Many are drawn from retired school or service buses.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Promotion">Promotion</span></h3>
<p>Buses are often used for advertising, <a href="/wiki/Political_campaign" title="Political campaign">political campaigning</a>, <a href="/wiki/Category:Public_service_announcements" title="Category:Public service announcements">public information campaigns</a>, <a href="/wiki/Public_relation" class="mw-redirect" title="Public relation">public relations</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Promotion_(marketing)" title="Promotion (marketing)">promotional</a> purposes. These may take the form of temporary charter hire of service buses, or the temporary or permanent conversion and operation of buses, usually of <a href="/wiki/Used_good" title="Used good">second-hand</a> buses. Extreme examples include converting the bus with displays and decorations or <a href="/wiki/Awning" title="Awning">awnings</a> and fittings. Interiors may be fitted out for exhibition or information purposes with special equipment or <a href="/wiki/Audio_visual" class="mw-redirect" title="Audio visual">audio visual</a> devices.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Bus_advertising" title="Bus advertising">Bus advertising</a> takes many forms, often as interior and exterior adverts and all-over advertising liveries. The practice often extends into the exclusive private hire and use of a bus to promote a brand or product, appearing at large public events, or touring busy streets. The bus is sometimes staffed by promotions personnel, giving out free gifts. <a href="/wiki/Campaign_bus" title="Campaign bus">Campaign buses</a> are often specially decorated for a <a href="/wiki/Political_campaign" title="Political campaign">political campaign</a> or other social awareness information campaign, designed to bring a specific message to different areas, or used to transport campaign personnel to local areas/meetings. Exhibition buses are often sent to public events such as <a href="/wiki/Fair" title="Fair">fairs</a> and festivals for purposes such as recruitment campaigns, for example by private companies or the <a href="/wiki/Armed_force" class="mw-redirect" title="Armed force">armed forces</a>. Complex <a href="/wiki/Urban_planning" title="Urban planning">urban planning</a> proposals may be organised into a mobile exhibition bus for the purposes of public consultation.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Goods_transport">Goods transport</span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Bruck_(vehicle)" title="Bruck (vehicle)">Bruck (vehicle)</a></div>
<p>In some sparesly populated areas, it's common to use Brucks, buses with a cargo area to transport both passengers and cargo at the same time. They are especially common in the <a href="/wiki/Nordic_countries" title="Nordic countries">Nordic countries</a>.</p>

<p>Historically, the types and features of buses have developed according to local needs. Buses were fitted with technology appropriate to the local climate or passenger needs, such as air conditioning in Asia, or <a href="/wiki/Bicycle" title="Bicycle">cycle</a> mounts on North American buses. The bus types in use around the world where there was little mass production were often sourced second hand from other countries, such as the <a href="/wiki/Malta_bus" title="Malta bus">Malta bus</a>, and buses in use in Africa. Other countries such as Cuba required novel solutions to import restrictions, with the creation of the "camellos" (<a href="/wiki/Camel" title="Camel">camel</a> bus), a specially manufactured <a href="/wiki/Trailer_bus" title="Trailer bus">trailer bus</a>.</p>
<p>After the Second World War, manufacturers in Europe and the Far East, such as <a href="/wiki/Mercedes-Benz_buses" title="Mercedes-Benz buses">Mercedes-Benz buses</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mitsubishi_Fuso_Truck_and_Bus_Corporation" title="Mitsubishi Fuso Truck and Bus Corporation">Mitsubishi Fuso</a> expanded into other continents influencing the use of buses previously served by local types. Use of buses around the world has also been influenced by colonial associations or political alliances between countries. Several of the <a href="/wiki/Commonwealth_of_Nations" title="Commonwealth of Nations">Commonwealth nations</a> followed the British lead and sourced buses from British manufacturers, leading to a prevalence of <a href="/wiki/Double-decker_bus" title="Double-decker bus">double-decker buses</a>. Several <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Bloc" title="Eastern Bloc">Eastern Bloc</a> countries adopted trolleybus systems, and their manufacturers such as <a href="/wiki/Trolza" title="Trolza">Trolza</a> exported trolleybuses to other friendly states.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> In the 1930s, Italy designed the world's only<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Accuracy_dispute#Disputed_statement" title="Wikipedia:Accuracy dispute"><span title="The material near this tag is possibly inaccurate or nonfactual. (March 2013)">dubious</span></a> <span class="metadata">‚Äì <a href="/wiki/Talk:Bus#Triple_decker_bus" title="Talk:Bus">discuss</a></span></i>]</sup> triple decker bus for the busy route between Rome and Tivoli that could carry eighty-eight passengers. It was unique not only in being a triple decker but having a separate smoking compartment on the third level.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>The buses to be found in countries around the world often reflect the quality of the local road network, with high floor resilient truck-based designs prevalent in several less developed countries where buses are subject to tough operating conditions. Population density also has a major impact, where dense urbanisation such as in Japan and the far east has led to the adoption of high capacity long <a href="/wiki/Multi-axle_bus" title="Multi-axle bus">multi-axle buses</a>, often double-deckers while South America and China are implementing large numbers of <a href="/wiki/Articulated_bus" title="Articulated bus">articulated buses</a> for <a href="/wiki/Bus_rapid_transit" title="Bus rapid transit">bus rapid transit</a> schemes.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Bus_expositions">Bus expositions</span></h3>
<p>Euro Bus Expo is a <a href="/wiki/Trade_fair" title="Trade fair">trade show</a>, which is held bi-ennially at the UK's <a href="/wiki/National_Exhibition_Centre" title="National Exhibition Centre">National Exhibition Centre</a> in Birmingham. As the official show of the <a href="/wiki/Confederation_of_Passenger_Transport" title="Confederation of Passenger Transport">Confederation of Passenger Transport</a>, the UK's trade association for the bus, coach and light rail industry, the three-day event offers visitors from Europe and beyond the chance to see and experience, at first hand, the very latest vehicles and product and service innovations right across the industry. The next show will be held in November 2016.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>Busworld Kortrijk in <a href="/wiki/Kortrijk" title="Kortrijk">Kortrijk</a>, Belgium, is the leading bus trade fair in Europe. It is held bi-ennially, last time October 2013 and next time October 2015.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Use_of_retired_buses">Use of retired buses</span></h2>

<p>Most public or private buses and coaches, once they have reached the end of their service with one or more operators, are sent to the <a href="/wiki/Wrecking_yard" title="Wrecking yard">wrecking yard</a> for breaking up for <a href="/wiki/Scrap" title="Scrap">scrap</a> and <a href="/wiki/Spare_part" title="Spare part">spare parts</a>. Some buses, while not economical to keep running as service buses, are often converted in some way for use by the operator, or another user, for purposes other than revenue-earning transport. Much like old cars and trucks, buses often pass through a <a href="/wiki/Car_dealership" title="Car dealership">dealership</a> where they can be bought for a price or at auction.</p>
<p>Bus operators will often find it economical to convert retired buses to use as permanent <a href="/wiki/Training_bus" title="Training bus">training buses</a> for driver training, rather than taking a regular service bus out of use. Some large operators also converted retired buses into tow bus vehicles, to act as <a href="/wiki/Tow_truck" title="Tow truck">tow trucks</a>. With the outsourcing of maintenance staff and facilities, the increase in company <a href="/wiki/Health_and_safety" class="mw-redirect" title="Health and safety">health and safety</a> regulations, and the increasing <a href="/wiki/Curb_weight" title="Curb weight">curb weights</a> of buses, many operators now contract their towing needs to a professional <a href="/wiki/Vehicle_recovery" title="Vehicle recovery">vehicle recovery</a> company.</p>
<p>Some retired buses have been converted to static or mobile caf√©s, often using historic buses as a <a href="/wiki/Tourist_attraction" title="Tourist attraction">tourist attraction</a>. Food is also provided from a catering bus, in which a bus is converted into a mobile <a href="/wiki/Canteen_(place)" class="mw-redirect" title="Canteen (place)">canteen</a> and <a href="/wiki/Coffee_break" class="mw-redirect" title="Coffee break">break room</a>. These are commonly seen at external <a href="/wiki/Filming_location" title="Filming location">filming locations</a> to feed the cast and crew, and at other large events to feed staff. Another use is as an emergency vehicle, such as high-capacity <a href="/wiki/Ambulance_bus" title="Ambulance bus">ambulance bus</a> or mobile <a href="/wiki/Command_center" title="Command center">command center</a>.</p>
<p>Some organisations adapt and operate <a href="/wiki/Playbus" class="mw-redirect" title="Playbus">playbuses</a> or learning buses to provide a <a href="/wiki/Playground" title="Playground">playground</a> or learning environments to children who might not have access to proper play areas. An ex-London <a href="/wiki/AEC_Routemaster" title="AEC Routemaster">AEC Routemaster</a> bus has been converted to a mobile theatre and <a href="/wiki/Runway_(fashion)" title="Runway (fashion)">catwalk fashion show</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<p>Some buses meet a destructive end by being entered in <a href="/wiki/Banger_racing" title="Banger racing">banger races</a> or at <a href="/wiki/Demolition_derby" title="Demolition derby">demolition derbys</a>. A larger number of old retired buses have also been converted into mobile holiday homes and campers.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Bus_preservation">Bus preservation</span></h3>
<p>Rather than being <a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/scrapped" class="extiw" title="wikt:scrapped">scrapped</a> or converted for other uses, sometimes retired buses are saved for preservation. This can be done by individuals, volunteer preservation groups or charitable trusts, museums, or sometimes by the operators themselves as part of a <a href="/wiki/Heritage_fleet" title="Heritage fleet">heritage fleet</a>. These buses often need to undergo a degree of <a href="/wiki/Vehicle_restoration" class="mw-redirect" title="Vehicle restoration">vehicle restoration</a> to restore them to their original condition and will have their livery and other details such as internal notices and <a href="/wiki/Rollsign" class="mw-redirect" title="Rollsign">rollsigns</a> restored to be authentic to a specific time in the bus's actual history. Some buses that undergo preservation are rescued from a state of great disrepair, but others enter preservation with very little wrong with them. As with other historic vehicles, many preserved buses either in a working or static state form part of the collections of <a href="/wiki/List_of_transport_museums" title="List of transport museums">transport museums</a>. Working buses will often be exhibited at rallies and events, and they are also used as charter buses. While many preserved buses are quite old or even vintage, in some cases, relatively new examples of a bus type can enter restoration. In-service examples are still in use by other operators. This often happens when a change in design or operating practice, such as the switch to one person operation or low floor technology, renders some buses redundant while still relatively new.</p>
