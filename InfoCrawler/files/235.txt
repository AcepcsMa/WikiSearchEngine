<p><b>Elisha</b> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ɪ/: 'i' in 'kit'">ɪ</span><span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'l' in 'lie'">l</span><span title="/aɪ/: 'i' in 'tide'">aɪ</span><span title="/ʃ/: 'sh' in 'shy'">ʃ</span><span title="/ə/: 'a' in 'about'">ə</span></span>/</a></span></span>;<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Hebrew_language" title="Hebrew language">Hebrew</a>: <span style="white-space:nowrap;" lang="he" dir="rtl" xml:lang="he">אֱלִישָׁע</span>, <small><a href="/wiki/Modern_Hebrew" title="Modern Hebrew">Modern</a></small>&#160;<i><span title="he transliteration" class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none">Elisha</span></i>, <small><a href="/wiki/Tiberian_vocalization" title="Tiberian vocalization">Tiberian</a></small>&#160;<i><span title="Hebrew transliteration" class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none">ʼĔlîšāʻ</span></i>; "My <a href="/wiki/Elohim" title="Elohim">God</a> is salvation", Greek: <span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc">Ἐλισσαῖος</span>, <i>Elissaîos</i> or <span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc">Ἐλισαιέ</span>, <i>Elisaié</i>, <a href="/wiki/Arabic" title="Arabic">Arabic</a>: الْيَسَع <i>Al-Yasaʿ</i>) is a <a href="/wiki/Hebrew" class="mw-redirect" title="Hebrew">Hebrew</a> <a href="/wiki/Prophet" title="Prophet">prophet</a> and a <a href="/wiki/Thaumaturgy" title="Thaumaturgy">wonder-worker</a> whose life is documented in the <a href="/wiki/Hebrew_Bible" title="Hebrew Bible">Hebrew Bible</a>. Also mentioned in the <a href="/wiki/New_Testament" title="New Testament">New Testament</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Quran" title="Quran">Quran</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Elisha is venerated as a prophet in <a href="/wiki/Judaism" title="Judaism">Judaism</a>, <a href="/wiki/Christianity" title="Christianity">Christianity</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Islam" title="Islam">Islam</a>. Amongst new religious movements, <a href="/wiki/Bah%C3%A1%27%C3%AD" class="mw-redirect" title="Bahá'í">Bahá'í</a> writings refer to him by name.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> His name is commonly transliterated into <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">English</a> as <b>Elisha</b> via <a href="/wiki/Hebrew_language" title="Hebrew language">Hebrew</a>, <b>Eliseus</b> via <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a> and <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a>, or <b>Alyasa</b> via <a href="/wiki/Arabic_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Arabic language">Arabic</a>, <b>Elyesa</b> via Turkish. He was a disciple, and even protégé, of <a href="/wiki/Elijah" title="Elijah">Elijah</a> and, after Elijah was taken up into the whirlwind, Elisha was accepted as the leader of the sons of the prophets.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biography">Biography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Elisha&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Biography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Elisha-Eliseus.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b2/Elisha-Eliseus.jpg/220px-Elisha-Eliseus.jpg" width="220" height="258" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b2/Elisha-Eliseus.jpg/330px-Elisha-Eliseus.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/b/b2/Elisha-Eliseus.jpg 2x" data-file-width="427" data-file-height="500" /></a>

<a href="/wiki/Russian_icons" title="Russian icons">Russian icon</a> of Elisha (18th century, <a href="/wiki/Kizhi" class="mw-redirect" title="Kizhi">Kizhi</a> <a href="/wiki/Monastery" title="Monastery">Monastery</a>, Russia).</div>

<p>Elisha's story is related in the <a href="/wiki/Books_of_Kings" title="Books of Kings">Book of Kings</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Hebrew_Bible" title="Hebrew Bible">Hebrew Bible</a> (in Judaism, part of the <a href="/wiki/Nevi%27im" title="Nevi'im">Nevi'im</a>). He was a prophet and a wonder-worker of the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Israel_(Samaria)" title="Kingdom of Israel (Samaria)">Northern Kingdom of Israel</a> who was active during the reign of <a href="/wiki/Jehoram_of_Israel" title="Jehoram of Israel">Joram</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jehu" title="Jehu">Jehu</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jehoahaz_of_Israel" title="Jehoahaz of Israel">Jehoahaz</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Jehoash_of_Israel" title="Jehoash of Israel">Jehoash</a> (Joash).<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated1996_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1996-4">[4]</a></sup> Elisha was the son of <a href="/wiki/Shaphat" title="Shaphat">Shaphat</a>, a wealthy land-owner of <a href="/wiki/Abel-meholah" title="Abel-meholah">Abel-meholah</a>; he became the attendant and disciple of Elijah.<sup id="cite_ref-je_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-je-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>His name first occurs in the command given to Elijah to <a href="/wiki/Anointing" title="Anointing">anoint</a> him as his successor. After learning in the <a href="/wiki/Cave_of_Elijah" title="Cave of Elijah">cave</a> on <a href="/wiki/Mount_Horeb" title="Mount Horeb">Mount Horeb</a>, that Elisha, the son of Shaphat, had been selected by <a href="/wiki/Yahweh" title="Yahweh">Yahweh</a> as his successor in the prophetic office, Elijah set out to find him. On his way from <a href="/wiki/Sinai_Peninsula" title="Sinai Peninsula">Sinai</a> to <a href="/wiki/Damascus" title="Damascus">Damascus</a>, Elijah found Elisha "one of them that were ploughing with twelve yoke of <a href="/wiki/Ox" title="Ox">oxen</a>". Elisha delayed only long enough to kill the yoke of oxen, whose flesh he boiled with the wood of his plough. He went over to him, threw his <a href="/wiki/Mantle_(vesture)" title="Mantle (vesture)">mantle</a> over Elisha's shoulders, and at once adopted him as a son, investing him with the prophetic office.<sup id="cite_ref-je_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-je-5">[5]</a></sup> Elisha accepted this call about four years before the death of Israel's King <a href="/wiki/Ahab" title="Ahab">Ahab</a>. For the next seven or eight years Elisha became Elijah's close attendant until Elijah was taken up into <a href="/wiki/Heaven" title="Heaven">heaven</a>. During all these years we hear nothing of Elisha except in connection with the closing scenes of Elijah's life.</p>
<p>After he had shared this farewell repast with his father, mother, and friends, the newly chosen prophet "went after Elijah, and ministered unto him."<sup id="cite_ref-dothie_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dothie-6">[6]</a></sup> He went with his master from <a href="/wiki/Gilgal" title="Gilgal">Gilgal</a> to <a href="/wiki/Bethel" title="Bethel">Bethel</a>, to <a href="/wiki/Jericho" title="Jericho">Jericho</a>, and thence to the eastern side of the <a href="/wiki/Jordan_River" title="Jordan River">Jordan</a>, the waters of which, touched by the mantle, divided, so as to permit both to pass over on dry ground. Elisha then was separated from Elijah by a fiery chariot, and Elijah was taken up by a whirlwind into Heaven.</p>
<p>Before Elijah was taken up into the whirlwind, Elisha asked to "inherit a double-portion" of Elijah's spirit. Some scholars see this as indicative of the property inheritance customs of the time, where the oldest son received twice as much of the father's inheritance as each of the younger sons. In this interpretation Elisha is asking that he may be seen as the "rightful heir" and successor to Elijah.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Critics of this view point out that Elisha was already appointed as Elijah's successor earlier in the narrative and that Elisha is described as performing twice as many miracles as Elijah. In this interpretation the "double-portion" isn't merely an allusion to primacy in succession, but is instead a request for greater prophetic power even than Elijah.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Much of this confusion comes from translations which incorrectly translate the phrase as a "double portion" while in Hebrew Elisha asks for "two thirds of a portion" of the prophetic spirit that imbued Elijah.</p>
<p>By means of the mantle let fall from Elijah, Elisha miraculously recrossed the Jordan, and Elisha returned to Jericho, where he won the gratitude of the people by purifying the unwholesome waters of their spring and making them drinkable.<sup id="cite_ref-ocarm_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ocarm-9">[9]</a></sup></p>

<p>Before he settled in Samaria, Elisha passed some time on <a href="/wiki/Mount_Carmel" title="Mount Carmel">Mount Carmel</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-duffy_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-duffy-10">[10]</a></sup> When the armies of Judah, Israel and Edom, then allied against <a href="/wiki/Mesha" title="Mesha">Mesha</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Moab" title="Moab">Moabite</a> king, were being tortured by drought in the <a href="/wiki/Edom" title="Edom">Idumean</a> desert, Elisha consented to intervene. His double prediction regarding relief from drought and victory over the Moabites was fulfilled on the following morning.<sup id="cite_ref-je_5-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-je-5">[5]</a></sup> When a group of boys (or youths<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup>) from Bethel taunted the prophet for his baldness, Elisha cursed them in the name of <a href="/wiki/Yahweh" title="Yahweh">Yahweh</a> and two female bears came out of the forest and tore forty-two of the boys.<sup id="cite_ref-duffy_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-duffy-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>He became noted in Israel, and for six decades (892–832 B.C.) held the office of "prophet in Israel". He is called a patriot because of his help to soldiers and kings.<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated1996_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1996-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Wonder-worker">Wonder-worker</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Elisha&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Wonder-worker">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft">

<p>To relieve the widow importuned by a hard creditor, Elisha so multiplied a little oil as to enable her, not only to pay her debt, but to provide for her family needs. To reward the <a href="/wiki/Woman_of_Shunem" title="Woman of Shunem">rich lady of Shunem</a> for her hospitality, he obtained for her from Yahweh, at first the birth of a son, and subsequently the <a href="/wiki/Raising_of_the_son_of_the_woman_of_Shunem" title="Raising of the son of the woman of Shunem">resurrection of her child</a>, who had died. To nourish the sons of the prophets pressed by famine, Elisha changed into wholesome food the pottage made from poisonous gourds.<sup id="cite_ref-ocarm_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ocarm-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:ElijahRefusingGifts_PieterDeGrebber.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/07/ElijahRefusingGifts_PieterDeGrebber.jpg/220px-ElijahRefusingGifts_PieterDeGrebber.jpg" width="220" height="149" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/07/ElijahRefusingGifts_PieterDeGrebber.jpg/330px-ElijahRefusingGifts_PieterDeGrebber.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/07/ElijahRefusingGifts_PieterDeGrebber.jpg/440px-ElijahRefusingGifts_PieterDeGrebber.jpg 2x" data-file-width="950" data-file-height="642" /></a>

<i>Elisha refusing the gifts of <a href="/wiki/Naaman" title="Naaman">Naaman</a></i>, by <a href="/wiki/Pieter_de_Grebber" title="Pieter de Grebber">Pieter de Grebber</a> 1630</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Elisha cured the Syrian military commander <a href="/wiki/Naaman" title="Naaman">Naaman</a> of <a href="/wiki/Leprosy" title="Leprosy">leprosy</a> but punished his own servant <a href="/wiki/Gehazi" title="Gehazi">Gehazi</a>, who took money from Naaman.<sup id="cite_ref-oca_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-oca-12">[12]</a></sup> Naaman, at first reluctant, obeyed Elisha, and washed seven times in the Jordan. Finding his flesh "restored like the flesh of a little child", the general was so impressed by this evidence of God's power, and by the disinterestedness of His Prophet, as to express his deep conviction that "there is no other God in all the earth, but only in Israel". In the <a href="/wiki/Christianity" title="Christianity">Christian</a> tradition, <a href="/wiki/Jesus" title="Jesus">Jesus</a> referred to this event when he said, "And there were many lepers in Israel in the time of Elisha the prophet: and none of them was cleansed but Naaman the Syrian".<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>Elisha's public political actions included repeatedly saving King Jehoram of Israel from the ambushes planned by <a href="/wiki/Hadadezer" title="Hadadezer">Benhadad</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-duffy_10-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-duffy-10">[10]</a></sup> ordering the elders to shut the door against the messenger of Israel's ungrateful king,<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> bewildering with a strange blindness the soldiers of the Syrian king,<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> making iron float to relieve from embarrassment a son of a prophet,<sup id="cite_ref-bibleverse.7C2.7CKings.7C6:1.E2.80.937.7CHE_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bibleverse.7C2.7CKings.7C6:1.E2.80.937.7CHE-16">[16]</a></sup> confidently predicting the sudden flight of the enemy and the consequent cessation of the famine,<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> and unmasking the treachery of <a href="/wiki/Hazael" title="Hazael">Hazael</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-bibleverse.7C2.7CKings.7C8:7.E2.80.9315.7CHE_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bibleverse.7C2.7CKings.7C8:7.E2.80.9315.7CHE-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>Other miracles Elisha accomplishes include multiplying the twenty loaves of new <a href="/wiki/Barley" title="Barley">barley</a> into a sufficient supply for a hundred men, and for a disciple of the schools of the prophets, he recovers an axe fallen into the waters of the Jordan.<sup id="cite_ref-ocarm_9-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ocarm-9">[9]</a></sup> He administered the miracle at <a href="/wiki/Dothan_(ancient_city)" title="Dothan (ancient city)">Dothan</a>, half-way on the road between <a href="/wiki/Samaria" title="Samaria">Samaria</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jezreel_Valley" title="Jezreel Valley">Jezreel</a>, and at the siege of Samaria by the king of Syria, Elisha prophesied about the terrible sufferings of the people of Samaria and their eventual relief.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>Elisha then journeyed to Damascus and prophesied that Hazael would be king over Syria;<sup id="cite_ref-bibleverse.7C2.7CKings.7C8:7.E2.80.9315.7CHE_18-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bibleverse.7C2.7CKings.7C8:7.E2.80.9315.7CHE-18">[18]</a></sup> thereafter he directs one of the sons of the prophets to anoint Jehu, the son of <a href="/wiki/Jehoshaphat" title="Jehoshaphat">Jehoshaphat</a>, king of Israel, in place of Ahab. Mindful of the order given to Elijah, Elisha delegated a son of one of the prophets to quietly anoint Jehu King of Israel, and to commission him to cut off the house of Ahab.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> The death of Jehoram, pierced by an arrow from Jehu's bow, the ignominious end of <a href="/wiki/Jezebel" title="Jezebel">Jezebel</a>, the slaughter of Ahab's seventy sons, proved how faithfully executed was the Divine command.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> After predicting to Jehoash his victory over the Syrians at <a href="/wiki/Aphek_(biblical)" title="Aphek (biblical)">Aphek</a>, as well as three other subsequent victories, ever bold before kings, ever kindly towards the lowly, "Elisha died, and they buried him".<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>

<p>While Elisha lay on his death-bed in his own house,<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> Jehoash, the grandson of Jehu, came to mourn over his approaching departure, and uttered the same words as those of Elisha when Elijah was taken away, indicating his value to him: "My father, my father! the chariot of Israel, and the horsemen thereof."</p>
<p>The touch of his corpse served to resuscitate a dead man. A year after Elisha's death and burial a body was placed in his grave. As soon as the body touched Elisha's remains the man "revived, and stood up on his feet".<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> It has been said<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag may use weasel words or too-vague attribution. (March 2017)">by whom?</span></a></i>]</sup>, that this dead man was Shallum (son of Tikvah), keeper of the temple-wardrobe in the reign of <a href="/wiki/Josiah" title="Josiah">Josiah</a><sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> and husband of <a href="/wiki/Huldah" title="Huldah">Huldah</a> the prophetess.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Veneration">Veneration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Elisha&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Veneration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>He is <a href="/wiki/Veneration" title="Veneration">venerated</a> as a <a href="/wiki/Saint" title="Saint">saint</a> in a number of Christian Churches. His <a href="/wiki/Calendar_of_saints" title="Calendar of saints">feast day</a> is on June 14, on the <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Orthodox_Church" title="Eastern Orthodox Church">Eastern Orthodox</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Catholic_Churches" title="Eastern Catholic Churches">Eastern Catholic</a> <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Orthodox_liturgical_calendar" title="Eastern Orthodox liturgical calendar">liturgical calendars</a> (for those churches which use the traditional <a href="/wiki/Julian_calendar" title="Julian calendar">Julian calendar</a>, June 14 falls on June 27 of the modern <a href="/wiki/Gregorian_calendar" title="Gregorian calendar">Gregorian calendar</a>). <a href="/wiki/John_of_Damascus" title="John of Damascus">John of Damascus</a> composed a <a href="/wiki/Canon_(hymnography)" title="Canon (hymnography)">canon</a> in honor of Elisha, and a church was built at <a href="/wiki/Constantinople" title="Constantinople">Constantinople</a> in his honor.</p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Western_Christianity" title="Western Christianity">Western Christianity</a> he is commemorated on the <a href="/wiki/Carmelites" title="Carmelites">Carmelites</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Catholic_religious_order" title="Catholic religious order">Catholic religious order</a>, calendar of saints.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> He is also commemorated as a prophet on the <a href="/wiki/Calendar_of_Saints_(Lutheran)" class="mw-redirect" title="Calendar of Saints (Lutheran)">Calendar of Saints</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Lutheran_Church%E2%80%93Missouri_Synod" title="Lutheran Church–Missouri Synod">Lutheran Church–Missouri Synod</a>. Both calendars also celebrate him on June 14. Both the Orthodox and Roman Catholics believe that he was unmarried and celibate.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Julian_the_Apostate" class="mw-redirect" title="Julian the Apostate">Julian the Apostate</a> (361–363) gave orders to burn the <a href="/wiki/Relic" title="Relic">relics</a> of the prophets Elisha, <a href="/wiki/Obadiah" title="Obadiah">Obadiah</a> and <a href="/wiki/John_the_Baptist" title="John the Baptist">John the Baptist</a>, who were buried next to each other in <a href="/wiki/Sebastia,_Nablus" title="Sebastia, Nablus">Sebastia</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> but they were rescued by the Christians, and part of them were transferred to <a href="/wiki/Alexandria" title="Alexandria">Alexandria</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-oca_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-oca-12">[12]</a></sup> Today, the relics of Elisha are claimed to be among the possessions of the <a href="/wiki/Coptic_Orthodox_Church_of_Alexandria" title="Coptic Orthodox Church of Alexandria">Coptic Orthodox</a> <a href="/wiki/Monastery_of_Saint_Macarius_the_Great" title="Monastery of Saint Macarius the Great">Monastery of Saint Macarius the Great</a> in <a href="/wiki/Wadi_El_Natrun" title="Wadi El Natrun">Scetes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Egypt" title="Egypt">Egypt</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p>

<p>Elisha (<a href="/wiki/Arabic_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Arabic language">Arabic</a>: اليسع, <i>Al-Yasaʿ</i>) is venerated as a <a href="/wiki/Prophets_in_Islam" class="mw-redirect" title="Prophets in Islam">prophet</a> in all of <a href="/wiki/Islam" title="Islam">Islam</a>, and is also honored by <a href="/wiki/Muslim" title="Muslim">Muslims</a> as the prophetic successor to <a href="/wiki/Elijah" title="Elijah">Elijah</a> (Arabic: <i>Ilyās</i> = Greek: <i>Elias</i>). Elisha is mentioned twice in the <a href="/wiki/Quran" title="Quran">Quran</a> as a prophet,<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> and is mentioned both times alongside fellow prophets.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> According to the Quran, Elisha is exalted "above the rest of creation" (<i>faḍḍalnā ʿalā l-ʿālamīna</i>)<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> and is "among the excellent" (<i>mina l- akhyāri</i>).<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> Although the Quran does not give any details about Elisha's life, later Muslim tradition fleshed out Elisha's narrative through consulting the <a href="/wiki/Hebrew_Bible" title="Hebrew Bible">Hebrew Bible</a>. Sources that identify Elisha with al-Khidr cite the strong relationship between al-Khidr and Elijah in Islamic tradition.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
<p>Some Muslims believe the tomb of Elisha is in <a href="/wiki/Al-Awjam" title="Al-Awjam">Al-Awjam</a> in the eastern region of <a href="/wiki/Saudi_Arabia" title="Saudi Arabia">Saudi Arabia</a>. The shrine was removed by the <a href="/wiki/Politics_of_Saudi_Arabia" title="Politics of Saudi Arabia">Saudi Government</a> because such veneration is not in accordance with the <a href="/wiki/Wahhabi" class="mw-redirect" title="Wahhabi">Wahhabi</a> or <a href="/wiki/Salafi" class="mw-redirect" title="Salafi">Salafi</a> reform movement dominant in Saudi Arabia.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> It had been an important landmark for many centuries during and before the <a href="/wiki/Sunni_Islam" title="Sunni Islam">Sunni</a> <a href="/wiki/Ottoman_Empire" title="Ottoman Empire">Ottoman</a> rule of the Middle-East, and had been a very popular pilgrimage destination for Muslims of all sects throughout the pre-modern period.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> A shrine of Elisha is also present in the <a href="/wiki/E%C4%9Fil" title="Eğil">Eğil</a> district of <a href="/wiki/Diyarbakir_Province" class="mw-redirect" title="Diyarbakir Province">Diyarbakir Province</a>, Turkey.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup></p>
