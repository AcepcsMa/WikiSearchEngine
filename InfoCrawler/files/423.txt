			<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en"><i>Zollverein</i></h1>
									<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
									<div id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
								<div id="contentSub"></div>
												<div id="jump-to-nav" class="mw-jump">

<p>The <i><b>Zollverein</b></i> (<span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Standard_German" title="Help:IPA/Standard German">[ˈtsɔlfɛɐ̯ˌʔaɪn]</a></span>) or <b>German Customs Union</b> was a coalition of <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">German</a> states formed to manage <a href="/wiki/Tariff" title="Tariff">tariffs</a> and economic policies within their territories. Organized by the 1833 <b><i>Zollverein</i> treaties</b>, the <i>Zollverein</i> formally started on 1 January 1834. However, its foundations had been in development from 1818 with the creation of a variety of <a href="/wiki/Custom_union" class="mw-redirect" title="Custom union">custom unions</a> among the German states. By 1866, the <i>Zollverein</i> included most of the German states. The foundation of the <i>Zollverein</i> was the first instance in history in which independent states had consummated a full economic union without the simultaneous creation of a political federation or union.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Prussia" title="Kingdom of Prussia">Prussia</a> was the primary driver behind the creation of the customs union. <a href="/wiki/Austrian_Empire" title="Austrian Empire">Austria</a> was excluded from the <i>Zollverein</i> because of its highly protected industry and also because <a href="/wiki/Prince_von_Metternich" class="mw-redirect" title="Prince von Metternich">Prince von Metternich</a> was against the idea. By the founding of the <a href="/wiki/North_German_Confederation" title="North German Confederation">North German Confederation</a> in 1867, the <i>Zollverein</i> covered states of approximately 425,000 square kilometres, and had produced economic agreements with several non-German states, including <a href="/wiki/Sweden-Norway" class="mw-redirect" title="Sweden-Norway">Sweden-Norway</a>. After the founding of the <a href="/wiki/German_Empire" title="German Empire">German Empire</a> in 1871, the Empire assumed the control of the customs union. However, not all states within the Empire were part of the <i>Zollverein</i> until 1888. Conversely, <a href="/wiki/Luxembourg" title="Luxembourg">Luxembourg</a> was independent and not a state in the German Reich, it remained in the <i>Zollverein</i> until 1919.</p>

<p>The splintering of territory and states over generations meant that by the 1790s in the <a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a>-speaking <a href="/wiki/Holy_Roman_Empire" title="Holy Roman Empire">Holy Roman Empire</a> in Central Europe, there were approximately 1800 customs barriers. Even within the Prussian state itself there were, at the beginning of the 19th century, more than 67 local customs and tariffs, with as many customs borders. To travel from <a href="/wiki/K%C3%B6nigsberg" title="Königsberg">Königsberg</a> in East Prussia to <a href="/wiki/Cologne" title="Cologne">Cologne</a>, for example, a shipment was inspected and taxed 18 times.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Each customs inspection at each border slowed the shipment's progress from source to destination, and each assessment on the shipment reduced profit and increased the price of goods, dramatically stifling trade.</p>
<p>When <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a> defeated the <a href="/wiki/Second_Coalition" class="mw-redirect" title="Second Coalition">Second Coalition</a>, made up of <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russian</a>, Austrian and German forces, and annexed territories up to the <a href="/wiki/Rhine" title="Rhine">Rhine</a>, there was a general consolidation of the myriad of tiny states in Germany in the <a href="/wiki/German_Mediatisation" class="mw-redirect" title="German Mediatisation">Mediatization of 1803</a>. This was also called the <i>Principal Conclusion of the Extraordinary Imperial Delegation</i> (or, in German, <i>Hauptschluss der außerordentlichen Reichsdeputation</i>, usually called the <i>Reichsdeputationshauptschluss</i>). This last piece of major legislation enacted by the Holy Roman Empire re-arranged the map of Central Europe, especially in the southwestern territories. The <i>Reichshauptschluss</i> resulted in the secularization of many ecclesiastical territories, and the so-called <i>mediatization</i>, i.e. the annexation to larger neighboring territories, of many of the formerly free imperial territories, including most of the <a href="/wiki/Imperial_cities" class="mw-redirect" title="Imperial cities">imperial cities</a>. Considerable portions of the <a href="/wiki/Habsburg" class="mw-redirect" title="Habsburg">Habsburg</a> family territories in southwestern Central Europe were "mediatized", or given as compensation, to the princes and dukes who had lost territories in the French expansion. Most of the imperial cities, imperial abbeys, and ecclesiastical states and cities were mediatized or secularized in 1803. With the final dissolution of the Holy Roman Empire in 1806, most of the remaining tiny principalities were annexed by larger neighbors.</p>
<p>Historians have analyzed three Prussian goals in the development of the <i>Zollverein</i>: first, as a political tool to eliminate Austrian influence in Germany; second, as a way to improve the economies; and third, to strengthen Germany against potential French aggression while reducing the economic independence of smaller states.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> The <i>Zollverein</i> created a larger market for German-made farm and handicraft products and promoted commercial unification under fiscally sound economic parameters. While the Union sought to limit trade and commercial barriers between and among member states, it continued to uphold the protectionist barriers against outsiders.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Initial_efforts_at_a_single-toll_system">Initial efforts at a single-toll system</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Zollverein&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Initial efforts at a single-toll system">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>During the Napoleonic Era, efforts in the <a href="/wiki/Rhineland" title="Rhineland">Rhineland</a> toward economic unity had mixed success. The <a href="/wiki/Confederation_of_the_Rhine" title="Confederation of the Rhine">Confederation of the Rhine</a>, and the other satellite creations of Napoleonic France, sought to establish economic <a href="/wiki/Autarky" title="Autarky">autarky</a> in European trade. By 1806, as <a href="/wiki/Napoleon_I" class="mw-redirect" title="Napoleon I">Napoleon I</a> sought to secure his hegemony in Europe, the <a href="/wiki/Continental_System" title="Continental System">Continental System</a> offered a semblance of unified effort toward a widespread domestic market for European goods. However, the main purpose of the Continental System was military, not economic. Napoleon wanted a trade embargo against <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">Britain</a>, through which he hoped to wreck the British economy. The combination of war and isolation from Britain's trading system destroyed markets for external raw materials and for manufactured goods, resulting in the near ruin of the Central European economy. Especially hard hit were the trading economies of the Lowlands and Rhineland states, which had relied heavily upon imports of raw materials from throughout the world, and on the export of finished products. The domestic markets in Central Europe were not large enough to sustain consumption of their own production. These problems were dramatically exacerbated by the numerous excise taxes and tolls which were the main source of state income. Reduction in trade meant the near bankruptcy of the smaller states.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>At the <a href="/wiki/Congress_of_Vienna" title="Congress of Vienna">Congress of Vienna</a> in 1814 and 1815, diplomats&#160;– principally those from the <a href="/wiki/Congress_of_Vienna#Participants" title="Congress of Vienna">Great Powers</a>&#160;– confirmed the remapping of Europe, and broadly, the rest of the world, into spheres of influence. Central Europe, or German-speaking Europe, remained largely within the influence of the Austrian Habsburgs, balanced at the periphery by the Russian empire in the east, and the French in the west. Prussia was expected to play some role in these spheres of influence, but the ambiguities of the Austrian and Prussian relationship were unresolved. The German states retained autonomy; however, the old imperial institution of the <i>Reichstag</i> was converted to the form of a Confederation <i>Diet,</i> to meet in Frankfurt. The Habsburg archdukes, now Emperors of Austria, were to serve as permanent presidents of this institution. Isolated voices, such as <a href="/wiki/Joseph_G%C3%B6rres" title="Joseph Görres">Joseph Görres</a> and <a href="/wiki/Heinrich_Friedrich_Karl_vom_Stein" class="mw-redirect" title="Heinrich Friedrich Karl vom Stein">Freiherr vom Stein</a>, called for the abolition of domestic tolls and the creation of a German tariff on imports.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> The mandate from the Vienna Congress, however, established the <a href="/wiki/German_Confederation" title="German Confederation">German Confederation</a>, but did not deal with the economic circumstances, nor did it make any effort to achieve economic and trade standardization. Instead, the articles that established the Confederation suggested that trade and transportation questions be discussed at a later date.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Problems_with_unifying_the_customs_and_toll_agreements">Problems with unifying the customs and toll agreements</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Zollverein&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Problems with unifying the customs and toll agreements">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Prussia and the central and southwestern states of <a href="/wiki/Hesse-Nassau" title="Hesse-Nassau">Hesse-Nassau</a> and <a href="/wiki/Grand_Duchy_of_Hesse" title="Grand Duchy of Hesse">Hesse-Darmstadt</a>, <a href="/wiki/W%C3%BCrttemberg" title="Württemberg">Württemberg</a>, <a href="/wiki/Baden" title="Baden">Baden</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Bavaria" title="Bavaria">Bavaria</a> were leaders in the modernization of the toll system within the German states. In the Prussian case, the experience of the Confederation of the Rhine in removing customs barriers offered an example of how it could be done, and <a href="/wiki/Hans,_Count_von_B%C3%BClow" title="Hans, Count von Bülow">Hans, Count von Bülow</a>, who until 1811 had been the Finance Minister in <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Westphalia" title="Kingdom of Westphalia">Westphalia</a>, and who had accepted this position in 1813 in Prussia, modeled the Prussian customs statutes on those of the former states of the Confederation. The addition of territory to the existing Prussian state made elimination of customs barriers a powerful factor in Prussian politics. The significant differences between "old" Prussia and the newly acquired territories complicated the debate. The "newer" Prussian provinces in the Rhineland and Westphalia, with their developing manufacturing sectors, contended with the heavily agricultural territories of "old" Prussia. The dissimilarities in the two sides of Prussia confirmed regional perceptions for the need for their own political and administrative units, which became an important element of the customs debate. Within "old" Prussia itself, the customs statutes from 1818 reduced domestic customs barriers. After 1818, goods coming into Prussia and leaving Prussia were charged a high tariff. Goods moved freely within the state itself. The Prussian toll was therefore very simple and efficient. Manufactured goods were heavily taxed, especially textiles, and the most important taxes were for food, necessities and luxury goods.</p>
<p>Similarly, in the southwest German states, it became urgent to integrate the newly acquired territories into the states' existing economic systems.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> The territorial growth of the southwestern middle-sized states, in particular the two Hessian principalities, but also the growth of Baden and Württemberg, had split the territorial continuity of Prussia; the Prussian state was no longer linked entirely by territory, but rather was separated from many of its <i>newer</i> acquisitions by territories newly acquired by other states. These states often saw their own interests as conflicting generally and specifically with Prussian expansionism, and resented Prussian dominance and authority. Furthermore, these newly expanded states, usually referred as "middle-sized states" (or, in German, <i>Mittelstaaten</i>), faced problems in integrating their newly acquired territories and populations into an existing political, economic and legal structure.</p>
<p>These problems were exacerbated by European wide economic woes following the Napoleonic Wars. Unemployment and high prices, especially for foodstuffs, characterized an economy not yet converted back to peace-time needs. The problem in Britain was particularly severe and the British response created a ripple effect that worsened problems in the German states: In trying to manage the post-war economy, the British government was caught between the <a href="/wiki/Malthusianism" title="Malthusianism">Malthusian</a> understanding of the relationship of wages, prices, and population, and the <a href="/wiki/Ricardian_economics" title="Ricardian economics">Ricardian model</a>. On the one hand, adherents to the Malthusian model believed it was dangerous for Britain to rely on imported corn, because lower prices would reduce wages, and landlords and farmers would lose purchasing power.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> On the other hand, adherents to the Ricardian model thought that Britain could use its capital and population to advantage in a system of free trade.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> The problems in Britain established precedent for problems in the German states; the British limitation on grain imports, through the <a href="/wiki/Corn_Laws" title="Corn Laws">Corn Laws</a>, blocked economic recovery in the German states, particularly in eastern Prussia, by limiting the amount of grain that could be imported into Britain. Not only did the Corn Laws keep the price of grain in Britain high, they undermined the viability of <i>Junker</i> producers in east Prussia, and limited their access to external markets.</p>
<p>The commercial reform efforts sponsored by Bavaria in 1856 led to the General German Commercial Code in 1861 that was quickly approved by a majority of the confederation. It proved highly successful in reducing barriers and increasing trade.</p>

<p>The original agreements that set the foundation for <i>Zollverein</i> cemented economic ties between the various <a href="/wiki/Prussia" title="Prussia">Prussian</a> and <a href="/wiki/House_of_Hohenzollern" title="House of Hohenzollern">Hohenzollern</a> territories, and ensured economic contact between the non-contiguous holdings of the Hohenzollern family, which was also the ruling family of <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Prussia" title="Kingdom of Prussia">Prussia</a>. It was formed to remove the various obstacles (such as different weights and measures in German states) to economic exchange and growth by the new commercial classes, creating a national unity in economic matter at a time when Germany was divided.</p>
<p>Surmounting the domestic customs, and the individual states' dependence on those customs as their primary source of income, proved to be a difficult problem. The myriad of customs barriers restricted trade and hampered the industrial development, but the rulers of the states were reluctant to forgo their income from the customs. The impasse was overcome through external forces. With the repeal of the Continental System, the German tradesmen stood in direct conflict with the English industry. A united German Trade and Tradesmens Union demanded protection from English exports. Their spokesman, the economist <a href="/wiki/Friedrich_List" title="Friedrich List">Friedrich List</a>, feared that the German people would end up as "drawers of water and hewers of wood for Britain".<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Similarly, <a href="/wiki/Karl_Friedrich_Nebenius" title="Karl Friedrich Nebenius">Karl Friedrich Nebenius</a>, later president of the Ducal Ministry in the Grand Duchy of Baden and the author of Baden's 1819 proposed customs initiative with the German Confederation, offered a widely publicized description about the difficulties of surmounting such protections:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>The 830 toll barriers in Germany cripple domestic traffic and bring more or less the same results: how if every limb of the human body were bound together, so that blood could not flow from one limb to the other? In order to trade from Hamburg to Austria, from Berlin to the Swiss Cantons, one must cut through the statutes of ten states, study ten tolls and toll barriers, ten times go through the toll barriers, and ten times pay the tolls. Who but the unfortunate has to negotiate such borders? To live with such borders? Where three or four states collide, there one must live his whole life under evil, senseless tolls and toll restrictions. That is no Fatherland!<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>In 1820, <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_W%C3%BCrttemberg" title="Kingdom of Württemberg">Württemberg</a> planned to start a customs union among the so-called Third Germany: the middle-sized German states, including itself, <a href="/wiki/Grand_Duchy_of_Baden" title="Grand Duchy of Baden">Baden</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Bavaria" title="Kingdom of Bavaria">Bavaria</a>, and the two Hessian states (<a href="/wiki/Grand_Duchy_of_Hesse" title="Grand Duchy of Hesse">Hesse-Darmstadt</a> and <a href="/wiki/Electorate_of_Hesse" title="Electorate of Hesse">Hesse-Kassel</a>). This customs union excluded both Austria and Prussia, primarily because the two major German powers were considered too overbearing. Plans foundered on the differing interests of the affected states. While the economic development in Baden proceeded relatively well, with its long borders and well entrenched infrastructure for trade, economic development in Bavaria lagged well behind it, and the Bavarian regime enacted a protective tariff on goods produced outside its border. The result was a short lived trade agreement between Baden and Hesse-Darmstadt. Nevertheless, a second agreement, reached in Stuttgart in 1825, established rapport between Württemberg and Bavaria, with the foundation of the South German Customs Union. In opposition to the Prussian activities, <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Hanover" title="Kingdom of Hanover">Hanover</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Saxony" title="Kingdom of Saxony">Saxony</a>, Hesse, and other states (Austria, France, Great Britain, and the Netherlands), developed their own economic agreements. While they promised one another not to join the Prussian union, they did develop trade agreements of their own. The Union remained unsuccessful, because it only sought to maintain the <i>status quo</i>, not to fix the problems created by toll barriers.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> In 1834, Baden and Württemberg joined the Prussian union, which was renamed the German Customs Union.</p>
<p>By 1835, the German Customs Union had expanded to include the majority of the states of the <a href="/wiki/German_Confederation" title="German Confederation">German Confederation</a>, even Saxony, the Thuringian states, Württemberg and Baden, Bavaria, and the Hessian states. Functionally, it removed many internal customs barriers, while upholding a protectionist tariff system with foreign trade partners.</p>

<p>The original customs union was not ended in 1866 with outbreak of the <a href="/wiki/Austro-Prussian_War" title="Austro-Prussian War">Austro-Prussian War</a>, but a substantial reorganization emerged in 1867. The new <i>Zollverein</i> was stronger, in that no individual state had a veto.<sup id="cite_ref-Columbia_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Columbia-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Impact">Impact</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Zollverein&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Impact">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Economic_impact">Economic impact</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Zollverein&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Economic impact">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>According to one study, "The Zollverein was the most important institutional development for Germany's economic unification during the middle of the 19th century. It had a strong impact on regional development, changing regional fortunes by the opening and closing of markets."<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Role_in_the_unification_of_Germany">Role in the unification of Germany</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Zollverein&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Role in the unification of Germany">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Unification_of_Germany" title="Unification of Germany">Unification of Germany</a></div>
<p>According to some economic historians, such as Helmut Böhme and H.-U. Wehler, the <i>Zollverein</i> set the groundwork for the unification of Germany under Prussian guidance. This traditional view is disputed by historians such as Hans-Joachim Voth who contend that far from allowing Prussia to increase its political influence over the smaller states, the customs union may have had the contrary effect: many governments used the increased revenue brought by the customs union to try to consolidate their independence. The smaller states entered the customs union for purely fiscal reasons, and as the <a href="/wiki/Austro-Prussian_War" title="Austro-Prussian War">events of 1866</a> were to demonstrate, membership in the <i>Zollverein</i> did not in the least lead to any form of political commitment toward Berlin, as many states remained suspicious of Prussia and generally pro-Austrian.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>The impact of the <i>Zollverein</i> on German unification may have been more incidental. As it constituted the main feature of Berlin's "German policy" for many years, Prussian ministers and other government officials became accustomed to think in terms of Germany as a whole and to look beyond specifically Prussian benefits when looking for a consensus across Germany.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>According to revisionist historians, the <i>Zollverein</i> may not even have been instrumental in bringing about Prussia's economic preeminence in Germany. They argue that nothing seems to indicate that industrial investments increased decisively during the period in Prussia, or that the customs union played a significant role in reducing the dominance of agriculture in the kingdom's economy.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
