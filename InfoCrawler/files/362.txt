<p><b>Host desecration</b> is a form of <a href="/wiki/Sacrilege" title="Sacrilege">sacrilege</a> in Christianity (most frequently identified as such in the traditions of <a href="/wiki/Anglicanism" title="Anglicanism">Anglicanism</a>, <a href="/wiki/Orthodox_Church" class="mw-redirect" title="Orthodox Church">Eastern</a> and <a href="/wiki/Oriental_Orthodoxy" title="Oriental Orthodoxy">Oriental</a> <a href="/wiki/Orthodox_Christianity" class="mw-redirect" title="Orthodox Christianity">Orthodoxy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lutheranism" title="Lutheranism">Lutheranism</a>, <a href="/wiki/Methodism" title="Methodism">Methodism</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic_Church" class="mw-redirect" title="Roman Catholic Church">Roman Catholicism</a>) involving the mistreatment or malicious use of a <a href="/wiki/Consecration" title="Consecration">consecrated</a> <a href="/wiki/Sacramental_bread" title="Sacramental bread">host</a>—the sacred bread used in the <a href="/wiki/Eucharist" title="Eucharist">Eucharistic</a> service of the <a href="/wiki/Divine_Liturgy" title="Divine Liturgy">Divine Liturgy</a> or <a href="/wiki/Mass_(liturgy)" title="Mass (liturgy)">Mass</a>. In Catholicism, where the host is held to have been <a href="/wiki/Transubstantiation" title="Transubstantiation">transubstantiated</a> into the body of <a href="/wiki/Jesus_in_Christianity" title="Jesus in Christianity">Jesus Christ</a>, host <a href="/wiki/Desecration" title="Desecration">desecration</a> is among the gravest of sins. Intentional host desecration is not only a <a href="/wiki/Mortal_sin" title="Mortal sin">mortal sin</a> but also incurs the penalty of <a href="/wiki/Excommunication" title="Excommunication">excommunication</a> <a href="/wiki/Latae_sententiae" title="Latae sententiae">latae sententiae</a>. Throughout history, a number of groups have been accused of desecrating the Eucharist, often with grave consequences due to the spiritual importance of the consecrated host.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Overview">Overview</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Host_desecration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Overview">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Accusations against <a href="/wiki/Jews" title="Jews">Jews</a> were a common reason given for massacres and expulsions throughout the <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">Middle Ages</a> in Europe.<sup id="cite_ref-je_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-je-1">[1]</a></sup> Similar accusations were made in <a href="/wiki/Witch_trials" class="mw-redirect" title="Witch trials">witchcraft trials</a>; witch-hunter's guides such as the <a href="/wiki/Malleus_Maleficarum" title="Malleus Maleficarum">Malleus Maleficarum</a> refer to hosts as being objects of desecration by witches.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> It is part of many descriptions of the <a href="/wiki/Black_Mass" title="Black Mass">Black Mass</a>, both in ostensibly historical works and in fiction.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Background">Background</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Host_desecration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:482px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Host_desecration1.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/11/Host_desecration1.jpg/480px-Host_desecration1.jpg" width="480" height="663" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/11/Host_desecration1.jpg/720px-Host_desecration1.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/1/11/Host_desecration1.jpg 2x" data-file-width="801" data-file-height="1107" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Host_desecration1.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
From a 15th-century German woodcut of the host desecration by the Jews of <a href="/wiki/Passau" title="Passau">Passau</a>, 1477. The hosts are stolen and sold to the Jewish community, who pierce them in a ritual. When guards come to question the Jews, they (the Jews) attempt to burn the Hosts, but are unsuccessful, as the Hosts transform into an infant carried by angels. The Jews, now proven guilty, are arrested, beheaded, and tortured with hot <a href="/wiki/Pincer_(tool)" class="mw-redirect" title="Pincer (tool)">pincers</a>, the entire community is driven out with their feet bound and held to the fire, and the Christian who sold the hosts to the Jews is punished. At the end the Christians kneel and pray.</div>
</div>

<p>In Christianity, within the <a href="/wiki/Anglican_Communion" title="Anglican Communion">Anglican Church</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-Neal2014_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Neal2014-4">[4]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Catholic_Church" title="Catholic Church">Catholic Church</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-ce_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ce-5">[5]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Orthodox_Church" title="Eastern Orthodox Church">Eastern Orthodox Church</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-HarperMetzger2009_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HarperMetzger2009-6">[6]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Lutheranism" title="Lutheranism">Lutheran Church</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-MattoxRoeber2012_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MattoxRoeber2012-7">[7]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Methodism" title="Methodism">Methodist Church</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-Neal2014_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Neal2014-4">[4]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Oriental_Orthodoxy" title="Oriental Orthodoxy">Oriental Orthodox Church</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-Houlden2003_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Houlden2003-8">[8]</a></sup> during the celebration of the <a href="/wiki/Eucharist" title="Eucharist">Eucharist</a>, the offerings of bread and wine are changed into the body and blood of <a href="/wiki/Jesus" title="Jesus">Jesus</a>, a doctrine known as the <a href="/wiki/Real_presence_of_Christ_in_the_Eucharist" title="Real presence of Christ in the Eucharist">Real presence of Christ in the Eucharist</a>, which has been believed from the earliest days of the Church.<sup id="cite_ref-realpresencefathers_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-realpresencefathers-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>During the <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">Middle Ages</a>, Roman Catholic theology offered the concept of <a href="/wiki/Transubstantiation" title="Transubstantiation">transubstantiation</a> to explain this change of <a href="/wiki/Substance_theory" title="Substance theory">substance</a> which was believed to be actual and not merely symbolic. Transubstantiation, <a href="/wiki/Dogmatic_definition" class="mw-redirect" title="Dogmatic definition">defined as a dogma</a> at the <a href="/wiki/Fourth_Lateran_Council" class="mw-redirect" title="Fourth Lateran Council">Fourth Lateran Council</a> of 1215, holds that the substances of the offerings are literally transformed, while the appearance of bread and wine remain. Many Christians believe Jesus to be "<a href="/wiki/Hypostatic_union" title="Hypostatic union">true God and true man</a>." In the Catholic Church, therefore, his "body, blood, soul and divinity" in the form of the consecrated host are <a href="/wiki/Adoration" title="Adoration">adored</a>. Theft, sale, or use of the host for a profane purpose is considered a grave sin and sacrilege,<sup id="cite_ref-ce_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ce-5">[5]</a></sup> which incurs the penalty of <a href="/wiki/Excommunication" title="Excommunication">excommunication</a>, which is imposed <a href="/wiki/Latae_sententiae" title="Latae sententiae">automatically</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Latin_Rite" class="mw-redirect" title="Latin Rite">Latin Rite</a> (See <a href="/wiki/Code_of_Canon_Law" class="mw-redirect" title="Code of Canon Law">Code of Canon Law</a>, Latin Rite Code canon 1367, or Eastern Rite Code canon 1442.)</p>
<p>Some denominations, especially Lutherans, have similar beliefs regarding the Eucharist and the <a href="/wiki/Real_Presence" class="mw-redirect" title="Real Presence">Real Presence</a>, though they reject the Roman Catholic concept of transubstantiation, preferring instead, the doctrine of the <a href="/wiki/Sacramental_union" title="Sacramental union">sacramental union</a>, in which "the body and blood of Christ are so truly united to the bread and wine of the Holy Communion that the two may be identified. They are at the same time body and blood, bread and wine...in this sacrament the Lutheran Christian receives the very body and blood of Christ precisely for the strengthening of the union of faith."<sup id="cite_ref-MattoxRoeber2012_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MattoxRoeber2012-7">[7]</a></sup> Both the Eastern Orthodox Churches and the Oriental Orthodox Churches, such as the <a href="/wiki/Coptic_Church" class="mw-redirect" title="Coptic Church">Coptic Church</a>, insist "on the <i>reality</i> of the change from bread and wine into the body and the blood of Christ at the consecration of the elements", although they have "never attempted to explain the <i>manner</i> of the change",<sup id="cite_ref-HarperMetzger2009_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HarperMetzger2009-6">[6]</a></sup> thus rejecting philosophical terms to describe it.<sup id="cite_ref-Houlden2003_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Houlden2003-8">[8]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Methodist_Church" class="mw-redirect" title="Methodist Church">Methodist Church</a> similarly holds that Christ is truly present in the Eucharist "through the elements of bread and wine", but maintains that how He is present is a Holy Mystery.<sup id="cite_ref-Neal2014_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Neal2014-4">[4]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-AbrahamWatson2013_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AbrahamWatson2013-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>Host desecration has been associated with groups identified as inimical to Christianity. It is a common belief that desecration of the host is part of <a href="/wiki/Satanism" title="Satanism">Satanic practice</a>, especially the <a href="/wiki/Black_Mass" title="Black Mass">Black Mass</a>. LaVeyan Satanists do not typically perform Black Mass as a regular ritual, though "Le Messe Noir" from <a href="/wiki/Anton_LaVey" title="Anton LaVey">Anton LaVey</a>'s work <a href="/wiki/The_Satanic_Rituals" title="The Satanic Rituals">The Satanic Rituals</a> does include some elements.</p>
<p>Since the publication of a document called Memoriale Domini in 1969,<sup id="cite_ref-ewtn_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ewtn-11">[11]</a></sup> the Apolistic See of the Catholic Church has allowed certain countries to allow communicants to receive the Host in the hand, rather than directly onto the tongue, reviving an "ancient custom".<sup id="cite_ref-ewtn_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ewtn-11">[11]</a></sup> Communion in the hand is now widespread in many parts of the world. The practice means that access to consecrated Hosts is easier than in the past, since the person receiving it in the hand may pretend to place it in their mouth for consumption. However, recent statements and practices of <a href="/wiki/Pope_Benedict_XVI" title="Pope Benedict XVI">Pope Benedict XVI</a><sup id="cite_ref-light_of_the_world_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-light_of_the_world-12">[12]</a></sup> have caused a recent shift in Catholic practice (notably at Papal Masses and amongst more traditional-minded Catholics) of receiving on the tongue while kneeling, which is also an ancient practice. (This practice was still performed commonly and consistently, even as recently as the early 1970s in America, and is still received orally in many churches and countries presently. Receiving on the tongue is still the official norm of the Catholic Church, while receiving in the hand [via the Memoriale Domini indult] is, in English-speaking countries, the practical norm.)</p>

<p>Accusations of host desecration (German <i>Hostienschändung</i>) leveled against <a href="/wiki/Jews" title="Jews">Jews</a> were a common pretext for massacres and expulsions throughout the <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">Middle Ages</a> in Europe.<sup id="cite_ref-je_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-je-1">[1]</a></sup> The libel of "<a href="/wiki/Jewish_deicide" title="Jewish deicide">Jewish deicide</a>": that the Jewish people were responsible for the killing of Jesus, whom Christians regard as God become man, was a generally accepted Christian belief. It was spuriously claimed that Jews stole hosts (objects to which they attached no significance, religious or otherwise), and further spuriously claimed that they abused these hosts to re-enact the <a href="/wiki/Crucifixion_of_Jesus" title="Crucifixion of Jesus">crucifixion of Jesus</a> by stabbing or burning them.</p>
<p>It has been asserted by modern scholars, such as the Catholic priest Gavin Langmuir, that these accusations against Jews represented profound doubt about the truth of Christianity.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> Catholics were being told, in the <a href="/wiki/Transubstantiation" title="Transubstantiation">transubstantiation</a> doctrine, that they, by consuming the host, were eating human flesh and drinking human blood. To dispel their doubts about transubstantiation, Christians projected a system of belief onto Jews that was completely alien to <a href="/wiki/Judaism" title="Judaism">Judaism</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jewish_law" class="mw-redirect" title="Jewish law">Jewish law</a> where strict dietary laws forbid the consumption of blood; even when consuming <a href="/wiki/Kosher_animals" title="Kosher animals">kosher animals</a>.</p>
<p>Jews in the Middle Ages were frequently victims of similar accusations, considered more serious desecration of other revered items, such as relics or images of Jesus and the saints. The accusations were often supported only by the testimony of the accuser, who may potentially bear a prejudice against the accused Jew or the Jewish people. Despite this, some alleged perpetrators were tried and found guilty, on little evidence or through torture.<sup id="cite_ref-je_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-je-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>The penalties for Jews accused of defiling hosts were severe. Many Jews, after accusations and torture, "confessed" to abusing hosts, and the accused Jews were condemned and burned, sometimes with all the other Jews in the community, as happened in <a href="/wiki/Beelitz" title="Beelitz">Beelitz</a> in 1247,<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> in <a href="/wiki/Prague" title="Prague">Prague</a> in 1389,<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> and in many German cities, according to Ocker's writings in the <i>Harvard Theological Review</i>. According to William Nichol in <i>Christian Antisemitism</i>, "over 100 instances of the charge have been recorded, in many cases leading to massacres."</p>
<p>As mentioned above, the first recorded accusation was made in 1247 at <a href="/wiki/Beelitz" title="Beelitz">Beelitz</a>, south of Potsdam. Tradition records that as a consequence the Jews of Beelitz were burned on a hill before the Mill Gate, which was subsequently, and until 1945, called the Judenberg, although there is no contemporary evidence for the burnings in documents of the 13th Century.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Another famous case that took place in 1290, in Paris, was commemorated in the <a href="https://fr.wikipedia.org/wiki/Clo%C3%AEtre_et_%C3%A9glise_des_Billettes" class="extiw" title="fr:Cloître et église des Billettes">Church of the Rue des Billettes</a> and in a local confraternity. The case of 1337, at <a href="/wiki/Deggendorf" title="Deggendorf">Deggendorf</a>, celebrated locally as part of the <i>"Deggendorfer Gnad"</i> until 1992, led to a series of massacres across the region. In 1370 in Brussels the charge of host desecration, long celebrated in a special fest and depicted in artistic relics in the Church of St. Gudule, led to the burning of twenty Jews and expulsion in the <a href="/wiki/Brussels_massacre" title="Brussels massacre">Brussels massacre</a>. In 1510, at <a href="https://de.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knoblauch_(Ketzin)" class="extiw" title="de:Knoblauch (Ketzin)">Knoblauch</a> in <a href="/wiki/Havelland" title="Havelland">Havelland</a> 38 Jews were executed and more expelled from <a href="/wiki/Brandenburg" title="Brandenburg">Brandenburg</a>.</p>
<p>An alleged host desecration in 1410, at <a href="/wiki/Segovia" title="Segovia">Segovia</a>, was said to have brought about an earthquake; as a result, leading Jews in the city were executed and the local synagogue was seized and re-dedicated as the convent and Church of Corpus Christi.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>Similar accusations, resulting in extensive persecution of Jews, were brought forward in 1294, at <a href="/wiki/Laa_an_der_Thaya" title="Laa an der Thaya">Laa</a>, Austria; 1298, at <a href="/wiki/R%C3%B6ttingen" title="Röttingen">Röttingen</a>, near <a href="/wiki/W%C3%BCrzburg" title="Würzburg">Würzburg</a>, and at Korneuburg, near <a href="/wiki/Vienna" title="Vienna">Vienna</a>; 1299, at <a href="/wiki/Ratisbon" class="mw-redirect" title="Ratisbon">Ratisbon</a>; 1306, at <a href="/wiki/St._P%C3%B6lten" class="mw-redirect" title="St. Pölten">St. Pölten</a>; 1330, at <a href="/wiki/G%C3%BCstrow" title="Güstrow">Güstrow</a>; 1338, at <a href="/wiki/Pulkau" title="Pulkau">Pulkau</a>; 1388, at <a href="/wiki/Prague" title="Prague">Prague</a>; 1401, at <a href="/wiki/Glogau" class="mw-redirect" title="Glogau">Glogau</a>; 1420, at Ems; 1453, at <a href="/wiki/Breslau" class="mw-redirect" title="Breslau">Breslau</a>; 1478, at <a href="/wiki/Passau" title="Passau">Passau</a>; 1492, at <a href="/wiki/Sternberg,_Mecklenburg-Vorpommern" title="Sternberg, Mecklenburg-Vorpommern">Sternberg</a>, in <a href="/wiki/Mecklenburg" title="Mecklenburg">Mecklenburg</a>; 1514, at Mittelberg, in <a href="/wiki/Alsace" title="Alsace">Alsace</a>; 1556, at <a href="/wiki/Sochaczew" title="Sochaczew">Sochaczew</a>, in Poland. The last Jew burned for stealing a host died in 1631, according to <a href="/wiki/Jacques_Basnage" title="Jacques Basnage">Jacques Basnage</a>, quoting from Manasseh b. Israel. In some cases host desecration legends emerged without actual accusations, as was the case of the host desecration legend of Poznan (Posen).<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>

Based on the Paris 1290 legend, a Jewish moneylender is cooking the host, which emanates blood. The Jew's wife, her unborn child, and her children look on in terror as the blood pours into the street in rivers while soldiers break through the door. The painting is structured around the Golden Section.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The accusation of host desecration gradually ceased after the <a href="/wiki/Protestant_Reformation" class="mw-redirect" title="Protestant Reformation">Reformation</a> when first <a href="/wiki/Martin_Luther" title="Martin Luther">Martin Luther</a> in 1523 and then <a href="/wiki/Sigismund_August_of_Poland" class="mw-redirect" title="Sigismund August of Poland">Sigismund August of Poland</a> in 1558 were among those who repudiated the accusation. However, sporadic instances of host desecration libel occurred even in the 18th and 19th century. In 1761 in <a href="/wiki/Nancy,_France" title="Nancy, France">Nancy</a>, several Jews from <a href="/wiki/Alsace" title="Alsace">Alsace</a> were executed on a charge of host desecration. The last recorded accusation was brought up in <a href="/wiki/Berlad" class="mw-redirect" title="Berlad">Berlad</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Romania" title="Romania">Romania</a>, in 1836.<sup id="cite_ref-je_1-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-je-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="2008_controversy_in_the_US">2008 controversy in the US</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Host_desecration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: 2008 controversy in the US">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In his July 8 blog entry, <a href="/wiki/University_of_Minnesota_Morris" title="University of Minnesota Morris">University of Minnesota Morris</a> biology professor <a href="/wiki/PZ_Myers" title="PZ Myers">Paul Zachary Myers</a> criticized the reaction to a <a href="/wiki/University_of_Central_Florida" title="University of Central Florida">University of Central Florida</a> student's perceived act of host desecration (the student had attempted to bring the host to a friend who was curious about communion). Myers described the level of harassment against the student and expressed his intent to desecrate the wafer, which Catholics consider a <a href="/wiki/Mortal_sin" title="Mortal sin">mortal sin</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>Myers expressed outrage that <a href="/wiki/Fox_News" title="Fox News">Fox News</a> appeared to be inciting viewers to cause further problems for the student, and ridiculed reports that armed guards would attend the next Mass. Myers suggested that if any of his readers could acquire some consecrated Eucharistic hosts for him, he would treat the wafers "with profound disrespect and heinous cracker abuse, all photographed and presented here on the web."<sup id="cite_ref-frackincracker_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-frackincracker-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>A number of Catholics immediately reacted strongly. <a href="/wiki/William_A._Donohue" class="mw-redirect" title="William A. Donohue">William A. Donohue</a> of <a href="/wiki/Catholic_League_(U.S.)" title="Catholic League (U.S.)">The Catholic League</a> accused Myers of <a href="/wiki/Anti-Catholicism" title="Anti-Catholicism">anti-catholic</a> <a href="/wiki/Bigotry" class="mw-redirect" title="Bigotry">bigotry</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-holyheck_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-holyheck-21">[21]</a></sup> described his proposal as a threat to <a href="/wiki/Desecration" title="Desecration">desecrate</a> what <a href="/wiki/Transubstantiation" title="Transubstantiation">Catholics hold to be the Body of Christ</a>, and sent a letter asking the University of Minnesota and the Minnesota State Legislature to take action against Myers.<sup id="cite_ref-holyheck_21-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-holyheck-21">[21]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-pledge2_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pledge2-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>Myers pierced a Host with a rusty nail, which he also used to pierce a few ripped-out pages of the <a href="/wiki/Qur%27an" class="mw-redirect" title="Qur'an">Qur'an</a> and <i><a href="/wiki/The_God_Delusion" title="The God Delusion">The God Delusion</a></i>, put them all in the trash along with old coffee grounds and a banana peel.<sup id="cite_ref-Myers_.28July_24.2C_2008.29_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Myers_.28July_24.2C_2008.29-23">[23]</a></sup> He provided a photograph on his blog of these items in the garbage and wrote that nothing must be held sacred, encouraging people to question everything.<sup id="cite_ref-Myers_.28July_24.2C_2008.29_23-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Myers_.28July_24.2C_2008.29-23">[23]</a></sup> In addition, he described the history of allegations of host desecration, emphasizing the frequent use of such allegations in medieval Europe to justify <a href="/wiki/Anti-Semitism" class="mw-redirect" title="Anti-Semitism">anti-Semitism</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Myers_.28July_24.2C_2008.29_23-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Myers_.28July_24.2C_2008.29-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>According to Donohue, as the <a href="/wiki/Pharyngula_(blog)" title="Pharyngula (blog)">Pharyngula</a> website was accessible via a link from the University of Minnesota website, it should be bound by the institution's code of conduct which requires faculty to be "respectful, fair and civil" when dealing with others.<sup id="cite_ref-pledge2_22-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pledge2-22">[22]</a></sup> Subsequently, Myers explained to the <i><a href="/wiki/Star_Tribune" title="Star Tribune">Star Tribune</a></i> that while his post was "satire and protest", he had received death threats regarding the incident but was not taking them too seriously.<sup id="cite_ref-holyheck_21-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-holyheck-21">[21]</a></sup> The University of Minnesota, Morris (UMM) Chancellor defended Myers, and stated: "I believe that behaviors that discriminate against or harass individuals or groups on the basis of their religious beliefs are reprehensible" and that the school "affirms the freedom of a faculty member to speak or write as a public citizen without institutional discipline or restraint."<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Al-Islam_magazine"><i>Al-Islam</i> magazine</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Host_desecration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Al-Islam magazine">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Al-islam-may09.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/5/5f/Al-islam-may09.jpg/220px-Al-islam-may09.jpg" width="220" height="328" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/5/5f/Al-islam-may09.jpg 1.5x" data-file-width="258" data-file-height="385" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Al-islam-may09.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Cover of the May 2009 edition of <i>Al-Islam</i> with the page containing the photograph of the desecrated Host superimposed over it.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>In 2009, two Muslim reporters from <i>Al-Islam</i>, a small <a href="/wiki/Malaysia" title="Malaysia">Malaysian</a> magazine, participated in a Catholic Mass, while undercover writing an article on cases of <a href="/wiki/Apostasy_in_Islam" title="Apostasy in Islam">apostasy from Islam</a> (<i>riddah</i>) and received Holy Communion. The reporters afterwards spat out the Host and photographed it to prove they had not <a href="/wiki/Apostasy" title="Apostasy">apostatised</a> themselves.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> The resulting photo was then published in their May 2009 edition.<sup id="cite_ref-SunIslam_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SunIslam-26">[26]</a></sup> The magazine, which is owned by Utusan Karya, part of the <a href="/wiki/Utusan_Malaysia" title="Utusan Malaysia">Utusan Malaysia</a> Group, sent its reporters, including Muhd Ridwan Abdul Jalil, to two churches in the <a href="/wiki/Klang_Valley" title="Klang Valley">Klang Valley</a>, as part of a special investigative report.<sup id="cite_ref-SunIslam_26-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SunIslam-26">[26]</a></sup> The act of desecration occurred at St Anthony's Church in Jalan Robertson, <a href="/wiki/Kuala_Lumpur" title="Kuala Lumpur">Kuala Lumpur</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-NST_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NST-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p>After its publication, two <a href="/wiki/Laity" title="Laity">lay</a> Catholics from <a href="/wiki/Penang" title="Penang">Penang</a>, Sudhagaran Stanley and Joachim Francis Xavier, jointly lodged a police report against the reporters.<sup id="cite_ref-cathnews-islam_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cathnews-islam-28">[28]</a></sup> The police took no action despite a potential charge under Section 298A (1) of the Penal Code for causing disharmony, disunity or feelings of enmity, hatred or ill will, or prejudicing the maintenance of harmony or unity, on grounds of religion.<sup id="cite_ref-SunIslam_26-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SunIslam-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<p>The desecration caused widespread outrage and condemnation from non-Muslims as well as Muslims<sup id="cite_ref-TMI1_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TMI1-29">[29]</a></sup> across the country. Parties including the Archbishop of Kuala Lumpur, <a href="/wiki/Murphy_Pakiam" title="Murphy Pakiam">Murphy Pakiam</a>; the Catholic Lawyers Society; as well as numerous editorials in the media,<sup id="cite_ref-SunIslam_26-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SunIslam-26">[26]</a></sup> criticised the government and the Attorney-General for its failure to act.<sup id="cite_ref-AFP-islam_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AFP-islam-30">[30]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-AsiaOne_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AsiaOne-31">[31]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-SC1_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SC1-32">[32]</a></sup> Many saw this inaction as a case of the government's double standards, when dealing with religious issues.</p>
<p>Some nine months later, in early March 2010, <i>Al-Islam</i> published an apology to the <a href="/wiki/Catholic_Church_in_Malaysia" title="Catholic Church in Malaysia">Catholic Church</a> and other Christians for the article. It was posted on the website of its publisher.<sup id="cite_ref-starapology_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-starapology-33">[33]</a></sup> Archbishop Pakiam, who is also president of the Catholic Bishops’ Conference of Malaysia, Singapore and Brunei, accepted the apology and said that no further (legal) action would be taken.<sup id="cite_ref-herald_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-herald-34">[34]</a></sup> The journalist and his colleague have personally never made any public statements on the matter nor apologised.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Desecration_during_a_Black_Mass">Desecration during a Black Mass</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Host_desecration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Desecration during a Black Mass">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A <a href="/wiki/Black_mass" class="mw-redirect" title="Black mass">Black mass</a> is a Satanic ritual designed to satirize or invert a traditional <a href="/wiki/Catholic_mass" class="mw-redirect" title="Catholic mass">Catholic mass</a>. Consecrated hosts are a common ingredient in black masses, becoming the subject of desecration. The hosts must first be stolen from the tabernacle of a Catholic church, and/or secreted away by people who are posing as parishioners taking communion.</p>
<p>In 2014, the Dakhma of Angra Mainyu held a public black mass at the Oklahoma Civic Center<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> and planned to include the desecration of a consecrated host, which was to be "stomped on". That did not transpire: instead, the host was returned through an attorney after the archdiocese filed a lawsuit for its recovery<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup>.</p>
