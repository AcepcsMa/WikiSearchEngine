<p>The <b>Golden Bull of 1356</b> (<a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a>: <span lang="de" xml:lang="de"><i>Goldene Bulle</i></span>, <a href="/wiki/Latin_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Latin language">Latin</a>: <span lang="la" xml:lang="la"><i>Bulla Aurea</i></span>) was a decree issued by the <a href="/wiki/Imperial_Diet_(Holy_Roman_Empire)" title="Imperial Diet (Holy Roman Empire)">Imperial Diet</a> at <a href="/wiki/Diet_of_Nuremberg" title="Diet of Nuremberg">Nuremberg</a> and <a href="/wiki/Metz" title="Metz">Metz</a> (<a href="/wiki/Diet_of_Metz_(1356/57)" title="Diet of Metz (1356/57)">Diet of Metz (1356/57)</a>) headed by the Emperor <a href="/wiki/Charles_IV,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Charles IV, Holy Roman Emperor">Charles IV</a> which fixed, for a period of more than four hundred years, important aspects of the <a href="/wiki/Constitution" title="Constitution">constitutional</a> structure of the <a href="/wiki/Holy_Roman_Empire" title="Holy Roman Empire">Holy Roman Empire</a>. It was named the <i><a href="/wiki/Golden_Bull" class="mw-redirect" title="Golden Bull">Golden Bull</a></i> for the golden seal it carried.</p>
<p>In June 2013 the Golden Bull was included in the <a href="/wiki/UNESCO" title="UNESCO">UNESCO</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Memory_of_the_World_Programme" title="Memory of the World Programme">Memory of the World Register</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Background">Background</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Golden_Bull_of_1356&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>According to the written text of the Golden Bull of 1356</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>We have promulgated, decreed and recommended for ratification the subjoined laws for the purpose of cherishing unity among the Electors, and of bringing about a unanimous election, and of closing all approach to the aforesaid detestable discord and to the various dangers which arise from it.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>Though the election of the <a href="/wiki/King_of_the_Romans" title="King of the Romans">King of the Romans</a> by the chief ecclesiastical and secular princes of the Holy Roman Empire was well established, disagreements about the process and papal involvement had repeatedly resulted in controversies, most recently in 1314 when <a href="/wiki/Louis_IV,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Louis IV, Holy Roman Emperor">Louis of Bavaria</a> and <a href="/wiki/Frederick_the_Fair" title="Frederick the Fair">Frederick of Austria</a> had been elected by opposing sets of electors. Louis, who had eventually subdued his rival's claim on the battlefield,<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> made a first attempt to clarify the process in the <a href="/wiki/Declaration_of_Rhense" title="Declaration of Rhense">Declaration of Rhense</a> of 1338, which renounced any papal involvement and had restricted the right to choose a new king to the <a href="/wiki/Prince-elector" title="Prince-elector">prince-electors</a>. The Golden Bull, promulgated by Louis's successor and rival, Charles IV, was more precise in several ways.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Prince-electors">Prince-electors</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Golden_Bull_of_1356&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Prince-electors">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Firstly, the Bull explicitly named the seven <a href="/wiki/Prince-elector" title="Prince-elector">Prince-electors</a> (<i>Kurfürsten</i>) who were to choose the King and also defined the <i>Reichserzämter</i>, their (largely ceremonial) offices at court:<sup id="cite_ref-Bryce_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bryce-4">[4]</a></sup></p>

<p>Secondly, the principle of <a href="/wiki/Majority_rule" title="Majority rule">majority voting</a> was explicitly stated for the first time in the Empire. The Bull prescribed that four (out of seven) votes would always suffice to elect a new King; as a result, three Electors could no longer block the election. Thirdly, the Electoral principalities were declared <a href="/wiki/Primogeniture" title="Primogeniture">indivisible</a>, and succession to them was regulated to ensure that the votes would never be divided. Finally, the Bull cemented a number of privileges for the Electors, confirming their elevated role in the Empire. It is therefore also a milestone in the establishment of largely independent states in the Empire, a process to be concluded only centuries later, notably with the <a href="/wiki/Peace_of_Westphalia" title="Peace of Westphalia">Peace of Westphalia</a> of 1648.</p>
<p>This codification of prince-electors, though largely based on precedence, was not uncontroversial, especially in regard to the two chief rivals of the ruling <a href="/wiki/House_of_Luxembourg" title="House of Luxembourg">House of Luxembourg</a>:</p>
<ul>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/House_of_Wittelsbach" title="House of Wittelsbach">House of Wittelsbach</a> ruled the <a href="/wiki/Duchy_of_Bavaria" title="Duchy of Bavaria">Duchy of Bavaria</a> as well as the County Palatinate. Dynastic divisions had caused the two territories to devolve upon distinct branches of the house. The <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Pavia_(1329)" title="Treaty of Pavia (1329)">Treaty of Pavia</a>, which in 1329 restored the Palatinate branch, stipulated that Bavaria and the Palatinate would alternate in future elections but the Golden Bull fixed the electoral vote upon the Palatinate and not upon Bavaria, partly because Charles's predecessor and rival, <a href="/wiki/Louis_IV,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Louis IV, Holy Roman Emperor">Louis IV</a> was of that branch. Louis IV's sons, <a href="/wiki/Louis_V,_Duke_of_Bavaria" title="Louis V, Duke of Bavaria">Louis V</a> and <a href="/wiki/Stephen_II,_Duke_of_Bavaria" title="Stephen II, Duke of Bavaria">Stephen II of Bavaria</a>, protested this omission, feeling that Bavaria, one of the <a href="/wiki/History_of_Bavaria" title="History of Bavaria">original duchies</a> of the realm and their family's chief territory for over 170 years, deserved primacy over the Palatinate. The omission of Bavaria from the list of prince-electors also allowed Bavaria, which had only recently been reunited, to fall into dynastic fragmentation again. Brandenburg was in the hands of the Bavarian Wittelsbachs (though held by a junior member of the house) in 1356, they eventually lost the territory to the Luxemburgs in 1373, leaving the Bavarian branch without representation on the Electoral college until 1623.</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/House_of_Habsburg" title="House of Habsburg">House of Habsburg</a>, long-time rivals of the Luxembourgs, were completely omitted from the list of prince-electors, leading to decreased political influence and dynastic fragmentation. In retaliation, Duke <a href="/wiki/Rudolf_IV,_Duke_of_Austria" title="Rudolf IV, Duke of Austria">Rudolf IV</a>, one of the dukes of fragmented Austria, had the <i><a href="/wiki/Privilegium_Maius" title="Privilegium Maius">Privilegium Maius</a></i> forged, a document supposedly issued by Emperor <a href="/wiki/Frederick_I,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Frederick I, Holy Roman Emperor">Frederick Barbarossa</a>. The document gave Austria - elevated to the position of an <a href="/wiki/Archduchy_of_Austria" title="Archduchy of Austria">Archduchy</a> - special privileges, including <a href="/wiki/Primogeniture" title="Primogeniture">primogeniture</a>. While ignored by the Emperor and other princes at the time, the document was eventually ratified when <a href="/wiki/Frederick_III,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Frederick III, Holy Roman Emperor">Frederick of Austria</a> himself became Emperor in the 15th century. Still, the Habsburgs remained without an electoral vote until they succeeded to the Kingdom of Bohemia in 1526.</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Procedures">Procedures</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Golden_Bull_of_1356&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Procedures">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">

Imperial Diet in <a href="/wiki/Metz" title="Metz">Metz</a> during which the Golden Bull of 1356 was issued.</div>

<p>The bull regulated the whole election process in great detail, listing explicitly where, when, and under which circumstances what should be done by whom, not only for the <a href="/wiki/Prince-elector" title="Prince-elector">prince-electors</a> but also (for example) for the population of <a href="/wiki/Frankfurt" title="Frankfurt">Frankfurt</a>, where the elections were to be held, and also for the counts of the regions the prince-electors had to travel through to get there. The decision to hold the elections in Frankfurt reflected a traditional feeling dating from <a href="/wiki/East_Frankish" class="mw-redirect" title="East Frankish">East Frankish</a> days that both election and coronation ought to take place on Frankish soil.<sup id="cite_ref-Bryce_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bryce-4">[4]</a></sup> However, the election location was not the only specified location; the bull specified that the coronation would take place in Aachen, and Nuremberg would be the place where the first diet of a reign should be held.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> The elections were to be concluded within thirty days; failing that, the bull prescribed that the prince-electors were to receive only bread and water until they had decided:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p><span lang="la" xml:lang="la">Quod si facere distulerint infra triginta dies, a die prestiti juramenti prefati continuo numerandos, extunc transactis eisdem triginta diebus amodo panem manducent et aquam et nullatenus civitatem exeant antedictam, nisi prius per ipsos vel majorem partem ipsorum rector seu temporale caput fidelium electum fuerit, ut prefertur.</span><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
[Translation:] But if they shall fail to do this within thirty days, counting continuously from the day when they took the aforesaid oath: when those thirty days are over, from that time on they shall live on bread and water, and by no means leave the aforesaid city [<a href="/wiki/Frankfurt" title="Frankfurt">Frankfurt</a>] unless first through them, or the majority of them, a ruler or temporal head of the faithful shall have been elected, as was said before.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>
<div class="templatequotecite"><cite>— Chapter 2, §3.</cite></div>
</blockquote>
<p>Besides regulating the election process, the chapters of the Golden Bull contained many minor decrees. For instance, it also defined the order of marching when the emperor was present, both with and without his <a href="/wiki/Insignia" title="Insignia">insignia</a>. A relatively major decision was made in chapter 15, where Charles IV outlawed any <i><span lang="la" xml:lang="la">conjurationes, confederationes,</span></i> and <i><span lang="la" xml:lang="la">conspirationes</span></i>, meaning in particular the city alliances (<i><a href="/wiki/St%C3%A4dtebund" class="mw-redirect" title="Städtebund">Städtebünde</a></i>), but also other communal leagues that had sprung up through the <a href="/wiki/Communal_movement_in_mediaeval_Europe" class="mw-redirect" title="Communal movement in mediaeval Europe">communal movement in mediaeval Europe</a>. Most <i>Städtebünde</i> were subsequently dissolved,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> sometimes forcibly, and where refounded, their political influence was much reduced .<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Thus the Golden Bull also strengthened the nobility in general to the detriment of the cities .<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>

<p><a href="/wiki/Pope_Innocent_VI" title="Pope Innocent VI">The pope</a>'s involvement with the Golden Bull of 1356 was basically nonexistent, which was significant in the history of relations between the popes and the emperors. When Charles IV laid down procedure for electing a King of the Romans, he mentioned nothing about receiving papal confirmation of the election. However, <a href="/wiki/Pope_Innocent_VI" title="Pope Innocent VI">Pope Innocent VI</a> did not protest this because he needed Charles’s support against the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Visconti" title="House of Visconti">Visconti</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Pope Innocent continued to have good relations with Charles IV after the Golden Bull of 1356 until his death in 1362.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
