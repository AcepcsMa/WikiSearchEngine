<p>The <b>Social Democratic Party of Germany</b> (<a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a>: <span lang="de"><i>Sozialdemokratische Partei Deutschlands</i></span>, <b>SPD</b>) is a <a href="/wiki/Social_democracy" title="Social democracy">social-democratic</a><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Almeida_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Almeida-5">[5]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Lavelle2013_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lavelle2013-6">[6]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/List_of_political_parties_in_Germany" title="List of political parties in Germany">political party in Germany</a>. The party, led by Chairman <a href="/wiki/Martin_Schulz" title="Martin Schulz">Martin Schulz</a> since 2017, has become one of the two <a href="/wiki/Major_party" title="Major party">major</a> contemporary political parties in Germany, along with the <a href="/wiki/Christian_Democratic_Union_of_Germany" title="Christian Democratic Union of Germany">Christian Democratic Union</a> (CDU).</p>
<p>The SPD has governed at the federal level in Germany as part of a <a href="/wiki/Grand_Coalition_(Germany)" class="mw-redirect" title="Grand Coalition (Germany)">grand coalition</a> with the CDU and the <a href="/wiki/Christian_Social_Union_in_Bavaria" title="Christian Social Union in Bavaria">Christian Social Union</a> (CSU) since December 2013 following the results of the <a href="/wiki/German_federal_election,_2013" title="German federal election, 2013">2013 federal election</a>. The SPD participates in 14 <a href="/wiki/States_of_Germany" title="States of Germany">state</a> governments, nine of them governed by SPD <a href="/wiki/Minister-President" class="mw-redirect" title="Minister-President">Minister-Presidents</a>.</p>
<p>The SPD is a member of the <a href="/wiki/Party_of_European_Socialists" title="Party of European Socialists">Party of European Socialists</a> and of the <a href="/wiki/Socialist_International" title="Socialist International">Socialist International</a>, and became a founding member of the <a href="/wiki/Progressive_Alliance" title="Progressive Alliance">Progressive Alliance</a> on 22 May 2013.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Established in 1863, the SPD is the oldest extant political party represented in the <a href="/wiki/Bundestag" title="Bundestag">German Parliament</a> and was one of the first <a href="/wiki/Marxism" title="Marxism">Marxist</a>-influenced parties in the world.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Social_Democratic_Party_of_Germany&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/History_of_the_Social_Democratic_Party_of_Germany" title="History of the Social Democratic Party of Germany">History of the Social Democratic Party of Germany</a></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/General_German_Workers%27_Association" title="General German Workers' Association">General German Workers' Association</a> (<i>Allgemeiner Deutscher Arbeiterverein</i>, ADAV), founded in 1863, and the <a href="/wiki/Social_Democratic_Workers%27_Party_of_Germany" title="Social Democratic Workers' Party of Germany">Social Democratic Workers' Party</a> (<i>Sozialdemokratische Arbeiterpartei Deutschlands</i>, SDAP), founded in 1869, merged in 1875, under the name Socialist Workers' Party of Germany (<i>Sozialistische Arbeiterpartei Deutschlands</i>, SAPD). From 1878 to 1890, any grouping or meeting that aimed at spreading socialist principles was banned under the <a href="/wiki/Anti-Socialist_Laws" title="Anti-Socialist Laws">Anti-Socialist Laws</a>, but the party still gained support in elections. In 1890, when the ban was lifted and it could again present electoral lists, the party adopted its current name. In the years leading up to <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a>, the party remained ideologically radical in official principle, although many party officials tended to be moderate in everyday politics. By 1912, the party claimed the most votes of any German party.</p>
<p>Despite the agreement of the <a href="/wiki/Second_International" title="Second International">Second International</a> to oppose the First World War, the SPD voted in favor of war in 1914. In response to this and the <a href="/wiki/October_Revolution" title="October Revolution">Bolshevik Revolution</a>, members of the left and of the <a href="/wiki/Far-left_politics" title="Far-left politics">far-left</a> of the SPD formed alternative parties, first the <a href="/wiki/Spartacus_League" title="Spartacus League">Spartacus League</a>, then the <a href="/wiki/Independent_Social_Democratic_Party_of_Germany" title="Independent Social Democratic Party of Germany">Independent Social Democratic Party of Germany</a> and later the <a href="/wiki/Communist_Party_of_Germany" title="Communist Party of Germany">Communist Party of Germany</a>. After 1918 the SPD played an important role in the political system of the <a href="/wiki/Weimar_Republic" title="Weimar Republic">Weimar Republic</a>, although it took part in coalition governments only in few years (1918–1921, 1923, 1928–1930). <a href="/wiki/Adolf_Hitler" title="Adolf Hitler">Adolf Hitler</a> prohibited the party in 1933 under the <a href="/wiki/Enabling_Act_of_1933" title="Enabling Act of 1933">Enabling Act</a> – party officials were imprisoned, killed or went into exile. In exile, the party used the name <a href="/wiki/Sopade" title="Sopade">Sopade</a>. The SPD had been the only party to vote against the Enabling Act (while the Communist Party was blocked from voting).</p>
<p>In 1945, the allied occupants in the Western zones initially allowed four parties to be established, which led to the <a href="/wiki/Christian_Democratic_Union_(Germany)" class="mw-redirect" title="Christian Democratic Union (Germany)">Christian Democratic Union</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Free_Democratic_Party_(Germany)" title="Free Democratic Party (Germany)">Free Democratic Party</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Communist_Party_of_Germany" title="Communist Party of Germany">Communist Party of Germany</a>, and the SPD being established. In the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Zone_of_Occupation" class="mw-redirect" title="Soviet Zone of Occupation">Soviet Zone of Occupation</a>, the Soviets forced the Social Democrats to form a common party with the Communists (<a href="/wiki/Socialist_Unity_Party_of_Germany" title="Socialist Unity Party of Germany">Socialist Unity Party of Germany</a> or SED). In the Western zones, the Communist Party was later (1956) banned by West Germany's Federal Constitutional Court. Since 1949, in the Federal Republic of Germany, the SPD has been one of the two major parties, with the other being the Christian Democratic Union. From 1969 to 1982 and 1998 to 2005 the <a href="/wiki/Chancellor_of_Germany_(Federal_Republic_of_Germany)" class="mw-redirect" title="Chancellor of Germany (Federal Republic of Germany)">Chancellors of Germany</a> were Social Democrats whereas the other years the Chancellors were Christian Democrats.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Party_platform">Party platform</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Social_Democratic_Party_of_Germany&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Party platform">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The SPD was established as a <a href="/wiki/Marxism" title="Marxism">Marxist</a> party in 1875. However, the SPD underwent a major shift in policies reflected in the differences between the Heidelberg Program of 1925, which "called for the transformation of the capitalist system of private ownership of the means of production to social ownership",<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> and the <a href="/wiki/Godesberg_Program" title="Godesberg Program">Godesberg Program</a> of 1959, which aimed to broaden its voter base and move its political position toward the centre.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> After <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>, under the leadership of <a href="/wiki/Kurt_Schumacher" title="Kurt Schumacher">Kurt Schumacher</a>, the SPD re-established itself as a socialist party, representing the interests of the <a href="/wiki/Working_class" title="Working class">working class</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Trade_union" title="Trade union">trade unions</a>. With the Godesberg Program of 1959, however, the party evolved from a socialist working-class party to a modern social-democratic party working within capitalism.</p>

<p>The current <a href="/wiki/Party_platform" title="Party platform">party platform</a> of the SPD espouses the goal of <a href="/wiki/Social_democracy" title="Social democracy">social democracy</a>, which is seen as a vision of a societal arrangement in which freedom and <a href="/wiki/Social_justice" title="Social justice">social justice</a> are paramount. According to the party platform, <a href="/wiki/Freedom_(political)" class="mw-redirect" title="Freedom (political)">freedom</a>, <a href="/wiki/Justice" title="Justice">justice</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Social_solidarity" class="mw-redirect" title="Social solidarity">social solidarity</a>, form the basis of social democracy. The coordinated <a href="/wiki/Social_market_economy" title="Social market economy">social market economy</a> should be strengthened, and its output should be distributed fairly. The party sees that economic system as necessary in order to ensure the affluence of the entire population. The SPD also tries to protect the society's poor with a <a href="/wiki/Welfare_state" title="Welfare state">welfare state</a>. Concurrently, it advocates a sustainable <a href="/wiki/Fiscal_policy" title="Fiscal policy">fiscal policy</a> that doesn't place a burden on future generations while eradicating budget deficits. In <a href="/wiki/Social_policy" title="Social policy">social policy</a>, the SPD stands for <a href="/wiki/Civil_and_political_rights" title="Civil and political rights">civil and political rights</a> in an <a href="/wiki/Open_society" title="Open society">open society</a>. In <a href="/wiki/Foreign_policy" title="Foreign policy">foreign policy</a>, the SPD aims at ensuring global peace by balancing global interests with democratic means. Thus, <a href="/wiki/European_integration" title="European integration">European integration</a> is one of the main priorities of the SPD. SPD supports economic regulations to limit potential losses for banks and people. They support a common European economic and financial policy, and to prevent speculative bubbles. They support environmentally sustainable growth.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Internal_factions">Internal factions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Social_Democratic_Party_of_Germany&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Internal factions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The SPD is mostly composed of members belonging to either of the two main wings: <a href="/wiki/Keynesian_economics" title="Keynesian economics">Keynesian</a> social democrats and <a href="/wiki/Third_Way_(Centrism)" class="mw-redirect" title="Third Way (Centrism)">Third Way</a> moderate social democrats belonging to the <a href="/wiki/Seeheimer_Kreis" title="Seeheimer Kreis">Seeheimer Kreis</a>. While the more moderate Seeheimer Kreis generally support the <a href="/wiki/Agenda_2010" title="Agenda 2010">Agenda 2010</a> programs introduced by Chancellor <a href="/wiki/Gerhard_Schr%C3%B6der" title="Gerhard Schröder">Gerhard Schröder</a>, the Keynesian social democrats continue to defend classical left-wing policies and the <a href="/wiki/Welfare_state" title="Welfare state">welfare state</a>. The classical <a href="/wiki/Left-wing_politics" title="Left-wing politics">left-wing</a> of the SPD claims that in recent years the welfare state has been curtailed through reform programs such as the Agenda 2010, <a href="/wiki/Hartz_concept" title="Hartz concept">Hartz IV</a> and the more <a href="/wiki/Economic_liberalism" title="Economic liberalism">economic liberal</a> stance of the SPD, which were endorsed by centrist social democrats.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> As a reaction to the Agenda 2010, there was in 2005 the ascension of an inner party dissident movement, which led ultimately to the foundation of the new party <a href="/wiki/Labour_and_Social_Justice_%E2%80%93_The_Electoral_Alternative" title="Labour and Social Justice – The Electoral Alternative">Labour and Social Justice – The Electoral Alternative</a> (<i>Arbeit &amp; soziale Gerechtigkeit – Die Wahlalternative</i>, WASG). The WASG was later merged into the party <i><a href="/wiki/The_Left_(Germany)" title="The Left (Germany)">Die Linke</a></i> ("The Left") in 2007.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Base_of_support">Base of support</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Social_Democratic_Party_of_Germany&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Base of support">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Social_structure">Social structure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Social_Democratic_Party_of_Germany&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Social structure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Before World War II, as the main non-revolutionary left-wing party, the Social Democrats fared best among non-<a href="/wiki/Catholic" class="mw-redirect" title="Catholic">Catholic</a> workers as well as intellectuals favouring <a href="/wiki/Social_progressivism" class="mw-redirect" title="Social progressivism">social progressive</a> causes and increased economic equality. Led by <a href="/wiki/Kurt_Schumacher" title="Kurt Schumacher">Kurt Schumacher</a> after World War II, the SPD initially opposed both the <a href="/wiki/Social_market_economy" title="Social market economy">social market economy</a> and <a href="/wiki/Konrad_Adenauer" title="Konrad Adenauer">Konrad Adenauer</a>'s drive towards western integration fiercely, but after Schumacher's death, it accepted the social market economy and Germany's position in the Western alliance in order to appeal to a broader range of voters. It still remains associated with the economic causes of unionised employees and working class voters. In the 1990s, the left and moderate wings of the party drifted apart, culminating in a secession of a significant number of party members, which later joined the socialist party <a href="/wiki/Labour_and_Social_Justice_%E2%80%93_The_Electoral_Alternative" title="Labour and Social Justice – The Electoral Alternative">WASG</a>, which later merged into <a href="/wiki/The_Left_(Germany)" title="The Left (Germany)">The Left</a> (<i>Die Linke</i>) party.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Geographic_distribution">Geographic distribution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Social_Democratic_Party_of_Germany&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Geographic distribution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Geographically, much of the SPD's current-day support comes from large cities, especially of northern and western Germany and <a href="/wiki/Berlin" title="Berlin">Berlin</a>. The metropolitan area of the <a href="/wiki/Ruhr_Area" class="mw-redirect" title="Ruhr Area">Ruhr Area</a>, where <a href="/wiki/Coal_mining" title="Coal mining">coal mining</a> and <a href="/wiki/Steel" title="Steel">steel</a> production were once the biggest sources of revenues, have provided a significant base for the SPD in the 20th century. In the state <a href="/wiki/Bremen_(state)" title="Bremen (state)">Free Hanseatic City of Bremen</a> the SPD has governed without interruption since 1949. In southern Germany, the SPD typically garners less support except in the largest cities. At the <a href="/wiki/German_federal_election,_2009" title="German federal election, 2009">2009 federal election</a>, the party lost its only constituency in the entire state of <a href="/wiki/Bavaria" title="Bavaria">Bavaria</a> (in <a href="/wiki/Munich" title="Munich">Munich</a>). Small town and rural support comes especially from the traditionally <a href="/wiki/Protestantism" title="Protestantism">Protestant</a> areas of northern Germany and <a href="/wiki/Brandenburg" title="Brandenburg">Brandenburg</a> (with notable exceptions such as <a href="/wiki/Western_Pomerania" title="Western Pomerania">Western Pomerania</a> where CDU leader <a href="/wiki/Angela_Merkel" title="Angela Merkel">Angela Merkel</a> was re-elected in 2005) and a number of university towns. A striking example of the general pattern is the traditionally Catholic <a href="/wiki/Emsland" title="Emsland">Emsland</a>, where the Social Democrats generally gain a low percentage of votes, whereas the <a href="/wiki/Reformed_churches" class="mw-redirect" title="Reformed churches">Reformed Protestant</a> region of <a href="/wiki/East_Frisia" title="East Frisia">East Frisia</a> directly to the north, with its strong traditional streak of <a href="/wiki/Anti-Catholicism" title="Anti-Catholicism">Anti-Catholicism</a>, is one of their strongest constituencies. Further south, the SPD also enjoys solid support in northern <a href="/wiki/Hesse" title="Hesse">Hesse</a> (<a href="/wiki/Hans_Eichel" title="Hans Eichel">Hans Eichel</a> was mayor of <a href="/wiki/Kassel" title="Kassel">Kassel</a>, then Hesse's minister president, then finance minister in the <a href="/wiki/Gerhard_Schr%C3%B6der" title="Gerhard Schröder">Schröder</a> administration, while <a href="/wiki/Brigitte_Zypries" title="Brigitte Zypries">Brigitte Zypries</a> served as Justice Minister), parts of <a href="/wiki/Palatinate_(region)" title="Palatinate (region)">Palatinate</a> (<a href="/wiki/Kurt_Beck" title="Kurt Beck">Kurt Beck</a> was party leader until 7 September 2008), and the <a href="/wiki/Saarland" title="Saarland">Saarland</a> (political home of one-time candidate for federal chancellor <a href="/wiki/Oskar_Lafontaine" title="Oskar Lafontaine">Oskar Lafontaine</a>, defected from the SPD in 2005).</p>

<p>The Party is led by the Leader of the Social Democratic Party, he/she is supported by 5 Deputy Leaders and the Party Executive.</p>
<p>The current Party Leader is <a href="/wiki/Martin_Schulz" title="Martin Schulz">Martin Schulz</a>, who was elected into office with a 100% election result. The current Deputy Leaders are <a href="/wiki/Manuela_Schwesig" title="Manuela Schwesig">Manuela Schwesig</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ralf_Stegner" title="Ralf Stegner">Ralf Stegner</a>, <a href="/wiki/Olaf_Scholz" title="Olaf Scholz">Olaf Scholz</a> <a href="/wiki/Thorsten_Sch%C3%A4fer-G%C3%BCmbel" title="Thorsten Schäfer-Gümbel">Thorsten Schäfer-Gümbel</a> and <a href="/wiki/Aydan_%C3%96zo%C4%9Fuz" title="Aydan Özoğuz">Aydan Özoğuz</a>.</p>
<p>As Germany is a Federal Republic, each of Germany's States have their own SPD Party at the state level. The current Leaders of the SPD state parties are:</p>
