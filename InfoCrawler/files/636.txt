<p><b>Bundesautobahn&#160;9</b> (translates from <a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a> as <i>Federal Motorway&#160;9</i>, short form <b>Autobahn&#160;9</b>, abbreviated as <b>BAB&#160;9</b> or <b>A&#160;9</b>) is an <a href="/wiki/German_autobahns" class="mw-redirect" title="German autobahns">autobahn</a> in <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a>, connecting <a href="/wiki/Berlin" title="Berlin">Berlin</a> and <a href="/wiki/Munich" title="Munich">Munich</a> via <a href="/wiki/Leipzig" title="Leipzig">Leipzig</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nuremberg" title="Nuremberg">Nuremberg</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Route">Route</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bundesautobahn_9&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Route">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Autobahndreieck_Potsdam.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b2/Autobahndreieck_Potsdam.jpg/220px-Autobahndreieck_Potsdam.jpg" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b2/Autobahndreieck_Potsdam.jpg/330px-Autobahndreieck_Potsdam.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b2/Autobahndreieck_Potsdam.jpg/440px-Autobahndreieck_Potsdam.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3818" data-file-height="2545" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Autobahndreieck_Potsdam.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Potsdam interchange</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The northern terminus of the A&#160;9 is at the <a href="/wiki/Potsdam" title="Potsdam">Potsdam</a> interchange, where it merges into the <a href="/wiki/Bundesautobahn_10" title="Bundesautobahn 10">A&#160;10</a>, also known as the "<i>Berliner Ring</i>", about 30 kilometres (19&#160;mi) away from the Berlin city limits. The shortest route from there into Berlin would be the A&#160;10 (east) and the <a href="/wiki/Bundesautobahn_115" title="Bundesautobahn 115">A&#160;115</a> (<a href="/wiki/AVUS" title="AVUS">AVUS</a>). The southern end is in the Munich borough of <a href="/wiki/Schwabing" title="Schwabing">Schwabing</a>.</p>
<p>On its way, the A&#160;9 passes through the German <a href="/wiki/States_of_Germany" title="States of Germany">states</a> of <a href="/wiki/Brandenburg" title="Brandenburg">Brandenburg</a>, <a href="/wiki/Saxony-Anhalt" title="Saxony-Anhalt">Saxony-Anhalt</a>, <a href="/wiki/Thuringia" title="Thuringia">Thuringia</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Bavaria" title="Bavaria">Bavaria</a>. West of Leipzig, the border between Saxony-Anhalt and <a href="/wiki/Saxony" title="Saxony">Saxony</a> crisscrosses along the autobahn. In Bavaria, long sections of the <a href="/wiki/Nuremberg%E2%80%93Munich_high-speed_railway" title="Nuremberg–Munich high-speed railway">Nuremberg–Munich high-speed railway</a> run parallel to the autobahn.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bundesautobahn_9&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Plans for a European motorway connection from Berlin to <a href="/wiki/Rome" title="Rome">Rome</a> were already developed from 1927 by a private <i>MüLeiBerl</i> (Munich-Leipzig-Berlin) company. However, construction of the A&#160;9 was not begun until the 1930s as part of the <i><a href="/wiki/Reichsautobahn" title="Reichsautobahn">Reichsautobahn</a></i> project set up by <a href="/wiki/Nazi_Germany" title="Nazi Germany">Nazi Germany</a>.</p>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Bundesarchiv_Bild_146-1979-096-13A,_Reichsautobahn,_Saalebr%C3%BCcke_bei_Hirschberg.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7b/Bundesarchiv_Bild_146-1979-096-13A%2C_Reichsautobahn%2C_Saalebr%C3%BCcke_bei_Hirschberg.jpg/220px-Bundesarchiv_Bild_146-1979-096-13A%2C_Reichsautobahn%2C_Saalebr%C3%BCcke_bei_Hirschberg.jpg" width="220" height="153" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7b/Bundesarchiv_Bild_146-1979-096-13A%2C_Reichsautobahn%2C_Saalebr%C3%BCcke_bei_Hirschberg.jpg/330px-Bundesarchiv_Bild_146-1979-096-13A%2C_Reichsautobahn%2C_Saalebr%C3%BCcke_bei_Hirschberg.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7b/Bundesarchiv_Bild_146-1979-096-13A%2C_Reichsautobahn%2C_Saalebr%C3%BCcke_bei_Hirschberg.jpg/440px-Bundesarchiv_Bild_146-1979-096-13A%2C_Reichsautobahn%2C_Saalebr%C3%BCcke_bei_Hirschberg.jpg 2x" data-file-width="800" data-file-height="556" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Bundesarchiv_Bild_146-1979-096-13A,_Reichsautobahn,_Saalebr%C3%BCcke_bei_Hirschberg.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Rudolphstein Saale Bridge in the 1930s, graced by a <a href="/wiki/Mercedes-Benz_buses" title="Mercedes-Benz buses">Mercedes-Benz touring coach</a>. The bridge was later destroyed at the end of the war but rebuilt due to a West and East German cooperation until 1966. The river marked the inner German border</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>It was inaugurated in sections, beginning in 1936 with the <i>Strecke 16</i> between what is today's <a href="/wiki/Schkeuditz" title="Schkeuditz">Schkeuditz</a> interchange (present-day A&#160;9 and A&#160;14) near Leipzig and <a href="/wiki/Bad_Berneck_im_Fichtelgebirge" title="Bad Berneck im Fichtelgebirge">Bad Berneck im Fichtelgebirge</a>, 164 kilometres (102&#160;mi) in length. With the opening of the second lane near Schnaittach in 1941, the last gap was closed. This makes the A&#160;9 Germany's first completed autobahns.</p>
<p>After <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>, the section from the <a href="/wiki/Inner_German_border" title="Inner German border">inner German border</a> to Berlin served as one of four transit access roads through <a href="/wiki/East_Germany" title="East Germany">East Germany</a> (GDR) toward <a href="/wiki/West_Berlin" title="West Berlin">West Berlin</a> during the <a href="/wiki/Cold_War" title="Cold War">Cold War</a> era. Yellow signs with "TRANSIT" in black letters marked the allowed route. Distances were usually given towards <i>Berlin - Hauptstadt der DDR</i> ("Berlin - Capital of the GDR"), i.e. <a href="/wiki/East_Berlin" title="East Berlin">East Berlin</a>. Despite funding from West Germany, road conditions were generally poor. The surface was made up of 1930s concrete slabs rather than blacktop or continuously cast concrete. A section in Thuringia between <a href="/wiki/Schleiz" title="Schleiz">Schleiz</a> and the Rodaborn <a href="/wiki/Rest_area" title="Rest area">rest area</a> even was a <a href="/wiki/Cobblestone" title="Cobblestone">cobblestone</a> road, later paved over by the East German authorities until being replaced by concrete in the 1980s. Until the introduction of a new numbering system in 1974, the southern part was known as the West German A&#160;3.</p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Bayreuth_Autobahnbr%C3%BCcke.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b9/Bayreuth_Autobahnbr%C3%BCcke.jpg/220px-Bayreuth_Autobahnbr%C3%BCcke.jpg" width="220" height="148" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b9/Bayreuth_Autobahnbr%C3%BCcke.jpg/330px-Bayreuth_Autobahnbr%C3%BCcke.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b9/Bayreuth_Autobahnbr%C3%BCcke.jpg/440px-Bayreuth_Autobahnbr%C3%BCcke.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2328" data-file-height="1571" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Bayreuth_Autobahnbr%C3%BCcke.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Uerdingen_railbus" title="Uerdingen railbus">Railbus</a> crossing the A&#160;9 near Bayreuth, 1986</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>After <a href="/wiki/German_reunification" title="German reunification">German reunification</a>, the A&#160;9 was to be extensively rebuilt and upgraded, being "German Unity Transport Project" No.&#160;12. The old two-lane profile without emergency lane was in no way suited to modern levels of traffic, speed, and expected safety. In 1990, 50,000 vehicles per day were counted near <a href="/wiki/Bayreuth" title="Bayreuth">Bayreuth</a>, about as much as the motorway could handle. After the work was finished near Bayreuth in 2006, the only part of the old profile was in Thuringia between the <a href="/wiki/Schleiz" title="Schleiz">Schleiz</a> and <a href="/wiki/Triptis" title="Triptis">Triptis</a> junctions. This construction was finished 2014, so there are at least three (and near Munich more) lanes plus emergency lane in each direction for the whole route. Only Hermsdorf interchange still has a short 2 lane section.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Current_condition_and_future_plans">Current condition and future plans</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bundesautobahn_9&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Current condition and future plans">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:M%C3%BCnchberger_Senke.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fb/M%C3%BCnchberger_Senke.jpg/220px-M%C3%BCnchberger_Senke.jpg" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fb/M%C3%BCnchberger_Senke.jpg/330px-M%C3%BCnchberger_Senke.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fb/M%C3%BCnchberger_Senke.jpg/440px-M%C3%BCnchberger_Senke.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3611" data-file-height="2407" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:M%C3%BCnchberger_Senke.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A&#160;9 near <a href="/wiki/M%C3%BCnchberg" title="Münchberg">Münchberg</a>, Bavaria</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>With one exception, <a href="/wiki/Hermsdorf,_Thuringia" title="Hermsdorf, Thuringia">Hermsdorf</a> interchange, the A&#160;9 has a profile of at least three lanes and one emergency lane per direction. The section between <a href="/wiki/Neufahrn_bei_Freising" title="Neufahrn bei Freising">Neufahrn</a> and the München-Nord interchange north of Munich was upgraded between 2004 and 2006 to four lanes each way. A survey of this section conducted in 2008<sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Bundesautobahn_9&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup> recorded an average number of 143,000 vehicles per day and a maximum of 184,000.</p>
<p>Since 2006 talks have been underway about turning the three-way interchange Holledau into a four-way, and extending the <a href="/wiki/Bundesautobahn_93" title="Bundesautobahn 93">A&#160;93</a> into the Pfaffenhofen area. Further plans and visions include widening the Hermsdorfer Kreuz to six lanes, and widening the sections Nuremberg – Nuremberg-East and Holledau – Neufahrn to eight lanes.</p>
<p>On January 26, 2015 German transport minister <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Dobrindt" title="Alexander Dobrindt">Alexander Dobrindt</a> announced plans to outfit an as yet undesignated section of the A9 in Bavaria as a test track for <a href="/wiki/Autonomous_car" title="Autonomous car">autonomous cars</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Along_the_route">Along the route</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bundesautobahn_9&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Along the route">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Vockerode_Plaste_und_Elaste_Leuchtreklame_DDR.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b8/Vockerode_Plaste_und_Elaste_Leuchtreklame_DDR.png/220px-Vockerode_Plaste_und_Elaste_Leuchtreklame_DDR.png" width="220" height="173" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b8/Vockerode_Plaste_und_Elaste_Leuchtreklame_DDR.png/330px-Vockerode_Plaste_und_Elaste_Leuchtreklame_DDR.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b8/Vockerode_Plaste_und_Elaste_Leuchtreklame_DDR.png/440px-Vockerode_Plaste_und_Elaste_Leuchtreklame_DDR.png 2x" data-file-width="1885" data-file-height="1480" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Vockerode_Plaste_und_Elaste_Leuchtreklame_DDR.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Vockerode Elbe Bridge tower, c. 1984</div>
</div>

<p>East of <a href="/wiki/Dessau" title="Dessau">Dessau</a>, the autobahn crosses the <a href="/wiki/Elbe" title="Elbe">Elbe</a> River via the 654&#160;m (2,146&#160;ft) long <a href="/wiki/Vockerode" title="Vockerode">Vockerode</a> Bridge. Built in 1938 according to plans designed by <a href="/wiki/Paul_Bonatz" title="Paul Bonatz">Paul Bonatz</a>, it was replaced by a new construction in the course of the A&#160;9 extension from 1996 to 2000. The prominent tower at the northern end is preserved, it was widely known to transit travellers for its <i>Plaste und Elaste aus Schkopau</i> ("<a href="/wiki/Plastic" title="Plastic">plastic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Elastomer" title="Elastomer">elastomer</a> from <a href="/wiki/Schkopau" title="Schkopau">Schkopau</a>") neon sign, now on display at the <a href="/wiki/Deutsches_Historisches_Museum" title="Deutsches Historisches Museum">German Historical Museum</a> in Berlin.</p>
<p>Close to exit 10, Dessau-East, a gravel pit had provided material for the construction of the autobahn. After 1939 the pit was renaturated and became the "Reichsautobahnbad Mildensee", with cabins to change, and eateries. It is still in use today as a beach, but not under the old name.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Dessauer_Rennstrecke">The <i>Dessauer Rennstrecke</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bundesautobahn_9&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: The Dessauer Rennstrecke">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>South of exit 11, Dessau-South, nearly ten kilometers of the roadbed — roughly from <a href="/wiki/Thurland" title="Thurland">Thurland</a> southwards to just north of the B 183 (ex-B 186) interchange (exit 12 for Bitterfeld/Wolfen of the modern A9 roadbed) — were upgraded with a paved-over <a href="/wiki/Median" title="Median">median</a> to become the "Dessauer Rennstrecke" (Dessau Racetrack), a 25-meter (82 ft) wide high-speed track intended for races and record attempts, such as by <a href="/wiki/Rudolf_Caracciola" title="Rudolf Caracciola">Rudolf Caracciola</a> in 1938/39, <a href="/wiki/Goldie_Gardner" class="mw-redirect" title="Goldie Gardner">Goldie Gardner</a> in 1939 or those by <a href="/wiki/Hans_Stuck" title="Hans Stuck">Hans Stuck</a> with the <a href="/wiki/Mercedes-Benz_T80" title="Mercedes-Benz T80">Mercedes-Benz T80</a> land speed record car, intended to start in January 1940 - the <i>Rennstrecke</i> segment itself had even been marked out on road maps as early as 1938.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> With its pillarless bridges and no interchange exits before 1945, but especially with the straight alignment and the broad concrete surface without a distinct median, it was also intended to function as an <a href="/wiki/Highway_strip" title="Highway strip">auxiliary airfield</a> in World War II. After the war, annual races were resumed from 1949 to 1956. The East German driver <a href="/wiki/Paul_Greifzu" title="Paul Greifzu">Paul Greifzu</a> was killed in a training accident on 10 May 1952.</p>
<p>The four-way interchange at Schkeuditz was the first cloverleaf interchange in Germany, as well as the first autobahn interchange in Europe. It was opened in 1936, two years before construction was finished.</p>
<p>On June 30, 2004, Germany's oldest autobahn inn, Rodaborn near <a href="/wiki/Triptis" title="Triptis">Triptis</a>, was closed due to the upgrading of the old two-lane to the modern three-lane profile. Located near the source of the <a href="/wiki/Roda_(river)" title="Roda (river)">Roda</a> River, the restaurant was opened in 1928 as a country inn for the local population and turned into a rest area on 20 December 1936 when the <i>Reichsautobahn</i> was opened. During the days of the GDR, it was run by the state-owned <a href="/wiki/Handelsorganisation" title="Handelsorganisation">Handelsorganisation</a> (HO) company, later by <a href="/wiki/Mitropa" title="Mitropa">Mitropa</a>. The rest area was open only for transit travellers, not for East German citizens. In 2009, in a loop of history, it was rented by a local couple to re-open it as an inn for local daytrippers. Though the connection to the adjacent autobahn parking lot is cut off by a fence, Rodaborn still is a popular stop for motorway travellers.</p>

<p>The Rudolphstein <a href="/wiki/Saale" title="Saale">Saale</a> Bridge south of <a href="/wiki/Bad_Lobenstein" title="Bad Lobenstein">Bad Lobenstein</a>, an <a href="/wiki/Arch_bridge" title="Arch bridge">arch bridge</a> designed by Paul Bonatz, was opened on 27 September 1936 and soon became an icon of the <i>Reichsautobahn</i> development program. Between 1945 and 1966 the A&#160;9 was interrupted at the inner German border between Bavaria and Thuringia since an arch of the bridge had been blown up by retiring <a href="/wiki/Wehrmacht" title="Wehrmacht">Wehrmacht</a> forces near the end of the war. Traffic to and from <a href="/wiki/West_Berlin" title="West Berlin">West Berlin</a> was diverted via <a href="/wiki/Bundesautobahn_72" title="Bundesautobahn 72">A&#160;72</a> until 1951 and from then until 1966 via <a href="/wiki/Bundesstra%C3%9Fe_2" title="Bundesstraße 2">B&#160;2</a>. Not until then the bridge could be rebuilt in a complicated joint venture of West and East German authorities. In the course of the autobahn extension, a second bridge was erected prallel to the original construction.</p>
<p>The Frankenwald rest area near exit Rudolphstein is one of two rest areas in Germany with a restaurant bridging the road.</p>
<p>In Lanzendorf near Bad Berneck, the autobahn passed right through the village on a bridge. The route was realigned during the construction after 1990. Right next to the old route, the autobahn church Lanzendorf was built. Sanctification took place October 6, 1996.</p>
<p>Exit 40b, Bindlacher Berg, was until after reunification no public exit, but used exclusively by the US Forces stationed on the Bindlacher Berg.</p>
<p>Until the upgrade to six lanes, from Bayreuth-Nord to Bindlach existed the last autobahn alley of trees in Germany. It has been replanted since.</p>
<p>Near Trockau, the former steep and curvy section also was realigned after 1990.</p>
<p>Exit 45, Weidensees, was called "Veldensteiner Forst" until the 1970s and was probably built on behalf of <a href="/wiki/Hermann_G%C3%B6ring" title="Hermann Göring">Hermann Göring</a>, who had a hunt nearby. Weidensees itself did not merit its own exit.</p>
<p>Interchange 51, Nuremberg, was originally a cloverleaf. It had been improved by adding a long, sweeping bridge from the northern lane of the A9 coming from Munich towards the A3 in the direction of Würzburg and Frankfurt, since the traffic in that direction was much heavier than the traffic heading north. The old, small curve in the former south-east quadrant still shows what is probably the original 1930's cobblestone.</p>
<p>Despite the upgrade to six lanes total, some original bridges have been retained, but carry just one direction, like the bridge crossing the <a href="/wiki/Saale" title="Saale">Saale</a> at the old border crossing between Bavaria and Thuringia.</p>
<p>From 2000 until construction began to upgrade to four lanes for each direction, the section between Neufahrn and Munich-Nord was among the first in Germany where it was allowed in appropriately heavy (and correspondingly slow) traffic to legally use the emergency lane as an additional traffic lane. Since further adding of two more lanes north of Neufahrn towards Holledau is not in sight, a similar regulation is considered for this section.</p>
<p>Near exit 73, München-Fröttmaning, there's a Berlin Bear statue placed on the median (<span class="plainlinks nourlexpansion"><a class="external text" href="//tools.wmflabs.org/geohack/geohack.php?pagename=Bundesautobahn_9&amp;params=48_12_14_N_11_37_14_E_region:DE-BY_type:landmark"><span class="geo-default"><span class="geo-dms" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location"><span class="latitude">48°12′14″N</span> <span class="longitude">11°37′14″E</span></span></span><span class="geo-multi-punct">﻿ / ﻿</span><span class="geo-nondefault"><span class="geo-dec" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location">48.20389°N 11.62056°E</span><span style="display:none">﻿ / <span class="geo">48.20389; 11.62056</span></span></span></a></span>). Its equivalent is placed on the median of the A&#160;115 (as the extension of the A&#160;9 into Berlin) near former rest area Dreilinden, north of the former <a href="/wiki/Berlin_border_crossings" title="Berlin border crossings">checkpoint Bravo</a> <span class="plainlinks nourlexpansion"><a class="external text" href="//tools.wmflabs.org/geohack/geohack.php?pagename=Bundesautobahn_9&amp;params=52_25_05_N_13_11_47_E_region:DE-BE_type:landmark"><span class="geo-default"><span class="geo-dms" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location"><span class="latitude">52°25′05″N</span> <span class="longitude">13°11′47″E</span></span></span><span class="geo-multi-punct">﻿ / ﻿</span><span class="geo-nondefault"><span class="geo-dec" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location">52.41806°N 13.19639°E</span><span style="display:none">﻿ / <span class="geo">52.41806; 13.19639</span></span></span></a></span>.</p>
<p>At the entrance to the rest area "Köschinger Forst", direction Berlin, there's a milestone with the inscription Berlin - 500&#160;km.</p>
