<p><b>Gustav II Adolf</b> (9 December 1594 – 6 November 1632, <a href="/wiki/Old_Style_and_New_Style_dates" title="Old Style and New Style dates">O.S.</a>), widely known in English by his <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latinised</a> name <b>Gustavus Adolphus</b> or as <b>Gustav II Adolph</b>,<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> was the <a href="/wiki/King_of_Sweden" class="mw-redirect" title="King of Sweden">King of Sweden</a> from 1611 to 1632 and is credited as the founder of <a href="/wiki/Swedish_Empire" title="Swedish Empire">Sweden as a Great Power</a> (<a href="/wiki/Swedish_language" title="Swedish language">Swedish</a>: <span lang="sv"><i>Stormaktstiden</i></span>). He led <a href="/wiki/Sweden" title="Sweden">Sweden</a> to military supremacy during the <a href="/wiki/Thirty_Years%27_War" title="Thirty Years' War">Thirty Years' War</a>, helping to determine the political as well as the religious balance of power in Europe. He was formally and posthumously given the name <b>Gustavus Adolphus the Great</b><sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> (<a href="/wiki/Swedish_language" title="Swedish language">Swedish</a>: <span lang="sv"><i>Gustav Adolf den store</i></span>, <a href="/wiki/Latin_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Latin language">Latin</a>: <span lang="la"><i>Gustavus Adolphus Magnus</i></span>) by the <a href="/wiki/Riksdag_of_the_Estates" title="Riksdag of the Estates">Riksdag of the Estates</a> in 1634.</p>
<p>He is often regarded as one of the greatest military commanders of all time, with innovative use of <a href="/wiki/Combined_arms" title="Combined arms">combined arms</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> His most notable military victory was the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Breitenfeld_(1631)" title="Battle of Breitenfeld (1631)">Battle of Breitenfeld (1631)</a>. With a superb military machine, good weapons, excellent training, and effective field artillery, backed by an efficient government that could provide necessary funds, Gustavus Adolphus was poised to make himself a major European leader.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> He was killed a year later, however, at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_L%C3%BCtzen_(1632)" title="Battle of Lützen (1632)">Battle of Lützen (1632)</a>. He was ably assisted in his efforts by <a href="/wiki/Axel_Oxenstierna" title="Axel Oxenstierna">Count Axel Oxenstierna</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Lord_High_Chancellor_of_Sweden" title="Lord High Chancellor of Sweden">Lord High Chancellor of Sweden</a>, who also acted as <a href="/wiki/Regent" title="Regent">regent</a> after his death.</p>
<p>In an era characterized by almost endless warfare, Gustavus Adolphus king from 1611 (at age 16) until his death, inherited three simultaneous and ongoing wars of his father's (<a href="/wiki/Charles_IX_of_Sweden" title="Charles IX of Sweden">Charles IX</a>). Two of these were border wars with Russia and Denmark, and a more personal war (for his father, at least) with Gustavus first cousin, king <a href="/wiki/Sigismund" title="Sigismund">Sigismund</a> of Poland<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>. Of these three wars, was the the Danish war the most acute one, as of 1611. <sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>During his reign, Sweden rose from the status of a <a href="/wiki/Baltic_Sea" title="Baltic Sea">Baltic Sea</a> power to one of the <a href="/wiki/Great_power" title="Great power">great powers</a> of Europe and a model of <a href="/wiki/Early_modern_era" class="mw-redirect" title="Early modern era">early modern era</a> government.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Some have called him the "father of modern warfare",<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> or the first great modern general. Under his tutelage, Sweden and the <a href="/wiki/Protestantism" title="Protestantism">Protestant</a> cause developed a number of excellent commanders, such as <a href="/wiki/Lennart_Torstensson" title="Lennart Torstensson">Lennart Torstensson</a>, who would go on to defeat Sweden's enemies and expand the boundaries and the power of the empire long after Gustavus Adolphus' death in battle.</p>
<p>Called "The Golden King" and "The Lion of the North", he made Sweden one of the great powers of Europe, in part by reforming the administrative structure. For example, he began parish registration of the population, so that the central government could more efficiently tax and conscript the people.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Historian Christer Jorgensen argues that his achievement in the field of economic reform, trade, modernization, and the creation of the modern bureaucratic autocracy was as great as his exploits on the battlefields. His domestic reforms, which transformed a backward, almost medieval economy and society, were in fact not only the foundations for his victories in Germany, but also absolutely crucial for the creation and survival of the Swedish Empire.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Life">Life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gustavus_Adolphus_of_Sweden&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Gustavus Adolphus was born in <a href="/wiki/Stockholm" title="Stockholm">Stockholm</a> as the oldest son of <a href="/wiki/Charles_IX_of_Sweden" title="Charles IX of Sweden">Duke Charles</a> of the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Vasa" title="House of Vasa">Vasa dynasty</a> and his second wife, <a href="/wiki/Christina_of_Holstein-Gottorp" title="Christina of Holstein-Gottorp">Christina of Holstein-Gottorp</a>. At the time, the King of Sweden was Gustavus Adolphus' cousin <a href="/wiki/Sigismund_III_Vasa" title="Sigismund III Vasa">Sigismund</a>. The staunch Protestant Duke Charles <a href="/wiki/War_against_Sigismund" title="War against Sigismund">forced the Catholic Sigismund</a> to let go of the throne of Sweden in 1599, a part of the preliminary religious strife before the <a href="/wiki/Thirty_Years%27_War" title="Thirty Years' War">Thirty Years' War</a>, and reigned as regent before taking the throne as <a href="/wiki/Charles_IX_of_Sweden" title="Charles IX of Sweden">Charles IX of Sweden</a> in 1604. Crown Prince Gustav Adolph had Gagnef-Floda in <a href="/wiki/Dalarna" title="Dalarna">Dalecarlia</a> as a duchy from 1610. Upon his father's death in October 1611, a sixteen-year-old Gustavus inherited the throne (declared of age and able to reign himself at seventeen as of 16 December<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup>), as well as an ongoing succession of occasionally belligerent dynastic disputes with his Polish cousin. Sigismund III wanted to regain the throne of Sweden and tried to force Gustavus Adolphus to renounce the title.</p>
<p>In a round of this dynastic dispute, Gustavus invaded Livonia when he was 31, beginning the <a href="/wiki/Polish%E2%80%93Swedish_War_(1626%E2%80%9329)" title="Polish–Swedish War (1626–29)">Polish–Swedish War (1626–29)</a>. He intervened on behalf of the Lutherans in Germany, who opened the gates to their cities to him. His reign became famous from his actions a few years later when in June 1630 he landed in Germany, marking the <a href="/wiki/Swedish_Intervention_in_the_Thirty_Years%27_War" class="mw-redirect" title="Swedish Intervention in the Thirty Years' War">Swedish Intervention in the Thirty Years' War</a>. Gustavus intervened on the anti-Imperial side, which at the time was losing to the Holy Roman Empire and its Catholic allies; the Swedish forces would quickly reverse that situation.</p>
<p>Gustavus was married to <a href="/wiki/Maria_Eleonora_of_Brandenburg" title="Maria Eleonora of Brandenburg">Maria Eleonora of Brandenburg</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[a]</a></sup> the daughter of <a href="/wiki/John_Sigismund,_Elector_of_Brandenburg" title="John Sigismund, Elector of Brandenburg">John Sigismund, Elector of Brandenburg</a>, and chose the <a href="/wiki/Prussia" title="Prussia">Prussian</a> city of <a href="/wiki/Elbl%C4%85g" title="Elbląg">Elbing</a> as the base for his operations in <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a>. He died in the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_L%C3%BCtzen_(1632)" title="Battle of Lützen (1632)">Battle of Lützen</a> in 1632. His early death was a great loss to the Lutheran side. This resulted in large parts of Germany and other countries, which had been conquered for Lutheranism, to be reconquered for Catholicism (via the <a href="/wiki/Counter-Reformation" title="Counter-Reformation">Counter-Reformation</a>). His involvement in the Thirty Years' War gave rise to the saying that he was the incarnation of <b>"the Lion of the North"</b>, or as it is called in German <b>"Der Löwe aus Mitternacht"</b> (<i>Literally: "The Lion of Midnight"</i>).</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Military_innovator">Military innovator</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gustavus_Adolphus_of_Sweden&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Military innovator">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Military_of_the_Swedish_Empire" title="Military of the Swedish Empire">Military of the Swedish Empire</a></div>
<p>Historian Ronald S. Love finds that in 1560–1660 there were "a few innovators, notably Maurice of Nassau and Gustavus Adolphus of Sweden, whom many scholars credit with revolutionary developments in warfare and with having laid the foundations of military practice for the next two centuries."<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[13]</a></sup> Scholars all agree that Gustavus Adolphus was an extremely able military commander.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[14]</a></sup> His innovative tactical integration of infantry, cavalry, logistics and particularly his use of artillery, earned him the title of the "Father of Modern Warfare". Future commanders who studied and admired Gustav II Adolf include <a href="/wiki/Napoleon_I_of_France" class="mw-redirect" title="Napoleon I of France">Napoleon I of France</a> and <a href="/wiki/Carl_von_Clausewitz" title="Carl von Clausewitz">Carl von Clausewitz</a>. His advancements in military science made Sweden the dominant <a href="/wiki/Baltic_region" title="Baltic region">Baltic</a> power for the next one hundred years (<i>see <a href="/wiki/Swedish_Empire" title="Swedish Empire">Swedish Empire</a></i>). He is also the only Swedish monarch to be styled "<a href="/wiki/List_of_people_known_as_%22the_Great%22" title="List of people known as &quot;the Great&quot;">the Great</a>". This decision was made by the Swedish Estates of the Realm, when they convened in 1633. Thus, by their decision he is officially, to this day, to be called Gustaf Adolf the Great (<i>Gustavus Adolphus Magnus</i>).</p>

<p>Gustavus Adolphus was the main figure responsible for the success of Swedish arms during the Thirty Years' War and led his nation to great prestige. As a <a href="/wiki/General" class="mw-redirect" title="General">general</a>, Gustavus Adolphus is famous for employing mobile artillery on the battlefield, as well as very aggressive tactics, where attack was stressed over defense, and mobility and cavalry initiative were emphasized.</p>
<p>Among other innovations, he installed an early form of <a href="/wiki/Combined_arms" title="Combined arms">combined arms</a> in his formations, where the cavalry could attack from the safety of an infantry line reinforced by cannon, and retire again within to regroup after their foray. Inspired by the reform of <a href="/wiki/Maurice_of_Nassau" class="mw-redirect" title="Maurice of Nassau">Maurice of Nassau</a> he adopted much shallower infantry formations than were common in the <a href="/wiki/Pike_and_shot" title="Pike and shot">pike and shot</a> armies of the era, with formations typically fighting in 5 or 6 ranks, occasionally supported at some distance by another such formation—the gaps being the provinces of the artillery and cavalry as noted above. His artillery were themselves different—in addition to the usual complements of heavy cannon he introduced light mobile guns for the first time into the Renaissance battlefield. These were grouped in batteries supporting his more linearly deployed formations, replacing the cumbersome and unmaneuverable traditional deep squares (such as the Spanish <a href="/wiki/Tercio" title="Tercio">Tercios</a> that were up to 50 ranks deep) used in other pike and shot armies of the day. In consequence, his forces could redeploy and reconfigure very rapidly, confounding his enemies.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[15]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[16]</a></sup> He created the modern Swedish navy, which successfully transported troops and supplies to the Continental battlefront.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>von Clausewitz and Napoleon Bonaparte considered him one of the greatest generals of all time, an evaluation agreed with by <a href="/wiki/George_S._Patton" title="George S. Patton">George S. Patton</a> and others. He was also renowned for his constancy of purpose and the equality of his troops—no one part of his armies was considered better or received preferred treatment, as was common in other armies where the cavalry were the elite, followed by the artillery, and both disdained the lowly infantry. In Gustavus' army the units were extensively cross trained. Both cavalry and infantry could service the artillery, as his heavy cavalry did when turning captured artillery on the opposing Catholic Tercios at <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Breitenfeld_(1631)" title="Battle of Breitenfeld (1631)">First Breitenfeld</a>. Pikemen could shoot—if not as accurately as those designated musketeers—so a valuable firearm could be kept in the firing line. His infantrymen and gunners were taught to ride, if needed. Napoleon thought highly of the achievement, and copied the tactics. However, recent historians have challenged velocity reputation. <a href="/wiki/B._H._Liddell_Hart" title="B. H. Liddell Hart">B. H. Liddell Hart</a> says it is an exaggeration to credit him with a uniquely disciplined conscript army, or call his the first military state to fight a protracted war on the continent. He argues that he improved existing techniques and used them brilliantly. Richard Brzezinski says his legendary status was based on inaccurate myths created by later historians. Many of his innovations were developed by his senior staff.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[18]</a></sup></p>

<p>While Gustavus has been widely credited for re-emphasizing the shock role of European cavalry, his innovations were hardly new, Huguenot cavalry under <a href="/wiki/Henry_IV_of_France" title="Henry IV of France">Henry IV</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gaspard_II_de_Coligny" title="Gaspard II de Coligny">Gaspard II de Coligny</a> having fought in exactly the same fashion during the French <a href="/wiki/Wars_of_Religion" class="mw-redirect" title="Wars of Religion">Wars of Religion</a>. As a matter of fact his opponent <a href="/wiki/Johann_Tserclaes,_Count_of_Tilly" title="Johann Tserclaes, Count of Tilly">Johann Tserclaes, Count of Tilly</a> also favored the same ferocious charges the Swedish cavalry would become famous for. Neither was the Swedish practice of integrating shot and horse, the so-called "commanded shot", a new one, with the Huguenot horsemen at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Coutras" title="Battle of Coutras">battle of Coutras</a> having the same supporting shooters. What made the Swedish army unique in this regard was the fact that the use of "commanded shot" became the standard tactical doctrine of its horse, and this in turn was adopted by other armies of the period, including its Imperial opponents and that of the <a href="/wiki/English_Civil_War" title="English Civil War">English Civil War</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>Adolphus better deserved the credit of introducing a standard caliber light muskets to his infantry forces, replacing the previous mix of <a href="/wiki/Arquebus" title="Arquebus">arquebus</a> and heavy <a href="/wiki/Musket" title="Musket">musket</a> common in Imperial <a href="/wiki/Tercios" class="mw-redirect" title="Tercios">tercios</a>. The shallower infantry formation of the Swedish brigade, much more conducive to massed firepower, was inspired by the work of Maurice. However Adolphus perfected the system and introduced the use of <a href="/wiki/Salvo" title="Salvo">salvo</a> fire, where two or three ranks of musketeers fired simultaneously, usually at <a href="/wiki/Point_blank_range" class="mw-redirect" title="Point blank range">point blank range</a>, rather than one rank at a time counter marching as was common in that era. Delivered at point-blank range, and immediately followed up by a charge with swords, and pikes, a salvo became the infantry's most feared tactic because it was much more effective at breaking the enemy's morale and repulsing cavalry charges than the earlier method.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>Perhaps Adolphus’ greatest contribution, however, was his work in <a href="/wiki/Field_artillery" title="Field artillery">field artillery</a>. Equipping each of his brigades with up to 12 light <a href="/wiki/Infantry_support_gun" title="Infantry support gun">regimental guns</a>, he greatly increased the organic firepower of his infantry and for the first time allowed the artillery arm to play a role in the offensive instead of being a static spectator in a battle of maneuvers.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[21]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Political_reforms">Political reforms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gustavus_Adolphus_of_Sweden&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Political reforms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

</div>
<p>Gustav II Adolf's politics in the conquered territory of Estonia also show progressive tendencies. In 1631 he forced the nobility to grant the peasants greater autonomy. He also encouraged education, opening a school in <a href="/wiki/Tallinn" title="Tallinn">Tallinn</a> in 1631, today known as <a href="/wiki/Gustav_Adolf_Grammar_School" title="Gustav Adolf Grammar School">Gustav Adolf Grammar School</a> (<a href="/wiki/Estonian_language" title="Estonian language">Estonian</a>: <span lang="et"><i>Gustav Adolfi Gümnaasium</i></span>).<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[22]</a></sup> On 30 June 1632, Gustav II Adolf signed the Foundation Decree of Academia Dorpatensis in Estonia, today known as the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Tartu" title="University of Tartu">University of Tartu</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[23]</a></sup> With policies that supported the common people, the period of Swedish rule over Estonia initiated by Gustav II Adolf and continued by his successors is popularly known by Estonians as the "good old Swedish times" (Estonian: vana hea Rootsi aeg).<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[24]</a></sup></p>
<p>On 27 August 1617, he spoke before his <a href="/wiki/Coronation" title="Coronation">coronation</a>, and his words included these:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>I had carefully learned to understand, about that experience which I could have upon things of rule, how fortune is failing or great, subject to such rule in common, so that otherwise I would have had scant reason to desire such a rule, had I not found myself obliged to it through God’s bidding and nature. Now it was of my acquaintance, that inasmuch as God had let me be born a prince, such as I then am born, then my good and my destruction were knotted into one with the common good; for every reason then, it was now my promise that I should take great pains about their well-being and good governance and management, and thereabout bear close concern.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[25]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Military_commander">Military commander</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gustavus_Adolphus_of_Sweden&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Military commander">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>Gustavus Adolphus inherited three wars from his father when he ascended the throne: against Denmark, which had attacked Sweden earlier in 1611; against Russia, due to Sweden having tried to take advantage of the Russian <a href="/wiki/Time_of_Troubles" title="Time of Troubles">Time of Troubles</a>; and against Poland, due to King Charles's having deposed King Sigismund III, his nephew, as King of Sweden.</p>
<p>The war against Denmark (<a href="/wiki/Kalmar_War" title="Kalmar War">Kalmar War</a>) was concluded in 1613 with a peace that did not cost Sweden any territory, but it was forced to pay a heavy indemnity to Denmark (<a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Kn%C3%A4red" title="Treaty of Knäred">Treaty of Knäred</a>). During this war, Gustavus Adolphus let his soldiers plunder towns and villages, and as he met little resistance from Danish forces in <a href="/wiki/Scania" title="Scania">Scania</a>, they pillaged and devastated twenty-four Scanian parishes. His memory in <a href="/wiki/Scania" title="Scania">Scania</a> has been negative because of that fear.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERoberts199233_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERoberts199233-27">[26]</a></sup> In the winter of 1612, during a period of two weeks, did he burn down, or otherwise destroyed 24 Scanian parishes and most of their population without meeting any enemy troops. The largest destroyed settlement was the Town <a href="/wiki/V%C3%A4" title="Vä">Væ</a>, which two years later was replaced by Danish King <a href="/wiki/Christian_IV_of_Denmark" title="Christian IV of Denmark">Christian IV</a> as the nearby <a href="/wiki/Kristianstad" title="Kristianstad">Christiansted</a> (after the Swedification process, spelled Kristianstad), the last Scanian town to be founded by a Danish king.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[27]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[28]</a></sup></p>
<p>The war against Russia (<a href="/wiki/Ingrian_War" title="Ingrian War">Ingrian War</a>) ended in 1617 with the <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Stolbovo" title="Treaty of Stolbovo">Treaty of Stolbovo</a>, which excluded Russia from the Baltic Sea. The final inherited war, the <a href="/wiki/Polish%E2%80%93Swedish_War_(1600%E2%80%931629)" class="mw-redirect" title="Polish–Swedish War (1600–1629)">war against Poland</a>, ended in 1629 with the <a href="/wiki/Truce_of_Altmark" title="Truce of Altmark">Truce of Altmark</a>, which transferred the large province <a href="/wiki/Swedish_Livonia" title="Swedish Livonia">Livonia</a> to Sweden and freed the Swedish forces for the subsequent intervention in the <a href="/wiki/Thirty_Years%27_War" title="Thirty Years' War">Thirty Years' War</a> in Germany, where Swedish forces had <a href="/wiki/Siege_of_Stralsund_(1628)" title="Siege of Stralsund (1628)">already established a bridgehead in 1628.</a></p>
<p>The weak electorate of <a href="/wiki/Margraviate_of_Brandenburg" title="Margraviate of Brandenburg">Brandenburg</a> was especially torn apart by a quarrel between the Protestant and Catholic parties. The Brandenburg minister and diplomat <a href="/w/index.php?title=Baron_Samuel_von_Winterfeld&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Baron Samuel von Winterfeld (page does not exist)">Baron Samuel von Winterfeld</a> influenced Gustavus Adolphus to support and protect the Protestant side in Germany. When Gustavus Adolphus <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Stettin_(1630)" title="Treaty of Stettin (1630)">began his push</a> into northern Germany in June–July 1630, he had just 4,000 troops. He was soon able to consolidate the <a href="/wiki/Protestant" class="mw-redirect" title="Protestant">Protestant</a> position in the north, however, using reinforcements from Sweden and money supplied by France at the <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_B%C3%A4rwalde" title="Treaty of Bärwalde">Treaty of Bärwalde</a>. After Swedish plundering in Brandenburg (1631) endangered the system of retrieving <a href="/wiki/Bellum_se_ipsum_alet" title="Bellum se ipsum alet">war contributions from occupied territories</a>, "marauding and plundering" by Swedish soldiers was prohibited.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[29]</a></sup> Meanwhile, a <a href="/wiki/Catholic_League_(German)" title="Catholic League (German)">Catholic army</a> under <a href="/wiki/Johann_Tserclaes,_Count_of_Tilly" title="Johann Tserclaes, Count of Tilly">Johann Tserclaes, Count of Tilly</a> was laying waste to <a href="/wiki/Saxony" title="Saxony">Saxony</a>. Gustavus Adolphus met Tilly's army and crushed it at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Breitenfeld_(1631)" title="Battle of Breitenfeld (1631)">First Battle of Breitenfeld</a> in September 1631. He then marched clear across Germany, establishing his winter quarters near the <a href="/wiki/Rhine" title="Rhine">Rhine</a>, making plans for the invasion of the rest of the <a href="/wiki/Holy_Roman_Empire" title="Holy Roman Empire">Holy Roman Empire</a>.</p>
<p>In March 1632, Gustavus Adolphus invaded <a href="/wiki/Bavaria" title="Bavaria">Bavaria</a>, a staunch ally of the Emperor. He forced the withdrawal of his Catholic opponents at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Rain" title="Battle of Rain">Battle of Rain</a>, marking the high point of the campaign. In the summer of that year, he sought a political solution that would preserve the existing structure of states in Germany, while guaranteeing the security of its Protestants. But achieving these objectives depended on his continued success on the battlefield.</p>
<p>Gustavus is reported to have entered battle without wearing any armor, proclaiming, "The Lord God is my armor!" It is more likely that he simply wore a leather <a href="/wiki/Cuirass" title="Cuirass">cuirass</a> rather than going into battle wearing no battle protection whatsoever. In 1627, near Dirschau in Prussia, a Polish soldier shot him in the muscles above his shoulders. He survived, but the doctors could not remove the bullet, so from that point on, he could not wear iron armor; two fingers of his right hand were paralyzed.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[30]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources"><span title="This citation requires a reference to the specific page or range of pages in which the material appears. (May 2017)">page&#160;needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Death">Death</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gustavus_Adolphus_of_Sweden&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Death">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p><a href="/wiki/Battle_of_L%C3%BCtzen_(1632)" title="Battle of Lützen (1632)">The Battle of Lützen</a> (November 6, 1632) was one of the most decisive battles of the Thirty Years' War. It was a Protestant victory, but the Protestant alliance lost one of its most important leaders, which caused the Protestant campaign to lose direction. Gustavus Adolphus was killed when, at a crucial point in the battle, he became separated from his troops while leading a cavalry charge on his wing.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">[31]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources"><span title="This citation requires a reference to the specific page or range of pages in which the material appears. (May 2017)">page&#160;needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Towards 1:00 pm, in the thick mix of gun smoke and fog covering the field, the king was separated from his fellow riders and suffered multiple shots. A bullet crushed his left arm below the elbow. Almost simultaneously his horse suffered a shot to the neck that made it hard to control. In the mix of fog and smoke from the burning town of Lützen the king rode astray behind enemy lines. There he sustained yet another shot in the back, was stabbed and fell from his horse. Lying on the ground, he received a final, fatal shot to the temple. His fate remained unknown for some time. However, when the gunnery paused and the smoke cleared, his horse was spotted between the two lines, Gustavus himself not on it and nowhere to be seen. His disappearance stopped the initiative of the hitherto successful Swedish right wing, while a search was conducted. His partly stripped body was found an hour or two later, and was secretly evacuated from the field in a Swedish artillery wagon.</p>
<p>After his death, Gustavus's wife initially kept his body, and later his heart, in the castle of <a href="/wiki/Nyk%C3%B6ping" title="Nyköping">Nyköping</a> for over a year. His remains (including his heart) now rest in <a href="/wiki/Riddarholm_Church" title="Riddarholm Church">Riddarholm Church</a> in Stockholm.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Aftermath">Aftermath</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gustavus_Adolphus_of_Sweden&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Aftermath">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In February 1633, following the death of the king, the Swedish <a href="/wiki/Riksdag_of_the_Estates" title="Riksdag of the Estates">Riksdag of the Estates</a> decided that his name would be styled Gustav Adolf the Great (or <i>Gustaf Adolf den Store</i> in <a href="/wiki/Swedish_language" title="Swedish language">Swedish</a>, Latinized as <i>Gustavus Adolphus Magnus</i>). No such honor has been bestowed on any other Swedish monarch before or since.</p>
<p>The crown of Sweden was inherited in the Vasa family, and from Charles IX’s time excluded those Vasa princes who had been traitors or descended from deposed monarchs. Gustavus Adolphus’ younger brother had died ten years before, and therefore there was only the King’s daughter left as a female heir. Maria Eleonora and the king’s ministers took over the government on behalf of Gustavus Adolphus’ underage daughter <a href="/wiki/Christina_I_of_Sweden" class="mw-redirect" title="Christina I of Sweden">Christina</a> upon her father’s death. He left one other known child, his illegitimate son <a href="/wiki/Gustav_Gustavsson_af_Vasaborg" class="mw-redirect" title="Gustav Gustavsson af Vasaborg">Gustav, Count of Vasaborg</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Memorials">Memorials</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gustavus_Adolphus_of_Sweden&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Memorials">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Gustavus Adolphus is commemorated today with <a href="/wiki/City_square" class="mw-redirect" title="City square">city squares</a> in major Swedish cities like <a href="/wiki/Gustav_Adolfs_torg,_Stockholm" title="Gustav Adolfs torg, Stockholm">Stockholm</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gustaf_Adolfs_torg,_G%C3%B6teborg" class="mw-redirect" title="Gustaf Adolfs torg, Göteborg">Gothenburg</a> and <a href="/wiki/Helsingborg" title="Helsingborg">Helsingborg</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Gustav-Adolf-Werk" title="Gustav-Adolf-Werk">Gustav-Adolf-Werk</a> (GAW) of the <a href="/wiki/Evangelical_Church_in_Germany" title="Evangelical Church in Germany">Evangelical Church in Germany</a>, founded on the bicentennial celebration of the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_L%C3%BCtzen_(1632)" title="Battle of Lützen (1632)">Battle of Lützen</a>, has as its object the aid of feeble sister churches and commemorates the king's legacy. Swedish royalty visited the GAW headquarters in <a href="/wiki/Leipzig" title="Leipzig">Leipzig</a> on the festivities of Gustavus Adolphus' 400th birthday, in 1994.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">[32]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Gustavus_Adolphus_College" title="Gustavus Adolphus College">Gustavus Adolphus College</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Lutheran" class="mw-redirect" title="Lutheran">Lutheran</a> college in <a href="/wiki/St._Peter,_Minnesota" title="St. Peter, Minnesota">St. Peter, Minnesota</a>, is also named for the Swedish King.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Evaluations">Evaluations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gustavus_Adolphus_of_Sweden&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Evaluations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>The <i><a href="/wiki/Columbia_Encyclopedia" title="Columbia Encyclopedia">Columbia Encyclopedia</a></i> sums up his record:</p>
<dl>
<dd>In military organization and strategy, Gustavus was ahead of his time. While most powers relied on mercenary troops, he organized a national standing army that distinguished itself by its discipline and relatively high moral standards. Deeply religious, the king desired his soldiers to behave like a truly Christian army; his stern measures against the common practices of looting, raping, and torture were effective until his death. His successes were due to this discipline, his use of small, mobile units, the superiority of his firearms, and his personal charisma. Although he was deeply interested in the internal progress of his kingdom, much of the credit for the development of Swedish industry and the fiscal and administrative reforms of his reign belongs to Oxenstierna.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">[33]</a></sup></dd>
</dl>
<p>The German Socialist <a href="/wiki/Franz_Mehring" title="Franz Mehring">Franz Mehring</a> wrote a biography of Gustavus Adolphus with a <a href="/wiki/Marxist" class="mw-redirect" title="Marxist">Marxist</a> perspective on the actions of the Swedish king during the Thirty Years' War. In it, he makes a case that the war was fought over economics and trade rather than religion. The Swedes discovered huge deposits of copper, which were used to build brass cannon. The cottage-industrial growth stimulated an armaments industry.</p>

<p>In his book "Ofredsår" ("Years of Warfare"), the Swedish historian and author <a href="/wiki/Peter_Englund" title="Peter Englund">Peter Englund</a> argues that there was probably no single all-important reason for the king's decision to go to war. Instead, it was likely a combination of religious, security, as well as economic considerations.</p>
<p>This view is supported by German historian <a href="/w/index.php?title=Johannes_Burkhardt&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Johannes Burkhardt (page does not exist)">Johannes Burkhardt</a>, who writes that Gustavus entered the 30 Years War exactly 100 years after the publication of the <a href="/wiki/Confessio_Augustana" class="mw-redirect" title="Confessio Augustana">Confessio Augustana</a>, the core confession of faith of the <a href="/wiki/Lutheran_Church" class="mw-redirect" title="Lutheran Church">Lutheran Church</a>, and let himself be praised as its saviour. Yet Gustavus' own "manifesto of war" does not mention any religious motivations at all but speaks of political and economical reasons. Sweden would have to maintain its integrity in the face of several provocations and aggressions by the Habsburgian Empire. The manifesto was written by scholar Johann Adler Salvius in a style common of the time that promotes a "just war". Burkhardt argues that traditional Swedish historiography constructed a defensive interest in security out of that by taking the manifesto's text for granted. But to defend Stockholm, the occupation of the German Baltic territories would have been an extreme advance and the imperial Baltic Sea fleet mentioned as a threat in the manifesto had never reached more than a quarter of the size of the Swedish fleet. Moreover, it was never maintained to challenge Sweden but to face the separatist Netherlands. So if ruling the Baltic Sea was a goal of Swedish strategy, the conquests in Germany were not a defensive war but an act of expansion. From Swedish Finland, Gustavus advanced along the Baltic Sea coast and eventually to Augsburg and Munich and he even urged the <a href="/wiki/Old_Swiss_Confederacy" title="Old Swiss Confederacy">Swiss Confederacy</a> to join him. This was no longer about Baltic interests but the imperial capital of Vienna and the alpine passes that were now in close reach of the Swedish army. Burkhardt points out that the <a href="/wiki/Goths" title="Goths">Gothic</a> legacy of the Swedes, coalesced as a political program. The Swedish king was also "<i>Rex Gotorum</i>" (<a href="/wiki/Latin_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Latin language">Latin</a>: <span lang="la"><i>King of the Goths</i></span>), and the list of kings was traced back to the Gothic rulers to construct continuity. Prior to his embarkment to northern Germany, Gustavus urged the <a href="/wiki/Swedish_nobility" title="Swedish nobility">Swedish nobility</a> to follow the example of conquests set by their Gothic ancestors. Had he lived longer, it would have been likely that Gustavus had reached out for the imperial crown of the Holy Roman Empire.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">[34]</a></sup></p>

<ul>
<li>December 1594. Gustavus is born in the castle of <a href="/wiki/Tre_Kronor_(castle)" title="Tre Kronor (castle)">Tre Kronor</a>, Sweden.</li>
<li>October 1611. Gustavus gains the Swedish throne and three wars (<a href="/wiki/Kalmar_War" title="Kalmar War">Kalmar War</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ingrian_War" title="Ingrian War">Ingrian War</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Polish%E2%80%93Swedish_War_(1600%E2%80%931629)" class="mw-redirect" title="Polish–Swedish War (1600–1629)">Polish War</a>) after his father, <a href="/wiki/Charles_IX_of_Sweden" title="Charles IX of Sweden">Charles IX's</a>, death.</li>
<li>February 1612. The <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Vittsj%C3%B6" title="Battle of Vittsjö">Battle of Vittsjö</a> against Denmark where Gustavus almost drowns.</li>
<li>January 1613. Gustavus negotiates <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Kn%C3%A4red" title="Treaty of Knäred">peace</a> after repulsing the Danish invasion in the Kalmar War with the <a href="/wiki/Status_quo_ante_bellum" title="Status quo ante bellum">status quo ante bellum</a>. However, <a href="/wiki/%C3%84lvsborg_Ransom_(1613)" title="Älvsborg Ransom (1613)">Älvsborg Ransom (1613)</a> had to be paid for <a href="/wiki/%C3%84lvsborg_fortress" title="Älvsborg fortress">Älvsborg fortress</a>.</li>
<li>February 1617. After the pressures of Gustavus <a href="/w/index.php?title=Siege_of_Pskov_(1615)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Siege of Pskov (1615) (page does not exist)">siege of Pskov</a>, he excludes Russia from the Baltic Sea in the <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Stolbovo" title="Treaty of Stolbovo">Ingrian War</a>, who cedes <a href="/wiki/Ingria" title="Ingria">Ingria</a> to Sweden.</li>
<li>November 1620. Gustav Adolph marries Maria Eleanora.</li>
<li>January 1626. The <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Wallhof" title="Battle of Wallhof">battle of Wallhof</a> where Gustavus successfully uses effective cooperation between infantry and cavalry.</li>
<li>July 1626. Gustavus Adolphus and his army disembark at <a href="/wiki/Pillau" class="mw-redirect" title="Pillau">Pillau</a>, <a href="/wiki/Prussia" title="Prussia">Prussia</a>, during the <a href="/wiki/Polish%E2%80%93Swedish_War_(1626%E2%80%9329)" title="Polish–Swedish War (1626–29)">Polish–Swedish War (1626–29)</a>.</li>
<li>September 1626. Gustavus defeats a Polish force of <a href="/wiki/Sigismund_III_Vasa" title="Sigismund III Vasa">Sigismund III Vasa</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Gniew" title="Battle of Gniew">battle of Gniew</a>.</li>
<li>December 1626. Daughter and successor Christina is born.</li>
<li>May 1627. Gustavus is shot and seriously wounded (close to dying) in the <a href="/wiki/Assault_on_Danzig" title="Assault on Danzig">assault on Danzig</a>.</li>
<li>August 1627. The King is seriously wounded in the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Dirschau" title="Battle of Dirschau">battle of Dirschau</a> (<a href="/wiki/Tczew" title="Tczew">Tczew</a>), after being shot twice.</li>
<li>June 1629. His troops meet up with forces of Polish crown field Hetman <a href="/wiki/Stanis%C5%82aw_Koniecpolski" title="Stanisław Koniecpolski">Stanisław Koniecpolski</a> and imperial troops under <a href="/wiki/Hans_Georg_von_Arnim-Boitzenburg" title="Hans Georg von Arnim-Boitzenburg">Hans Georg von Arnim-Boitzenburg</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Trzciana" title="Battle of Trzciana">battle of Trzciana</a>, and there Gustavus is almost killed or captured twice.</li>
<li>September 1629. <a href="/wiki/Truce_of_Altmark" title="Truce of Altmark">Truce of Altmark</a> — <a href="/wiki/Livonia" title="Livonia">Livonia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Estonia" title="Estonia">Estonia</a> is ceded to Sweden as a result of Gustavus <a href="/wiki/Polish%E2%80%93Swedish_War_(1600%E2%80%9329)" title="Polish–Swedish War (1600–29)">Polish wars</a>.</li>
<li>May 1630 and 6 July Gustav Adolph lands in <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a> to enter the <a href="/wiki/Thirty_Years_War" class="mw-redirect" title="Thirty Years War">Thirty Years War</a>.</li>
<li>April 1631. Gustavus <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Frankfurt_an_der_Oder" title="Battle of Frankfurt an der Oder">besieges and captures</a> the town of <a href="/wiki/Frankfurt_an_der_Oder" class="mw-redirect" title="Frankfurt an der Oder">Frankfurt an der Oder</a> in the war.</li>
<li>July 1631. <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Werben" title="Battle of Werben">Werben</a>, First major field-battle between Swedish and Catholic forces where Gustavus is victorious.</li>
<li>September 1631. At the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Breitenfeld_(1631)" title="Battle of Breitenfeld (1631)">Battle of Breitenfeld</a>, Gustavus Adolphus decisively defeats the Catholic forces led by <a href="/wiki/Johann_Tserclaes,_Count_of_Tilly" title="Johann Tserclaes, Count of Tilly">Tilly</a>, even after the allied Protestant Saxon army had been routed and fled with the baggage train.</li>
<li>April 1632. At the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Lech" class="mw-redirect" title="Battle of Lech">Battle of Lech</a>, Gustavus Adolphus defeats Tilly once more, and in the battle Tilly sustains a fatal wound.</li>
<li>May 1632. Munich yields to the Swedish army.</li>
<li>September 1632. Gustavus Adolphus attacks the stronghold of <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_the_Alte_Veste" title="Battle of the Alte Veste">Alte Veste</a>, which is under the command of <a href="/wiki/Albrecht_von_Wallenstein" title="Albrecht von Wallenstein">Wallenstein</a>, but is repulsed, marking the first defeat in the Thirty Years' War of the previously invincible Swedes.</li>
<li>November 1632. At the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_L%C3%BCtzen_(1632)" title="Battle of Lützen (1632)">Battle of Lützen</a>, Gustavus Adolphus is <a href="/wiki/Killed_in_action" title="Killed in action">killed in action</a>, but the Swedes win the fight thanks to <a href="/wiki/Bernhard_of_Saxe-Weimar" class="mw-redirect" title="Bernhard of Saxe-Weimar">Bernhard of Saxe-Weimar</a>, who assumes command and defeats Wallenstein. The Swedish war effort was kept up by generals <a href="/wiki/Gustav_Horn,_Count_of_Pori" title="Gustav Horn, Count of Pori">Gustav Horn</a>, <a href="/wiki/Johan_Ban%C3%A9r" title="Johan Banér">Johan Banér</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lennart_Torstenson" class="mw-redirect" title="Lennart Torstenson">Lennart Torstenson</a>, <a href="/wiki/Carl_Gustaf_Wrangel" title="Carl Gustaf Wrangel">Carl Gustaf Wrangel</a> and chancellor <a href="/wiki/Axel_Oxenstierna" title="Axel Oxenstierna">Axel Oxenstierna</a> until the <a href="/wiki/Peace_of_Westphalia" title="Peace of Westphalia">Peace of Westphalia</a>.</li>
</ul>
<p>A history of Gustavus Adolphus' wars was written by <a href="/wiki/Johann_Philipp_Abelin" title="Johann Philipp Abelin">Johann Philipp Abelin</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legacy">Legacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gustavus_Adolphus_of_Sweden&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Legacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:102px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Fahne_Gustav-Adolf-Verein.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9e/Fahne_Gustav-Adolf-Verein.jpg/100px-Fahne_Gustav-Adolf-Verein.jpg" width="100" height="250" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9e/Fahne_Gustav-Adolf-Verein.jpg/150px-Fahne_Gustav-Adolf-Verein.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9e/Fahne_Gustav-Adolf-Verein.jpg/200px-Fahne_Gustav-Adolf-Verein.jpg 2x" data-file-width="221" data-file-height="553" /></a>

<p><a href="/wiki/Gustavus_Adolphus_Day" title="Gustavus Adolphus Day">Gustavus Adolphus Day</a> is celebrated in Sweden, Estonia and Finland each year on 6 November, the day the king died at Lützen. One of the traditions on this day is the <a href="/wiki/Gustavus_Adolphus_pastry" title="Gustavus Adolphus pastry">Gustavus Adolphus pastry</a>. In Finland, the day is also called "the Swedish day".</p>
<p>The <b><a href="/wiki/Gustav-Adolf-Werk" title="Gustav-Adolf-Werk">Gustav-Adolf-Werk</a></b> (<b>GAW</b>), a society under the roof of the <a href="/wiki/Evangelical_Church_in_Germany" title="Evangelical Church in Germany">Evangelical Church in Germany</a>, has for its objects the aid of feeble sister churches. Its responsible for the taking care of the <a href="/wiki/Diaspora" title="Diaspora">Diasporawork</a> of the EKD and has separate branches internationally. The organization in Austria is still called the <b>Gustav-Adolf-Verein</b>. The project of forming such a society was first broached in connexion with the bicentennial celebration of the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_L%C3%BCtzen_(1632)" title="Battle of Lützen (1632)">battle of Lützen</a> on November 6, 1832; a proposal to collect funds for a monument to Gustavus Adolphus having been agreed to, it was suggested by Superintendent Grossmann that the best memorial to the great champion of Protestantism would be the formation of a union for propagating his ideas. It quickly gained popularity in German. The lack of political correctness received some criticism however, the organization uses GAW as its brand in the meanwhile. The Swedish royalties have been visiting the GAW headquarters in Leipzig on the 400th birthday of Gustav Adolf 1994.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">[35]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_popular_culture">In popular culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gustavus_Adolphus_of_Sweden&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: In popular culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/August_Strindberg" title="August Strindberg">August Strindberg</a>'s play <i><a href="/wiki/Gustaf_Adolf_(play)" class="mw-redirect" title="Gustaf Adolf (play)">Gustaf Adolf</a></i> from 1900</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Bertolt_Brecht" title="Bertolt Brecht">Bertolt Brecht</a>'s play <i><a href="/wiki/Mother_Courage_and_Her_Children" title="Mother Courage and Her Children">Mother Courage and Her Children</a></i> mentions Gustavus Adolphus several times in the earlier scenes during which the characters are traveling with the Protestant Army. The Cook lampoons the "Hero King" by pointing out that first he sought to liberate Poland from the Germans, then sought to liberate Germany from the Germans, and made a profit on the deal. His irreverence for the king also includes the fact that, unlike Mother Courage and the Chaplain, the Cook is a Dutchman not a Swede.</li>
<li>In the <i><a href="/wiki/163x" class="mw-redirect" title="163x">Ring of Fire</a></i> hypernovel by <a href="/wiki/Eric_Flint" title="Eric Flint">Eric Flint</a> and others, Gustavus Adolphus is a major character, having chosen not to attend the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_L%C3%BCtzen_(1632)" title="Battle of Lützen (1632)">Battle of Lützen</a> in which, historically, he was killed. He helps a community of West Virginians, cosmically transported back into time, bring about a revolution of democracy throughout the <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germanies</a>. They in turn help to grow the <a href="/wiki/Swedish_empire" class="mw-redirect" title="Swedish empire">Swedish empire</a> through their technological knowledge of modern-day warfare and the capabilities of mankind. They introduce many ideas to 17th century Europe such as <a href="/wiki/Radio" title="Radio">radio</a>, <a href="/wiki/Submarines" class="mw-redirect" title="Submarines">submarines</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Airplanes" class="mw-redirect" title="Airplanes">airplanes</a>. Gustavus Adolphus is portrayed as a tough, yet compassionate king with tolerant tendencies toward religion and the rights of the people to establish their own civil liberties.</li>
<li>Swedish <a href="/wiki/Power_metal" title="Power metal">power metal</a> band <a href="/wiki/Sabaton_(band)" title="Sabaton (band)">Sabaton</a> made a song about Gustavus Adolphus, entitled, "Lion from the North." Its parent album, <i><a href="/wiki/Carolus_Rex_(album)" title="Carolus Rex (album)">Carolus Rex</a></i>, is a <a href="/wiki/Concept_album" title="Concept album">concept album</a> based on the rise and fall of the <a href="/wiki/Swedish_Empire" title="Swedish Empire">Swedish Empire</a>.</li>
<li>Gustavus Adolphus is the leader of Sweden in the turn-based strategy game, <a href="/wiki/Civilization_V" title="Civilization V">Civilization V</a>, introduced in the <i><a href="/wiki/Civilization_V:_Gods_and_Kings" class="mw-redirect" title="Civilization V: Gods and Kings">Gods and Kings</a></i> update.</li>
</ul>
