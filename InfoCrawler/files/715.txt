<p><b>Kaspar Hauser</b> (30 April 1812 (?) – 17 December 1833) was a <a href="/wiki/German_people" class="mw-redirect" title="German people">German</a> youth who claimed to have grown up in the total isolation of a darkened cell. Hauser's claims, and his subsequent death by stabbing, sparked much debate and controversy. Theories propounded at the time linked him with the grand ducal <a href="/wiki/House_of_Z%C3%A4hringen" title="House of Zähringen">House of Baden</a>. These have long since been rejected by historians.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kaspar_Hauser&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="First_appearance">First appearance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kaspar_Hauser&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: First appearance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On 26 May 1828, a teenage boy appeared in the streets of <a href="/wiki/Nuremberg" title="Nuremberg">Nuremberg</a>, Germany. He carried a letter with him addressed to the captain of the 4th <a href="/wiki/Squadron_(cavalry)" class="mw-redirect" title="Squadron (cavalry)">squadron</a> of the 6th <a href="/wiki/Cavalry" title="Cavalry">cavalry</a> regiment, Captain von Wessenig. Its heading read: <i>Von der Bäierischen Gränz / daß Orte ist unbenant / 1828</i> ("From the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Bavaria" title="Kingdom of Bavaria">Bavarian</a> border / The place is unnamed / 1828"). The anonymous author said that the boy was given into his custody as an infant on 7 October 1812 and that he instructed him in reading, writing and the Christian religion, but never let him "take a single step out of my house". The letter stated that the boy would now like to be a cavalryman "as his father was" and invited the captain either to take him in or to hang him.</p>
<p>There was another short letter enclosed purporting to be from his mother to his prior caretaker. It stated that his name was Kaspar, that he was born on 30 April 1812 and that his father, a cavalryman of the 6th regiment, was dead. In fact this letter was found to have been written by the same hand as the other one (whose line "he writes my handwriting exactly as I do" led later analysts to assume that Kaspar himself wrote both of them).<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>A shoemaker named Weickmann took the boy to the house of Captain von Wessenig, where he would repeat only the words "I want to be a cavalryman, as my father was" and "Horse! Horse!" Further demands elicited only tears or the obstinate proclamation of "Don't know." He was taken to a police station, where he would write a name: Kaspar Hauser. He showed that he was familiar with money, could say some prayers and read a little, but he answered few questions and his vocabulary appeared to be rather limited.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Because he provided no account of himself, he was imprisoned as a <a href="/wiki/Vagrancy_(people)" title="Vagrancy (people)">vagabond</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>He spent the following two months in Luginsland Tower in <a href="/wiki/Nuremberg_Castle" title="Nuremberg Castle">Nuremberg Castle</a> in the care of a jailer named Andreas Hiltel. Despite what many later accounts would say, he was in good physical condition and could walk well; for example, he climbed over 90 steps to his room. He was of a "healthy facial complexion"<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> and approximately 16 years old, but appeared to be intellectually impaired. Mayor Binder, however, claimed that the boy had an excellent memory and was learning quickly. Various curious people visited him to his apparent delight. He refused all food except bread and water.</p>

<h4><span id="Hauser's_story_about_his_life_in_a_dungeon"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Hauser.27s_story_about_his_life_in_a_dungeon">Hauser's story about his life in a dungeon</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kaspar_Hauser&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Hauser's story about his life in a dungeon">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>At first it was assumed that he was raised <a href="/wiki/Feral_child" title="Feral child">half-wild</a> in forests, but during many conversations with Mayor Binder, Hauser told a different version of his past life, which he later also wrote down in more detail.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> According to this story, for as long as he could remember he spent his life totally alone in a darkened cell about two metres long, one metre wide and one and a half high with only a straw bed to sleep on and two horses and a dog carved out of wood for toys.</p>
<p>He claimed that he found rye bread and water next to his bed each morning. Periodically the water would taste bitter and drinking it would cause him to sleep more heavily than usual. On such occasions, when he awakened, his straw was changed and his hair and nails were cut. Hauser claimed that the first human being with whom he ever had contact was a mysterious man who visited him not long before his release, always taking great care not to reveal his face to him. This man, Hauser said, taught him to write his name by leading his hand. After learning to stand and walk, he was brought to Nuremberg. Furthermore, the stranger allegedly taught him to say the phrase "I want to be a cavalryman, as my father was" (in <a href="/wiki/Bavarian_language" title="Bavarian language">Old Bavarian dialect</a>), but Hauser claimed that he did not understand what these words meant.</p>
<p>This tale aroused great curiosity and made Hauser an object of international attention. Rumours arose that he was of princely parentage, possibly of Baden origin, but there were also claims that he was an <a href="/wiki/Impostor" class="mw-redirect" title="Impostor">impostor</a>.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Further_life_in_Nuremberg">Further life in Nuremberg</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kaspar_Hauser&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Further life in Nuremberg">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Paul_Johann_Anselm_Ritter_von_Feuerbach" title="Paul Johann Anselm Ritter von Feuerbach">Paul Johann Anselm Ritter von Feuerbach</a>, president of the Bavarian <a href="/wiki/Court_of_appeals" class="mw-redirect" title="Court of appeals">court of appeals</a>, began to investigate the case. Hauser was formally adopted by the town of Nuremberg and money was donated for his upkeep and education.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> He was given into the care of <a href="/wiki/Georg_Friedrich_Daumer" title="Georg Friedrich Daumer">Friedrich Daumer</a>, a schoolmaster and speculative philosopher, who taught him various subjects and who thereby discovered his talent for drawing. He appeared to flourish in this environment. Daumer also subjected him to <a href="/wiki/Homeopathy" title="Homeopathy">homeopathic</a> treatments and <a href="/wiki/Animal_magnetism" title="Animal magnetism">magnetic experiments</a>. As Feuerbach told the story, "When Professor Daumer held the north pole [of a magnet] towards him, Kaspar put his hand to the pit of his stomach, and, drawing his waistcoat in an outward direction, said that it drew him thus; and that a current of air seemed to proceed from him. The south pole affected him less powerfully; and he said that it blew upon him."<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Cut_wound">Cut wound</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kaspar_Hauser&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Cut wound">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>On 17 October 1829, Hauser did not come to the midday meal, but was found in the cellar of Daumer's house bleeding from a cut wound on the forehead. He asserted that while sitting on the privy, he was attacked and wounded by a hooded man who also threatened him with the words: "You still have to die ere you leave the city of Nuremberg." Hauser said that by the voice, he recognized the man as the one who had brought him to Nuremberg. As was obvious from his blood trail, Hauser at first fled to the first floor where his room was, but then, instead of moving on to his caretakers, he returned downstairs and climbed through a trap door into the cellar. Alarmed officials called for a police escort and transferred him to the care of Johann Biberbach, one of the municipal authorities. The alleged attack on Hauser also fueled rumours about his possible descent from Hungary, England or the House of Baden. Hauser's critics are of the opinion that he inflicted the wound on himself with a razor, which he then took back to his room before going to the cellar.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> He might have done so to arouse pity and thus escape chiding for a recent quarrel with Daumer, who had come to believe that the boy had a tendency to lie.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>

<h4><span id="The_&quot;pistol_accident&quot;"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="The_.22pistol_accident.22">The "pistol accident"</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kaspar_Hauser&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: The &quot;pistol accident&quot;">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>On 3 April 1830, a pistol shot went off in Hauser's room at the Biberbachs' house. His escort hurriedly entered the room and found him bleeding from a wound to the right side of his head. Hauser quickly revived and stated that he climbed on a chair to get some books, the chair fell and while trying to hold on to something he accidentally tore down the pistol hanging on the wall, causing the shot to go off. There are doubts whether the benign wound was actually caused by the shot and some authors associate the incident with a preceding quarrel in which, again, Hauser was reproached for lying.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> Whatever the case, the occurrence led the municipal authorities to come to another decision on Hauser, whose initially good relationship with the Biberbach family had soured. In May 1830, he was transferred to the <a href="/wiki/Tucher_von_Simmelsdorf" title="Tucher von Simmelsdorf">house of Baron von Tucher</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> who later also complained about Hauser's exorbitant vanity and lies. Perhaps the sharpest judgment passed on Hauser was the one by Mrs. Biberbach, who commented on his "horrendous mendacity" and "art of dissimulation" and called him "full of vanity and spite".<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Lord_Stanhope">Lord Stanhope</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kaspar_Hauser&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Lord Stanhope">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A British nobleman, <a href="/wiki/Philip_Henry_Stanhope,_4th_Earl_Stanhope" title="Philip Henry Stanhope, 4th Earl Stanhope">Lord Stanhope</a>, took an interest in Hauser and gained custody of him late in 1831. He spent a great deal of money attempting to clarify Hauser's origin. In particular, he paid for two visits to <a href="/wiki/Hungary" title="Hungary">Hungary</a> hoping to jog the boy's memory, as Hauser seemed to remember some Hungarian words and had once declared that the Hungarian Countess Maytheny was his mother. Hauser failed to recognize any buildings or monuments in Hungary. A Hungarian nobleman who had met Hauser later told Stanhope that he and his son had a good laugh when they recollected the strange boy and his <a href="/wiki/Histrionic" class="mw-disambig" title="Histrionic">histrionic</a> behavior.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> Stanhope later wrote that the complete failure of these inquiries led him to doubt Hauser's credibility. In December 1831, he transferred Hauser to <a href="/wiki/Ansbach" title="Ansbach">Ansbach</a>, to the care of a schoolmaster named Johann Georg Meyer, and in January 1832, Stanhope left Hauser for good. Stanhope continued to pay for Hauser's living expenses but never made good on his promise that he would take him to England. After Hauser's death, Stanhope published a book in which he presented all known evidence against Hauser, taking it as his "duty openly to confess that I had been deceived."<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> Followers of Hauser suspect Stanhope of ulterior motives and connections to the House of Baden, but academic historiography defends him as a philanthropist, a pious man and a seeker of truth.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Life_and_death_in_Ansbach">Life and death in Ansbach</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kaspar_Hauser&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Life and death in Ansbach">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Schoolmaster Meyer, a strict and pedantic man, disliked Hauser's many excuses and apparent lies and their relationship was thus rather strained. In late 1832, Hauser was employed as a copyist in the local law office. Still hoping that Stanhope would take him to England, he was very dissatisfied with his situation, which deteriorated further when his patron, Anselm von Feuerbach, died in May 1833. This certainly was a grievous loss to him.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> Some authors, however, point out that Feuerbach, by the end of his life, had lost faith in Hauser—writing a note, to be found in his legacy, which read: "Caspar Hauser is a smart scheming codger, a rogue, a good-for-nothing that ought to be killed."<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> But there is no indication that Feuerbach, already seriously ill, let Hauser feel this change of opinion.</p>
<p>On 9 December 1833, Hauser had a serious argument with Meyer. Lord Stanhope was expected to visit Ansbach at Christmas and Meyer said that he did not know how he would face him.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Fatal_stab_wound">Fatal stab wound</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kaspar_Hauser&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Fatal stab wound">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>

<p>Five days later, on 14 December 1833, Hauser came home with a deep wound in his left breast. He said that he was lured to the Ansbach Court Garden and that a stranger stabbed him there while giving him a bag. When policeman Herrlein searched the Court Garden, he found a small violet purse containing a pencilled note in "Spiegelschrift" (<a href="/wiki/Mirror_writing" title="Mirror writing">mirror writing</a>). The message read, in German:</p>
<p>"Hauser will be able to tell you quite precisely how I look and from where I am. To save Hauser the effort, I want to tell you myself from where I come _ _ . I come from from _ _ _ the Bavarian border _ _ On the river _ _ _ _ _ I will even tell you the name: M.&#160;L.&#160;Ö."</p>
<p>Hauser died of his wound on 17 December 1833.</p>
<p>Inconsistencies in Hauser's account led the Ansbach court of enquiry to suspect that he stabbed himself and invented a tale about being attacked. The note in the purse that was found in the Court Garden contained one spelling error and one grammatical error, both of which were typical for Hauser, who, on his deathbed, kept muttering incoherencies about "writing with pencil". Although he was very eager that the purse be found, he did not ask for its contents. The note itself was folded in a specific triangular form, just the way Hauser used to fold his letters, according to Mrs. Meyer. Forensic doctors agreed that the wound could indeed be self-inflicted. Many authors<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> believe that he wounded himself in a bid to revive public interest in his story and to convince Stanhope to fulfil his promise to take him to England, but that he stabbed himself more deeply than planned.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>

<p>Hauser was buried in the Stadtfriedhof (city cemetery) in Ansbach, where his headstone reads, in Latin, "Here lies Kaspar Hauser, riddle of his time. His birth was unknown, his death mysterious. 1833."<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> A monument to him was later erected in the Court Garden which reads <i>Hic occultus occulto occisus est</i>, meaning "Here a mysterious one was killed in a mysterious manner."</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Medical_opinions">Medical opinions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kaspar_Hauser&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Medical opinions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Hauser's various accounts of the story of his incarceration include several contradictions.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> Psychiatrist <a href="/wiki/Karl_Leonhard" title="Karl Leonhard">Karl Leonhard</a> concluded: "If he had been living since childhood under the conditions he describes, he would not have developed beyond the condition of an idiot; indeed he would not have remained alive long. His tale is so full of absurdities that it is astonishing that it was ever believed and is even today still believed by many people."<sup id="cite_ref-Karl_Leonhard_1970.2C_p..C2.A0229_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Karl_Leonhard_1970.2C_p..C2.A0229-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>Dr. Heidenreich, one of the physicians present at the autopsy, claimed that the brain of Kaspar Hauser was notable for small cortical size and few, non-distinct cortical gyri, indicating to some that he suffered from cortical atrophy or, as G. Hesse argued, from <a href="/wiki/Epilepsy" title="Epilepsy">epilepsy</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> Heidenreich may have been influenced by his <a href="/wiki/Phrenology" title="Phrenology">phrenological</a> ideas when examining Hauser's brain.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> Dr. Albert, who conducted the autopsy and wrote the official report, did not find any anomalies in Hauser's brain.</p>
<p>Karl Leonhard rejected the views of both Heidenreich and Hesse. He came to the following conclusion: "Kaspar Hauser was, as other authors already opined, a pathological swindler. In addition to his <a href="/wiki/Histrionic_personality_disorder" title="Histrionic personality disorder">hysterical make-up</a> he probably had the persistence of a <a href="/wiki/Paranoid_personality_disorder" title="Paranoid personality disorder">paranoid personality</a> since he was able to play his role so imperturbably. From many reports on his behaviour one can recognise the hysterical as well as the paranoid trend of his personality."<sup id="cite_ref-Karl_Leonhard_1970.2C_p..C2.A0229_23-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Karl_Leonhard_1970.2C_p..C2.A0229-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>A 1928 medical study<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> supported the view that Hauser accidentally stabbed himself too deeply, while a 2005 forensic analysis argued that it seems "unlikely that the stab to the chest was inflicted exclusively for the purpose of self-damage, but both a suicidal stab and a homicidal act (assassination) cannot be definitely ruled out."<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Hauser_as_hereditary_prince_of_Baden">Hauser as hereditary prince of Baden</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kaspar_Hauser&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Hauser as hereditary prince of Baden">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>According to contemporary rumours, probably current as early as 1829, Kaspar Hauser was the hereditary prince of Baden who was born 29 September 1812, and who, according to known history, died 16 October 1812. It was alleged that this prince was switched with a dying baby and subsequently surfaced 16 years later as Kaspar Hauser in Nuremberg. In this case, his parents would have been <a href="/wiki/Charles,_Grand_Duke_of_Baden" title="Charles, Grand Duke of Baden">Charles, Grand Duke of Baden</a> and <a href="/wiki/St%C3%A9phanie_de_Beauharnais" title="Stéphanie de Beauharnais">Stéphanie de Beauharnais</a>, cousin by marriage and adopted daughter of <a href="/wiki/Napoleon" title="Napoleon">Napoleon</a>. Because Charles had no surviving male <a href="/wiki/Offspring" title="Offspring">progeny</a>, his successor was his uncle <a href="/wiki/Louis_I,_Grand_Duke_of_Baden" title="Louis I, Grand Duke of Baden">Louis</a>, who was later succeeded by his half-brother, <a href="/wiki/Leopold_I_of_Baden" class="mw-redirect" title="Leopold I of Baden">Leopold</a>. Leopold's mother, the <a href="/wiki/Louise_Caroline_of_Hochberg" title="Louise Caroline of Hochberg">Countess of Hochberg</a>, was the alleged culprit of the boy's captivity. The Countess was supposed to have disguised herself as a ghost, the "White Lady", when kidnapping the prince. Her motive evidently would have been to secure the succession for her sons.</p>
<p>After Hauser's death, it was claimed further that he was murdered, again because of his being the prince.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Repudiation_in_the_1870s">Repudiation in the 1870s</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kaspar_Hauser&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Repudiation in the 1870s">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1876, Otto Mittelstädt presented evidence against this theory based on the official documents about the prince's emergency baptism, autopsy and burial.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Andrew_Lang" title="Andrew Lang">Andrew Lang</a> summarizes the results in his <i>Historical Mysteries</i>: "It is true that the Grand Duchess was too ill to be permitted to see her dead baby, in 1812, but the baby's father, grandmother, and aunt, with the ten Court physicians, the nurses and others, must have seen it, in death, and it is too absurd to suppose, on no authority, that they were all parties to the White Lady's plot."<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> Historian Fritz Trautz went so far to write: "The silly fairytale, which to this day moves many pens and has found much belief, was fully disproved in Otto Mittelstädt's book."<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> Furthermore, letters of the Grand Duke's mother, published in 1951, give detailed accounts of the child's birth, illness and death, corroborating the evidence against the alleged switch of babies.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Differing_DNA_analyses">Differing DNA analyses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kaspar_Hauser&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Differing DNA analyses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In November 1996, the German magazine <i><a href="/wiki/Der_Spiegel" title="Der Spiegel">Der Spiegel</a></i> reported an attempt to match genetically a blood sample from underwear assumed to have been Hauser's. This analysis was made in laboratories of <a href="/wiki/Forensic_Science_Service" title="Forensic Science Service">Forensic Science Service</a> in Birmingham and in the LMU Institute of Legal Medicine at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Munich" class="mw-redirect" title="University of Munich">University of Munich</a>. Comparisons with descendants of the princely family proved that the blood examined could not possibly stem from the hereditary prince of Baden.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2002, the Institute for Forensic Medicine of the <a href="/wiki/University_of_M%C3%BCnster" title="University of Münster">University of Münster</a> analyzed hair and body cells from locks of hair and items of clothing that also belonged to Kaspar Hauser. The analysts took from the items used in the test six different DNA samples, all of which turned out to be identical, but they differed substantially from the blood sample examined in 1996, the authenticity of which was therefore questioned. The new DNA samples were compared to a DNA segment from Astrid von Medinger, a descendant in the female line of Stéphanie de Beauharnais. The sequences were not identical but the deviation observed is not large enough to exclude a relationship as the difference could be caused by a mutation. (The <a href="/wiki/Mitochondrial_DNA" title="Mitochondrial DNA">mitochondrial DNA</a> which was examined is passed only through the female line and thus cannot change except through mutation.) On the other hand, the relatively high similarity by no means proves the alleged relationship, as the "Hauser samples" showed a pattern that is common among the German population.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
<p>The House of Baden does not allow any medical examination of the remains of Stéphanie de Beauharnais or of the child that was buried as her son in the family vault at the <a href="/wiki/Pforzheim" title="Pforzheim">Pforzheimer</a> Schlosskirche.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cultural_references">Cultural references</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kaspar_Hauser&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Cultural references">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Kaspar Hauser fits into the contemporary European image of the "<a href="/wiki/Feral_child" title="Feral child">wolf child</a>" (despite the fact that he almost certainly was not one), and he became possibly the best-known example of the genre. As a result, his story inspired numerous works.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Literature">Literature</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kaspar_Hauser&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Literature">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Kaspar Hauser was mentioned in a work of 1897 by <a href="/wiki/Leo_Tolstoy" title="Leo Tolstoy">Leo Tolstoy</a> entitled <i>What Is Art?</i> (chap. 5).</p>
<p>Kaspar Hauser inspired the French poet <a href="/wiki/Paul_Verlaine" title="Paul Verlaine">Paul Verlaine</a> to write the poem "Gaspard Hauser chante", published in his book <i><a href="/wiki/Sagesse" title="Sagesse">Sagesse</a></i> (1880). Kaspar Hauser is also referred to in <a href="/wiki/Herman_Melville" title="Herman Melville">Herman Melville</a>'s unfinished novella <i><a href="/wiki/Billy_Budd" title="Billy Budd">Billy Budd</a></i> (begun in 1886), as well as in his novels, both <i><a href="/wiki/Pierre:_or,_The_Ambiguities" class="mw-redirect" title="Pierre: or, The Ambiguities">Pierre: or, The Ambiguities</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/The_Confidence-Man" title="The Confidence-Man">The Confidence-Man</a></i>. He is also referenced in the <a href="/wiki/Hans_Christian_Andersen" title="Hans Christian Andersen">Hans Christian Andersen</a> story "Beauty of Form and Beauty of Mind" or "Beautiful".</p>
<p>Perhaps the most influential fictional treatment of Kasper Hauser was <a href="/wiki/Jakob_Wassermann" title="Jakob Wassermann">Jakob Wassermann</a>'s 1908 novel <i>Caspar Hauser oder Die Trägheit des Herzens</i> (<i>Caspar Hauser or the Inertia of the Heart</i>), which was largely responsible for its popularization in Germany.</p>
<p>In the mid-20th century, Kaspar Hauser was referred to in several works of science fiction or fantasy literature: <a href="/wiki/Eric_Frank_Russell" title="Eric Frank Russell">Eric Frank Russell</a>, in his 1943 novel <i><a href="/wiki/Sinister_Barrier" title="Sinister Barrier">Sinister Barrier</a></i>, described Kaspar Hauser as a person who originated from a non-human laboratory. <a href="/wiki/Fredric_Brown" title="Fredric Brown">Fredric Brown</a>, in his 1949 short story "Come and Go Mad", offered another theory about Kaspar Hauser. In <a href="/wiki/Henry_Kuttner" title="Henry Kuttner">Henry Kuttner</a>'s 1954 "The Portal in the Picture", the author suggests Hauser is from Malesco, a parallel world where science is treated as a religion and its secrets are hidden from the ordinary citizen. <a href="/wiki/Robert_A._Heinlein" title="Robert A. Heinlein">Robert A. Heinlein</a>, in his 1963 <i><a href="/wiki/Glory_Road" title="Glory Road">Glory Road</a></i>, refers to "Kaspar Hausers" as an analogue to persons popping in and out of metaphysical planes. <a href="/wiki/Harlan_Ellison" title="Harlan Ellison">Harlan Ellison</a>, in his 1967 story "<a href="/wiki/The_Prowler_in_the_City_at_the_Edge_of_the_World" title="The Prowler in the City at the Edge of the World">The Prowler in the City at the Edge of the World</a>", suggested that Hauser had been plucked out of time and later murdered by a female sadist named <a href="/wiki/Juliette_(novel)" title="Juliette (novel)">Juliette</a>.</p>
<p>In 1963, Marianne Hauser gave a fictional account of Kaspar Hauser's life in her novel <i>Prince Ishmael</i>.</p>
<p>In 1967, the Austrian playwright <a href="/wiki/Peter_Handke" title="Peter Handke">Peter Handke</a> published his play <i><a href="/wiki/Kaspar_(play)" title="Kaspar (play)">Kaspar</a></i>.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Paul_Auster" title="Paul Auster">Paul Auster</a>, in his 1985 novel <i><a href="/wiki/City_of_Glass_(Paul_Auster_book)" class="mw-redirect" title="City of Glass (Paul Auster book)">City of Glass</a></i>, compares the situation of one of its characters to that of Kaspar Hauser.</p>
<p>In 1994 the English poet <a href="/wiki/David_Constantine" title="David Constantine">David Constantine</a> explored the story and its personae in <i>Caspar Hauser: A Poem in Nine Cantos</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
<p>Kaspar Hauser is also referred to in <a href="/wiki/Jonathan_Lethem" title="Jonathan Lethem">Jonathan Lethem</a>'s 1997 novel <i><a href="/wiki/As_She_Climbed_Across_the_Table" title="As She Climbed Across the Table">As She Climbed Across the Table</a></i>, <a href="/wiki/Katherine_Neville_(author)" title="Katherine Neville (author)">Katherine Neville</a>'s novel <i>The Magic Circle</i> (1998), in <a href="/wiki/Steven_Millhauser" title="Steven Millhauser">Steven Millhauser</a>'s short story "Kaspar Hauser Speaks" (published in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Knife_Thrower_and_Other_Stories" title="The Knife Thrower and Other Stories">The Knife Thrower and Other Stories</a></i>, 1998), in <a href="/wiki/Ted_Chiang" title="Ted Chiang">Ted Chiang</a>'s short stories "Catching Crumbs from the Table" and "The Evolution of the Human Mind" (published in the journal <i><a href="/wiki/Nature_(journal)" title="Nature (journal)">Nature</a></i>, 2000), <a href="/wiki/Jeffrey_Eugenides" title="Jeffrey Eugenides">Jeffrey Eugenides</a>'s novel <i><a href="/wiki/Middlesex_(novel)" title="Middlesex (novel)">Middlesex</a></i> (2002), <a href="/wiki/Maggie_Nelson" title="Maggie Nelson">Maggie Nelson</a>'s poem "Kaspar Hauser" (2003, itself a probable reference to the Werner Herzog film), and <a href="/wiki/Lucie_Brock-Broido" title="Lucie Brock-Broido">Lucie Brock-Broido</a>'s poem "Self-Portrait as Kaspar Hauser" (published in <i>Trouble in Mind</i>, 2004). Canadian artist Diane Obomsawin tells the story of Kaspar Hauser in her 2007 graphic novel <i>Kaspar</i>. He is also referred to in <a href="/wiki/Nick_Hornby" title="Nick Hornby">Nick Hornby</a>'s <i>Juliet, Naked</i> (2009).</p>
<p>Kaspar Hauser serves as the namesake and inspiration for a character in <a href="/wiki/Dan_Abnett" title="Dan Abnett">Dan Abnett</a>'s Horus Heresy novel <i><a href="/wiki/Prospero_Burns" class="mw-redirect" title="Prospero Burns">Prospero Burns</a></i> (2010). The protagonist, "Kasper Hawser", shares a similar mysterious origin and childhood as attributed to Hauser, including his only toy being a wooden horse.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Film_and_television">Film and television</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kaspar_Hauser&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Film and television">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1974, the German filmmaker <a href="/wiki/Werner_Herzog" title="Werner Herzog">Werner Herzog</a> made Hauser's story into the film, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Enigma_of_Kaspar_Hauser" title="The Enigma of Kaspar Hauser">Jeder für sich und Gott gegen alle</a></i> ("Every Man for Himself and God Against All"). In English, the film was either known by that translation, or by the title <i>The Enigma of Kaspar Hauser</i>. The film includes the main known features of Hauser's life though omits most of his changes of location and career, and does not depict him as a frequent liar as accounts suggest.</p>
<p>In 1993, the German-<a href="/wiki/Austria" title="Austria">Austrian</a> co-production <i>Kaspar Hauser – Verbrechen am Seelenleben eines Menschen</i> ("Kaspar Hauser – Crimes against a man's soul"), directed by Peter de Sehr, espoused the "Prince of Baden" theory.</p>
<p>In the 1966 film <i><a href="/wiki/Fahrenheit_451_(film)" title="Fahrenheit 451 (film)">Fahrenheit 451</a></i>, the protagonist <a href="/wiki/Guy_Montag" title="Guy Montag">Guy Montag</a> discreetly puts a copy of a book entitled <i>Gaspard Hauser</i> into his bag before the rest of the books in that residence are torched.</p>
<p>In the TV series <i><a href="/wiki/Smallville" title="Smallville">Smallville</a></i>, in the <a href="/wiki/Smallville_(season_1)" title="Smallville (season 1)">episode "Stray"</a> (2002) <a href="/wiki/Clark_Kent" title="Clark Kent">Clark Kent</a> finds a boy who does not remember who he was or where he came from, except his name. Chloe refers to the boy as a "modern-day Kaspar Hauser."</p>
<p>In the Japanese horror movie <i><a href="/wiki/Marebito_(film)" title="Marebito (film)">Marebito</a></i> (2004), the protagonist Masuoka refers to a girl he found chained up underground as his "little Kaspar Hauser".</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/La_leggenda_di_Kaspar_Hauser" title="La leggenda di Kaspar Hauser">La leggenda di Kaspar Hauser</a> (2012, Davide Manuli) is a surreal drama based on "the legend" of Kaspar Hauser. In this modern <a href="/wiki/Western_(genre)" title="Western (genre)">western</a>-like re-interpretation featuring <a href="/wiki/Vincent_Gallo" title="Vincent Gallo">Vincent Gallo</a>, a music-obsessive Kaspar washes up on a Mediterranean beach, where half a dozen protagonists try to make sense of who he is.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Music">Music</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kaspar_Hauser&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Music">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In music Kaspar Hauser was the subject of an "Epitaph for Kaspar Hauser" (1997) by the composer <a href="/wiki/Claus_K%C3%BChnl" title="Claus Kühnl">Claus Kühnl</a>, a "meditation on a day in the life of Kaspar Hauser towards the end of his prison term" for an organist, a registrant and two ad hoc Player.</p>
<p>Kaspar Hauser's story has inspired numerous musical references. There have been at least two operas named <i>Kasper Hauser</i>, a 2007 work by American composer <a href="/wiki/Elizabeth_Swados" title="Elizabeth Swados">Elizabeth Swados</a> and a 2010 work by British composer <a href="/wiki/Rory_Boyle_(composer)" class="mw-redirect" title="Rory Boyle (composer)">Rory Boyle</a>.</p>
<p><i><a href="/wiki/Subterranea_(album)" title="Subterranea (album)">Subterranea</a></i>, a 1997 concept album by British <a href="/wiki/Progressive_rock" title="Progressive rock">progressive rock</a> band <a href="/wiki/IQ_(band)" title="IQ (band)">IQ</a> (1997), was loosely inspired by Hauser's story. Italian artists <a href="/wiki/Reinhold_Giovanett" class="mw-redirect" title="Reinhold Giovanett">Reinhold Giovanett</a> and <a href="/wiki/Josef_Oberhollenzer" class="mw-redirect" title="Josef Oberhollenzer">Josef Oberhollenzer</a> put out a CD titled <i>Kaspar Hauser</i> in 1999.</p>
<p>Numerous bands and musicians have released songs titled "Kaspar Hauser", including the German band <a href="/wiki/Dschinghis_Khan" title="Dschinghis Khan">Dschinghis Khan</a>, the Detroit band <a href="/wiki/Trial_(band)" title="Trial (band)">Trial</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Sun_City_Girls" title="Sun City Girls">Sun City Girls</a>. <a href="/wiki/Cologne" title="Cologne">Colonian</a>-dialect rock band <a href="/wiki/BAP_(German_band)" title="BAP (German band)">BAP</a> and German singer-songwriter <a href="/wiki/Reinhard_Mey" title="Reinhard Mey">Reinhard Mey</a><sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> have released songs called "Kaspar". French singer-songwriter <a href="/wiki/Georges_Moustaki" title="Georges Moustaki">Georges Moustaki</a> put out a song titled "Gaspard", based on <a href="/wiki/Paul_Verlaine" title="Paul Verlaine">Paul Verlaine</a>'s poem. <a href="/wiki/Suzanne_Vega" title="Suzanne Vega">Suzanne Vega</a> included "Wooden Horse (Caspar Hauser's Song)" on her 1987 album <i><a href="/wiki/Solitude_Standing" title="Solitude Standing">Solitude Standing</a></i>.</p>
<p>In 1984 Polish poet and musician Grzegorz Ciechowski has written a song titled "Kaspar Hauser", which is a part of long play "Obywatel G.C.".</p>
<p>Kaspar Hauser was taken as the name of an <a href="/wiki/Alternative_rock" title="Alternative rock">alternative rock</a> band based in <a href="/wiki/Amherst,_Massachusetts" title="Amherst, Massachusetts">Amherst, Massachusetts</a>, in the early 1980s, as well as an experimental musician from <a href="/wiki/Newcastle-Upon-Tyne" class="mw-redirect" title="Newcastle-Upon-Tyne">Newcastle-Upon-Tyne</a>, England. <i>Haüser</i> is the name of an independent recording artist/songwriter from Northern Ireland who has released several EPs since 2001 and is currently recording new material scheduled for release in late 2012.<i>The Hauser Project</i> is a musical project created in 2011 whose name was inspired by Kaspar Hauser.</p>
<p>In 1994, Birgit Scherzer, then director and choreographer of the Saarbrücken Staatstheater Ballet in Germany, used the Hauser story as the basis for a ballet <i>Kasper Hauser</i> which she presented at the Saarbrücken Staatstheater.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2012, the American <a href="/wiki/Mathcore" title="Mathcore">mathcore</a> band <a href="/wiki/Exotic_Animal_Petting_Zoo" title="Exotic Animal Petting Zoo">Exotic Animal Petting Zoo</a> included a song titled "Kaspar Hauser Could See the Stars in the Daytime" in its second album <i><a href="/wiki/Tree_of_Tongues" class="mw-redirect" title="Tree of Tongues">Tree of Tongues</a></i>.</p>
<p>In 2013, The Swedish singer-songwriter <a href="/wiki/H%C3%A5kan_Hellstr%C3%B6m" title="Håkan Hellström">Håkan Hellström</a> made a reference to Kaspar Hauser in the song "Fri tillslut (Finally free)" on his album <a href="/wiki/Det_kommer_aldrig_va_%C3%B6ver_f%C3%B6r_mig" title="Det kommer aldrig va över för mig">Det kommer aldrig va över för mig</a>.</p>
<p>In 2015, Scottish-born singer-songwriter <a href="/wiki/Momus_(musician)" title="Momus (musician)">Momus</a> referenced <i>the loneliness Kaspar Hauser</i> in his song <i>System of Usher</i>, the second track on his album <i><a href="/wiki/Turpsycore" title="Turpsycore">Turpsycore</a></i>.</p>
<p>The German gothic metal band <a href="/wiki/Pyogenesis" title="Pyogenesis">Pyogenesis</a> released a song called <i>Every Man For Himself...And God Against All</i> on their 2017 album <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=A_Kingdom_to_Disappear&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="A Kingdom to Disappear (page does not exist)">A Kingdom to Disappear</a></i> based on the story of Kaspar Hauser and the film of the same name.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Non-fiction">Non-fiction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kaspar_Hauser&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Non-fiction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In his later years, Paul Johann Anselm Ritter von Feuerbach took a deep interest in the fate of Kaspar Hauser. He was the first to publish a critical summary of the ascertained facts, under the title of <i>Kaspar Hauser, ein Beispiel eines Verbrechens am Seelenleben</i> (1832).</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Anthroposophy" title="Anthroposophy">Anthroposophists</a> have written several books on Kaspar Hauser. One in particular, a detailed work by Peter Tradowsky, addresses the mysteries surrounding Kaspar Hauser's life from the anthroposophical point of view. His analysis delves into the occult significance of the individuality he sees as incarnated in Kaspar Hauser. In 1996 <a href="/wiki/Jeffrey_Moussaieff_Masson" title="Jeffrey Moussaieff Masson">Jeffrey Moussaieff Masson</a> wrote <i>Lost Prince: The Unsolved Mystery of Kaspar Hauser</i> (1996).<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup></p>
<p>There was also a January 1861 <i>Atlantic Monthly</i> article which included an <a href="https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:Who_was_Kaspar_Hauser_-_The_Atlantic_Monthly_Jan._1861.pdf" class="extiw" title="commons:File:Who was Kaspar Hauser - The Atlantic Monthly Jan. 1861.pdf">unsigned article on Kaspar Hauser</a>. Circulated among the American intellectual establishment of the time, it provides a sense of perspective on many of the issues firing the debate about "Who was Kaspar Hauser?" which continues to this day.</p>
<p>Medical writers have referred to <a href="/wiki/Psychosocial_short_stature" title="Psychosocial short stature">psychosocial short stature</a> as "Kaspar Hauser syndrome."<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup></p>
