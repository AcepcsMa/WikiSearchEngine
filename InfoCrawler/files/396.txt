<p>The <b>Peace of Augsburg</b>, also called the <b>Augsburg Settlement</b>,<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> was a treaty between <a href="/wiki/Charles_V,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Charles V, Holy Roman Emperor">Charles V, Holy Roman Emperor</a> (the predecessor of Ferdinand I) and the <a href="/wiki/Schmalkaldic_League" title="Schmalkaldic League">Schmalkaldic League</a>, signed on 25 September 1555 at the imperial city of <a href="/wiki/Augsburg" title="Augsburg">Augsburg</a>. It officially ended the religious struggle between the two groups and made the legal division of <a href="/wiki/Christianity" title="Christianity">Christendom</a> permanent within the <a href="/wiki/Holy_Roman_Empire" title="Holy Roman Empire">Holy Roman Empire</a>, allowing rulers to choose either <a href="/wiki/Lutheranism" title="Lutheranism">Lutheranism</a> or <a href="/wiki/Roman_Catholicism" class="mw-redirect" title="Roman Catholicism">Roman Catholicism</a> as the official confession of their state. <a href="/wiki/Calvinism" title="Calvinism">Calvinism</a> was not allowed until the <a href="/wiki/Peace_of_Westphalia" title="Peace of Westphalia">Peace of Westphalia</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Overview">Overview</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Peace_of_Augsburg&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Overview">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Peace established the principle <i><a href="/wiki/Cuius_regio,_eius_religio" title="Cuius regio, eius religio">Cuius regio, eius religio</a></i> ("whose region, his religion"), which allowed the princes of states within the Holy Roman Empire to adopt either <a href="/wiki/Lutheranism" title="Lutheranism">Lutheranism</a> or <a href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic_Church" class="mw-redirect" title="Roman Catholic Church">Catholicism</a> within the domains they controlled, ultimately reaffirming their sovereignty over those domains. Subjects, citizens, or residents who did not wish to conform to the prince's choice were given a grace period in which they were free to emigrate to different regions in which their desired religion had been accepted.</p>
<p>Article 24 stated: "In case our subjects, whether belonging to the old religion or the <a href="/wiki/Augsburg_Confession" title="Augsburg Confession">Augsburg Confession</a>, should intend leaving their homes with their wives and children in order to settle in another, they shall be hindered neither in the sale of their estates after due payment of the local taxes nor injured in their honour."</p>
<p>Charles V had made an interim ruling, the <a href="/wiki/Augsburg_Interim" title="Augsburg Interim">Augsburg Interim</a> of 1548, on the legitimacy of two religious creeds in the empire, and this was codified in law on 30 June 1548 upon the insistence of Charles V, who wanted to work out religious differences under the auspices of a general council of the Catholic Church. The Interim largely reflected principles of religious behavior in its 26 articles, although it allowed for marriage of the clergy, and the giving of both bread and wine to the laity. This led to resistance by the Protestant territories, who proclaimed their own Interim at Leipzig the following year.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Interim was overthrown in 1552 by the revolt of the Protestant elector <a href="/wiki/Maurice_of_Saxony" class="mw-redirect" title="Maurice of Saxony">Maurice of Saxony</a> and his allies. In the negotiations at <a href="/wiki/Passau" title="Passau">Passau</a> in the summer of 1552, even the Catholic princes had called for a lasting peace, fearing the religious controversy would never be settled. The emperor, however, was unwilling to recognize the religious division in Western Christendom as permanent. This document was foreshadowed by the <a href="/wiki/Peace_of_Passau" title="Peace of Passau">Peace of Passau</a>, which in 1552 gave Lutherans religious freedom after a victory by Protestant armies. Under the Passau document, Charles granted a peace only until the next imperial Diet, whose meeting was called in early 1555.</p>
<p>The treaty, negotiated on Charles' behalf by his brother <a href="/wiki/Ferdinand_I,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Ferdinand I, Holy Roman Emperor">Ferdinand</a>, effectively gave Lutheranism official status within the domains of the <a href="/wiki/Holy_Roman_Empire" title="Holy Roman Empire">Holy Roman Empire</a>, according to the policy of <i><a href="/wiki/Cuius_regio,_eius_religio" title="Cuius regio, eius religio">cuius regio, eius religio</a></i>. Knights and towns who had practiced Lutheranism for some time were exempted under the <i><a href="/wiki/Declaratio_Ferdinandei" title="Declaratio Ferdinandei">Declaratio Ferdinandei</a></i>, but the <i><a href="/wiki/Reservatum_ecclesiasticum" title="Reservatum ecclesiasticum">Ecclesiastical reservation</a></i> supposedly prevented the principle of <i>cuius regio, eius religio</i> from being applied if an ecclesiastical ruler converted to Lutheranism.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Main_principles">Main principles</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Peace_of_Augsburg&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Main principles">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Peace of Augsburg contained three main principles:<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<ol>
<li>The principle of <span lang="la" xml:lang="la"><i><a href="/wiki/Cuius_regio,_eius_religio" title="Cuius regio, eius religio">cuius regio, eius religio</a></i></span> ("Whose realm, his religion") provided for internal religious unity within a state: the religion of the prince became the religion of the state and all its inhabitants. Those inhabitants who could not conform to the prince's religion were allowed to leave: an innovative idea in the 16th century. This principle was discussed at length by the various delegates, who finally reached agreement on the specifics of its wording after examining the problem and the proposed solution from every possible angle.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></li>
<li>The second principle, called the <span lang="la" xml:lang="la"><i><a href="/wiki/Reservatum_ecclesiasticum" title="Reservatum ecclesiasticum">reservatum ecclesiasticum</a></i></span> (ecclesiastical reservation), covered the special status of the ecclesiastical state. If the prelate of an ecclesiastic state changed his religion, the inhabitants of that state did not have to do so. Instead, the prelate was expected to resign from his post, although this was not spelled out in the agreement.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></li>
<li>The third principle, known as <span lang="la" xml:lang="la"><i><a href="/wiki/Declaratio_Ferdinandei" title="Declaratio Ferdinandei">Declaratio Ferdinandei</a></i></span> (Ferdinand's Declaration), exempted knights and some of the cities from the requirement of religious uniformity, if the reformed religion had been practiced there since the mid-1520s. This allowed for a few mixed cities and towns where Catholics and Lutherans had lived together. It also protected the authority of the princely families, the knights and some of the cities to determine what religious uniformity meant in their territories. Ferdinand inserted this at the last minute, on his own authority.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></li>
</ol>
<p>The third principle exempted knights and some of the cities under the jurisdiction of an ecclesiastical prince if they had practiced <a href="/wiki/Lutheranism" title="Lutheranism">Lutheranism</a> for some time (Lutheranism was the only branch of Protestantism recognized under the Peace). The provision was not publicized as part of the treaty, and was kept secret for almost two decades.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Problems">Problems</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Peace_of_Augsburg&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Problems">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Peace-of-augsburg_1555.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8d/Peace-of-augsburg_1555.jpg/220px-Peace-of-augsburg_1555.jpg" width="220" height="202" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8d/Peace-of-augsburg_1555.jpg/330px-Peace-of-augsburg_1555.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8d/Peace-of-augsburg_1555.jpg/440px-Peace-of-augsburg_1555.jpg 2x" data-file-width="600" data-file-height="550" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Peace-of-augsburg_1555.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Representatives of the German estates at the Augsburg conference discuss the possibilities of a religious peace.</div>
</div>

<p>The document itself had critical problems. While it gave legal basis for the practice of the Lutheran confession, it did not accept any of the Reformed traditions, such as <a href="/wiki/Calvinism" title="Calvinism">Calvinism</a>, nor did it recognize <a href="/wiki/Anabaptism" title="Anabaptism">Anabaptism</a>. Although the Peace of Augsburg was moderately successful in relieving tension in the empire and increasing tolerance, it left important things undone. Neither the Anabaptists nor the Calvinists were protected under the peace, so many Protestant groups living under the rule of a Lutheran prince still found themselves in danger of the charge of <a href="/wiki/Heresy" title="Heresy">heresy</a>. (Article 17: "However, all such as do not belong to the two above named religions shall not be included in the present peace but be totally excluded from it.") These minorities did not achieve any legal recognition until the <a href="/wiki/Peace_of_Westphalia" title="Peace of Westphalia">Peace of Westphalia</a> in 1648. The intolerance towards Calvinists caused them to take desperate measures that led to the <a href="/wiki/Thirty_Years%27_War" title="Thirty Years' War">Thirty Years' War</a>. One of the more notable measures was the <a href="/wiki/Defenestrations_of_Prague#Second_Defenestration_of_Prague" title="Defenestrations of Prague">Second Defenestration of Prague</a> (1618) in which two representatives of the fiercely Catholic Holy Roman Emperor <a href="/wiki/Ferdinand_II,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Ferdinand II, Holy Roman Emperor">Ferdinand II</a> were thrown out of a castle window in <a href="/wiki/Prague" title="Prague">Prague</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Aftermath">Aftermath</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Peace_of_Augsburg&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Aftermath">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The principle of <a href="/wiki/Reservatum_ecclesiasticum" title="Reservatum ecclesiasticum">ecclesiastical reservation</a> was tested in the <a href="/wiki/Cologne_War" title="Cologne War">Cologne War</a> (1583-1588), which grew out of the scenario envisioned by Ferdinand when he wrote the proviso: the reigning <a href="/wiki/Prince-Bishop" class="mw-redirect" title="Prince-Bishop">Prince-Bishop</a> converted to Protestantism; although he did not insist that the population convert, he placed <i>Calvinism</i> on a parity with Catholicism throughout the <a href="/wiki/Electorate_of_Cologne" title="Electorate of Cologne">Electorate of Cologne</a>. This in itself created a two-fold legal problem: first, Calvinism was considered a heresy; second, the Elector did not resign his <a href="/wiki/Episcopal_see" title="Episcopal see">see</a>, which made him eligible, at least in theory, to cast a ballot for emperor. Finally, his marriage posed a very real potential to convert the Electorate into a dynastic principality, shifting the balance of religious power in the Empire.</p>
<p>A side effect of the religious turmoil was Charles' decision to abdicate and divide Habsburg territory into two sections. His brother Ferdinand ruled the Austrian lands, and Charles' fervently Catholic son, <a href="/wiki/Philip_II_of_Spain" title="Philip II of Spain">Philip II</a>, became administrator of Spain, the Spanish Netherlands, parts of Italy, and other overseas holdings.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Notes">Notes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Peace_of_Augsburg&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Notes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
