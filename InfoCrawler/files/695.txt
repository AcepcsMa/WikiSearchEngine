<p><b>Brașov</b> (<small>Romanian pronunciation:&#160;</small><span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Romanian" title="Help:IPA/Romanian">[braˈʃov]</a></span><small class="nowrap metadata">&#160;(<span class="unicode haudio"><span class="fn"><span style="white-space:nowrap"><a href="/wiki/File:Ro-Bra%C8%99ov.ogg" title="About this sound"><img alt="About this sound" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/11px-Loudspeaker.svg.png" width="11" height="11" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/17px-Loudspeaker.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/22px-Loudspeaker.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="20" data-file-height="20" /></a>&#160;</span><a href="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/0/09/Ro-Bra%C8%99ov.ogg" class="internal" title="Ro-Brașov.ogg">listen</a></span></span>)</small>) is a city in <a href="/wiki/Romania" title="Romania">Romania</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Administrative_centre" title="Administrative centre">administrative centre</a> of <a href="/wiki/Bra%C8%99ov_County" title="Brașov County">Brașov County</a>.</p>
<p>According to the last Romanian census (2011), Brașov has a population of 253,200, making it the 7th most populous city in Romania. The <a href="/wiki/Bra%C5%9Fov_metropolitan_area" class="mw-redirect" title="Braşov metropolitan area">metropolitan area</a> is home to 369,896 residents.<sup id="cite_ref-INSSER_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-INSSER-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Brașov is located in the central part of the country, about 166 kilometres (103 miles) north of <a href="/wiki/Bucharest" title="Bucharest">Bucharest</a> and 380 kilometres (236 miles) from the <a href="/wiki/Black_Sea" title="Black Sea">Black Sea</a>. It is surrounded by the <a href="/wiki/Southern_Carpathians" title="Southern Carpathians">Southern Carpathians</a> and is part of the <a href="/wiki/Transylvania" title="Transylvania">Transylvania</a> region.</p>
<p>The city is notable for being the birthplace of the <a href="/wiki/De%C8%99teapt%C4%83-te,_rom%C3%A2ne!" title="Deșteaptă-te, române!">national anthem of Romania</a> and for hosting the <a href="/wiki/Golden_Stag_Festival" title="Golden Stag Festival">Golden Stag International Music Festival</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bra%C8%99ov&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The city was described in 1235 AD under the name <b>Corona</b>, a Latin word meaning "crown", a name given by the German colonists. According to D. Moldovanu, the name of Braşov came from the name of local river named Bârsa (also pronounced as "Bărsa"). Bărsa was adopted by Slavs and transformed in Barsa and later in Barsov and finally in Brasov<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> According to Binder, the current Romanian and the Hungarian name <span lang="hu">Brassó</span> (<small>Hungarian pronunciation:&#160;</small><span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Hungarian" title="Help:IPA/Hungarian">[ˈbrɒʃːoː]</a></span>) are derived from the <a href="/wiki/Pecheneg_language" title="Pecheneg language">Turkic</a> word <i>barasu</i>, meaning "white water" with a Slavic suffix <i>-ov</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Other linguists proposed various etymologies including an Old Slavic anthroponym Brasa.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>The first attested mention of Brașov is <i>Terra Saxonum de Barasu</i> ("<a href="/wiki/Transylvanian_Saxons" title="Transylvanian Saxons">Saxon</a> Land of Baras") in a 1252 document. The German name <i>Kronstadt</i> means "Crown City" and is reflected in the city's <a href="/wiki/Coat_of_arms" title="Coat of arms">coat of arms</a> as well as in its Medieval Latin name, <i>Corona</i>. The two names of the city, <i>Kronstadt</i> and <i>Corona</i>, were used simultaneously in the <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">Middle Ages</a>, along with the <a href="/wiki/Medieval_Latin" title="Medieval Latin">Medieval Latin</a> <i>Brassovia</i>.</p>
<p>From 1950 to 1960, during part of the <a href="/wiki/Communist_Romania" class="mw-redirect" title="Communist Romania">Communist period</a> in Romania, the city was <a href="/wiki/List_of_places_in_Romania_whose_names_were_changed" title="List of places in Romania whose names were changed">called</a> <i>Orașul Stalin</i> (<a href="/wiki/List_of_places_named_after_Joseph_Stalin" title="List of places named after Joseph Stalin">Stalin City</a>), after the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet</a> leader <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Stalin" title="Joseph Stalin">Joseph Stalin</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bra%C8%99ov&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The oldest traces of human activity and settlements in Brașov date back to the <a href="/wiki/Neolithic" title="Neolithic">Neolithic</a> age (about 9500 BCE). Archaeologists working from the last half of the 19th century discovered continuous traces of human settlements in areas situated in Brașov: Valea Cetății, Pietrele lui Solomon, Șprenghi, Tâmpa, Dealul Melcilor, and Noua. The first three locations show traces of <a href="/wiki/Dacians" title="Dacians">Dacian</a> citadels; Șprenghi Hill housed a <a href="/wiki/Roman_Dacia" title="Roman Dacia">Roman</a>-style construction. The last two locations had their names applied to <a href="/wiki/Bronze_Age" title="Bronze Age">Bronze Age</a> cultures—<i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Schneckenberg_culture&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Schneckenberg culture (page does not exist)">Schneckenberg</a></i> ‘Hill of the Snails’ (Early Bronze Age<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEColesHarding1979140_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEColesHarding1979140-7">[7]</a></sup>) and <i><a href="/wiki/Noua_culture" class="mw-redirect" title="Noua culture">Noua</a></i> 'The New’ (Late Bronze Age<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEColesHarding1979410_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEColesHarding1979410-8">[8]</a></sup>).</p>
<p>German colonists known as the <a href="/wiki/Transylvanian_Saxons" title="Transylvanian Saxons">Transylvanian Saxons</a> played a decisive role in Brașov's development. These Germans were invited by <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Hungary" title="Kingdom of Hungary">Hungarian</a> kings to develop towns, build mines, and cultivate the land of <a href="/wiki/Transylvania" title="Transylvania">Transylvania</a> at different stages between 1141 and 1300. The settlers came primarily from the <a href="/wiki/Rhineland" title="Rhineland">Rhineland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Flanders" title="Flanders">Flanders</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Moselle" title="Moselle">Moselle</a> region, with others from <a href="/wiki/Thuringia" title="Thuringia">Thuringia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bavaria" title="Bavaria">Bavaria</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wallonia" title="Wallonia">Wallonia</a>, and even <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a>.</p>
<p>In 1211, by order of King <a href="/wiki/Andrew_II_of_Hungary" title="Andrew II of Hungary">Andrew II of Hungary</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Teutonic_Knights" class="mw-redirect" title="Teutonic Knights">Teutonic Knights</a> fortified the <a href="/wiki/Burzenland" title="Burzenland">Burzenland</a> to defend the border of the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Hungary" title="Kingdom of Hungary">Kingdom of Hungary</a>. On the site of the village of Brașov, the Teutonic Knights built Kronstadt – the city of the crown.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Although the crusaders were evicted by 1225, the colonists they brought in remained, along with local population, as did three distinct settlements they founded on the site of Brașov:</p>
<ul>
<li><i>Corona</i>, around the Black Church (Biserica Neagră);</li>
<li><i>Martinsberg</i>, west of Cetățuia Hill;</li>
<li><i>Bartholomä</i>, on the eastern side of Sprenghi Hill.</li>
</ul>

<p>Germans living in Brașov were mainly involved in trade and crafts. The location of the city at the intersection of trade routes linking the <a href="/wiki/Ottoman_Empire" title="Ottoman Empire">Ottoman Empire</a> and <a href="/wiki/Western_Europe" title="Western Europe">Western Europe</a>, together with certain tax exemptions, allowed Saxon merchants to obtain considerable wealth and exert a strong political influence. They contributed a great deal to the architectural flavor of the city. Fortifications around the city were erected and continually expanded, with several towers maintained by different craftsmen's guilds, according to medieval custom. Part of the fortification ensemble was recently restored using <a href="/wiki/UNESCO" title="UNESCO">UNESCO</a> funds, and other projects are ongoing. At least two entrances to the city, <i>Poarta Ecaterinei</i> (or <i>Katharinentor</i>) and <i>Poarta Șchei</i> (or <i>Waisenhausgässertor</i>), are still in existence. The city center is marked by the mayor's former office building (<a href="https://ro.wikipedia.org/wiki/Casa_Sfatului_din_Bra%C8%99ov" class="extiw" title="ro:Casa Sfatului din Brașov">Casa Sfatului</a>) and the surrounding square (<i>piaţa</i>), which includes one of the oldest buildings in Brașov, the Hirscher Haus. Nearby is the "Black Church" (<i><a href="/wiki/Biserica_Neagr%C4%83" title="Biserica Neagră">Biserica Neagră</a></i>), which some claim to be the largest <a href="/wiki/Gothic_architecture" title="Gothic architecture">Gothic style</a> church in <a href="/wiki/Southeastern_Europe" class="mw-redirect" title="Southeastern Europe">Southeastern Europe</a>.</p>
<p>The cultural and religious importance of the Romanian church and school in Șchei is underlined by the generous donations received from more than thirty hospodars of Moldavia and Wallachia, as well as that from <a href="/wiki/Elizabeth_of_Russia" title="Elizabeth of Russia">Elizabeth of Russia</a>. In the 17th and 19th centuries, the Romanians in Șchei campaigned for national, political, and cultural rights, and were supported in their efforts by Romanians from all other provinces, as well as by the local Greek merchant community. In 1838 they established the first Romanian language newspaper <i><a href="/wiki/Gazeta_Transilvaniei" class="mw-redirect" title="Gazeta Transilvaniei">Gazeta Transilvaniei</a></i> and the first Romanian institutions of higher education (<i>Școlile Centrale Greco-Ortodoxe</i> - "The Greek-Orthodox Central Schools", today named after <a href="/wiki/Andrei_%C8%98aguna" title="Andrei Șaguna">Andrei Șaguna</a>). The <a href="/wiki/Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Holy Roman Emperor">Holy Roman Emperor</a> and sovereign of Transylvania <a href="/wiki/Joseph_II,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Joseph II, Holy Roman Emperor">Joseph II</a> awarded Romanians citizenship rights for a brief period during the latter decades of the 18th century.</p>
<p>In 1850 the town had 21,782 inhabitants: 8,874 (40.7%) Germans, 8,727 (40%) Romanians, 2,939 (13.4%) Hungarians.<sup id="cite_ref-varga.adatbank.transindex.ro_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-varga.adatbank.transindex.ro-10">[10]</a></sup> In 1910 the town had 41,056 inhabitants: 10,841 (26.4%) Germans, 11,786 (28.7%) Romanians, 17,831 (43.4%) Hungarians. <sup id="cite_ref-varga.adatbank.transindex.ro_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-varga.adatbank.transindex.ro-10">[10]</a></sup> In World War I, the town was occupied by <a href="/wiki/Romanians" title="Romanians">Romanian</a> troops between 16 August and 4 October in 1916 during <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Transylvania" title="Battle of Transylvania">Battle of Transylvania</a>.</p>

<p>In 1918, after the <a href="/wiki/Union_of_Transylvania_with_Romania" title="Union of Transylvania with Romania">Proclamation of union</a> of <a href="/wiki/Alba_Iulia" title="Alba Iulia">Alba Iulia</a> (adopted by the Deputies of the Romanians from Transylvania), Deputies of the Saxons from Transylavania supported it, with their vote to be part of Romania, and declared their allegiance to the new Romanian state. The inter-war period was a time of flourishing economic and cultural life in general, which included the Saxons in Brașov as well. However, at the end of World War II many ethnic Germans were forcibly deported to the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet Union</a>, and many more emigrated to <a href="/wiki/West_Germany" title="West Germany">West Germany</a> after <a href="/wiki/Communist_Romania" class="mw-redirect" title="Communist Romania">Romania became a communist country</a>.</p>

<p>Jews have lived in Brașov since 1807, when <a href="/w/index.php?title=Aron_Ben_Jehuda&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Aron Ben Jehuda (page does not exist)">Aron Ben Jehuda</a> was given permission to live in the city, a privilege until then granted only to Saxons. The Jewish community of Brașov was officially founded 19 years later, followed by the first Jewish school in 1864, and the building of the synagogue in 1901. The Jewish population of Brașov was 67 in 1850, but it expanded rapidly to 1,280 people in 1910 and 4,000 by 1940. Today the community has about 230 members, after many families <a href="/wiki/Aliyah" title="Aliyah">left for Israel</a> between World War II and 1989.</p>
<p>Like many other cities in Transylvania, Brașov is also home to a significant <a href="/wiki/Hungarian_minority_in_Romania" class="mw-redirect" title="Hungarian minority in Romania">ethnic Hungarian minority</a>.</p>
<p>During the communist period, industrial development was vastly accelerated. Under <a href="/wiki/Nicolae_Ceau%C8%99escu" title="Nicolae Ceaușescu">Nicolae Ceaușescu</a>'s rule, the city was the site of the <a href="/wiki/1987_Bra%C8%99ov_strike" class="mw-redirect" title="1987 Brașov strike">1987 Brașov strike</a>. This was repressed by the authorities and resulted in numerous workers being imprisoned.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Economy">Economy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bra%C8%99ov&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Economy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Brasov,_Piata_Sfatului.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4d/Brasov%2C_Piata_Sfatului.jpg/220px-Brasov%2C_Piata_Sfatului.jpg" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4d/Brasov%2C_Piata_Sfatului.jpg/330px-Brasov%2C_Piata_Sfatului.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4d/Brasov%2C_Piata_Sfatului.jpg/440px-Brasov%2C_Piata_Sfatului.jpg 2x" data-file-width="4499" data-file-height="2999" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Brasov,_Piata_Sfatului.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The city center (<a href="/wiki/Bra%C5%9Fov_Council_Square_(Pia%C5%A3a_Sfatului)" class="mw-redirect" title="Braşov Council Square (Piaţa Sfatului)">Piaţa Sfatului</a>)</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Industrial development in Brașov started in the inter-war period, with one of the largest factories being the airplane manufacturing plant (<a href="/wiki/Industria_Aeronautic%C4%83_Rom%C3%A2n%C4%83" title="Industria Aeronautică Română">IAR</a> Brașov), which produced the first Romanian fighter planes, which were used in World War II against the Soviets. After Communist rule was imposed, the plant was converted to manufacture of agricultural equipment, being renamed "Uzina Tractorul Brașov" (internationally known as Universal Tractor Brașov).</p>
<p>Industrialization was accelerated in the Communist era, with special emphasis being placed on heavy industry, attracting many workers from other parts of the country. Heavy industry is still abundant, including <a href="/wiki/Roman_(vehicle_manufacturer)" title="Roman (vehicle manufacturer)">Roman</a>, which manufactures <a href="/wiki/MAN_SE" title="MAN SE">MAN AG</a> trucks as well as native-designed trucks and coaches. Although the industrial base has been in decline in recent years, Brașov is still a site for manufacturing hydraulic transmissions, auto parts, ball-bearings, helicopters (at the nearby IAR site in <a href="/wiki/Ghimbav" title="Ghimbav">Ghimbav</a>), building materials, tools, furniture, textiles, shoes. There is also a large brewery.</p>
<p>The large <a href="/wiki/Longwave" title="Longwave">longwave</a> <a href="/wiki/Bod_Transmitter" title="Bod Transmitter">Bod Transmitter</a>, a broadcasting facility, is located near Brașov, at <a href="/wiki/Bod,_Bra%C8%99ov" title="Bod, Brașov">Bod</a>.</p>

<p>Brașov has a total population of 253,200 (2011 census). Its ethnic composition includes:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Romanians" title="Romanians">Romanians</a>: 208,019 (91.3%)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Hungarians" title="Hungarians">Hungarians</a>: 16,172 (7.1%)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Germans_in_Romania" class="mw-redirect" title="Germans in Romania">Germans</a>: 1,079 (0.5%)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Romani_people" title="Romani people">Romani people</a>: 916 (0.4%)</li>
<li>Other ethnicities: 1,037 (0.7%)</li>
</ul>
<p>In 2005, the <a href="/wiki/Bra%C8%99ov_metropolitan_area" title="Brașov metropolitan area">Brașov metropolitan area</a> was created. With its surrounding localities, Brașov had 369,896 inhabitants as of 2011<sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Bra%C8%99ov&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>.<sup id="cite_ref-INSSER_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-INSSER-1">[1]</a></sup></p>

<p>The Brașov local transport network is well-developed, with around 50 bus and trolleybus lines. There is also a regular bus line serving <a href="/wiki/Poiana_Bra%C8%99ov" title="Poiana Brașov">Poiana Brașov</a>, a nearby winter resort. All are operated by <a href="/wiki/Public_transportation_in_Bra%C8%99ov" title="Public transportation in Brașov">RAT Brașov</a>. Because of its central location, the <a href="/wiki/Bra%C8%99ov_railway_station" title="Brașov railway station">Brașov railway station</a> is one of the busiest stations in Romania with trains to/from most destinations in the country served by rail.</p>

<p>The construction of <a href="/wiki/Bra%C5%9Fov_Airport" class="mw-redirect" title="Braşov Airport">Braşov Airport</a><sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> was initiated by Intelcan Canada on April 15, 2008. Although construction was planned to be finalized in 24 to 30 months, works have lagged and there is no term by which it will be operational. The project consists of a terminal capable of handling 1 million passengers per year and a 2,800-metre-long (9,200-foot) runway. The <a href="/wiki/A3_(Romania)" class="mw-redirect" title="A3 (Romania)">A3</a> highway is also planned to pass the city. However, there is no foreseeable date for starting construction.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Tourism">Tourism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bra%C8%99ov&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Tourism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>With its central location, Brașov is a suitable location from which to explore Romania, and the distances to several tourist destinations (including the <a href="/wiki/Black_Sea" title="Black Sea">Black Sea</a> resorts, the monasteries in northern Moldavia, and the wooden churches of <a href="/wiki/Maramure%C5%9F_region" class="mw-redirect" title="Maramureş region">Maramureș</a>) are similar. It is also the largest city in a mountain resorts area. The old city is very well preserved and is best seen by taking the cable-car to the top of Tâmpa Mountain.</p>
<p>Temperatures from May to September fluctuate around 23&#160;°C (73&#160;°F). Brașov benefits from a winter tourism season centered on <a href="/wiki/Winter_sports" class="mw-redirect" title="Winter sports">winter sports</a> and other activities. <a href="/wiki/Poiana_Bra%C8%99ov" title="Poiana Brașov">Poiana Brașov</a> is the most popular Romanian <a href="/wiki/Ski_resort" title="Ski resort">ski resort</a> and an important tourist center preferred by many tourists from other European states.</p>
<p>The city also has several restaurants that serve local as well as international cuisine (e.g. Hungarian and Chinese). Some of these are situated in the city center.</p>

<li><i><a href="/wiki/Biserica_Neagr%C4%83" title="Biserica Neagră">Biserica Neagră</a></i> ("The Black Church"), a celebrated <a href="/wiki/Gothic_architecture" title="Gothic architecture">Gothic</a> site - the building dates from 1477, when it replaced an older church (demolished around 1385). <p>The city has a long tradition in sports, the first sport associations being established at the end of the 19th century (Target shooting Association, Gymnastics School). The Transylvanian Sports Museum is among the oldest in the country and presents the evolution of consecrated sports in the city. During the communist period, <a href="/wiki/Universiade" title="Universiade">universiades</a> and <a href="/wiki/Daciad" title="Daciad">Daciads</a> were held, where local sportsmen were obliged to participate. Nowadays, the infrastructure of the city allows other sports to be practiced, such as football, rugby, tennis, cycling, handball, gliding, skiing, skating, mountain climbing, paintball, bowling, swimming, target shooting, basketball, martial arts, equestrian, volleyball or gymnastics. Annually, at "Olimpia" sports ground, the "Brașov Challenge Cup" tennis competition is held.</p>
<p>The only football champion team based in the city was <a href="/wiki/Col%C5%A3ea_Bra%C8%99ov" class="mw-redirect" title="Colţea Brașov">Colţea Brașov</a>, winning the championship in 1928 and managing second place in 1927, in only 10 years of existence (1921–1931). It was succeeded by <a href="/wiki/Bra%C8%99ovia_Bra%C8%99ov" class="mw-redirect" title="Brașovia Brașov">Brașovia Brașov</a>. Brașov hosted the Group A fixtures of the <a href="/wiki/2008_IIHF_World_Championship_Division_II" title="2008 IIHF World Championship Division II">2008 IIHF World Championship Division II</a> ice hockey tournament. There were 15 games held between April 3 and April 13.</p>
<p>Brașov hosted the <a href="/wiki/2013_European_Youth_Winter_Olympic_Festival" class="mw-redirect" title="2013 European Youth Winter Olympic Festival">2013 European Youth Winter Olympic Festival</a>.</p>
<p>As of 2012<sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Bra%C8%99ov&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, Brasov is hosting two trail semi-marathons: <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://semimaraton.ro">Semimaraton "Intersport Brasov"</a> and <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://brasovmarathon.ro">Brasov Marathon</a>.</p>
<p>In November 2013, Brasov submitted their bid for the <a href="/wiki/2020_Winter_Youth_Olympics" title="2020 Winter Youth Olympics">2020 Winter Youth Olympics</a>. They were up against <a href="/wiki/Lausanne,_Switzerland" class="mw-redirect" title="Lausanne, Switzerland">Lausanne, Switzerland</a> to be awarded the event. In December that year, the city was signed the Youth Olympic Game Candidature Procedure. The host city was to be announced in July 2015,<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> in which Lausanne was selected.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sports_venues">Sports venues</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bra%C8%99ov&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Sports venues">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul>
<li><b>Planned</b>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Bra%C8%99ov_Arena" class="mw-redirect" title="Brașov Arena">Brașov Arena</a> (23,000 seats) – planned football stadium on the site of the former Municipal Stadium</li>
</ul>
</li>
<li><b>Existing</b>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Stadionul_Silviu_Ploe%C5%9Fteanu" class="mw-redirect" title="Stadionul Silviu Ploeşteanu">Silviu Ploeșteanu Stadium</a> (8,500 seats) - built in 1960 for football, floodlights were installed in 2009</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Dumitru_Popescu_Coliba%C8%99i_Sports_Hall" title="Dumitru Popescu Colibași Sports Hall">Dumitru Popescu Colibași Sports Hall</a> (2,300 seats) – built around 1970 for indoor sports: handball, basketball, volleyball, sometimes others</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Bra%C8%99ov_Olympic_Ice_Rink" title="Brașov Olympic Ice Rink">Brașov Olympic Ice Rink</a> (2,000 capacity: 1,600 seated, 400 standing) – inaugurated on February 18, 2010, used mainly for ice hockey and public skating</li>
</ul>
</li>
<li><b>Demolished</b>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Stadionul_Municipal_(Bra%C5%9Fov)" class="mw-redirect" title="Stadionul Municipal (Braşov)">Municipal Stadium</a> (30,000 capacity) – built in 1975, used for 1 May and 23 August parades, rarely used for football matches (demolished in 2008)</li>
</ul>
</li>
<li><b>Others</b>
<ul>
<li><a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://paradisulacvatic.ro">Paradisul Acvatic</a> - aquatic complex with 40m long swimming pool and three jumping platform (1m, 3m, 5.20m).</li>
</ul>
</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Media">Media</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bra%C8%99ov&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Media">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The city of Braşov is home to several local media publications such as Transilvania Express, Monitorul Express, Bună Ziua Braşov or Braşovul Tău.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> Also, several local television stations exist, such as RTT, MIX TV and Nova TV.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
