<p>The <b>3rd Infantry Division</b> (nicknamed the "Marne Division)"<sup id="cite_ref-SUD_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SUD-3">[3]</a></sup> is a <a href="/wiki/Brigade_combat_team#armored_brigade_combat_team" title="Brigade combat team">combined arms</a> and <a href="/wiki/Brigade_combat_team#infantry_brigade_combat_team" title="Brigade combat team">light infantry</a> <a href="/wiki/Division_(military)" title="Division (military)">division</a> of the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Army" title="United States Army">United States Army</a> based at <a href="/wiki/Fort_Stewart" title="Fort Stewart">Fort Stewart</a>, <a href="/wiki/Georgia_(U.S._state)" title="Georgia (U.S. state)">Georgia</a>. It is a direct subordinate unit of the <a href="/wiki/XVIII_Airborne_Corps" title="XVIII Airborne Corps">XVIII Airborne Corps</a> and <a href="/wiki/U.S._Army_Forces_Command" class="mw-redirect" title="U.S. Army Forces Command">U.S. Army Forces Command</a>. Its current organization includes three <a href="/wiki/Brigade" title="Brigade">brigade</a> combat teams, one <a href="/wiki/Army_aviation" title="Army aviation">aviation</a> <a href="/wiki/Brigade" title="Brigade">brigade</a>, a division <a href="/wiki/Artillery" title="Artillery">artillery</a> and support elements. The division has a distinguished history, having seen active service in both <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a> and <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=3rd_Infantry_Division_(United_States)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The division fought <a href="/wiki/Western_Front_(World_War_I)" title="Western Front (World War I)">in France</a> in World War I. In World War II, it landed with Gen. Patton's task force in a contested amphibious landing on the coast of Morocco, <a href="/wiki/North_Africa_Campaign" class="mw-redirect" title="North Africa Campaign">North Africa</a>, overwhelming Vichy French defenders in November 1942. In 1943, the division invaded Sicily in July, and invaded Italy at Salerno in September, before fighting in France and finally Germany. <a href="/wiki/Medal_of_Honor" title="Medal of Honor">Medal of Honor</a> recipient <a href="/wiki/Audie_Murphy" title="Audie Murphy">Audie Murphy</a>, featured in the Hollywood movie, "To Hell and Back," was a member. The division also served in the Korean War. From 1957 until 1996, the division was a major part of the United States Army's presence in the <a href="/wiki/NATO" title="NATO">NATO</a> alliance in West Germany.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="World_War_I">World War I</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=3rd_Infantry_Division_(United_States)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: World War I">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:The_Rock_of_the_Marne._DA_Poster_21-42.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/08/The_Rock_of_the_Marne._DA_Poster_21-42.jpg/220px-The_Rock_of_the_Marne._DA_Poster_21-42.jpg" width="220" height="140" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/08/The_Rock_of_the_Marne._DA_Poster_21-42.jpg/330px-The_Rock_of_the_Marne._DA_Poster_21-42.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/08/The_Rock_of_the_Marne._DA_Poster_21-42.jpg/440px-The_Rock_of_the_Marne._DA_Poster_21-42.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2680" data-file-height="1700" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:The_Rock_of_the_Marne._DA_Poster_21-42.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Near Mézy, France, July 1918. Here the German Army made its last great attack of World War I. It struck in the Marne River area along the road to Paris, and the weight of the blow fell on the 38th U.S. Infantry Regiment under the command of MG <a href="/wiki/Ulysses_G._McAlexander" title="Ulysses G. McAlexander">Ulysses G. McAlexander</a>, of the 3rd Division. This was their first fight. Firing in three directions, blasted by artillery fire, taking all flesh and blood could stand, the regiments held on doggedly and threw the enemy back across the Marne. This defense checked the Germans' assault and made an Allied offensive possible. General Pershing called it "one of the most brilliant pages of our military annals."</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The 3rd Division was activated in November 1917, seven months after the <a href="/wiki/American_entry_into_World_War_I" title="American entry into World War I">American entry into World War I</a>, at <a href="/wiki/Camp_Greene" title="Camp Greene">Camp Greene</a>, <a href="/wiki/North_Carolina" title="North Carolina">North Carolina</a>. Eight months later, it saw combat for the first time in France on the <a href="/wiki/Western_Front_(World_War_I)" title="Western Front (World War I)">Western Front</a>. At midnight on 14 July 1918, the division earned lasting distinction. Engaged in the <a href="/wiki/Second_Battle_of_the_Marne" title="Second Battle of the Marne">Aisne-Marne Offensive</a> as a member of the <a href="/wiki/American_Expeditionary_Forces" title="American Expeditionary Forces">American Expeditionary Force</a> (AEF) to Europe, the division was protecting the French capital of <a href="/wiki/Paris" title="Paris">Paris</a> with a position on the banks of the <a href="/wiki/Marne_River" class="mw-redirect" title="Marne River">Marne River</a>. The <a href="/w/index.php?title=7th_Machine_Gun_Battalion_(United_States)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="7th Machine Gun Battalion (United States) (page does not exist)">7th Machine Gun Battalion</a> of the 3rd Division rushed to <a href="/wiki/Ch%C3%A2teau-Thierry" title="Château-Thierry">Château-Thierry</a> amid retreating French troops and held the Germans back at the <a href="/wiki/Marne" title="Marne">Marne River</a>. While surrounding units retreated, the 3rd Division, including the <a href="/wiki/4th_Infantry_Regiment_(United_States)" title="4th Infantry Regiment (United States)">4th</a>, <a href="/wiki/30th_Infantry_Regiment_(United_States)" title="30th Infantry Regiment (United States)">30th</a> and <a href="/wiki/38th_Infantry_Regiment_(United_States)" title="38th Infantry Regiment (United States)">38th Infantry Regiments</a>, remained steadfast throughout the <a href="/wiki/Second_Battle_of_the_Marne" title="Second Battle of the Marne">Second Battle of the Marne</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Colonel_(United_States)" title="Colonel (United States)">Colonel</a> <a href="/wiki/Ulysses_G._McAlexander" title="Ulysses G. McAlexander">Ulysses G. McAlexander</a>'s dogged defense earned the 3rd Division its nickname as the "Rock of the Marne".<sup id="cite_ref-History_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-History-4">[4]</a></sup> During the massive attack, the 3rd Infantry Division's commanding officer, Major General <a href="/wiki/Joseph_T._Dickman" title="Joseph T. Dickman">Joseph T. Dickman</a>, famously cried out <i>"Nous Resterons La"</i> (We Shall Remain Here). Their Blue and White insignia also earned them the nickname <i>The Blue and White Devils."</i> <sup id="cite_ref-Resterons_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Resterons-5">[5]</a></sup> The rest of the division was absorbed under French command until brought back together under the command of Major General <a href="/wiki/Joseph_T._Dickman" title="Joseph T. Dickman">Joseph T. Dickman</a> and by 15 July 1918 they took the brunt of what was to be the last German offensive of the war. <a href="/wiki/General_(United_States)" title="General (United States)">General</a> <a href="/wiki/John_J._Pershing" title="John J. Pershing">John Joseph "Black Jack" Pershing</a>, <a href="/wiki/Commander-in-chief" title="Commander-in-chief">Commander-in-chief</a> (C-in-C) of the AEF on the Western Front, called this stand "one of the most brilliant pages in the annals of military history".<sup id="cite_ref-Hemenway1919_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hemenway1919-6">[6]</a></sup> During the war two members of the division were awarded the <a href="/wiki/Medal_of_Honor" title="Medal of Honor">Medal of Honor</a>.</p>
<p>Casualties during the war were 3,177 <a href="/wiki/Killed_in_action" title="Killed in action">killed in action</a> with 12,940 <a href="/wiki/Wounded_in_action" title="Wounded in action">wounded</a>.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="World_War_II">World War II</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=3rd_Infantry_Division_(United_States)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: World War II">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Combat_chronicle">Combat chronicle</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=3rd_Infantry_Division_(United_States)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Combat chronicle">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:10_Luglio_1943_Sbarco_in_Sicilia_degli_Alleati_a_Licata_Settore_Joss_Spiaggia_di_Mollarella_e_Poliscia.jpg" class="image"><img alt="10 Luglio 1943 Sbarco in Sicilia degli Alleati a Licata Settore Joss Spiaggia di Mollarella e Poliscia.jpg" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/44/10_Luglio_1943_Sbarco_in_Sicilia_degli_Alleati_a_Licata_Settore_Joss_Spiaggia_di_Mollarella_e_Poliscia.jpg/220px-10_Luglio_1943_Sbarco_in_Sicilia_degli_Alleati_a_Licata_Settore_Joss_Spiaggia_di_Mollarella_e_Poliscia.jpg" width="220" height="293" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/44/10_Luglio_1943_Sbarco_in_Sicilia_degli_Alleati_a_Licata_Settore_Joss_Spiaggia_di_Mollarella_e_Poliscia.jpg/330px-10_Luglio_1943_Sbarco_in_Sicilia_degli_Alleati_a_Licata_Settore_Joss_Spiaggia_di_Mollarella_e_Poliscia.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/44/10_Luglio_1943_Sbarco_in_Sicilia_degli_Alleati_a_Licata_Settore_Joss_Spiaggia_di_Mollarella_e_Poliscia.jpg/440px-10_Luglio_1943_Sbarco_in_Sicilia_degli_Alleati_a_Licata_Settore_Joss_Spiaggia_di_Mollarella_e_Poliscia.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1944" data-file-height="2592" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:10_Luglio_1943_Sbarco_in_Sicilia_degli_Alleati_a_Licata_Settore_Joss_Spiaggia_di_Mollarella_e_Poliscia.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>

<p>The 3rd Division is the only division of the U.S. Army during <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a> that fought the <a href="/wiki/Axis_powers" title="Axis powers">Axis</a> on all European fronts, and was among the first American combat units to engage in offensive ground combat operations. <a href="/wiki/Audie_Murphy" title="Audie Murphy">Audie Murphy</a>, the most highly decorated American soldier of the war, served with the 3rd Division.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> The 3rd Infantry Division saw combat in North Africa, Sicily, Italy, France, Germany and Austria for 531 consecutive days.<sup id="cite_ref-Chronicle_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Chronicle-8">[8]</a></sup> During the war the 3rd Infantry Division consisted of the <a href="/wiki/7th_Infantry_Regiment_(United_States)" title="7th Infantry Regiment (United States)">7th</a>, <a href="/wiki/15th_Infantry_Regiment_(United_States)" title="15th Infantry Regiment (United States)">15th</a> and <a href="/wiki/30th_Infantry_Regiment_(United_States)" title="30th Infantry Regiment (United States)">30th Infantry Regiments</a>, together with supporting units.</p>
<p>The 3rd Division, under the command of <a href="/wiki/Major_general_(United_States)" title="Major general (United States)">Major General</a> <a href="/w/index.php?title=Jonathan_W._Anderson_(United_States_Army_officer)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Jonathan W. Anderson (United States Army officer) (page does not exist)">Jonathan W. Anderson</a>, after spending many months training in the United States after the <a href="/wiki/Empire_of_Japan" title="Empire of Japan">Japanese</a> <a href="/wiki/Attack_on_Pearl_Harbor" title="Attack on Pearl Harbor">attack on Pearl Harbor</a>, first saw action during the war as a part of the Western Task Force in <a href="/wiki/Operation_Torch" title="Operation Torch">Operation Torch</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Allies_of_World_War_II" title="Allies of World War II">Allied</a> <a href="/wiki/Operation_Torch" title="Operation Torch">invasion of North Africa</a>, landing at <a href="/wiki/Fedala" class="mw-redirect" title="Fedala">Fedala</a> on 8 November 1942, and captured half of <a href="/wiki/French_protectorate_in_Morocco" title="French protectorate in Morocco">French Morocco</a>. The division remained there for the next few months and therefore took no part in the <a href="/wiki/Tunisian_Campaign" title="Tunisian Campaign">Tunisian Campaign</a>, which came to an end in May 1943 with the surrender of almost 250,000 <a href="/wiki/Axis_powers" title="Axis powers">Axis</a> soldiers who subsequently became <a href="/wiki/Prisoner_of_war" title="Prisoner of war">prisoners of war</a> (POWs). While there a battalion of the 30th Infantry Regiment acted as security guards during the <a href="/wiki/Casablanca_Conference" title="Casablanca Conference">Casablanca Conference</a> in mid-January 1943. In late February Major General Anderson left the division and was replaced by Major General <a href="/wiki/Lucian_Truscott" title="Lucian Truscott">Lucian K. Truscott, Jr.</a>, who instituted a tough training regime and ensured that all ranks in the division could march five miles in one hour, and four miles an hour thereafter. The troops called it "the Truscott Trot". The division began intensive training in <a href="/wiki/Amphibious_warfare" title="Amphibious warfare">amphibious landing</a> <a href="/wiki/Military_operation" title="Military operation">operations</a>.</p>
<p>On 10 July 1943, the division made another amphibious assault landing on the <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italian</a> island of <a href="/wiki/Sicily" title="Sicily">Sicily</a> (codenamed <a href="/wiki/Allied_invasion_of_Sicily" title="Allied invasion of Sicily">Operation Husky</a>), landing at <a href="/wiki/Licata" title="Licata">Licata</a> town on the beach, to west, called Torre di Gaffi and Mollarella and on the beach, to east, called Falconara. The division, serving under the command of <a href="/wiki/Lieutenant_general_(United_States)" title="Lieutenant general (United States)">Lieutenant General</a> <a href="/wiki/George_S._Patton" title="George S. Patton">George S. Patton</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Seventh_United_States_Army" title="Seventh United States Army">U.S. Seventh Army</a>, fought its way into <a href="/wiki/Palermo" title="Palermo">Palermo</a> before elements of the <a href="/wiki/2nd_Armored_Division_(United_States)" title="2nd Armored Division (United States)">2nd Armored Division</a> could get there, in the process marching 90 miles in three days, and raced on to capture <a href="/wiki/Messina" title="Messina">Messina</a> on 17 August, thus ending the brief Sicilian campaign, where the division had a short rest to absorb replacements. During the campaign the 3rd Division gained a reputation as one of the best divisions in the Seventh Army.</p>
<p>Nine days after the Allied <a href="/wiki/Allied_invasion_of_Italy" title="Allied invasion of Italy">invasion of mainland Italy</a>, on 18 September 1943, the 3rd Division came ashore at <a href="/wiki/Salerno" title="Salerno">Salerno</a>, where they came under the command of <a href="/wiki/VI_Corps_(United_States)" title="VI Corps (United States)">VI Corps</a>, under Major General <a href="/wiki/Ernest_J._Dawley" title="Ernest J. Dawley">Ernest J. Dawley</a> who was replaced two days later by Major General <a href="/wiki/John_P._Lucas" title="John P. Lucas">John P. Lucas</a> (who had commanded the division from September 1941 to March 1942). The corps was part of Lieutenant General <a href="/wiki/Mark_W._Clark" title="Mark W. Clark">Mark W. Clark</a>'s <a href="/wiki/United_States_Army_North" title="United States Army North">U.S. Fifth Army</a>. The 3rd Division was destined to see some of the fiercest and toughest fighting of the war thus far, serving on the <a href="/wiki/Italian_Campaign_(World_War_II)" title="Italian Campaign (World War II)">Italian Front</a>. Seeing intensive action along the way, the division drove to and across the <a href="/wiki/Volturno_Line" title="Volturno Line">Volturno River</a> by October 1943, and then to <a href="/wiki/Monte_Cassino" title="Monte Cassino">Monte Cassino</a>, where the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Monte_Cassino" title="Battle of Monte Cassino">Battle of Monte Cassino</a> would later be fought, before, with the rest of the <a href="/wiki/15th_Army_Group" title="15th Army Group">15th Army Group</a>, being held up at the <a href="/wiki/Winter_Line" title="Winter Line">Winter Line</a> (also known as the Gustav Line). In mid-November the division, after spearheading the Fifth Army's advance and suffering heavy casualties during the past few weeks, was relieved by the <a href="/wiki/36th_Infantry_Division_(United_States)" title="36th Infantry Division (United States)">36th Infantry Division</a> and pulled out of the line to rest and absorb replacements, coming under the command of Major General <a href="/wiki/Geoffrey_Keyes" title="Geoffrey Keyes">Geoffrey Keyes</a>' <a href="/wiki/II_Corps_(United_States)" title="II Corps (United States)">II Corps</a>. The division remained out of action until late December.</p>
<p>After a brief rest, the division was part of the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Anzio" title="Battle of Anzio">amphibious landing at Anzio</a>, codenamed <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Anzio" title="Battle of Anzio">Operation Shingle</a>, on 22 January 1944, still as part of VI Corps, and serving alongside the <a href="/wiki/1st_Infantry_Division_(United_Kingdom)" title="1st Infantry Division (United Kingdom)">British 1st Infantry Division</a> and other units. It would remain there for just under four months in a toe-hold against numerous furious German counterattacks, and enduring <a href="/wiki/Trench_warfare" title="Trench warfare">trench warfare</a> similar to that suffered on the <a href="/wiki/Western_Front_(World_War_I)" title="Western Front (World War I)">Western Front</a> during <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a>. On 29 February 1944, the 3rd Division fought off an attack by three German divisions, who fell back with heavy losses two days later. In a single day of combat at Anzio, the 3rd Infantry Division suffered more than 900 casualties, the most of any American division on one day in World War II.<sup id="cite_ref-Chronicle_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Chronicle-8">[8]</a></sup> The division's former commander, Major General Lucas, was replaced as commander of VI Corps by the 3rd Division's commander, Major General Truscott. He was replaced in command of the 3rd Division by <a href="/wiki/Brigadier_general_(United_States)" title="Brigadier general (United States)">Brigadier General</a> <a href="/wiki/John_W._O%27Daniel" title="John W. O'Daniel">John W. "Iron Mike" O'Daniel</a>, previously the assistant division commander (ADC) and a distinguished World War I veteran.</p>
<p>In late May, VI Corps broke out of the Anzio beachhead in <a href="/wiki/Operation_Diadem" title="Operation Diadem">Operation Diadem</a> with the 3rd Division in the main thrust. Instead of defeating the Germans, Lieutenant General Clark, the Fifth Army commander, disobeying orders from <a href="/wiki/General_(United_Kingdom)" title="General (United Kingdom)">General</a> <a href="/wiki/Harold_Alexander,_1st_Earl_Alexander_of_Tunis" title="Harold Alexander, 1st Earl Alexander of Tunis">Sir Harold Alexander</a>, <a href="/wiki/Commander-in-chief" title="Commander-in-chief">Commander-in-Chief</a> (C-in-C) of the <a href="/wiki/Allied_Armies_in_Italy" title="Allied Armies in Italy">Allied Armies in Italy</a> (formerly the 15th Army Group), sent the division on to the Italian capital of <a href="/wiki/Rome" title="Rome">Rome</a>. This allowed the majority of the <a href="/wiki/10th_Army_(Wehrmacht)" title="10th Army (Wehrmacht)">German 10th Army</a>, which would otherwise have been trapped, to escape, thus prolonging the campaign in Italy. The division was then removed from the front line and went into training for the <a href="/wiki/Operation_Dragoon" title="Operation Dragoon">Operation Dragoon</a>, the Allied <a href="/wiki/Operation_Dragoon" title="Operation Dragoon">invasion of Southern France</a>.</p>

<p>On 15 August 1944, <a href="/wiki/D-Day_(military_term)" title="D-Day (military term)">D-Day</a> for Dragoon, the division, still under VI Corps command but now under the U.S. Seventh Army, landed at <a href="/wiki/St._Tropez" class="mw-redirect" title="St. Tropez">St. Tropez</a>, advanced up the <a href="/wiki/Rhone_Valley" class="mw-redirect" title="Rhone Valley">Rhone Valley</a>, through the <a href="/wiki/Vosges" title="Vosges">Vosges</a> Mountains, and reached the <a href="/wiki/Rhine" title="Rhine">Rhine</a> at <a href="/wiki/Strasbourg" title="Strasbourg">Strasbourg</a>, 26–27 November 1944. After maintaining defensive positions it took part in clearing the <a href="/wiki/Colmar_Pocket" title="Colmar Pocket">Colmar Pocket</a> on 23 January, and on 15 March struck against <a href="/wiki/Siegfried_Line" title="Siegfried Line">Siegfried Line</a> positions south of <a href="/wiki/Zweibr%C3%BCcken" title="Zweibrücken">Zweibrücken</a>. The division advanced through the defenses and crossed the Rhine, 26 March 1945; then drove on to take <a href="/wiki/Nuremberg" title="Nuremberg">Nuremberg</a> in a fierce battle, capturing the city in block-by-block fighting, 17–20 April. The 3rd pushed on to take <a href="/wiki/Augsburg" title="Augsburg">Augsburg</a> and <a href="/wiki/Munich" title="Munich">Munich</a>, 27–30 April, and was in the vicinity of <a href="/wiki/Salzburg" title="Salzburg">Salzburg</a> when the <a href="/wiki/Victory_in_Europe_Day" title="Victory in Europe Day">war in Europe ended</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Elements of the <a href="/wiki/7th_Infantry_Regiment_(United_States)" title="7th Infantry Regiment (United States)">7th Infantry Regiment</a> serving under the 3rd Infantry Division captured Hitler's retreat at <a href="/wiki/Berchtesgaden" title="Berchtesgaden">Berchtesgaden</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>

<p>During the <a href="/wiki/Korean_War" title="Korean War">Korean War</a>, the division was known as the "Fire Brigade" for its rapid response to crisis. 3rd Infantry Division had been headquartered at Fort Benning along with its <a href="/wiki/15th_Infantry_Regiment_(United_States)" title="15th Infantry Regiment (United States)">15th Infantry Regiment</a>. The <a href="/wiki/7th_Infantry_Regiment_(United_States)" title="7th Infantry Regiment (United States)">7th Infantry Regiment</a> was located at Fort Devens. 3rd Infantry Division initially arrived in Japan where, as the Far East Command Reserve,<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> it planned post conflict occupation missions in northern Korea.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> In Japan their strength was increased by augmentation from South Korean soldiers. They landed at <a href="/wiki/Wonsan" title="Wonsan">Wonsan</a> and received the <a href="/wiki/65th_Infantry_Regiment_(United_States)" title="65th Infantry Regiment (United States)">65th Infantry Regiment</a> as their third maneuver element before moving north to <a href="/wiki/Hungnam" title="Hungnam">Hungnam</a> and <a href="/wiki/Chongam-guyok" title="Chongam-guyok">Majon-dong</a>. At Majon-dong they established a defensive position with the 65th Infantry and began fighting. 1st and 2nd Battalions of the 7th Infantry were on the left flank. The 15th Infantry was between the 7th and 65th Regiments. 3rd Battalion, 7th Infantry was set as the nucleus for <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Chosin_Reservoir_order_of_battle" title="Battle of Chosin Reservoir order of battle">Task Force Dog</a> which was commanded by Brigadier General Armistead D. Mead, assistant 3rd Division commander and sent north to conduct a relief in place with <a href="/wiki/1st_Battalion_1st_Marines" class="mw-redirect" title="1st Battalion 1st Marines">1st Battalion, 1st Marine Regiment</a> at Chinhung-ni; the south end of the <a href="/wiki/1st_Marine_Division_(United_States)" title="1st Marine Division (United States)">1st Marine Division</a> and support the withdrawal of 1st Marine Division and <a href="/wiki/Task_Force_Faith" title="Task Force Faith">Regimental Combat Team 31</a> from the Chosin Reservoir. 3rd Infantry Division's TF Dog was the rearguard keeping the pressure off of the Marine column. The division established, along with the <a href="/wiki/7th_Infantry_Division_(United_States)" title="7th Infantry Division (United States)">7th Infantry Division</a> a collapsing perimeter around the port of Hungnam until the last of <a href="/wiki/X_Corps_(United_States)" title="X Corps (United States)">X Corps</a> was off the beach. The port of Hungnam was <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Chosin_Reservoir#Evacuation_at_Hungnam" title="Battle of Chosin Reservoir">blown up</a> to deprive the enemy the use of those facilities as the last of the 7th, 15th, and 65th Infantry units boarded ships.</p>
<p>The division went on to support combat missions of the <a href="/wiki/Eighth_United_States_Army" title="Eighth United States Army">Eighth Army</a> until 1953 when it was withdrawn. Notably, the division fought valiantly, besides its extremely essential and able contribution during the Battle of the Chosin Reservoir, at the Chorwon-Kumwha area, Jackson Heights and Arrowhead outposts, and blocked a CCF push in the Kumsong Area in July 1953.</p>
<p>3rd Infantry Division received ten Battle Stars. Eleven more members of the unit received Medals of Honor during the Korean War. Eight were from the 7th Infantry Regiment: Jerry K. Crump (6 and 7 September 1951), John Essebagger, Jr. (25 April 1951), Charles L. Gilliland (25 April 1951), Clair Goodblood (24 and 25 April 1951), Noah O. Knight (23 and 24 November 1951), Darwin K. Kyle (16 February 1951), Leroy A. Mendonca (4 July 1951), and Hiroshi H. Miyamura, whose award was <a href="/wiki/Hiroshi_H._Miyamura#Classified" title="Hiroshi H. Miyamura">classified</a> Top Secret until his repatriation (24 and 25 April 1951). Three more recipients were with the 15th Infantry Regiment: Emory L. Bennett (24 June 1951), Ola L. Mize (10 and 11 June 1953) and Charles F. Pendleton (16 and 17 July 1953).</p>
<p>During the Korean War, the division had 2,160 killed in action and 7,939 wounded.</p>
<p>After the armistice, the division remained in Korea until 1954, when it was reduced to near zero strength, the colors were transferred to Fort Benning, Georgia and, in December 1954, the <a href="/wiki/47th_Infantry_Division_(United_States)" title="47th Infantry Division (United States)">47th Infantry Division</a> was reflagged as the Third.<br /></p>
<p>On 1 July 1957, the division was reorganized as a <a href="/wiki/Pentomic" title="Pentomic">Pentomic</a> Division. The division’s three infantry regiments (the <a href="/wiki/7th_Infantry_Regiment_(United_States)" title="7th Infantry Regiment (United States)">7th</a>, <a href="/wiki/15th_Infantry_Regiment_(United_States)" title="15th Infantry Regiment (United States)">15th</a> and <a href="/wiki/30th_Infantry_Regiment_(United_States)" title="30th Infantry Regiment (United States)">30th</a>) were inactivated, with their elements reorganized into five infantry battle groups (the <a href="/wiki/7th_Infantry_Regiment_(United_States)" title="7th Infantry Regiment (United States)">1-7 IN</a>, <a href="/wiki/15th_Infantry_Regiment_(United_States)" title="15th Infantry Regiment (United States)">1-15 IN</a>, <a href="/wiki/30th_Infantry_Regiment_(United_States)" title="30th Infantry Regiment (United States)">1-30 IN</a>, <a href="/wiki/38th_Infantry_Regiment_(United_States)" title="38th Infantry Regiment (United States)">2-38 IN</a> and the <a href="/wiki/4th_Infantry_Regiment_(United_States)" title="4th Infantry Regiment (United States)">2-4 IN</a>).<br /></p>
<p>In April 1958, the division deployed to Germany as part of an <a href="/wiki/Operation_Gyroscope" title="Operation Gyroscope">Operation_Gyroscope</a> rotation (soldiers and families, no equipment), switching places with the <a href="/wiki/10th_Infantry_Division_(United_States)" class="mw-redirect" title="10th Infantry Division (United States)">10th Infantry Division</a> (which was reflagged as the <a href="/wiki/2nd_Infantry_Division_(United_States)" title="2nd Infantry Division (United States)">2nd Infantry Division</a> upon its arrival at Fort Benning).<br /></p>
<p>In 1963, the division was reorganized as a <a href="/wiki/Reorganization_Objective_Army_Division" class="mw-redirect" title="Reorganization Objective Army Division">Reorganization Objective Army Division</a> (ROAD). Three Brigade Headquarters were activated and Infantry units were reorganized into battalions.<br /></p>

<p>The division was stationed with the <a href="/wiki/V_Corps_(United_States)" title="V Corps (United States)">V Corps</a> (1958–63, 1992-1996) and <a href="/wiki/VII_Corps_(United_States)" title="VII Corps (United States)">VII Corps</a> (1963–92) in West Germany from near the Czech border westward throughout various towns including Wūrzburg (Div. Hq. &amp; Support Command), Schweinfurt (1st Brigade), Kitzingen (2nd Brigade), and Aschaffenburg (3rd Brigade). In August 1961, a few days after the Berlin Wall was erected, a reinforced company from the 7th Infantry Regiment (a unit of the 3rd Infantry Division) in full battle gear, was ordered to travel along the <a href="/wiki/Autobahn" title="Autobahn">Autobahn</a> (a major highway) from Aschaffenburg in Bavaria to West Berlin. This was to assert the right of US forces to travel unhindered from West Germany across the western part of East Germany to West Berlin. After the Berlin Wall was built, it was not known if the East German forces would attempt to impede or restrict the movement of US troops when crossing East Germany while trying to reach West Berlin. The unit arrived in West Berlin without incident confirming the right of free passage.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. <p>In November 1990, following <a href="/wiki/Iraq" title="Iraq">Iraq</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Gulf_War" title="Gulf War">invasion of Kuwait</a>, more than 6,000 3rd Infantry Division men and women deployed with the <a href="/wiki/1st_Armored_Division_(United_States)" title="1st Armored Division (United States)">1st Armored Division</a> on <a href="/wiki/Operation_Desert_Storm" class="mw-redirect" title="Operation Desert Storm">Operation Desert Storm</a> as part of the Allied Coalition. They participated in the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Medina_Ridge" title="Battle of Medina Ridge">Battle of Medina Ridge</a> which was the second largest tank battle of the conflict.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> The 3rd Brigade was credited with the destruction of 82 tanks, 31 Armored Personnel Carriers, 11 artillery pieces, 48 trucks, 3 AAA guns and captured 72 EPW's with the loss of 2 Bradley Cavalry vehicles, 30 WIA's and 1 KIA. Later nearly 1,000 soldiers deployed to southeastern Turkey and northern Iraq to provide comfort to Kurdish refugees. In late Spring of 1991, the division supplied senior ranking officers and non-commissioned officers, along with a military police company to Task Force Victory (Forward). Stationed in Kuwait the Task Force was to provide division level support to the 11th Armored Cavalry Regiment (which shared the same duty station). Those elements of V Corps attached to the task force (including those of division) returned to their home units in early September 1991.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>As part of the Army's reduction to a ten-division force, the <a href="/wiki/24th_Infantry_Division_(United_States)" title="24th Infantry Division (United States)">24th Infantry Division</a> was inactivated on 15 February 1996, and reflagged to become the 3rd Infantry Division.</p>
<p>In 1996 the division was redeployed to <a href="/wiki/Fort_Stewart" title="Fort Stewart">Fort Stewart</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fort_Benning" title="Fort Benning">Fort Benning</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Hunter_Army_Airfield" title="Hunter Army Airfield">Hunter Army Airfield</a>, Georgia. The division repeatedly demonstrated its deployability since then by maintaining a battalion, and later a brigade task force presence in Kuwait. It has also moved sizable forces to <a href="/wiki/Egypt" title="Egypt">Egypt</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bosnia_and_Herzegovina" title="Bosnia and Herzegovina">Bosnia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kosovo" title="Kosovo">Kosovo</a> in partnership training and peacekeeping missions.</p>
<p>In 1996–97, the 3rd Infantry Division Detachment, Rear Tactical Operations Center (RTOC), which is a unit manned by the <a href="/wiki/Georgia_Army_National_Guard" title="Georgia Army National Guard">Georgia Army National Guard</a> was mobilized and served in Operation Joint Endeavor. During this time, the 3rd ID RTOC served under the <a href="/wiki/1st_Infantry_Division_(United_States)" title="1st Infantry Division (United States)">1st Infantry Division</a> and later the <a href="/wiki/1st_Armored_Division_(United_States)" title="1st Armored Division (United States)">1st Armored Division</a>. Respectively serving in Bosnia, at Camps Dallas and Angela, near <a href="/wiki/Tuzla" title="Tuzla">Tuzla</a> under the 1ID, and then in Croatia at <a href="/wiki/Slavonski_Brod" title="Slavonski Brod">Slavonski Brod</a>, under the 1AD, serving the Assistant Division Commander for Support, then BG George Casey.</p>

<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="OIF_I">OIF I</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=3rd_Infantry_Division_(United_States)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: OIF I">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Early in 2003 the entire division deployed in weeks to Kuwait. It was called on subsequently to spearhead Coalition forces in <a href="/wiki/Operation_Iraqi_Freedom" class="mw-redirect" title="Operation Iraqi Freedom">Operation Iraqi Freedom</a>, fighting its way to <a href="/wiki/Baghdad" title="Baghdad">Baghdad</a> in early April, leading to the end of the Saddam Hussein government. The First Brigade captured the <a href="/wiki/Baghdad_International_Airport" title="Baghdad International Airport">Baghdad International Airport</a> and cleared and secured the airport, which also resulted in the division's first Medal of Honor since the Korean War, awarded to SFC <a href="/wiki/Paul_Ray_Smith" title="Paul Ray Smith">Paul Ray Smith</a>. Second Brigade, Third Infantry division made the much-publicized "Thunder Run" into downtown Baghdad. The Second Brigade was redeployed to Fallujah, Iraq during the summer of 2003. The division returned to the United States in September 2003.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>

<p>Beginning in 2004, the 3rd began re-organizing. The division shifted from three maneuver brigades to four "units of action", which are essentially smaller brigade formations, with one infantry, one armor, one cavalry, and one artillery battalion in each. The former Engineer Brigade became the 4th Brigade at Fort Stewart. Each of these units of action engaged in several mock battles at the <a href="/wiki/National_Training_Center" class="mw-redirect" title="National Training Center">National Training Center</a> (NTC) and <a href="/wiki/Fort_Polk#JRTC_moves_to_Polk" title="Fort Polk">Joint Readiness Training Center</a> (JRTC), and preparation for a second deployment to Iraq.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="OIF_III">OIF III</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=3rd_Infantry_Division_(United_States)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: OIF III">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In January 2005, the Third Infantry Division became the first Army division to serve a second tour in Iraq.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The division headquarters took control of the Multi-National Division Baghdad, MND-B, headquartered at Camp Liberty and with responsibility for the greater Baghdad area. First and Third Brigades of the Third Infantry Division were placed under control of the <a href="/wiki/42nd_Infantry_Division_(United_States)" title="42nd Infantry Division (United States)">42nd Infantry Division</a>, and later under the 101st Airborne Division, in MND-North. In preparation of this deployment a Fourth Brigade was organized and became the first cohesive <a href="/wiki/Brigade_combat_team" title="Brigade combat team">brigade combat team</a> sent into a combat zone by the US Army, cohesive in that it fulfilled the table of organization requirement of such a unit. The <a href="/wiki/California_Army_National_Guard" title="California Army National Guard">California Army National Guard</a>'s 1st Battalion <a href="/wiki/184th_Infantry_Regiment_(United_States)" title="184th Infantry Regiment (United States)">184th Infantry Regiment</a> served as one of the brigade's two infantry battalions, as well as the detachment from the <a href="/wiki/Hawaii_Army_National_Guard" title="Hawaii Army National Guard">Hawaii Army National Guard</a>'s <a href="/wiki/29th_Brigade_Combat_Team" class="mw-redirect" title="29th Brigade Combat Team">29th Brigade Combat Team</a>, the 2/299th Infantry, also the 48th Brigade Combat Team from the Georgia Army National Guard and Charlie Company, 1st Battalion, 295th Infantry Battalion from the Puerto Rico Army National Guard served in this Operation.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>2/69 Armor was assigned to Camp War Horse in Iraq. By Mid 2005 Primary elements of 2/69 Armor 3rd Brigade 3rd Infantry Division was re-deployed to Ramadi Iraq, replacing elements of the 2nd ID. They ran joint missions with 2nd Mar Div. and elements of the Pennsylvania National Guard.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="OIF_V">OIF V</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=3rd_Infantry_Division_(United_States)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: OIF V">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The division redeployed to Fort Stewart and Fort Benning in January 2006. On 17 November 2006, the Army announced that the Third Infantry Division is scheduled to return to Iraq in 2007 and thus become the first Army division to serve three tours in Iraq. The division headquarters became the leadership organization of MND-C (Multi-National Division Central), a new command established south of Baghdad as part of the <a href="/wiki/Iraq_War_troop_surge_of_2007" title="Iraq War troop surge of 2007">Iraq War troop surge of 2007</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>In support of operations in Baghdad, the unit 3rd Squadron, 7th Cavalry was detached from 3ID and assigned by General Petraeus to 3rd BCT, 82nd Airborne who was under the command of the 1st Cavalry Division. In 2008, 82nd Airborne and 1st CAV redeployed home, and 3–7 CAV was handed over to 3rd BCT, 4th Infantry Division under the command of the 25th Infantry Division. They would remain under this command until 3–7 CAV's redeployment back to Fort Stewart, being reattached to the 3rd Infantry Division.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Similarly, 1st Battalion, <a href="/wiki/64th_Armor_Regiment" title="64th Armor Regiment">64th Armor</a> was detached from 3ID and attached to 2nd BCT, 1st Infantry Division under 1st Cavalry Division, and later under 2nd BCT, 101st Infantry Division under command of 4th Infantry Division.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>

<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Reassignment_of_1st_Brigade">Reassignment of 1st Brigade</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=3rd_Infantry_Division_(United_States)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Reassignment of 1st Brigade">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In the fall of 2008, the 3rd Infantry Division's 1st Brigade was assigned to serve as the on-call federal response force under the control of <a href="/wiki/NORTHCOM" class="mw-redirect" title="NORTHCOM">NORTHCOM</a>, the combatant command assigned responsibility for the continental United States. The brigade remained at its home station of <a href="/wiki/Fort_Stewart" title="Fort Stewart">Fort Stewart</a>, Georgia, and "is training to deploy domestically in response to terrorist attacks or other national emergencies."<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> The brigade will be trained in responding to WMD attacks, crowd control, and dealing with civil unrest.<sup id="cite_ref-Cavallaro_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cavallaro-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>The force was renamed "Chemical, biological, radiological, nuclear, or high-yield explosive Consequence Management Response Force". Its acronym, CCMRF, is pronounced "see-smurf",<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> and the unit is now under the daily control of <a href="/wiki/United_States_Northern_Command" title="United States Northern Command">United States Northern Command</a>'s Army North, whose mission is to "protect the United States homeland and support local, state, and federal authorities."<sup id="cite_ref-Cavallaro_20-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cavallaro-20">[20]</a></sup> The unit is a multi-branch force with servicemembers from the four branches of the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Defense" title="United States Department of Defense">United States Department of Defense</a>.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Reorganization_of_4th_Brigade">Reorganization of 4th Brigade</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=3rd_Infantry_Division_(United_States)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Reorganization of 4th Brigade">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In March 2009, 4th Brigade reorganized from a mechanized or heavy brigade to a light infantry brigade. As part of this reorganization, <a href="/wiki/4th_Battalion,_64th_Armor" class="mw-redirect" title="4th Battalion, 64th Armor">4th Battalion, 64th Armor</a> was reflagged as 3rd Battalion, <a href="/wiki/15th_Infantry_Regiment_(United_States)" title="15th Infantry Regiment (United States)">15th Infantry Regiment</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>

<p>The 3rd Infantry Division assumed command of the <a href="/wiki/MNF-I" class="mw-redirect" title="MNF-I">Multi-National</a> Division-North, <a href="/wiki/USF-I" class="mw-redirect" title="USF-I">now United States</a> Division-North, in October 2009. This milestone marked the division’s fourth tour in support of <a href="/wiki/Operation_Iraqi_Freedom" class="mw-redirect" title="Operation Iraqi Freedom">Operation Iraqi Freedom</a> (I, III, V, and VII). The division has elements operating in every area of Iraq as the mission changes from Operation Iraqi Freedom to <a href="/wiki/Iraq_War" title="Iraq War">Operation New Dawn</a> on 1 September 2010. With the advent of Operation New Dawn, the focus will shift from combat operations to stability and advise and assist operations throughout all Iraq’s provinces.</p>
<p>In the course of <a href="/wiki/Operation_Iraqi_Freedom" class="mw-redirect" title="Operation Iraqi Freedom">Operation Iraqi Freedom</a> up until 24 September 2010, 436 members of the division were killed in action<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>

<p>The Combat Aviation Brigade, 3rd Infantry Division deployed to Afghanistan for a 13-month tour. The brigade was the first unit from 3ID to deploy to Afghanistan. During that tour 3rd CAB soldiers flew about 26,000 missions, including 800 air assaults, and were responsible for about 2,500 enemy casualties. No soldiers from the brigade were killed during the tour.</p>

<p>3rd CAB is slated to deploy to Afghanistan again in January 2013. The 2500 soldiers will deploy with 3rd Special Troops Battalion for a 9-month tour. The Marne Air will be operating out of Kandahar Airfield in the <a href="/wiki/Train_Advise_Assist_Command_-_South" class="mw-redirect" title="Train Advise Assist Command - South">RC-South</a> area-of-operations, relieving the 25th CAB.</p>
<p>Both the 3rd CAB and 3rd STB will fall under their parent division when the 3ID Headquarters and Headquarters Battalion deploys in August and takes over command of RC-South from 82nd Airborne Division HQ.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<p>The 2nd Heavy BCT's two combined-arms battalions also deployed individually to Afghanistan. 1st Battalion, 64th Armor Regiment deployed in March 2012. They are attached to the 3rd Stryker Brigade, <a href="/wiki/2nd_Infantry_Division_(United_States)" title="2nd Infantry Division (United States)">2nd Infantry Division (United States)</a> from Joint Base Lewis-McChord, WA, to help train Afghanistan National Security Forces to take over in their country’s security operations.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> 1st Battalion, 30th Infantry Regiment deployed a month earlier. They are tasked with providing security to units conducting contingency operations. Both battalions will serve nine-month tour.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<p>In December 2012, the 3rd Sustainment Brigade deployed for its fifth deployment over the last decade and first to Afghanistan, for nine months in support of Operation Enduring Freedom 12-13, let by COL Ron Novack and CSM Daniels. Deployed to Kandahar with 276 soldiers the brigade provided sustainment and retrograde support to the 3rd and 4th Infantry Divisions, 1st and 2nd Marine Expeditionary Forces, and International Security Assistance Forces operating in Regional Commands South, Southwest, and National Support Element-West. The brigade assisted in the closure and transfer of over 61 Forward Operating Bases while simultaneously providing sustainment to the force. Additionally, the 3rd Sustainment Brigade provided direct support to the 1st and 2nd Brigade Combat Teams and the Combat Aviation Brigade of the 3rd Infantry Division.</p>
<p>In February 2013, the 4th Infantry Brigade Combat Team, 3rd Infantry Division (later reflagged as the 2nd Infantry Brigade Combat Team, 3rd Infantry Division), deployed to Logar Province and Wardak Province, Afghanistan in support of Operation Enduring Freedom. 6th Squadron, 8th Cavalry Regiment was tasked with securing Logar Province, and disrupting the almost daily rocket attacks on Forward Operation Base Shank.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> 3rd Battalion, 15th Infantry Regiment was tasked with securing Wardak Province's highly volatile Highway 1. The Soldier's of 4th Infantry Brigade Combat Team, 3rd Infantry Division returned home in late November 2013 after serving a 9-month tour.</p>
<h4><span id="Operation_Freedom's_Sentinel"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Operation_Freedom.27s_Sentinel">Operation Freedom's Sentinel</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=3rd_Infantry_Division_(United_States)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Operation Freedom's Sentinel">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In April 2017, <i>Military.com</i> reported that about 200 soldiers from the 3rd Infantry Division Headquarters will deploy to Afghanistan to replace the <a href="/wiki/1st_Cavalry_Division_(United_States)" title="1st Cavalry Division (United States)">1st Cavalry Division</a> Headquarters at Bagram Airfield taking over command of the U.S. Forces-Afghanistan's National Support Element, as part of <a href="/wiki/War_in_Afghanistan_(2001%E2%80%93present)#Post_ISAF_phase" title="War in Afghanistan (2001–present)">Operation Freedom's Sentinel</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Operation_Atlantic_Resolve">Operation Atlantic Resolve</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=3rd_Infantry_Division_(United_States)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Operation Atlantic Resolve">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In February 2015, <i>ArmyTimes</i> reported that More than 3,000 soldiers from the 1st Heavy Brigade Combat Team, 3rd Combat Aviation Brigade, 3rd ID's Artillery and other units of the 3rd Infantry Division began a three-month deployment to Europe in March 2015 in support of <a href="/wiki/Operation_Atlantic_Resolve" title="Operation Atlantic Resolve">Operation Atlantic Resolve</a>. Soldiers from 3rd ID deployed to various European countries including Germany, Estonia, Latvia, Lithuania, Poland, Romania and Bulgaria with the 1st Brigade acting as the European Rotational Force and NATO Response Force, which works and trains with NATO allies to remain prepared for contingency operations within the European Command's area of responsibility.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p>

<p>The 2nd Armored Brigade Combat Team deactivated on 15 January 2015 as part of the Army's modular brigade reorganization. Special Troops Battalion; 3rd Squadron, 7th Cavalry Regiment; 1st Battalion, 9th Field Artillery Regiment; and 26th Brigade Support Battalion were deactivated concurrently, with some of their companies joining other brigades' battalions. The maneuver battalions of 2nd Armored Brigade Combat Team moved to other brigades in the division: 1st Battalion, 64th Armor Regiment moved to 1st Armored Brigade Combat Team and 1st Battalion, 30th Infantry Regiment moved to 4th Infantry Brigade Combat Team. With the deactivation of 2nd Armored Brigade Combat Team, 4th Infantry Brigade Combat Team was reflagged as the 2nd Infantry Brigade Combat Team. After the reorganization, the division had three BCTs, each with three maneuver battalions.</p>
<p>The 42nd Fires Brigade was activated at Fort Stewart on 17 October 2013 and later reflagged as 3rd Infantry Division Artillery. Division Artillery has training oversight of the division's artillery battalions, although the battalions remain organic to their respective BCTs.</p>
<p>As part of budget driven downsizing, the Army announced that it would inactivate the 3rd Brigade Combat Team by the end of 2015 and replace it with a Battalion Task Force centered around the 1st Battalion, 28th Infantry. The 3rd Brigade Combat Team inactivated on 15 December 2015.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Division_Artillery">Division Artillery</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=3rd_Infantry_Division_(United_States)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Division Artillery">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul>
<li>Constituted 12 November 1917 in the Regular Army as Headquarters, 3rd Field Artillery Brigade, and assigned to the 3rd Division</li>
<li>Organized 26 November 1917 at <a href="/wiki/Camp_Stanley" title="Camp Stanley">Camp Stanley</a>, Texas</li>
<li>Disbanded 16 October 1939 at <a href="/wiki/Fort_Lewis" title="Fort Lewis">Fort Lewis</a>, Washington</li>
<li>Reconstituted 1 October 1940 in the Regular Army as Headquarters and Headquarters Battery, 3rd Division Artillery, and activated at Fort Lewis, Washington</li>
<li>Redesignated 1 July 1957 as Headquarters and Headquarters Battery, 3rd Infantry Division Artillery</li>
</ul>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Combat_Aviation_Brigade">Combat Aviation Brigade</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=3rd_Infantry_Division_(United_States)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: Combat Aviation Brigade">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Constituted 16 March 1985 in the Regular Army as Headquarters and Headquarters Company, Aviation Brigade, 3rd Infantry Division, and activated in Germany.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup></p>
<p>Deployed to Afghanistan in November 2009 and returned home in November 2010.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Division_Band">Division Band</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=3rd_Infantry_Division_(United_States)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=27" title="Edit section: Division Band">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul>
<li>Constituted 20 August 1943 in the Regular Army as the Band, 3d Infantry Division</li>
<li>Redesignated 1 December 1943 as the 3d Infantry Division Band and activated in North Africa</li>
<li>Consolidated 20 March 1963 with Headquarters and Headquarters Detachment, 3d Infantry Division Trains, and consolidated unit reorganized and redesignated as Headquarters, Headquarters and Band, 3d Infantry Division Support Command.</li>
<li>Reorganized and redesignated 15 March 1968 as Headquarters, Headquarters Company and Band, 3d Infantry Division Support Command.</li>
<li>Band element withdrawn 21 May 1972 from Headquarters, Headquarters Company and Band, 3d Infantry Division Support Command, and absorbed by the 3d Adjutant General Company</li>
<li>Band element withdrawn 1 October 1984 from the 3d Adjutant General Company and redesignated as the 3d Infantry Division Band.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
