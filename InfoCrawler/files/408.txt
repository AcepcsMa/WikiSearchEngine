<p><b>Frederick William II</b> (<a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a>: <span lang="de" xml:lang="de"><i>Friedrich Wilhelm&#160;II.</i></span>; 25 September 1744 – 16 November 1797) was King of <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Prussia" title="Kingdom of Prussia">Prussia</a>, from 1786 until his death. He was in <a href="/wiki/Personal_union" title="Personal union">personal union</a> the <a href="/wiki/Prince-elector" title="Prince-elector">Prince-elector</a> of <a href="/wiki/Electorate_of_Brandenburg" class="mw-redirect" title="Electorate of Brandenburg">Brandenburg</a> and (via the Orange-Nassau inheritance of his grandfather) sovereign prince of the <a href="/wiki/Canton_of_Neuch%C3%A2tel" title="Canton of Neuchâtel">Canton of Neuchâtel</a>. Pleasure-loving and indolent, he is seen as the antithesis to his predecessor, <a href="/wiki/Frederick_the_Great" title="Frederick the Great">Frederick II</a>. Under his reign, Prussia was weakened internally and externally, and he failed to deal adequately with the challenges to the existing order posed by the <a href="/wiki/French_Revolution" title="French Revolution">French Revolution</a>. His religious policies were directed against the <a href="/wiki/Age_of_Enlightenment" title="Age of Enlightenment">Enlightenment</a> and aimed at restoring a traditional <a href="/wiki/Protestantism" title="Protestantism">Protestantism</a>. However, he was a patron of the arts and responsible for the construction of some notable buildings, among them the <a href="/wiki/Brandenburg_Gate" title="Brandenburg Gate">Brandenburg Gate</a> in Berlin.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Frederick_William_II_of_Prussia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Frederick William was born in <a href="/wiki/Berlin" title="Berlin">Berlin</a>, the son of <a href="/wiki/Prince_Augustus_William_of_Prussia" title="Prince Augustus William of Prussia">Prince Augustus William of Prussia</a> (the second son of King <a href="/wiki/Frederick_William_I_of_Prussia" title="Frederick William I of Prussia">Frederick William&#160;I of Prussia</a>) and of <a href="/wiki/Duchess_Luise_of_Brunswick-Wolfenb%C3%BCttel" title="Duchess Luise of Brunswick-Wolfenbüttel">Duchess Luise of Brunswick-Wolfenbüttel</a>. His mother's elder sister, <a href="/wiki/Elisabeth_Christine_of_Brunswick-Wolfenb%C3%BCttel-Bevern" title="Elisabeth Christine of Brunswick-Wolfenbüttel-Bevern">Elisabeth</a>, was the wife of Augustus William's brother King <a href="/wiki/Frederick_the_Great" title="Frederick the Great">Frederick&#160;II</a> ("Frederick the Great"). Frederick William became <a href="/wiki/Heir-presumptive" class="mw-redirect" title="Heir-presumptive">heir-presumptive</a> to the throne of <a href="/wiki/Prussia" title="Prussia">Prussia</a> on his father's death in 1758, since Frederick&#160;II had no children. The boy was of an easy-going and pleasure-loving disposition, averse to sustained effort of any kind, and sensual by nature.</p>
<p>His marriage with <a href="/wiki/Elisabeth_Christine_of_Brunswick-Wolfenb%C3%BCttel,_Crown_Princess_of_Prussia" title="Elisabeth Christine of Brunswick-Wolfenbüttel, Crown Princess of Prussia">Elisabeth Christine of Brunswick-Wolfenbüttel, Crown Princess of Prussia</a>, daughter of <a href="/wiki/Charles_I,_Duke_of_Brunswick-Wolfenb%C3%BCttel" title="Charles I, Duke of Brunswick-Wolfenbüttel">Charles&#160;I, Duke of Brunswick-Lüneburg</a>, contracted 14&#160;July 1765 in <a href="/wiki/Charlottenburg" title="Charlottenburg">Charlottenburg</a>, was dissolved in 1769. He then married <a href="/wiki/Frederika_Louisa_of_Hesse-Darmstadt" title="Frederika Louisa of Hesse-Darmstadt">Frederika Louisa of Hesse-Darmstadt</a>, daughter of <a href="/wiki/Louis_IX,_Landgrave_of_Hesse-Darmstadt" title="Louis IX, Landgrave of Hesse-Darmstadt">Ludwig&#160;IX, Landgrave of Hesse-Darmstadt</a> on 14&#160;July 1769 also in Charlottenburg. Although he had seven children by his second wife, he had an ongoing relationship with his mistress, <a href="/wiki/Wilhelmine,_Gr%C3%A4fin_von_Lichtenau" title="Wilhelmine, Gräfin von Lichtenau">Wilhelmine Enke</a> (created Countess Wilhelmine von Lichtenau in 1796), a woman of strong intellect and much ambition, and had five children by her—the first when she was still in her teens.</p>

<p>Frederick William, before the corpulence of his middle age, was a man of singularly handsome presence, not without mental qualities of a high order; he was devoted to the arts — <a href="/wiki/Ludwig_van_Beethoven" title="Ludwig van Beethoven">Beethoven</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wolfgang_Amadeus_Mozart" title="Wolfgang Amadeus Mozart">Mozart</a> enjoyed his patronage, and his private orchestra had a Europe-wide reputation. He also was a talented <a href="/wiki/Cello" title="Cello">cellist</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Kings_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kings-1">[1]</a></sup> But an artistic temperament was hardly what was required of a king of Prussia on the eve of the <a href="/wiki/French_Revolution" title="French Revolution">French Revolution</a>, and Frederick the Great, who had employed him in various services (notably in an abortive confidential mission to the court of Russia in 1780), openly expressed his misgivings as to the character of the prince and his surroundings. For his part, Frederick William, who had never been properly introduced to diplomacy and the business of rulership, resented his uncle for not taking him seriously.<sup id="cite_ref-Kings_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kings-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reign">Reign</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Frederick_William_II_of_Prussia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Reign">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The misgivings of Frederick II appear justified in retrospect. Frederick William′s accession to the throne (17 August 1786) was, indeed, followed by a series of measures for lightening the burdens of the people, reforming the oppressive French system of tax-collecting introduced by Frederick, and encouraging trade by the diminution of customs dues and the making of roads and canals. This gave the new king much popularity with the masses; the educated classes were pleased by his removal of Frederick's ban on the <a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German language</a>, with the admission of German writers to the <a href="/wiki/Prussian_Academy_of_Arts" title="Prussian Academy of Arts">Prussian Academy</a>, and by the active encouragement given to schools and universities. Frederick William also terminated his predecessor's state monopolies for coffee and tobacco<sup id="cite_ref-Berlin_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Berlin-2">[2]</a></sup> and the sugar monopoly.<sup id="cite_ref-Chronik_1786_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Chronik_1786-3">[3]</a></sup> However, under his reign the codification known as <i><a href="/wiki/General_State_Laws_for_the_Prussian_States" title="General State Laws for the Prussian States">Allgemeines Preußisches Landrecht</a></i>, initiated by Frederick II, continued and was completed in 1794.<sup id="cite_ref-Chronik_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Chronik-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mysticism_and_religious_policies">Mysticism and religious policies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Frederick_William_II_of_Prussia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Mysticism and religious policies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1781 Frederick William, then prince of Prussia, inclined to <a href="/wiki/Mysticism" title="Mysticism">mysticism</a>, had joined the <a href="/wiki/Rosicrucianism" title="Rosicrucianism">Rosicrucians</a>, and had fallen under the influence of <a href="/wiki/Johann_Christoph_von_W%C3%B6llner" title="Johann Christoph von Wöllner">Johann Christoph von Wöllner</a> and Johann Rudolf von Bischoffwerder. On 26 August 1786 Wöllner was appointed privy councillor for finance (<i>Geheimer Oberfinanzrath</i>), and on 2 October 1786 was ennobled. Though not in name, he in fact became prime minister; in all internal affairs it was he who decided; and the fiscal and economic reforms of the new reign were the application of his theories. Bischoffswerder, too, still a simple major, was called into the king′s counsels; by 1789 he was already an adjutant-general. The opposition to Wöllner was, indeed, at the outset strong enough to prevent his being entrusted with the department of religion; but this too in time was overcome, and on 3 July 1788 he was appointed active privy councillor of state and of justice and head of the spiritual department for <a href="/wiki/Lutheran" class="mw-redirect" title="Lutheran">Lutheran</a> and Catholic affairs. From this position Wöllner pursued long lasting reforms concerning religion in the Prussian state.</p>
<p>The king proved eager to aid Wöllner's crusade. On 9 July 1788 the famous religious edict was issued, which forbade <a href="/wiki/Evangelicalism" title="Evangelicalism">Evangelical</a> ministers from teaching anything not contained in the letter of their official books, proclaimed the necessity of protecting the Christian <a href="/wiki/Religion" title="Religion">religion</a> against the "enlighteners" (<i>Aufklärer</i>), and placed educational establishments under the supervision of the orthodox <a href="/wiki/Clergy" title="Clergy">clergy</a>. On 18 December 1788 a new censorship law was issued, to secure the orthodoxy of all published books. This forced major Berlin journals like <a href="/wiki/Christoph_Friedrich_Nicolai" title="Christoph Friedrich Nicolai">Christoph Friedrich Nicolai</a>'s <i>Allgemeine Deutsche Bibliothek</i> and <a href="/wiki/Johann_Erich_Biester" title="Johann Erich Biester">Johann Erich Biester</a>'s <i>Berliner Monatsschrift</i> to publish only outside the Prussian borders. Moreover, people like <a href="/wiki/Immanuel_Kant" title="Immanuel Kant">Immanuel Kant</a> were forbidden to speak in public on the topic of religion.<sup id="cite_ref-Chronik_1786_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Chronik_1786-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Finally, in 1791, a Protestant commission was established at Berlin (<i>Immediate-Examinationscommission</i>) to watch over all ecclesiastical and scholastic appointments. Although Wöllner's religious edict had many critics, it was an important measure which, in fact, proved an important stabilizing factor for the Prussian state. Aimed at protecting the multi-confessional rights enshrined in the 1648 <a href="/wiki/Peace_of_Westphalia" title="Peace of Westphalia">Peace of Westphalia</a>, the provisions of Wöllner's edict were intended to safeguard against religious strife by imposing a system of state sponsored limits.<sup id="cite_ref-Iron_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Iron-5">[5]</a></sup> The edict was also a notable step forward regarding the rights of <a href="/wiki/Jews" title="Jews">Jews</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mennonites" class="mw-redirect" title="Mennonites">Mennonites</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Moravian_Church" title="Moravian Church">Herrnhut</a> brethren, who now received full state protection.<sup id="cite_ref-Iron_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Iron-5">[5]</a></sup> Given the confessional divides within Prussian society, primarily between <a href="/wiki/Calvinists" class="mw-redirect" title="Calvinists">Calvinists</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lutherans" class="mw-redirect" title="Lutherans">Lutherans</a> but increasingly <a href="/wiki/Catholics" class="mw-redirect" title="Catholics">Catholics</a> as well, such a policy was important for maintaining a stable civil society.</p>
<p>In his zeal for establishing Prussia as a paragon of stable Christian statehood, Frederick William outstripped his minister; he even blamed Wöllner′s "idleness and vanity" for the inevitable failure of the attempt to regulate opinion from above, and in 1794 deprived him of one of his secular offices in order that he might have more time "to devote himself to the things of God"; in edict after edict the king continued to the end of his reign to make regulations "in order to maintain in his states a true and active Christianity, as the path to genuine fear of God".</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Foreign_policies">Foreign policies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Frederick_William_II_of_Prussia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Foreign policies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>But far more fateful for Prussia was the king's attitude towards the army and foreign policy. The army was the very foundation of the Prussian state, a truth which both Frederick William I and Frederick the Great had fully realised; the army had been their first care, and its efficiency had been maintained by their constant personal supervision. Frederick William, who had no taste for military matters, put his authority as "<a href="/wiki/Warlord" title="Warlord">Warlord</a>" (<i>Kriegsherr</i>) into commission under a supreme college of war (<i>Oberkriegs-Collegium</i>) under the <a href="/wiki/Charles_William_Ferdinand,_Duke_of_Brunswick" class="mw-redirect" title="Charles William Ferdinand, Duke of Brunswick">Duke of Brunswick</a> and General <a href="/wiki/Wichard_Joachim_Heinrich_von_M%C3%B6llendorf" title="Wichard Joachim Heinrich von Möllendorf">Wichard Joachim Heinrich von Möllendorf</a>. It was the beginning of the process that ended in 1806 at the disastrous <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Jena" class="mw-redirect" title="Battle of Jena">Battle of Jena</a>. Although the Prussian army reached its highest peacetime level of manpower under Frederick William II (189,000 infantry and 48,000 cavalry), under his reign the Prussian state treasury incurred a substantial debt and the quality of the troops' training deteriorated.<sup id="cite_ref-Chronik_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Chronik-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Under the circumstances, Frederick William′s interventions in European affairs were of little benefit to Prussia. The <a href="/wiki/Dutch_Patriot_Revolt,_1787" class="mw-redirect" title="Dutch Patriot Revolt, 1787">Dutch campaign of 1787</a>, entered into for purely family reasons, was indeed successful, but Prussia received not even the cost of her intervention. An attempt to intervene in the <a href="/wiki/Austro-Turkish_War_(1787%E2%80%9391)" class="mw-redirect" title="Austro-Turkish War (1787–91)">war</a> of <a href="/wiki/Russian_Empire" title="Russian Empire">Russia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Habsburg_Monarchy" title="Habsburg Monarchy">Austria</a> against the <a href="/wiki/Ottoman_Empire" title="Ottoman Empire">Ottoman Empire</a> failed to achieve its objective; Prussia did not succeed in obtaining any concessions of territory, and the dismissal of minister <a href="/wiki/Ewald_Friedrich,_Count_von_Hertzberg" class="mw-redirect" title="Ewald Friedrich, Count von Hertzberg">Hertzberg</a> (5 July 1791) marked the final abandonment of the anti-Austrian tradition of Frederick the Great.</p>
<p>Meanwhile, the <a href="/wiki/French_Revolution" title="French Revolution">French Revolution</a> alarmed the ruling monarchs of Europe, and in August 1791 Frederick William, at the meeting at <a href="/wiki/Pillnitz_Castle" title="Pillnitz Castle">Pillnitz Castle</a>, agreed with Emperor <a href="/wiki/Leopold_II,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Leopold II, Holy Roman Emperor">Leopold II</a> to join in supporting the cause of King <a href="/wiki/Louis_XVI_of_France" title="Louis XVI of France">Louis XVI of France</a>. However the king's character and the confusion of the Prussian finances could not sustain effective action in this regard. A formal alliance was indeed signed on 7 February 1792, and Frederick William took part personally in the <a href="/wiki/War_of_the_First_Coalition" title="War of the First Coalition">campaigns of 1792 and 1793</a>, but the king was hampered by want of funds, and his counsels were distracted by the affairs of a deteriorating <a href="/wiki/Polish-Lithuanian_Commonwealth" class="mw-redirect" title="Polish-Lithuanian Commonwealth">Poland</a>, which promised a richer booty than was likely to be gained by the anti-revolutionary crusade into France. A <a href="/wiki/Subsidy" title="Subsidy">subsidy</a> treaty with the sea powers (Great Britain and the Netherlands, signed at The Hague, 19 April 1794) filled Prussia's coffers, but at the cost of a promise to supply 64,000 land troops to the coalition. The insurrection in Poland that followed the <a href="/wiki/Second_Partition_of_Poland" title="Second Partition of Poland">partition</a> of 1793, and the threat of unilateral intervention by Russia, drove Frederick William into the separate <a href="/wiki/Peace_of_Basel" title="Peace of Basel">Treaty of Basel</a> with the French Republic (5 April 1795), which was regarded by the other great monarchies as a betrayal, and left Prussia morally isolated in the struggle between the <a href="/wiki/Monarch" title="Monarch">monarchical</a> principle and the new <a href="/wiki/Republic" title="Republic">republican</a> creed of the Revolution. Although the land area of the Prussian state reached a new peak under his rule after the <a href="/wiki/Third_partition_of_Poland" class="mw-redirect" title="Third partition of Poland">third partition of Poland</a> in 1795, the new territories included parts of Poland such as Warsaw that had virtually no German population, severely straining administrative resources.<sup id="cite_ref-Berlin_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Berlin-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<p>Frederick William's first marriage, to Elisabeth Christine of Brunswick (his first cousin) had ended after four years during which both spouses had been unfaithful. Their uncle, Frederick II, granted a divorce reluctantly, as he was more fond of Elisabeth than of Frederick William.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> His second marriage lasted until his death, but he continued his relationship with Wilhelmine Enke. In 1794–1797 he had a castle built for her on the <a href="/wiki/Pfaueninsel" title="Pfaueninsel">Pfaueninsel</a>. Moreover, he was involved in two more (bigamist) <a href="/wiki/Morganatic_marriages" class="mw-redirect" title="Morganatic marriages">morganatic marriages</a>: with <a href="/wiki/Julie_von_Vo%C3%9F" class="mw-redirect" title="Julie von Voß">Elisabeth Amalie, Gräfin von Voß, Gräfin von Ingenheim</a> in 1787 and (after her death in 1789) with <a href="/wiki/Sophie_von_D%C3%B6nhoff" title="Sophie von Dönhoff">Sophie Juliane Gräfin von Dönhoff</a>. He had another seven children with those two women, which explains why his people also called him <i>der Vielgeliebte</i> ("the much loved") and <i>der dicke Lüderjahn</i> ("the fat scallywag").<sup id="cite_ref-Kings_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kings-1">[1]</a></sup> His favourite son—with Wilhelmine Enke—was Graf Alexander von der Mark.<sup id="cite_ref-Berlin_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Berlin-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Other buildings constructed under his reign were the <a href="/wiki/Marmorpalais" title="Marmorpalais">Marmorpalais</a> in Potsdam and the world-famous <a href="/wiki/Brandenburger_Tor" class="mw-redirect" title="Brandenburger Tor">Brandenburger Tor</a> in Berlin.<sup id="cite_ref-Kings_1-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kings-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>On 16 November 1797, Frederick William II died in <a href="/wiki/Potsdam" title="Potsdam">Potsdam</a>. He was succeeded by his son, <a href="/wiki/Frederick_William_III_of_Prussia" title="Frederick William III of Prussia">Frederick William III</a>, who had resented his father's lifestyle and acted swiftly to deal with what he considered the immoral state of the court. Frederick William II is buried in the <a href="/wiki/Berliner_Dom" class="mw-redirect" title="Berliner Dom">Berliner Dom</a>.</p>

<p>Frederick William II had the following children:</p>
<ul>
<li>By Elisabeth Christine of Brunswick-Lüneburg:
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Princess_Frederica_Charlotte_of_Prussia" title="Princess Frederica Charlotte of Prussia">Frederica Charlotte</a> (1767–1820), who became Duchess of York by her marriage to <a href="/wiki/Frederick,_Duke_of_York" class="mw-redirect" title="Frederick, Duke of York">Frederick, Duke of York</a></li>
</ul>
</li>
<li>By Frederika Louisa of Hesse-Darmstadt:
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Frederick_William_III_of_Prussia" title="Frederick William III of Prussia">Frederick William III of Prussia</a> (1770–1840)</li>
<li>Christine (1772–73)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Prince_Louis_Charles_of_Prussia" title="Prince Louis Charles of Prussia">Louis</a> (1773–96)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Wilhelmine_of_Prussia,_Queen_of_the_Netherlands" title="Wilhelmine of Prussia, Queen of the Netherlands">Frederika Louisa Wilhelmina</a> (1774–1837), wife of William of Orange, afterwards King <a href="/wiki/William_I_of_the_Netherlands" title="William I of the Netherlands">William I of the Netherlands</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Princess_Augusta_of_Prussia" title="Princess Augusta of Prussia">Augusta</a> (1780–1841), wife of <a href="/wiki/William_II,_Elector_of_Hesse" title="William II, Elector of Hesse">William II, Elector of Hesse</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Prince_Henry_of_Prussia_(1781%E2%80%931846)" title="Prince Henry of Prussia (1781–1846)">Henry (1781–1846)</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Prince_Wilhelm_of_Prussia_(1783%E2%80%931851)" title="Prince Wilhelm of Prussia (1783–1851)">William</a> (1783–1851)</li>
</ul>
</li>
</ul>
